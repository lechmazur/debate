It does not.
[C1.2] First, the affected population is vanishingly small. Severe inherited mitochondrial disease suitable for MRT prevention affects a tiny fraction of births. In any given country, the number of women who are both carriers of severe mitochondrial mutations and seeking to conceive may number in the dozens annually. Compare that to the millions affected by preventable cardiovascular disease, diabetes, maternal mortality, or mental health crises that remain chronically underfunded.
[C1.3] Directing scarce public resources toward an intervention serving so few people, when proven cost-effective interventions for common diseases remain unfunded, is a failure of prioritization.
[C1.4] Second, MRT remains experimental in a meaningful sense. The technique has produced very few live births worldwide. We lack long-term data on the health of individuals conceived through MRT—potential complications from mitochondrial-nuclear genome incompatibility, epigenetic disruption, or reversion to mutant mitochondrial DNA may take years or decades to manifest. Public health systems should not commit ongoing funding to a procedure whose long-term safety profile is essentially unknown.
[C1.5] Third, MRT constitutes heritable germline modification. Changes introduced through MRT are passed to all future descendants. This crosses an ethical threshold that most international frameworks on human genetic modification have deliberately avoided crossing. Publicly funding this practice normalizes germline engineering before society has adequately deliberated its implications.
[C1.6] Fourth, alternatives already exist. Preimplantation genetic diagnosis, egg donation, and adoption all allow affected families to have children free of mitochondrial disease without requiring germline modification or experimental techniques.
[C1.7] The proposition asks us to prioritize a costly, experimental, ethically fraught intervention benefiting extremely few people over proven measures that could save and improve far more lives. That is not sound public health policy.
