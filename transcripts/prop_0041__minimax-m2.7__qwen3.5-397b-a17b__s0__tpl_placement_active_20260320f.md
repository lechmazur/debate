[P1.1] Personalized dynamic pricing based on willingness to pay is a fundamentally predatory practice that transforms the market from a system of exchange into a system of extraction. When a retailer adjusts its price based on what it believes a specific customer can bear, it stops selling a product and starts exploiting a person.
[P1.2] Consider what this actually looks like in practice. The same retailer, the same product, the same moment—but a customer who has been identified as financially stressed, as having few nearby alternatives, or as desperately needing an item pays more. Not because supply is tighter. Not because demand is higher.
[P1.3] But because the algorithm has identified that this particular person is easier to squeeze.
[P1.4] This is not dynamic pricing in any legitimate sense. Airlines charge more for last-minute bookings because time genuinely affects scarcity. Surge pricing during peak demand reflects real supply constraints. These mechanisms charge more when the transaction itself is more costly to fulfill.
[P1.5] Personalized willingness-to-pay pricing charges more because the buyer is more vulnerable—and that distinction is everything.
[P1.6] The harm here is not merely economic. When pricing becomes personalized based on perceived ability to bear, it weaponizes the intimate data retailers have collected about individuals' financial circumstances, shopping habits, and desperation levels. A family struggling with medical bills who searches for essential supplies should not face a higher price than a wealthier household browsing the same items. Yet this is precisely what willingness-to-pay algorithms enable.
[P1.7] I would also note the transparency problem compounds the injustice. These price adjustments are hidden. Customers do not know they are being charged a personalized rate, cannot easily discover what others pay for identical goods, and therefore cannot make informed choices or comparison shop effectively. The market's self-correcting mechanism—consumer knowledge—has been deliberately disabled.
[P1.8] Banning this practice does not prevent retailers from competing, innovating, or using legitimate pricing strategies. It simply restores a basic principle: that the price you pay reflects the value of what you buy, not the depth of your pockets.

[C1.7] Ultimately, this ban protects competitors, not consumers. It favors large incumbents who can survive on thin uniform margins over agile retailers.
