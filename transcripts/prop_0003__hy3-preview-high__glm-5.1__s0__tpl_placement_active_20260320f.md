[P1.1] The defining urban crisis of our era is acute housing scarcity that has priced out working families, pushed essential workers to commute hours from their jobs, and entrenched segregation. Relaxing those protections where they block needed density is not an attack on cultural heritage, but a necessary prioritization of human welfare over aesthetic preservation.
[P1.2] Most historic districts extend far beyond truly irreplaceable landmarks. They routinely cover blocks of unremarkable mid-century commercial strips, cookie-cutter early 20th century rowhouses, and utilitarian industrial buildings that add no unique cultural value to the city. Preserving these structures at the cost of thousands of potential homes is a disproportionate sacrifice. In many high-cost cities, historic district boundaries have been expanded explicitly to block multi-family development, weaponizing preservation to protect property values of incumbent homeowners.
[P1.3] We are not proposing the demolition of iconic city halls or landmark theaters, but the removal of artificial density caps on blocks where added housing would alleviate acute shortage.
[P1.4] The tradeoff of losing some architecturally significant buildings is far outweighed by the human benefit of added density. A single mid-rise apartment complex can house hundreds of families, essential workers, and young people priced out of the city center. That is a concrete, life-improving public good that outweighs the preservation of a handful of buildings that could otherwise be adaptively reused or replaced with design that respects the neighborhood's character without capping growth. Preservationists often frame any density increase as a threat to neighborhood identity, but identity is defined by the people who live there, not the age of the brick and mortar.
[P1.5] Cities exist first to serve the people who live in them, not to preserve static snapshots of the past at the expense of those who need homes today.
