[P1.1] Cyberattacks on civilian critical infrastructure are not virtual nuisances; they are acts of sabotage that can kill, disrupt, and terrorize on the scale of bombs and bullets. A coordinated strike that collapses a power grid in midwinter, halts hospital ventilators, or poisons water-treatment systems produces precisely the civilian harm that international law has always sought to deter. The means—lines of code instead of explosives—do not lessen the injury. Effects, not delivery system, must govern the legal classification.
[P1.2] Treating such attacks as mere cyber espionage or economic disruption grants aggressors a safe domain to wage war without fear of meaningful response. States already accept that a physical raid on a dam or an air strike on an electrical substation triggers Article 51 self-defense rights. A cyber operation achieving the identical outcome must trigger the same rights; otherwise the law rewards the cheaper, harder-to-attribute method and leaves civilians exposed. Proportional military options restore symmetry: a defender may answer a grid-blackout cyber strike with proportionate force against the attacker’s command nodes or supporting infrastructure, calibrated to the harm inflicted.
[P1.3] This is not escalation; it is deterrence. Without it, revisionist actors test boundaries with ever-larger attacks, confident that legal ambiguity will paralyze response. Updating the armed-attack threshold to an effects-based standard closes that gap, clarifies red lines, and strengthens the very norms that protect civilian infrastructure in the first place. The alternative is a world where states absorb mounting damage until the only remaining option is unrestrained retaliation—precisely the spiral critics claim to fear.
[P1.4] Civilian lives do not become less worthy of defense because the weapon is digital. The proposition simply demands that international law catch up to the battlefield that already exists.
