[C1.3] Humans systematically misjudge risk, procrastinate on painful decisions, and respond to immediate pressure rather than abstract future benefits. A teenager who understands compound interest in a classroom may still sign a payday loan agreement when facing a genuine emergency and empty savings. [C1.4] Meanwhile, the proposal demands a "serious" course—one that takes time away from other subjects already competing in a crowded curriculum. Every hour in a dedicated financial literacy class is an hour not spent deepening algebra, reading comprehension, or science. [C1.5] There is also the implementation problem. The proposal assumes qualified teachers, coherent curriculum, and real assessment. In practice, financial literacy is often taught by teachers without backgrounds in economics or finance, using packaged curricula of variable quality. Mandating a course without controlling its quality produces a credential, not a competency.
[C1.6] My opponent correctly identifies that some students lack family guidance on these matters. That is a real inequity. But the solution is targeted support—workshops, community programs, online resources—delivered when and where students actually need them, not a classroom requirement that reaches everyone equally ineffectively.
