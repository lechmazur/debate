[P1.1] History is a public good, not a commodity. When governments outsource the recovery of family records—specifically those linked to slavery, adoption, indigenous dispossession, and wartime displacement—to private genealogy markets, they effectively gatekeep identity behind a paywall. This creates a two-tiered system where history becomes a luxury good, accessible only to those with the means to afford expensive monthly subscriptions.
[P1.2] The state bears a unique moral responsibility to facilitate the recovery of records that it was historically complicit in obscuring or destroying. Families separated by the slave trade, indigenous communities fractured by forced assimilation, and individuals displaced by war did not choose their alienation from their heritage. Expecting these families to rely on commercial firms—whose primary incentive is shareholder profit, not restorative justice—is an abdication of that duty.
[P1.3] Commercial genealogy markets prioritize "profitable" data; they are designed to service mass-market consumers with deep genealogical trails. They often deprioritize or ignore the fragmented, difficult-to-search, or "unprofitable" records vital to marginalized populations. When we leave history to the market, we allow corporate algorithms to decide which traumas are preserved and which are erased. Private companies hold proprietary rights over records that should belong to the public record, often using restrictive licensing to block researchers and descendants alike.
[P1.4] Publicly funded, digitized access is the only way to ensure archival integrity and universal equity. Public records must be open-source resources, not proprietary assets. Free access allows families to reconnect without the predatory economics of services that commodify the search for lost ancestors. It also fosters academic transparency, allowing historians to document structural harms without being blocked by subscription models.
[P1.5] The recovery of history is a prerequisite for justice. By digitizing and democratizing these archives, governments provide the tools necessary for families to reclaim their own narratives. This is about the state’s obligation to rectify the historical record for those whose pasts it helped sever. Public funding is the only path that prioritizes the rights of the vulnerable over the profits of the firm.

[C1.1] Governments operate with strictly constrained budgets, and the proposed digitization project would carry massive, undercounted costs. Scattered, fragmented, often damaged records linked to slavery, forced adoption, indigenous dispossession, and displacement exist across tens of thousands of local, regional, and national archival sites; full digitization with cross-referencing would cost tens of billions of dollars siphoned directly from existing programs that deliver immediate, tangible support to the marginalized communities at the center of this debate: cash assistance, affordable housing, healthcare, and direct reparations payments.
[C1.2] Delivering material justice to living people matters far more than funding an archival project that delivers mostly symbolic value for the vast majority of affected communities.
[C1.3] The proposal also poses catastrophic privacy risks to living people. Many of these records include unconsented personal data: adoption records list biological parent identities that many people have chosen to keep private, indigenous lineage records include sacred information not meant for broad public access, and wartime displacement records note religious or political affiliation that could expose living descendants to targeted discrimination or violence. Public, unfettered access would put the most vulnerable members of these communities at avoidable risk, with no built-in mechanism for individual redaction requests for people who wish to keep their family information private.
[C1.4] Finally, the claim that private genealogy markets ignore these records is demonstrably false. Most major commercial genealogy platforms already partner with tribal nations, Black heritage organizations, and refugee advocacy groups to digitize high-priority records for free public access, and their existing technical infrastructure and trained staff can complete this work far faster and with more rigorous privacy safeguards than underfunded, overstretched government archival teams. Forcing governments to take on this work exclusively would slow access to these records for decades, while wasting resources that could help people right now.
