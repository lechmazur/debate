[P1.1] The current asylum system at the southern border has failed everyone: migrants risk death crossing deserts and drown in rivers, border communities are overwhelmed, and legitimate refugees languish in years-long backlogs while meritless claims clog the system. A safe third country approach paired with expanded legal pathways offers a genuine solution.
[P1.2] Safe third country agreements require asylum seekers to request protection in the first safe country they reach, rather than bypassing multiple nations to reach the United States. This principle recognizes that true refugees seek safety, not a specific destination. When migrants cross through Mexico—a country with functional asylum infrastructure—demanding to reach the American border reveals something other than immediate fear of persecution.
[P1.3] The current system incentivizes exactly the wrong behavior. Smugglers market "asylum loopholes" to desperate people, promising that simply arriving on US soil guarantees years of presence while claims slowly process. The result: families separated by smugglers, children endangered on freight trains, and a system so flooded that genuine refugees cannot access timely protection.
[P1.4] Expanded legal pathways flip these incentives. Refugee processing from countries of origin, expanded visa programs, and coordinated resettlement create safe, orderly routes for those fleeing persecution. Instead of rewarding those with the resources to pay smugglers and survive dangerous journeys, this approach prioritizes the most vulnerable through official channels.
[P1.5] Mexico and Central American nations have demonstrated capacity to process asylum claims. The United States can and should invest in strengthening regional asylum systems rather than concentrating all pressure at one border. This distributes responsibility and brings processing closer to displacement.
[P1.6] The choice is not between open borders and closed ones. It is between continued chaos that serves no one—migrants, border communities, or American credibility—and a structured system that actually delivers protection to those who need it through legitimate means. The proposition offers that structure.
