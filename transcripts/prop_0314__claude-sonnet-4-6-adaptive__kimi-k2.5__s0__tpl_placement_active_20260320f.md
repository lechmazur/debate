[P1.1] Public money carries public obligations. That simple principle is what this proposition defends.
[P1.2] When a city subsidizes a stadium, it is transferring hundreds of millions of dollars—tax revenue that could fund schools, transit, or affordable housing—to a privately owned franchise. The team captures the upside: gate receipts, broadcast deals, naming rights, luxury suite revenue. The city captures the risk: debt service, infrastructure costs, and the near-certain reality that economic projections used to sell the deal will not materialize.
[P1.3] Economists across the ideological spectrum have reached the same conclusion about stadium subsidies: they do not generate the promised economic activity. Spending at a stadium largely displaces spending that would have happened elsewhere in the local economy. The multiplier effects are routinely overstated in the feasibility studies franchises commission themselves. And when the stadium ages and the team wants a new one, the cycle starts again.
[P1.4] None of that means cities must refuse to engage. The proposition is not a prohibition. It says: if public money is involved, attach enforceable conditions. Revenue sharing could mean a portion of team profits flow back to the city treasury, offsetting the subsidy over time.
[P1.5] Neighborhood-benefit requirements could mean local hiring agreements, affordable housing set-asides nearby, or community land funds. Both are standard tools in public finance for any other kind of development deal.
[P1.6] The objection will come: teams will leave, cities have no leverage. This is largely a bluff, and cities that believe it unconditionally will always lose. Franchise relocations are expensive, rare, and politically risky for leagues trying to protect their public image. The threat is a negotiating tactic, not an iron law—and even when real, capitulating unconditionally doesn't transform a bad deal into a good one.
[P1.7] The question is not whether cities should ever participate in stadium development. The question is whether participation should be unconditional. It should not. Public investment without enforceable accountability is a gift, not a partnership.
