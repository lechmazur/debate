[P1.1] Loot boxes are slot machines wearing video game costumes. You pay real money, pull the virtual lever, and receive a randomized reward of wildly varying value—sometimes a common skin worth pennies, sometimes a legendary item worth hundreds on secondary markets. This is not innocent fun; it is a psychological trap engineered to exploit the same variable-ratio reinforcement schedules that keep gamblers pulling levers in casinos.
[P1.2] The parallel is structural, not cosmetic. Both loot boxes and traditional gambling convert cash into probabilistic outcomes, creating anticipatory dopamine spikes independent of skill or merit. When these mechanisms target minors—whose prefrontal cortices remain years away from full development and who lack the impulse control and risk-assessment capacities of adults—the exploitation becomes predatory. We do not let twelve-year-olds sit at blackjack tables for good reason; neurobiological vulnerability demands legal protection, not corporate profit margins.
[P1.3] Opponents will claim these are merely "surprise mechanics" or optional entertainment. This ignores that modern game design engineers frustration—artificial difficulty spikes, competitive disadvantages, limited-time pressure—to push children toward the purchase button. The interface may feature cartoon dragons rather than cherries and sevens, but the addiction pathway is identical: near-misses flashing across the screen, loss chasing after disappointing drops, and the sunk-cost fallacy driving players to buy "just one more" box to complete the set.
[P1.4] Regulating loot boxes as gambling does not ban video games; it applies existing, tested frameworks to a new vector of financial harm. We already recognize that minors require special protections from industries that profit from psychological manipulation. Extending gambling regulations to cover randomized monetization simply closes a loophole that corporations exploited to turn children's pocket money into shareholder dividends while externalizing the costs of addiction onto families. The alternative is allowing a multi-billion dollar industry to operate the only casinos in the world where children are the target demographic, with no oversight and no age verification that meaningfully sticks.

[C1.4] It targets the wrong mechanism and ignores the actual point of control: the transaction itself.

The state’s role is not to declare every randomized digital product a casino, but to ensure transparent, controllable spending environments.
[C1.6] Precision protects kids. Category errors just break the law.
