[P1.1] Orbital altitude is finite real estate, yet operators currently treat disposal as someone else's problem. Every satellite left drifting becomes a high-velocity bullet threatening active missions for decades, multiplying collision risks exponentially as mega-constellations proliferate. A single failed disposal in crowded low-Earth orbit can generate thousands of trackable fragments, each capable of cascading into further collisions. The present regime allows companies to privatize launch profits while socializing the catastrophic risk of debris creation—a textbook market failure that demands correction before critical orbital bands become unusable junkyards.
[P1.2] Deorbit bonds solve this by forcing operators to internalize end-of-life costs at the moment of greatest leverage: before launch. By requiring operators to post capital held in escrow—released only upon verified atmospheric entry or proper graveyard orbit placement—we align financial incentives with orbital safety. The operator bears the financial risk of disposal failure, not taxpayers or competing satellite users. This transforms disposal from an externalized afterthought into a budgeted operational constraint that shapes spacecraft design and mission planning from day one, ensuring survival-critical systems like propulsion redundancy are built in rather than value-engineered out.
[P1.3] Voluntary guidelines and post-hoc liability cannot match this preventive power. Ex post lawsuits face insurmountable hurdles: bankrupt shell companies, international jurisdictional mazes, and the impossibility of calculating damages from a collision that destroys critical weather monitoring or GPS infrastructure years later. Governments struggle to impose retroactive fines on entities that may no longer exist or operate under flags of convenience. When failure means forfeiting millions in posted capital held by neutral third parties, compliance becomes cheaper than defection; when failure merely risks future litigation, defection is rational.
[P1.4] Implementation is technically straightforward. Verification relies on standard telemetry and tracking data already collected by space agencies; the bond releases upon confirmed retirement. For a multi-million dollar launch, the bond represents modest insurance against trillion-dollar orbital infrastructure loss. We require construction bonds for terrestrial buildings that might collapse; we should demand no less for objects that could rain debris across orbital highways for generations, poisoning the orbital commons for all humanity.
