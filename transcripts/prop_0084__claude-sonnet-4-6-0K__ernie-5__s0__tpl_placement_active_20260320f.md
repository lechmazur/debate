[P1.1] The core principle at stake is simple: whoever profits from creating a waste problem should pay to clean it up, not the public. Right now, that principle is systematically violated.
[P1.2] Beverage and packaging companies design products, choose materials, set production volumes, and collect the revenue. Taxpayers then fund the trucks, the sorting facilities, the landfill space, and the cleanup of what inevitably escapes into streets and waterways. That is a straightforward transfer of private costs onto the public—a subsidy disguised as municipal service.
[P1.3] The numbers behind this matter even without precise citations. Municipalities in most countries spend billions annually on solid waste management, and packaging and single-use beverage containers make up a substantial share of what they handle. Meanwhile, companies like major soft-drink brands and fast-food packaging suppliers earn enormous margins. The asymmetry is not incidental—it is structural.
[P1.4] When disposal is someone else's problem, producers have no financial incentive to reduce packaging weight, switch to genuinely recyclable materials, or design for easy sorting.
[P1.5] Extended Producer Responsibility programs—where producers fund and organize collection—have already demonstrated the alternative. Where deposit-return schemes exist for bottles and cans, return rates reliably exceed eighty percent. Where packaging levies exist, companies redesign products to avoid them. The evidence is that when producers pay, behavior changes upstream, which is where the real solution lives.
[P1.6] The CON position will likely argue this raises consumer prices or is administratively burdensome. Both objections misplace the blame. Prices already include the true cost of disposal—they just hide it in tax bills rather than product prices. Shifting the cost to producers makes it visible and correctable.
[P1.7] That is not a burden; it is accurate pricing.
[P1.8] The moral case, the economic case, and the demonstrated track record of producer-funded systems all point the same direction. Polluters should pay. Taxpayers should not subsidize the packaging choices of profitable corporations.
