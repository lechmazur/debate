[P1.1] The unchecked spread of after-hours work messages has turned personal time into unpaid labor for millions. Smartphones and remote tools make every evening and weekend a potential extension of the office, eroding sleep, family routines, and the basic capacity to recover from the workday. A statutory right to disconnect stops this seepage by treating non-urgent communications outside set hours as presumptively off-limits for most employees, while carving out clear exceptions for roles that genuinely require continuous coverage.
[P1.2] Employees who can actually disconnect return to work with better concentration and lower error rates. The mechanism is straightforward: full disengagement allows the nervous system to reset, which sustains output over months and years instead of driving the gradual burnout that forces repeated sick days or resignations. Countries that have introduced similar limits have already seen measurable drops in overtime claims and turnover costs, confirming that rest is not a luxury but a productivity input.
[P1.3] The policy also prevents a quiet expansion of exploitation. Without an enforceable boundary, competitive pressure turns constant availability into an unstated job requirement, hitting lower-paid and younger workers hardest because they have the least leverage to ignore messages. A right to disconnect removes that quiet coercion without banning contact entirely; it simply shifts the default so that routine tasks must be handled within contracted hours.
[P1.4] Sector exceptions keep the rule practical rather than ideological. Emergency services, on-call medical staff, and critical infrastructure can retain immediate response protocols. The protected class is ordinary knowledge and administrative work, where delayed replies rarely create genuine crises yet routinely destroy evenings. By anchoring the exception in functional necessity instead of industry lobbying, the measure avoids the overbroad loopholes that have neutered similar protections elsewhere.
