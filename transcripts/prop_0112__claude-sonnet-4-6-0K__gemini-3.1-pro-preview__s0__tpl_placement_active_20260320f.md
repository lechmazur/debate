[P1.1] The central flaw in employer-based insurance is built into its name: access depends on employment. That dependency creates a system where the people most likely to need medical care—those who are sick, disabled, or economically precarious—are precisely the people most likely to lose coverage. This isn't an edge case. Job loss, reduced hours, divorce from a covered spouse, or simply working in sectors that don't offer benefits all sever coverage.
[P1.2] Medical need and employment status are not correlated in the right direction. They move in opposite directions, which means the employer-based architecture fails at exactly the moment it matters most.
[P1.3] Single-payer financing resolves this by decoupling coverage from work status entirely. Every resident is covered by definition. There are no gaps created by labor market volatility. No one delays care because they're between jobs.
[P1.4] No one accepts a worse job or stays in a harmful relationship to keep insurance. The behavioral distortions alone—economists call this "job lock"—suppress entrepreneurship, reduce labor mobility, and bind workers to employers in ways that reduce their bargaining power and overall economic freedom.
[P1.5] The administrative cost argument compounds this. Employer-based systems require insurers to administer thousands of separate plans with different networks, formularies, and eligibility rules. Employers bear administrative overhead. Providers spend enormous resources verifying coverage and billing across dozens of payers.
[P1.6] Single-payer consolidates this into a uniform financing mechanism, freeing clinical resources for actual care.
[P1.7] The CON position must defend a system where basic medical coverage is a workplace benefit rather than a guaranteed social baseline. That framing already concedes the moral terrain. When we describe coverage as a workplace "perk," we've already admitted that people without that perk go without. The question isn't whether an ideal employer-based system could be designed.
[P1.8] The question is whether tying basic coverage to work status is preferable. It isn't. The structure is the problem.
