[P1.1] Pay secrecy systematically advantages employers over workers. When a qualified employee negotiates a salary without knowing the pay range for their role, they're negotiating blindfolded while management holds all the cards. This information asymmetry isn't a minor inefficiency—it structurally reproduces pay gaps that persist for decades.
[P1.2] The evidence is unambiguous. When Colorado's pay transparency law took effect, researchers found the gender pay gap narrowed significantly in affected job postings. Similar patterns emerged in other jurisdictions requiring disclosure. This isn't coincidence; it's what happens when workers can see the range they're negotiating within.
[P1.3] Pay bands let employees identify whether they're being offered less than peers for equivalent work, transforming individual negotiation from a guessing game into an informed transaction.
[P1.4] Promotion rate disclosure matters for the same reason. Without visible data on who advances and how often, patterns of bias remain invisible. A company can claim equal opportunity while promoting men at twice the rate of women, or white employees at three times the rate of Black colleagues. Aggregated promotion statistics make these patterns visible—and what's visible can be challenged, measured, and corrected.
[P1.5] The counterargument that transparency reduces "managerial flexibility" deserves scrutiny. Flexibility to do what, exactly? To offer different pay for identical work without justification? To promote based on subjective impressions rather than documented performance?
[P1.6] The flexibility defended here is precisely the discretion through which bias operates. When managers must defend pay decisions against an established standard, arbitrary or discriminatory decisions become harder to sustain.
[P1.7] Large employers already track this data internally. The cost of disclosure is primarily the discomfort of having decisions scrutinized. But employment decisions deserve scrutiny—they determine life trajectories, family security, and economic mobility. The modest constraint on managerial discretion is a price worth paying for the transparency that makes fair treatment verifiable rather than aspirational.

[C1.2] First, the compression problem. When bands are public, they become ceilings, not ranges. Employers bunch salaries toward the middle to avoid internal conflict and external embarrassment. High performers get less upside; workers whose value is hard to legibly justify—career-switchers, caregivers returning, self-taught talent—get screened out because managers no longer have room to take a chance on them at a non-standard number.
[C1.3] The evidence from Scandinavian transparency experiments suggests wage compression and, in some studies, *lower* average pay growth, particularly for women in male-dominated fields where the surplus from individual negotiation was largest.
[C1.4] Second, the disclosure mandate misidentifies the problem. Colorado-style rules already require posting pay ranges on job listings—that's negotiation-relevant information. The proposition goes further: internal bands and promotion rates by role. Promotion rates by role, at large employers, mean small cell sizes once you disaggregate meaningfully.
[C1.5] You either publish noisy numbers that invite lawsuits and PR crises over statistical artifacts, or you publish aggregates so coarse they reveal nothing. Neither helps the underpromoted employee.
[C1.6] Third, the flexibility PRO mocks is not a synonym for bias. It's the ability to pay a retention premium to a single parent who got a competing offer, to promote someone out-of-cycle because a project demanded it, to create a hybrid role for a returning veteran. Rigid, publicly-defensible decisions are decisions optimized for defensibility, not fairness. The marginal worker—the one who doesn't fit the template—loses.
[C1.7] Fourth, cost and chilling effects. "Large employers already track this" understates the compliance apparatus: legal review, dispute processes, standardized job architectures forced onto dynamic teams. The predictable response is fewer distinct roles, fewer internal promotions, and more hiring from outside where the mandate bites less.
[C1.8] The motion trades real flexibility for symbolic transparency. Workers pay the bill.
