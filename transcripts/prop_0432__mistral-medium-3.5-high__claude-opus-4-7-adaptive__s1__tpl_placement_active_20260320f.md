[P1.1] Defaulting to pet permissions with standardized damage insurance or deposits is the fair and practical approach that should replace blanket pet bans. Current policies treat every pet owner as a potential problem, punishing responsible individuals for the rare cases of damage. In reality, most pets cause no significant wear beyond ordinary use, and a reasonable deposit or small monthly insurance premium can easily cover typical damage like scratches or stains. This makes the risk entirely manageable for landlords, removing any legitimate basis for blanket prohibitions—especially since many landlords already successfully use deposits to cover pet-related wear.
[P1.2] The economic advantages for property owners are substantial and proven. Pet-owning tenants have far fewer housing options, so they tend to stay significantly longer, dramatically reducing costly turnover and vacancy periods. In competitive markets, pet-friendly units achieve higher occupancy rates. They often accept premium rents for the privilege.
[P1.3] Blanket bans, by contrast, unnecessarily restrict the tenant pool, turning away stable renters who would meet insurance requirements. Rejecting an entire demographic based on isolated incidents makes no economic sense, like banning all families with children because one child caused damage years ago.
[P1.4] Ethically, the case is compelling and timely. For many, especially those living alone or facing mental health challenges, pets provide essential companionship and emotional support. In an era of rising social isolation, these bonds are more important than ever. Forcing a choice between housing and a beloved pet imposes arbitrary and often devastating hardship.
[P1.5] With insurance, any damage is covered just like other tenant-caused wear. Current blanket bans allow rejection based on isolated past incidents; a default-allow policy lets landlords evaluate individually with protection in place.
[P1.6] This approach fully preserves landlord rights. Property owners can still deny specific high-risk pets or require additional coverage for unusual animals. But with standardized insurance as the baseline, the default should shift from exclusion to access. This creates a fairer, more efficient system benefiting both landlords and responsible pet owners, reducing unnecessary conflicts and housing barriers.
