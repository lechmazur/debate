[P1.1] A delivery driver in Berlin works the same route, for the same app, at the same hours every week for three years. Her "employer" treats her as a freelancer while controlling her through algorithm, rating systems, and the threat of deactivation. That is not freelancing.
[P1.2] That is employment by function, hidden behind a legal fiction, and it harms millions of workers across Europe and Asia.
[P1.3] The proposition before us demands a simple correction: legal status should reflect economic reality. When a platform sets your pay, monitors your performance, and can terminate you unilaterally, you are not a business owner running your own enterprise. You are an employee. The law should say so by default.
[P1.4] Current misclassification is not a marginal problem. It is a systemic one. Companies that misclassify workers avoid payroll taxes, skip minimum wage obligations, and externalize the cost of income volatility, illness, and injury onto the workers themselves and ultimately onto public safety nets. This is not an accident of the modern economy; it is a deliberate arbitrage, and workers are paying for it.
[P1.5] The objection that follows—that reclassification will destroy flexibility—is a false choice. The proposition explicitly preserves narrow exemptions for genuine freelancers: those who set their own rates, serve multiple clients, and operate independently. A photographer who chooses her own projects, an independent consultant who works for three companies, a writer who sells her services on the open market—none of this touches. What the proposition reaches is the warehouse worker told when to clock in, the driver forbidden from tipping the next customer in cash, the courier whose route is assigned by algorithm.
[P1.6] These workers are employees in everything but name.
[P1.7] The real choice is this: do we let platforms continue to capture the economic benefits of employment while offloading the risks onto workers, or do we align the law with what is actually happening on our streets and in our economies? Reclassification is not radical. It is overdue.
