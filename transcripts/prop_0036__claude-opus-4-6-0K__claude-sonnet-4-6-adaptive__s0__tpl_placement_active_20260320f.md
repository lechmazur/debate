[P1.1] Wearable devices track heart rate, sleep cycles, blood oxygen, menstrual cycles, location, and activity levels continuously. That stream of data is more medically revealing than most clinical visits. Allowing insurers unrestricted access to it for pricing and coverage decisions without meaningful consent protections would be one of the most significant erosions of health privacy in a generation.
[P1.2] Three arguments establish why the prohibition with opt-in framework is necessary.
[P1.3] **First, the power asymmetry makes genuine voluntariness impossible without legal guardrails.** When an insurer offers a discount for sharing wearable data, refusal carries an implicit penalty—higher premiums. Over time, as data-sharing customers cluster into cheaper pools, non-sharers face adverse selection pressure and rising costs. What begins as an invitation becomes economic coercion. A strict opt-in regime with defined limits on data use is the only way to preserve actual choice.
[P1.4] **Second, wearable data functions as a proxy for protected characteristics.** Activity levels correlate with physical disability. Sleep patterns correlate with mental health conditions. Heart-rate variability differs across age, sex, and medication use. Allowing insurers to price on this data lets them accomplish indirectly what anti-discrimination law prohibits directly—penalizing people for health status, disability, and conditions they cannot control.
[P1.5] The strict-limits requirement in the proposition exists precisely to prevent this laundering of discrimination through algorithmic proxies.
[P1.6] **Third, the intimacy and volume of wearable data demands a higher standard of consent than traditional underwriting information.** A blood test taken at a clinic visit is a snapshot; a wearable generates thousands of data points daily for years. The potential for mission creep—data collected for "wellness" repurposed for claims denial—is enormous. Without prohibition as the default, consumers cannot meaningfully track or challenge how their data shapes decisions about their coverage.
[P1.7] The proposition does not ban wearable data use outright. It sets prohibition as the default and allows use only when the customer affirmatively opts in under strict, defined limits. That is not paternalism; it is the minimum architecture for informed consent in an era of pervasive biosurveillance.
