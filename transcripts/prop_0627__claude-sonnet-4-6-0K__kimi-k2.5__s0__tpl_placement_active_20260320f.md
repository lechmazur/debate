[P1.1] When a child in a contaminated community cannot breathe without risking permanent lung damage, that is not merely a policy failure—it is a violation of something fundamental. The question before us is whether courts should recognize a constitutional or fundamental right to a minimally clean and healthy environment enforceable against governments. The answer is yes, and the path to that conclusion runs through both principle and practical necessity.
[P1.2] Start with first principles. Existing constitutional rights—to life, liberty, bodily integrity—are rendered hollow without a livable environment to exercise them in. You cannot meaningfully enjoy freedom of expression, education, or even the right to life itself if the air, water, and soil governments permit to be poisoned make sustained human functioning impossible. Environmental conditions are the substrate on which every other right depends.
[P1.3] Recognizing an environmental floor is not expanding rights beyond recognition; it is making existing rights coherent.
[P1.4] The objection that courts are ill-equipped to handle this misunderstands what the right asks. A minimum threshold—not pristine wilderness, not zero emissions, but conditions that do not actively destroy human health—is precisely the kind of judicially manageable standard courts already apply in other contexts. Courts adjudicate minimum standards in prison conditions, mental health commitments, and public education. The "minimally clean" framing is deliberately calibrated to that tradition.
[P1.5] The need is urgent because political processes systematically fail the most vulnerable. Polluting facilities are disproportionately sited in low-income communities with the least political leverage. Legislative action is slow, captured by industry interests, and reversible by subsequent administrations. A judicially enforceable right provides a floor that ordinary politics cannot erode.
[P1.6] Several nations and subnational governments—including provisions in Pennsylvania's constitution and decisions in courts from Colombia to Pakistan—have already moved in this direction, demonstrating that environmental rights are neither utopian nor unworkable.
[P1.7] The central claim is simple: where government action or inaction poisons the environment below the threshold of human health, courts must be available to say so.
