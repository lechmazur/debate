[P1.1] When someone spends decades building a library—curating titles, annotating margins, investing thousands of dollars—they expect that collection to outlive them. With physical books, it does. The shelves pass to children, estates, or friends. But with digital purchases, extinction is the default.
[P1.2] A Kindle library worth thousands vanishes. A music collection disappears. A lifetime of purchased films becomes inaccessible. This isn't a technical necessity—it's a policy choice, and a bad one.
[P1.3] The case for guaranteed digital inheritance rests on two pillars: consistency and consumer expectation.
[P1.4] Consistency first. We have centuries of inheritance law built on a simple principle—when you own something, your heirs inherit it. The switch from paper to pixels doesn't change the underlying transaction. When you pay for an ebook at full price, you're purchasing a permanent asset, not renting temporary access.
[P1.5] The "license not ownership" framing is a legal fiction that companies deploy selectively. They want the revenue of a sale but the control of a rental. That's double-dipping. If we accept that digital purchases are functionally equivalent to physical purchases during life, they should be equivalent in death.
[P1.6] Consumer expectation is equally decisive. No reasonable buyer thinks "I'm renting this until I die, then Amazon reclaims it." They think "I'm buying this book." The law should reflect how ordinary people understand their transactions, not how corporate lawyers draft end-user agreements.
[P1.7] When someone builds a library over thirty years, that collection represents real financial investment and real personal meaning—a reading history, annotated passages, curated selections that form part of someone's intellectual legacy.
[P1.8] The opposition will likely defend corporate control over these assets. But copyright holders already received full payment. Denying inheritance doesn't protect creators—it simply lets platforms recapture value they already sold. The deceased paid.
[P1.9] Their kin should inherit. Anything less turns every digital purchase into a gamble against your own mortality.

[C1.1] The PRO case rests entirely on a seductive but false analogy: that a digital purchase is the same kind of transaction as a physical one, just delivered differently. It isn't. And the differences aren't cosmetic—they're structural, and they matter.
[C1.2] When you buy a physical book, you own a particular object. It degrades, it can be lost, it exists in one place. Its transferability follows naturally from its physicality. A digital file is fundamentally different: it's infinitely reproducible, requires ongoing infrastructure to access, and exists by grace of a platform's servers and the licensing arrangements that fund its availability.
[C1.3] These aren't trivial distinctions. They're why digital content is priced differently and distributed differently in the first place.
[C1.4] The PRO dismisses the license-versus-ownership distinction as a "legal fiction," but it's the explicit basis on which these transactions occur. Every consumer who buys a Kindle book clicks "I agree" to terms that specify non-transferability. You can argue people don't read those terms, but the law doesn't generally void contracts because one party found the terms inconvenient after the fact. Adults who agree to licensing arrangements should be bound by them—even in death, especially when enforcement protects third parties.
[C1.5] Those third parties are creators and publishers who set prices reflecting the non-transferability guarantee. If inheritance law mandates transferability, we haven't merely corrected an injustice—we've retroactively rewritten every digital content contract in existence. Prices would rise, libraries would shrink, and the current model of affordable digital access would contract. The PRO frames this debate as consumers versus corporations, but the real losers would be future consumers who lose access to the affordable licensing model that non-transferability makes possible.
[C1.6] Finally, the sentimental appeal to "intellectual legacies" and "annotated margins" obscures a harder question: what exactly are heirs inheriting? Not files—they're inheriting a right to access someone else's licensed content on someone else's platform, funded by a transaction they weren't party to. That's not inheritance. It's a forced transfer of contractual obligations the original parties never agreed to.
