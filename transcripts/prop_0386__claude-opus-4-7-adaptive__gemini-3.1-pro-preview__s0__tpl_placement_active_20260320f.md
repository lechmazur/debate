[P1.1] The proposition draws a line that almost everyone, on reflection, already accepts: conscience matters, but not at the price of abandoning the patient. Defend that line, and you protect both the clinician's integrity and the patient's life.
[P1.2] Start with what a refusal actually is. When a pharmacist won't dispense contraception, or an obstetrician won't manage a miscarriage, or an ER doctor won't prescribe HIV prophylaxis after an assault, the patient doesn't simply "go elsewhere" in the abstract. She drives across a county that may not have an "elsewhere." She waits days she doesn't have.
[P1.3] She pays twice. She is told, in effect, that her medical need is the price of someone else's belief. That is not accommodation; that is conscription.
[P1.4] The proposition fixes this with a simple condition: refuse if you must, but only when timely, equivalent care is genuinely available without major burden. Three features make this the right rule.
[P1.5] First, it preserves real conscience protection. Clinicians in well-staffed hospitals, in urban systems, in elective contexts, retain the right to step back. The vast majority of conscience claims occur exactly where alternatives exist, and the proposition shields them.
[P1.6] Second, it tracks the moral asymmetry. The clinician chose a licensed profession that exists to serve patients; the patient did not choose her emergency. When those interests collide, the side that voluntarily entered the role should bear the inconvenience, not the side in the gown.
[P1.7] Third, it is administrable. "Timely, equivalent, without major burden" is the same standard medicine already uses for transfers, referrals, and on-call coverage. Hospitals can roster around known objections; rural sole-provider settings simply cannot let a single clinician's beliefs function as a veto over lawful care.
[P1.8] The CON side must defend one of two extremes: either conscience that overrides patient welfare even when patients are stranded, or no conscience accommodation at all. The proposition occupies the only stable ground between them, and that is why it should win.
