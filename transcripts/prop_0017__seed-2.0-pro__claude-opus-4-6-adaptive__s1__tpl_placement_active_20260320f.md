[P1.1] Bodily autonomy, the fundamental right to decide what is done to your own body without non-consensual external interference, inherently deserves more moral weight than appeals to dignity in personal medical choices, for two core, irrebuttable reasons.
[P1.2] First, appeals to dignity deployed to override medical choices are always third-party, subjective judgments of what makes a person’s life or decisions "worthy," imposed by actors who never bear the costs of the outcome. Take a terminal patient with untreatable, constant end-of-life pain who chooses medical aid in dying. Opponents routinely cite "human dignity" to ban this choice, claiming that enduring suffering until natural death is more dignified than the patient’s own, deeply personal decision to end their pain. The people imposing this dignity standard face zero physical or emotional harm from the choice, while the patient is forced to endure months of unendurable agony for the sake of someone else’s moral preference.
[P1.3] The same dynamic plays out when opponents cite "dignity" to ban gender-affirming care for trans adults, forcing people to live with crippling dysphoria to satisfy external judgments of how people "should" exist in their bodies.
[P1.4] Second, bodily autonomy is the only guardrail against state and institutional abuse of medical power. Forced sterilization of disabled people, non-consensual medical procedures on incarcerated people, all were defended using appeals to "dignity" and collective good, with zero regard for the bodily rights of the people harmed. There is no scenario where overriding bodily autonomy for the sake of dignity benefits the person whose body is at stake, only scenarios that prioritize outside preferences over the lived experience of the person most affected.

[C1.6] Autonomy without dignity is a legal form with no soul. The question is not which value overrides the other—it is whether autonomy can even function morally without dignity anchoring it.
