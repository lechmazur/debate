[P1.2] Start with dignity, because it shapes everything else. Means testing requires families to declare poverty to access food. [P1.3] Universal provision removes the marker entirely. Every child simply eats.
[P1.4] Second, means tests fail the people they're meant to help. Eligibility thresholds create a cliff-edge: a family just above the income cut-off may be genuinely food-insecure but receive nothing. In practice, means-tested programs consistently under-enroll eligible families due to bureaucratic complexity, documentation burdens, and social stigma. A family working two jobs doesn't have time to resubmit forms each semester.
[P1.5] The result is a program that sounds targeted but actually misses a large share of its intended beneficiaries—the worst of both worlds.
[P1.6] Third, the administrative apparatus of means testing is expensive. Schools must verify income, process appeals, maintain databases, and re-certify annually. That overhead consumes budget that could otherwise go directly into food. Universal systems eliminate that cost entirely and redirect it to the core purpose: feeding children.
[P1.7] The opponent will argue that universal provision wastes money on children who don't need it. But this framing is backward. The cost of a meal for a wealthy child is marginal; the cost of a hungry child unable to concentrate, unable to learn, is enormous. And if the goal is fiscal targeting, the answer is progressive taxation—make wealthier families contribute more through the tax system—not a system that humiliates poor children at the point of service.
[P1.8] Universal free school meals treat the meal as what it is: a precondition for learning, not a welfare benefit.
