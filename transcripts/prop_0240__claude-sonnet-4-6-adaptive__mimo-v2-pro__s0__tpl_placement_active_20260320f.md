[P1.1] The debate turns on a straightforward question: when someone does real work that creates real value for an employer, should the law allow that employer to pay them nothing? Outside genuinely educational placements, the answer must be no—and governments are right to enforce it.
[P1.2] Unpaid internships, as they actually operate in sectors like media, fashion, politics, and NGOs, are not educational experiences dressed up as jobs. They are jobs dressed up as educational experiences. The intern answers emails, manages social media, drafts reports, and fills roles that would otherwise require a paid hire. The "learning" rationale becomes a legal fiction that lets employers extract productive labor without triggering minimum wage obligations.
[P1.3] The inequality this produces is stark and compounding. Unpaid positions are only accessible to people who can afford to work without income—those with wealthy parents, savings, or a partner who covers rent. The graduate from a lower-income household must take paid work; the graduate from a comfortable one takes the unpaid internship at the prestigious firm and builds the network that locks in lifetime advantage. The internship economy does not merely reflect existing inequality—it manufactures it, turning parental wealth into professional credentials.
[P1.4] Labor markets are also distorted. When employers can staff positions for free, they have less incentive to create entry-level paid roles. The pipeline of genuine job creation narrows, and young workers are collectively worse off even as individual interns tell themselves they are getting ahead.
[P1.5] The proposition does not abolish internships. It preserves them where they genuinely belong: structured placements where learning is the primary purpose, skill transfer is documented, and the arrangement is tied to academic credit or formal training. What it removes is the loophole—the arrangement where a 22-year-old works full weeks for free because they are desperate enough and their employer is clever enough.
[P1.6] Restricting exploitation is not paternalism. It is the basic function of labor law.
