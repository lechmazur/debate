[P1.1] Every year, digital content vanishes. Websites go dark, publishers fold, formats become obsolete, and servers are decommissioned. Without organizations like the Internet Archive actively preserving this material, vast swaths of human knowledge simply cease to exist. The question before us is whether we allow traditional copyright enforcement—designed for a world of physical scarcity—to prevent nonprofit institutions from saving our collective digital heritage.
[P1.2] The answer must be yes, these institutions deserve an exemption, for three core reasons.
[P1.3] **First, the public interest in preservation dwarfs the private harm.** Copyright law exists to incentivize creation, not to ensure that works disappear when they become commercially inconvenient. Nonprofit digital libraries do not compete with active commercial markets. They preserve works that are out of print, orphaned, or abandoned—material no one else is bothering to keep available. When the Internet Archive's Wayback Machine captures a webpage, it is not stealing revenue; it is rescuing a record that would otherwise be lost forever.
[P1.4] The same logic applies to digitized books, software, and media that have fallen out of commercial circulation.
[P1.5] **Second, traditional copyright enforcement is structurally incapable of protecting digital heritage.** Copyright terms now extend decades beyond an author's death. Orphan works—those whose rights holders cannot be identified or located—number in the millions. Requiring nonprofit libraries to clear rights for every item before preservation means, in practice, that preservation does not happen. The transaction costs alone are prohibitive.
[P1.6] A meaningful exemption is the only mechanism that scales to the magnitude of the problem.
[P1.7] **Third, we already recognize this principle in analog contexts.** Physical libraries lend books, preserve deteriorating manuscripts, and maintain archives without paying per-use royalties. The digital exemption simply extends that centuries-old social contract to the medium where most knowledge now lives. Refusing to do so creates an absurd asymmetry: a crumbling paper book can be preserved, but its digital equivalent cannot.
[P1.8] The risk of inaction is permanent, irreversible loss. The risk of exemption is modest and manageable. That asymmetry should decide this debate.
