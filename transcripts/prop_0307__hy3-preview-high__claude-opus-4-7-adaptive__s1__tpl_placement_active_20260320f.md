[P1.1] When possible, defaulting to home-based elder care preserves the core autonomy and wellbeing that institutionalization routinely strips away. Older adults consistently report higher life satisfaction when aging in place, surrounded by familiar routines, possessions, and social ties. Institutional placement by default treats aging as a medical failure to be managed in sterile settings, rather than a normal life stage requiring flexible, person-centered support. This ignores the near-universal elder preference to remain in known spaces, handing daily control to facility staff instead of care recipients.
[P1.2] It frames elders as passive patients rather than active agents in their own lives.
[P1.3] Home care also delivers better health outcomes for most elders who do not need acute 24/7 facility intervention. Institutional settings carry higher risks of hospital-acquired infections, avoidable bedsores, and accelerated cognitive decline from disrupted routines. At-home support preserves mobility by encouraging elders to handle manageable tasks themselves, rather than over-relying on staff to do everything. These gains vanish when we move people to facilities without exhausting home-based options.
[P1.4] Even minor disruptions to familiar environments can trigger rapid health declines that are entirely avoidable.
[P1.5] Default home care is far more resource-efficient. Community-based support costs a fraction of full-time residential care, freeing limited institutional beds for the small subset of elders with complex needs that cannot be safely met at home. Default institutionalization wastes scarce resources on people who would thrive in familiar settings, while leaving no capacity for those who truly need facility-level care. This misallocation hurts both the elders forced into unnecessary facility care and those who cannot access a bed when they truly need one.
[P1.6] Home care also protects social connectedness, a critical driver of elder health. Moving to a facility severs local community ties, worsening isolation that is a larger mortality risk than many chronic conditions. Home-based care keeps elders engaged with neighbors, family, and community groups, building a more sustainable, person-centered system overall.
