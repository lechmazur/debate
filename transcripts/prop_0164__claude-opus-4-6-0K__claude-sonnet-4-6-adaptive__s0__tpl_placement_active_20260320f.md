Estimates vary by field, but credible large-scale efforts suggest failure rates between 50 and 70 percent in some disciplines. Journals reward first discoveries, tenure committees reward high-impact publications, and funders reward innovation. Nobody's career is made by confirming someone else's result. This is a textbook collective-action problem: everyone benefits from a trustworthy evidence base, but no individual actor has sufficient incentive to produce it.

[C1.2] **First, rigidity wastes resources.** A "meaningful fixed share" cannot distinguish between fields where replication is urgent and fields where it is redundant. High-energy physics already replicates continuously through independent detector teams and pre-registered analyses; mandating additional set-asides there taxes productivity for no epistemic gain. Meanwhile, fields genuinely plagued by irreproducibility—certain corners of social psychology, candidate-gene research—may need more than whatever arbitrary percentage the funder chooses. A blanket quota treats all disciplines as equally broken, which is empirically false.
[C1.3] **Second, the trade-off is sharper than PRO admits.** Grant budgets are not growing; in real terms, many national science agencies have been flat or declining for years. Every dollar earmarked for replication is a dollar subtracted from discovery. PRO waves this away as "quality control," but quality control that prevents novel work from ever occurring cannot improve the portfolio—it merely shrinks it. Breakthrough research is what recruits talent, attracts private co-investment, and generates the very findings worth replicating.
[C1.4] Starve the pipeline and replication studies will soon have nothing important to replicate.
[C1.5] **Third, better mechanisms already exist and can scale.** Registered Reports, adversarial collaborations, multi-site consortia, and post-publication peer review have all gained traction without rigid budget mandates. Funders can—and increasingly do—include replication requirements within existing grants, asking applicants to budget a confirmatory phase. These flexible tools let reviewers and investigators allocate effort where epistemic uncertainty is greatest, rather than spreading resources evenly across fields that need it least.
[C1.6] **Fourth, mandates risk gaming.** When money is set aside specifically for replication, strategic actors will replicate low-hanging fruit or friendly colleagues' work, harvesting easy publications rather than stress-testing controversial claims. A guaranteed funding line invites bureaucratic capture, not scientific rigor.
[C1.7] The goal—trustworthy science—is shared. The means—a fixed mandatory share—is blunt, inefficient, and counterproductive. Funders should strengthen flexible accountability, not impose quotas.
