[P1.1] AI is excellent for mastery practice precisely because mastery practice is structured, repetitive, and immediately checkable. A student drilling algebra steps, vocabulary, grammar conventions, or thesis construction benefits from endless low-stakes reps, instant hints, and adaptive difficulty. That is where speed and scale matter most. If the motion stopped there, the case would be easy.
[P1.2] The problem is writing feedback in secondary school is not just error detection. Good writing feedback is relational, contextual, and tied to classroom purpose. A teacher knows the assignment, the texts studied, the student’s prior drafts, the maturity level of the class, and which weakness matters most right now. Strong feedback often says, in effect, “Your evidence is fine, but your real issue is that you are avoiding a claim,” or “This voice sounds polished but no longer sounds like you.” That judgment is not a generic language task; it is pedagogical mentoring.
[P1.3] Making AI the main source of that feedback creates three harms. First, it flattens writing into what the machine can easily reward: surface fluency, formulaic organization, and safe phrasing. Secondary students quickly learn to write for the system rather than for thought. They produce cleaner prose without developing stronger judgment.
[P1.4] Second, it weakens authenticity. If the same system both critiques and suggests rewrites, students drift toward machine-shaped prose and teachers lose sight of what the student can actually do unaided. Third, it is unreliable in the most educationally costly way: not random nonsense every time, but confident, plausible advice that is misaligned with the assignment or the student’s actual need. Bad feedback on a math drill is usually easy to catch; bad feedback on an argument can redirect a whole essay.
[P1.5] So the sensible line is not anti-AI. Use it heavily where its strengths fit the task: practice, retrieval, sentence-level drills, brainstorming examples, checking comprehension. But keep the main writing feedback human-led, because writing in secondary school is where schools are teaching reasoning, voice, integrity, and audience awareness—not just producing smoother sentences.
