[P1.1] When a country doubles or triples its university intake, it does not magically double the number of skilled jobs. It doubles the number of degree holders competing for the same positions. The result is credential inflation: a positional arms race where yesterday’s required qualification becomes today’s baseline, without raising the underlying skill demands of the work.
[P1.2] This is not abstract. Receptionists, warehouse supervisors, and police recruits now routinely face bachelor’s-degree requirements that did not exist two decades ago. The jobs did not become more complex; the filter simply tightened because employers can demand degrees in a flooded market. The expansion of higher education therefore functions less as a ladder of real mobility than as a credential treadmill.
[P1.3] Individuals must run faster—borrowing years and money—just to stand still.
[P1.4] Genuine opportunity requires either an actual increase in productive, well-paid roles or a measurable surge in human capital that creates such roles. Mass expansion has delivered neither. Instead, it has burdened both non-graduates and graduates: the former are screened out by new degree barriers that have nothing to do with job competence, while the latter often find themselves underemployed and indebted, having traded four years of wages and tuition for a positional good that has lost relative value. The wage premium for an average degree has compressed precisely because supply outran demand.
[P1.5] Policymakers sold expansion as meritocratic leveling, but education is a positional good. When everyone stands up, nobody sees better. What changes is the cost of the seat. Governments that pushed universal higher education shifted the burden of entry onto families and students, while employers captured a better-sorted applicant pool without paying for the sorting.
[P1.6] Meanwhile, inequality persists because elite pathways remain scarce and selective. Expanding the base of the pyramid does not flatten the hierarchy; it stretches it, obliging more people to climb further for the same foothold. The proposition is straightforward: if university growth primarily changed the currency rather than the economy, credential inflation outpaced real opportunity.
