[P1.2] The core case rests on three claims.
[P1.3] First, markets require informed consent to produce fair outcomes. Algorithmic pricing weaponizes information asymmetry: the seller knows everything about your browsing history, ZIP code, device type, and past purchases; you know nothing about how those signals are being used against you. This is not ordinary price competition—it is covert extraction. Consent obtained without relevant information is not meaningful consent, and a transaction you would refuse if you understood its terms should not be imposed on you invisibly.
[P1.4] Second, algorithmic price discrimination correlates with protected characteristics in ways that reproduce illegal discrimination without triggering legal liability. Income, neighborhood, device type, and shopping patterns are proxies for race, ethnicity, and disability status. [P1.5] Secrecy forecloses that accountability entirely.
[P1.6] Third, the "efficiency" defense assumes gains are shared. In practice, algorithmic systems are designed to capture consumer surplus maximally—to find the highest price each individual will bear. That is a wealth transfer to sellers, not a social gain. Where the transfer is achieved through opacity, prohibiting or requiring disclosure of that opacity is the minimum correction.
[P1.7] The proposition does not demand price uniformity. It demands transparency or, in cases where transparency alone is inadequate, a ban.
