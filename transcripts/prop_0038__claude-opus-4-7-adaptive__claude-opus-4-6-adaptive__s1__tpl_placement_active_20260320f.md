[P1.1] Noise is not an inconvenience. It is a pollutant that moves through the body much like fine particulates move through the lungs. Chronic exposure raises blood pressure, disrupts sleep architecture, elevates stress hormones, and is linked in the public health literature to ischemic heart disease, cognitive delays in children living near airports and highways, and higher rates of anxiety and depression. The European Environment Agency has for years ranked environmental noise as one of the largest environmental causes of disease on the continent, second only to air pollution.
[P1.2] Treating these two pollutants with radically different urgency is not science. It is habit.
[P1.3] The proposition follows from three simple propositions.
[P1.4] First, parity of harm demands parity of response. If a factory emitted particulate matter at levels that measurably shortened nearby residents' lives, we would not ask it politely to consider quieter machinery. We would set a binding limit and enforce it. A jackhammer at 3 a.m., a modified exhaust cruising a residential street, or a rooftop compressor running at 85 decibels next to a bedroom window inflicts comparable, measurable harm.
[P1.5] The regulatory posture should match.
[P1.6] Second, voluntary and complaint-driven regimes have failed. Most cities technically have noise codes. They are enforced reactively, by overstretched police responding to 311 calls, with fines too small to alter behavior. Air quality improved precisely because we moved from nuisance-style complaints to ambient standards, monitoring networks, and source-level limits on vehicles, fuels, and industry.
[P1.7] Noise needs the same architecture: continuous monitoring, source standards for vehicles and equipment, construction-hour discipline, and penalties that scale.
[P1.8] Third, the tools already exist and are cheap. Noise cameras can ticket illegal exhausts automatically. Quieter pavement, electric fleets, and rubber-dampened jackhammers are off-the-shelf. Construction curfews and decibel caps on commercial HVAC cost almost nothing to write.
[P1.9] The question is not whether cities can do this. It is why, given the damage, they still treat silence as a luxury rather than infrastructure.
