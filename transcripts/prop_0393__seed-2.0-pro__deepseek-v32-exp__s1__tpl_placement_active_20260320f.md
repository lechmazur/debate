[P1.1] When a rented e-scooter or e-bike rider hits a pedestrian on a sidewalk or restricted pedestrian zone, the injured party almost always loses out under current rules. Most riders either flee the scene or cannot be reliably tracked down through the companies’ minimal temporary rider records, and even when identified, the average rider cannot cover thousands of dollars in medical bills, lost wages, or long-term disability costs for serious crashes. Shifting legal responsibility to the shared mobility companies closes this accountability gap first, guaranteeing injured pedestrians have a clear, well-resourced party to pursue for compensation, rather than being left to navigate unenforceable small claims against anonymous, often low-income riders.
[P1.2] The companies are also the only actors with the power to stop these crashes before they happen. Every major provider already owns geofencing technology that can automatically slow or disable vehicles operating on sidewalks, school zones, and car-free pedestrian plazas, but most have delayed full, reliable rollout of these safeguards because there is no financial or legal penalty for failing to do so. Holding them liable for injuries in these restricted zones gives them a concrete incentive to prioritize safety over cost-cutting: they will roll out stricter, more accurate geofencing, enforce permanent bans for repeat rule-breaking riders, add unskippable safety prompts for high-risk areas, and adjust parking zones to keep vehicles away from crowded pedestrian corridors.
[P1.3] This rule does not eliminate rider responsibility: companies can and will recoup costs from riders who intentionally break rules, so riders still face consequences, but the burden no longer falls on the innocent injured party while companies avoid their duty to prevent foreseeable harm.
