[P1.1] Courts derive their power from a single source: the people. When nine unelected judges can override the democratic will of 330 million citizens for thirty years, we have a serious legitimacy problem that lifetime tenure both conceals and perpetuates.
[P1.2] Fixed terms solve this. Constitutional courts in Germany, South Korea, and South Africa serve set terms and command some of the world's deepest public trust in judicial institutions. The reason is straightforward: when justices know their decisions will eventually be reviewed by the political system, they are incentivized to anchor rulings in broadly shared constitutional principles rather than personal ideology. This is accountability, not capitulation.
[P1.3] The independence argument rests on a category mistake. True judicial independence means protection from improper political pressure—threats, bribes, retaliation. Fixed terms, sensibly structured, offer exactly this protection during a justice's tenure. What they eliminate is the second kind of "independence": permanent insulation from all democratic consequence, which is not a constitutional value but an institutional privilege that breeds contempt.
[P1.4] Consider what lifetime tenure actually produces. Hyper-politicized appointment battles. Justices who drift into ideologies their appointing president never imagined. The grotesque spectacle of justices calculating "strategic retirements" based on election cycles.
[P1.5] These are not signs of a healthy institution—they are symptoms of a legitimacy deficit that fixed terms would cure.
[P1.6] The trade-off is clear: justices gain security and focus during their terms, while the system gains a predictable rhythm of democratic renewal. Courts become less political because appointments become routine. Justices become more careful because they know decisions carry forward with their name attached. The constitutional order becomes more durable because it rests on genuine consent, not inherited authority.
[P1.7] Fixed terms do not diminish judicial power. They legitimize it. And legitimacy, not tenure, is what makes courts authoritative.
