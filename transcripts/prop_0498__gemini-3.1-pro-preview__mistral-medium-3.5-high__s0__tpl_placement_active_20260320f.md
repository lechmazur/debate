[P1.1] When a single corporation acts as both the referee and a competing player in the same market, the game is inherently rigged. Major app-store operators wield ultimate control over the digital economy's most critical distribution bottlenecks, dictating the rules while fielding their own first-party applications against independent developers. This profound conflict of interest necessitates structural separation.
[P1.2] The current model enables rampant, unavoidable self-preferencing. App store operators control the search algorithms, dictate pre-installation defaults, and gatekeep crucial system features. Worse, they impose hefty commissions—often up to 30%—on third-party competitors while exempting their own services. Consider the music streaming market: a first-party app pays no platform tax and integrates seamlessly with the device's ecosystem, while an independent competitor must either raise prices for consumers, operate at a loss, or disable in-app subscriptions entirely to survive.
[P1.3] This is not competition on the merits; it is a landlord extracting rent to subsidize its own business.
[P1.4] This structural imbalance stifles innovation and harms consumers. Independent developers cannot attract venture capital or justify research and development when they know the platform operator can simply clone their features, grant the first-party clone preferential search placement, and squeeze the original creator's margins. Consequently, consumers suffer from reduced choices, artificially inflated prices, and an ecosystem where the most innovative products lose out to the best-placed products.
[P1.5] Behavioral remedies—such as regulatory fines or mandates demanding "fair play"—are hopelessly inadequate. Fines are frequently absorbed as the cost of doing business, and auditing opaque, constantly shifting search algorithms is a regulatory nightmare. As long as the platform operator has a financial incentive to favor its own apps, it will find subtle, untraceable ways to do so.
[P1.6] Structural separation is the only definitive solution. By prohibiting the entity that controls the app store from also competing on it, we eliminate the conflict of interest at its root. The platform becomes a neutral arbiter incentivized to promote the best third-party apps, restoring a genuinely competitive marketplace that rewards innovation rather than gatekeeper power.
