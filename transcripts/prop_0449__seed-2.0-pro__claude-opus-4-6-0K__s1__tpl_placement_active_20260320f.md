[C1.1] The proposition asks every nation on Earth to accept a hard deadline for eliminating unabated coal power, enforced through trade sanctions and financial penalties. This sounds decisive. It is, in fact, dangerous, inequitable, and unworkable.
[C1.2] **Sovereignty and energy security cannot be overridden by a single deadline.** Coal provides roughly 35 percent of global electricity. For countries like India, Indonesia, Bangladesh, and much of sub-Saharan Africa, coal is not a luxury—it is the backbone of electrification for hundreds of millions of people who gained reliable power only in the last decade. A 2040 hard cutoff treats a nation that commissioned a coal plant in 2022 to serve newly electrified villages the same as a wealthy country that has burned coal for a century.
[C1.3] No carveout language changes this fundamental injustice: you are asking sovereign nations to strand billions of dollars in recently built infrastructure on someone else's timeline.
[C1.4] **Enforceable trade penalties will hurt the global poor most.** Tariffs and restricted finance access sound targeted, but they function as economic sanctions. When a middle-income country faces trade penalties for noncompliance, the costs cascade to workers, consumers, and the poorest citizens—not to elites. History shows that sanctions regimes rarely change behavior cleanly; they create black markets, deepen poverty, and breed resentment that undermines future cooperation.
[C1.5] **The mechanism is practically unenforceable.** No existing international body has the authority or capacity to monitor coal plant operations in every country, verify abatement technologies, and impose calibrated penalties. The WTO already struggles with far simpler trade disputes. This proposal would require an unprecedented enforcement architecture that does not exist and cannot realistically be built by 2040.
[C1.6] **A rigid deadline crowds out better approaches.** Carbon pricing, technology transfer agreements, and bilateral financing deals allow countries to decarbonize at different speeds while maintaining energy reliability. The proposition replaces flexibility with coercion, making cooperation less likely, not more. The road to decarbonization runs through pragmatism, not punitive uniformity.
