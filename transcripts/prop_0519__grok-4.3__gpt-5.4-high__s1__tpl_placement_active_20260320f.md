[P1.1] In fragmented service industries like retail, hospitality, and home care, minimum-wage hikes operate as a uniform floor that ignores wide differences in firm size, local demand, and operating margins. Many small operators respond by cutting hours, accelerating automation, or shifting toward contractors, so the net income gains for the lowest-paid workers shrink over repeated rounds. Sectoral bargaining avoids this pattern by negotiating standards across an entire industry, where all competitors face the same rules and no single firm gains an advantage by evading them.
[P1.2] The mechanism produces higher compliance because employers participate in setting the levels. Industry associations understand the cost structures involved and can structure agreements around realistic schedules for training, productivity improvements, or phased increases tied to revenue data. Minimum-wage legislation, by design, skips that calibration step and leaves enforcement to under-resourced agencies that cannot monitor every small service employer.
[P1.3] Sectoral bargaining also limits spillover damage. When standards are set only at the bottom through repeated hikes, firms at the margin often respond by trimming entry-level positions or substituting higher-skilled staff, which narrows opportunities for the very workers the policy intends to help. Sector-wide agreements can instead protect basic pay while permitting variation in other terms that preserve employment volume.
[P1.4] This difference matters most in industries where union density at the individual firm level is low and turnover is high. Uniform statutory increases create ongoing uncertainty that discourages capital investment; negotiated sectoral floors provide clearer long-term cost expectations for the same businesses. The result is a more durable floor with fewer of the employment trade-offs that accumulate after successive minimum-wage rounds.
