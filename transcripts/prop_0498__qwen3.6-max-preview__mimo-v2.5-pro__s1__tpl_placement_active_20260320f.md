[P1.1] When a single company controls the gateway to a digital marketplace and simultaneously sells its own products through it, the conflict of interest is not a bug—it is the business model. App-store operators possess unmatched power over discovery, distribution, and pricing. When they also compete as first-party developers, they inevitably tilt the rules toward their own apps. We see this in search rankings that bury rivals, default placements that lock in house brands, and proprietary data harvested from competitors to reverse-engineer winning features.
[P1.2] The result is a marketplace where success depends less on innovation and more on proximity to the gatekeeper.
[P1.3] Behavioral rules and antitrust fines cannot fix this. Conduct remedies require constant monitoring, invite endless litigation, and are routinely circumvented through opaque algorithmic tweaks or redesigned fee structures. By the time a violation is proven, the competing developer has already lost its funding, its users, or its will to fight. Structural separation cuts the knot.
[P1.4] Forcing platform operators to divest their first-party app divisions removes the incentive to rig the marketplace. The gatekeeper’s revenue then depends on attracting the best third-party apps, not suppressing them. Competition shifts back to product quality, and developers can invest without fearing that their host will become their predator.
[P1.5] Opponents will point to integration efficiencies, but those gains are dwarfed by the deadweight loss of chilled innovation and monopolized niches. A separated ecosystem does not ban first-party apps; it simply requires them to compete on equal terms under an independent platform operator. Digital markets thrive on open contestability, not curated dominance. Structural separation is the only remedy that aligns the platform’s incentives with the market’s health, guarantees enforceable fairness, and restores the competitive dynamism that built these ecosystems in the first place.
