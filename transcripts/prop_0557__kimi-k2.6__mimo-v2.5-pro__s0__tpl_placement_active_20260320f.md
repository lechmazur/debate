[P1.1] The test-optional experiment at public universities has become a case study in unintended consequences. By abandoning the SAT and ACT, institutions traded a flawed but equalizing metric for an opaque system that amplifies privilege and masks disadvantage behind the veneer of holistic review.
[P1.2] The core problem is straightforward: a high school transcript is not a standard unit of measurement. Grade inflation has turned A-minuses into participation trophies at affluent suburban schools, while rigorous urban campuses still hand out Cs for competent work. A valedictorian from an under-resourced district may have mastered the same material as a B-plus student at a wealthy prep school, yet admissions officers have no common language to recognize that parity. Standardized tests remain the only nationally available instrument that asks every applicant to answer the same questions under the same conditions, offering a rare moment of direct comparability across zip codes.
[P1.3] The substitutes are even more regressive. When test scores disappear, their place is filled by paid essay consultants, expensive extracurricular portfolios, and recommendation letters from well-connected counselors. These proxies are far more correlated with family income than a fifty-dollar exam fee. The student working thirty hours a week after school cannot compete with the peer who spent summers on a three-thousand-dollar service trip, but both can afford to sit for a Saturday morning test.
[P1.4] In fact, the exam is often the one place where a teenager from a modest background can demonstrate raw capacity without first paying for a private admissions coach.
[P1.5] Public universities exist to serve the public, not to replicate private inequities. A state flagship that drops testing tells a gifted student in a struggling district that her measurable academic ability matters less than the unverifiable polish of wealthier competitors. Reinstating standardized test requirements does not ignore disadvantage; it confronts it with a transparent, common yardstick. That is precisely why public universities must bring them back.

[C1.2] Decades of data show test scores tracking family income and parental education with striking fidelity. Calling that "transparency" doesn't make it fair; it just makes the bias legible on a score report rather than in an essay folder.
[C1.3] The real question is what happened when public universities dropped the requirement. The answer is not the chaos PRO implies. Students from under-resourced schools, first-generation applicants, and rural communities applied and enrolled in greater numbers.
[C1.4] The sky did not fall. Admissions offices did not collapse into arbitrariness. They adapted, leaning more heavily on contextualized academic performance—grades considered within the rigor and resources of the school that awarded them.

A transcript is not a perfect instrument, but a grade earned over four years across dozens of courses, often from teachers who know the student, captures something a four-hour Saturday exam cannot: sustained effort, intellectual growth, and resilience within a real environment. When admissions officers are trained to read transcripts in school context—recognizing that a 3.6 at an underfunded district with limited AP offerings may signal greater promise than a 3.9 at a prep school offering twenty APs—the result is not regression.
[C1.6] It is more informed judgment.
[C1.7] Reinstating test requirements means closing the door that test-optional policies opened for students who would never have applied because a single number told them they weren't good enough.
