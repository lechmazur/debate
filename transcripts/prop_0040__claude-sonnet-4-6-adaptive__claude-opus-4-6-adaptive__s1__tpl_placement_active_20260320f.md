[P1.1] When you buy a smart thermostat, a connected baby monitor, or a home security camera, you are not buying hardware. You are buying a promise of ongoing functionality. The moment software support ends, that device can become insecure, non-functional, or simply obsolete—sometimes within two years of purchase. Right now, nothing on the box tells you when that moment arrives.
[P1.2] That is the problem this proposition solves. A mandatory guaranteed support lifespan on physical packaging closes a fundamental information gap at the only moment it can actually help: before money changes hands.
[P1.3] The consumer rights argument is decisive. Every other category of durable goods comes with material disclosures at point of sale—energy ratings, safety certifications, warranty periods. Smart devices are the glaring exception. A buyer comparing two $200 security cameras cannot tell whether one receives security patches for three years and the other for eighteen months.
[P1.4] That information asymmetry is not an accident; manufacturers have no incentive to advertise short support windows, so they bury the details in terms-of-service nobody reads. Requiring the information on the box is the minimum corrective.
[P1.5] The market correction this creates is equally important. Once support lifespan is visible and comparable, it becomes a competitive variable. Manufacturers who currently race to the bottom on support costs will face direct pressure from consumers choosing products with longer guarantees. This is how labeling regimes work in every analogous context—they shift incentives without micromanaging product design.
[P1.6] There is also an environmental dimension. Premature device replacement driven by abandoned software support is a significant and growing contributor to electronic waste. Transparency enables consumers to reward products designed for longevity.
[P1.7] The opposition will likely argue that manufacturers cannot predict the future, or that this burdens innovation. Neither survives scrutiny. A guaranteed minimum is not a prediction—it is a contractual commitment, exactly the kind manufacturers already make for hardware warranties. The debate is simply whether that commitment should be visible or hidden.
