[P1.1] The climate crisis is already displacing millions through mechanisms no prior generation faced at this scale. [P1.2] Such a right would be tightly cabined—triggered only by peer-reviewed attribution of displacement to sustained sea-level incursion or lethal heat thresholds, with annual quotas and relocation to countries with historical emission burdens. [P1.3] The moral logic is straightforward: the nations most responsible for cumulative emissions have profited from the activities that now flood and bake the homes of the least responsible. Unrecognized displacement breeds statelessness, radicalization, and unmanaged secondary migration that burdens border states far more than calibrated asylum pathways would.
[P1.4] A limited right does not erode sovereignty; it exercises it intelligently. It forces governments to weigh trade-offs explicitly rather than letting courts improvise under outdated refugee definitions designed for political persecution. The alternative is ad-hoc humanitarian gestures that prove both insufficient and more expensive.
[P1.5] Countries that recognize this right acknowledge a new category of harm without rewriting all migration law. They choose managed justice over unmanaged crisis. The proposition therefore succeeds on grounds of realism, equity, and self-interest.
