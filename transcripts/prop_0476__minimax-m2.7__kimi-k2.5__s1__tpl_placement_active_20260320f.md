[P1.1] The space above our heads is not an infinite dumping ground. Today, commercial operators launch constellations of hundreds or thousands of satellites, extract billions in revenue, and leave the rest of us to manage the debris when things go wrong. This debate asks a simple question: should the people who profit from space also be responsible for cleaning it up? The answer, on any principled reading of who creates risks and who bears costs, is yes.
[P1.2] Consider what happens without bonds. An operator launches a satellite, profits from its service, and then—through failure, bankruptcy, or simple indifference—lets it drift as dead metal in low Earth orbit. That dead metal does not disappear. It threatens active satellites, forces costly collision avoidance maneuvers, and accumulates toward Kessler syndrome, where cascading collisions render entire orbital shells unusable for generations.
[P1.3] The operator captures the profit; the world absorbs the risk.
[P1.4] Deorbit bonds fix this misalignment. By requiring operators to post financial guarantees before launch—guarantees they forfeit if their spacecraft are not safely retired—the proposition forces the cost of proper disposal to be internalized before a single dollar of revenue is earned. Operators must factor end-of-life plans into their business models, not treat them as optional afterthoughts.
[P1.5] This is not untested policy. Mining companies post reclamation bonds. Oil operators carry decommissioning funds. Environmental law has long recognized that industries creating lasting risks must fund their own cleanup.
[P1.6] Space, the ultimate commons, deserves the same logic.
[P1.7] Critics will claim bonds are too costly for commercial operators, that they stifle innovation, that enforcement is impractical. They will be wrong on each count. Bonds can be scaled to satellite mass and orbital lifetime, so they hit only those operators who create genuine debris risk. And "enforcement is hard" is never a reason to abandon accountability—it is a reason to design better accountability mechanisms, which is exactly what deorbit bonds do.
[P1.8] The question is not whether we want space to remain usable. We all do. The question is whether we will let operators privatize profit while socializing risk. [clipped]

[C1.1] The proposition treats orbital mechanics like surface mining, but the physics of space defy this analogy. Unlike the toxic tailings governed by the reclamation bonds cited in [P1.5], satellites in low Earth orbit naturally succumb to atmospheric drag within years. The majority of modern commercial constellations already operate at altitudes where decay is inevitable, rendering the bond unnecessary for swaths of the industry. Where operators do park assets in higher orbits, the proper parallel is not environmental bonds but maritime law—where strict liability, not prepaid escrow, governs accidents.
[C1.2] More dangerously, deorbit bonds distort operational decisions. By penalizing operators whose satellites fail unexpectedly—hardware malfunctions or solar flares beyond reasonable control—bonds punish risk rather than negligence. Operators facing forfeiture will prioritize premature deorbiting over mission extension, sacrificing valuable service life to protect their deposit. Worse, bonds incentivize regulatory arbitrage: operators may shift launches to flag-of-convenience states or boost satellites into higher graveyard orbits to avoid bonding entirely, ironically increasing long-term debris persistence.
[C1.3] The mechanism also functions as a barrier to entry that entrenches incumbents. A billion-dollar constellation operator can post bonds without altering its capital structure; a university CubeSat or propulsion startup cannot. Bonds scale with satellite count, creating diseconomies that protect legacy players while strangling the innovation needed to develop better deorbit technologies.
[C1.4] Rather than locking up capital in escrow accounts for years, governments should strengthen post-failure liability regimes and require comprehensive insurance. This targets actual negligence, preserves operational flexibility, and lets markets price risk without suffocating the commercial space sector before it reaches maturity.

[P2.4] On fairness: the opponent warns that bonds punish operators for hardware malfunctions and solar flares. This misunderstands the mechanism. [P2.6] On regulatory arbitrage: the flag-of-convenience objection applies to every safety regulation ever proposed. [P2.7] On alternatives: their preferred approach is post-failure liability plus insurance. But orbital debris is not a liability that can be paid after the fact. A collision creating thousands of fragments cannot be undone by damages. Reactive liability regimes address compensation; bonds address prevention.
[P2.8] One tool cleans up after disaster; the other averts it. Prevention is not optional for an environment where mistakes last centuries.
