[P1.1] Housing markets across the developed world share a common pathology: cities need more homes, and local governments need stable revenue, yet the two most popular interventions—transaction taxes and rent caps—actively undermine both goals. Land-value taxation solves the same problems without generating those perverse incentives.
[P1.2] Start with housing supply. A land-value tax charges owners based on the unimproved value of their site, not on what they build. This creates a powerful incentive to develop underused land. A parking lot in a high-demand neighborhood becomes expensive to hold idle, pushing the owner toward construction.
[P1.3] By contrast, transaction taxes like stamp duties punish the act of moving property into more productive hands. Every time a site changes ownership—from someone who won't develop it to someone who will—the tax raises the cost of that transfer, locking land into inefficient uses. Rent caps go further in the wrong direction: by suppressing returns on rental housing, they discourage new construction and incentivize conversion of rental units to condos or short-term lets. The well-documented shrinkage of rental supply in cities with strict rent controls illustrates this clearly.
[P1.4] Now consider local public finance. Transaction taxes are inherently volatile—revenue collapses when markets cool, precisely when fiscal needs often spike. Rent caps generate no public revenue at all; they simply redistribute rents from landlords to incumbent tenants, often regressively, since long-tenure tenants in controlled units are not necessarily low-income. Land-value taxation, by contrast, taps a base that cannot flee or hide: land doesn't move, and its value reflects the public investments—transit, schools, infrastructure—that created it.
[P1.5] Revenue is stable across business cycles because land values fluctuate less wildly than transaction volumes.
[P1.6] The efficiency case is textbook: taxing land value is among the least distortionary taxes available, because the supply of land is fixed. You cannot produce less land in response to the tax. Transaction taxes and rent caps both distort behavior—reducing mobility, discouraging construction, misallocating existing stock.
[P1.7] The proposition asks a comparative question, and on every relevant margin—construction incentives, allocative efficiency, revenue stability, and fairness—land-value taxation outperforms both alternatives.
