[P1.1] Cities are not laboratories, and their residents are not experimental subjects. The proposition asks only that robotaxi fleets remain geofenced and numerically capped *until* cities establish clear rules for curb access, labor transitions, and safety standards. This is not obstruction—it is the bare minimum of responsible governance.
[P1.2] Consider curb management first. Dense urban centers already struggle with congestion, delivery vehicles, rideshare pickup, and pedestrian flow. Uncapped robotaxi fleets have strong incentives to cruise empty rather than park—avoiding fees while occupying lane space. San Francisco's experience already demonstrated that autonomous vehicles can block emergency responders and public transit when they encounter edge cases their programming cannot handle.
[P1.3] A geofenced, numerically limited deployment lets cities identify these problems before they scale beyond control.
[P1.4] Labor displacement demands equal urgency. Taxi and rideshare drivers—often immigrants, often working-class—face a technological transition that could eliminate their livelihoods with no warning. The proposition does not oppose that transition; it insists that cities have rules in place first: retraining programs, transition assistance, or certification requirements that give workers a path forward. Unchecked robotaxi expansion before such frameworks exist treats these workers as acceptable collateral damage.
[P1.5] Safety requires the same disciplined approach. Autonomous vehicle companies release data on disengagement rates and crash statistics in formats they control. Cities need independent verification standards, clear liability frameworks, and transparent reporting requirements before granting operators open access to dense streets. The cost of premature deployment is not abstract: a single vehicle encountering a situation it cannot navigate can block an ambulance or strike a pedestrian.
[P1.6] The "until" clause matters most. This proposition does not oppose robotaxi deployment—it opposes *unprepared* deployment. Cities that establish clear curb, labor, and safety frameworks should welcome the technology. But until those frameworks exist, geofencing and numerical caps remain the only responsible path forward.
