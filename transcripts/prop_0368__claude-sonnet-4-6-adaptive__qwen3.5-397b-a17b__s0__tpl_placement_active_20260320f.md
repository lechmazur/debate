[P1.1] The case for lowering the voting age rests on a straightforward democratic principle: when someone has a genuine stake in political outcomes and the capacity to reason about them, excluding them from decisions that govern their life requires specific justification. That justification does not exist for sixteen-year-olds.
[P1.2] Start with capacity. The objection that teenagers cannot reason politically collapses under scrutiny. At sixteen, cognitive development for deliberative reasoning is largely mature. The parts of the brain governing impulse control continue developing past eighteen, but the relevant faculty—the ability to weigh competing values and form considered preferences—is well established by mid-adolescence.
[P1.3] We don't ask voters to suppress impulsive behavior at the ballot box; we ask them to think. Sixteen-year-olds do that every day when navigating complex institutions, managing employment, and making consequential decisions about education.
[P1.4] Now consider stake. A sixteen-year-old in school today will spend the next seven decades living with decisions made about climate policy, public debt, and constitutional structure by electorates that systematically exclude young people. The further a decision's consequences extend into the future, the more it affects the young and the less it affects older voters. Excluding those with the longest exposure to policy outcomes is not a neutral procedural choice—it is a structural bias baked into the electorate itself.
[P1.5] The inconsistency in existing law reinforces this. In most countries, sixteen-year-olds can work and pay taxes, consent to medical treatment, and in some contexts serve in the military. The proposition that someone is mature enough to be taxed but not to vote on how that revenue is spent is incoherent, not cautious.
[P1.6] Where this has been tested—Scotland's 2014 independence referendum, Austria's national elections, various local elections—turnout among sixteen and seventeen-year-olds met or exceeded that of young adults. The practical concern dissolves on contact with evidence.
[P1.7] The threshold of eighteen is a historical accident, not a principled line. Sixteen serves democratic values better.
