[P1.1] Recessions are not just accounting events. They are moments when a laid-off warehouse worker decides whether to skip rent, pull a kid out of daycare, or let a car loan default. The question before us is whether the state should respond to that moment automatically, through rules already on the books, or discretionarily, through whatever Congress can pass after months of bargaining. The answer is automatic — and here is why.
[P1.2] First, timing. Discretionary stimulus is almost always late. Legislatures debate, negotiate, and finally act after the damage has compounded. Automatic stabilizers fire the instant unemployment claims rise.
[P1.3] That speed is not a luxury; it is the difference between a short downturn and a long one, because consumer spending is what keeps other people employed. Cash to the unemployed has among the highest fiscal multipliers economists measure — close to or above one — precisely because that money is spent immediately on necessities at local businesses.
[P1.4] Second, the debt objection is a category error. Yes, transfers raise debt during downturns. But downturns themselves shrink GDP, erode the tax base, and produce long-term scarring — workers who never return, skills that atrophy, children whose outcomes drop for decades. The relevant comparison is not "debt vs. no debt."
[P1.5] It is "debt plus a faster recovery" versus "slightly less debt plus a deeper, longer recession that itself worsens the debt-to-GDP ratio." Austerity in 2010-era Europe is the cautionary tale: cutting into weakness made the denominator shrink faster than the numerator.
[P1.6] Third, automaticity beats discretion on political economy grounds. Rules-based stabilizers cannot be held hostage to brinkmanship, cannot be traded for unrelated concessions, and cannot be denied to the unemployed because the opposition wants the incumbent to fail. They also phase out automatically when unemployment falls, which discretionary programs rarely do cleanly.
[P1.7] Speed, multipliers, scarring prevention, insulation from dysfunction. Debt taken on for these reasons is an investment, not a vice.
