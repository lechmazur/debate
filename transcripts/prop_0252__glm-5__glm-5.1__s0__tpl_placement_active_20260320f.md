[P1.1] Unpaid internships extract labor from vulnerable workers while disguising exploitation as opportunity. Governments should restrict them because they violate basic principles of fair compensation, entrench class inequality, and impose hidden costs on society.
[P1.2] The core problem is straightforward: productive work deserves payment. When an intern staffs a front desk, drafts client communications, or manages social media accounts, they create real economic value. Calling this "learning" doesn't make it any less labor. A genuinely educational placement—like a medical residency or student-teaching practicum—is structured around pedagogy, with supervision, feedback, and curricular goals.
[P1.3] Too many unpaid internships lack these features entirely. They're entry-level jobs with a "trainee" label slapped on to avoid paying wages.
[P1.4] The burden falls heaviest on those least able to bear it. Students from working-class backgrounds cannot afford months of unpaid labor in expensive cities. They take paid service jobs instead, while wealthier peers accumulate résumé credentials and professional networks. The result is a class filter masquerading as meritocracy.
[P1.5] Industries that rely heavily on unpaid interns—journalism, fashion, politics—become dominated by those with family wealth, narrowing perspectives and perpetuating elite self-replication.
[P1.6] Employers benefit twice: they receive free labor, and they externalize training costs onto workers themselves. When governments refuse to regulate, they effectively subsidize this model by allowing firms to bypass minimum-wage laws under the cover of "education." Legitimate educational internships, properly supervised and integrated into academic programs, can serve real purposes.
[P1.7] But outside that narrow category, unpaid work is simply work without pay—and societies that claim to value fairness should not permit it to continue unchecked.

An unpaid position with real duties beats a nonexistent position every time.
[C1.3] The inequality argument proves too much. Yes, wealthier students can afford unpaid stints more easily. But wealthier students also can afford elite colleges, test prep, and family connections. The unequal playing field predates internships and will survive their prohibition.
[C1.4] The real question is whether closing one narrow pathway helps anyone or merely removes a ladder because not everyone can climb it at the same speed.
[C1.5] The dream of cleanly separating "genuinely educational" placements from everything else misunderstands how professional learning actually works. A congressional intern answering phones learns constituent relations, legislative process, and office dynamics—pedagogy embedded in productive tasks, not separate from them. A journalism intern pushing content learns editorial judgment through repeated practice. Restrictive regimes invariably draw the educational line too narrowly, trapping real learning inside bureaucratic definitions while strangling the informal apprenticeships that launch careers.
[C1.6] Finally, adults deserve the autonomy to evaluate tradeoffs for themselves. Interns choose unpaid positions because they value experience, contacts, and signaling over immediate wages. A policy that overrides that choice "for their own good" merely substitutes the state's judgment for the worker's assessment of what benefits them.
