[P1.1] Transportation infrastructure either binds a society together or fractures it along class lines. Robotaxi fleets are not merely another consumer convenience; they are poised to replace public transit, human taxis, and private vehicle ownership across metropolitan areas. If left to pure market logic, these fleets will cluster in wealthy districts where trip density maximizes profit per mile, effectively walling off low-income neighborhoods from reliable mobility. The proposition demands only that regulators treat autonomous transport as the essential utility it has become.
[P1.2] The analogy to regulated utilities holds, and the historical warning is clear. We do not permit electric companies to blacklist poor zip codes, and we legislated against redlining in banking because market-driven exclusion entrenches poverty. Mobility is equally foundational: without it, residents cannot reach hospitals, employment centers, or schools. When algorithms optimize exclusively for margin, they impose a spatial tax on poverty, forcing reliance on scarce, expensive alternatives or isolation.
[P1.3] The externalities are severe—missed medical appointments, unstable employment, and emergency responders stretched thin by stranded populations.
[P1.4] Opponents will claim that mandating coverage in low-demand areas forces inefficient cross-subsidization. Yet the standard is "reasonably similar reliability," not identical frequency. Fleets can adjust vehicle sizing, pool rides, or vary dynamic pricing modestly while maintaining basic continuity. A ten-minute wait in a sparse neighborhood versus five downtown satisfies the requirement without wrecking business models.
[P1.5] The efficiency cost is trivial against the social cost of creating permanent mobility deserts.
[P1.6] Unregulated concentration does not merely reflect demand; it actively shapes urban geography toward segregation. By guaranteeing baseline access everywhere, we prevent the hardening of economic exclusion and ensure autonomous mobility functions as infrastructure for all rather than becoming another gated amenity for the affluent.

[C1.1] The core flaw in the PRO case is its false analogy between robotaxis, which operate in a competitive, unfranchised market, and natural monopoly utilities like electric companies. Unlike power grids, where only one provider can serve an area, dozens of robotaxi operators can and do compete for riders in dense districts, and forcing all of them to maintain unprofitable service in low-demand areas will immediately wipe out small, low-overhead competitors that currently undercut the prices of large players like Cruise and Waymo.
[C1.2] This mandate would entrench corporate monopolies, driving up ride costs for every rider, including the millions of low-income service workers who rely on cheap robotaxi rides to get to late shifts in high-density employment and residential districts. PRO’s handwave that “reasonably similar reliability” only requires minor adjustments ignores basic math: low-demand neighborhoods have, by definition, too few trip requests to support consistent short wait times without stationing idle vehicles in those areas for hours at a time. Ride pooling does not work where there are rarely multiple riders going the same direction, and modest dynamic pricing hikes would only make rides unaffordable for the low-income residents the policy claims to help.
[C1.3] Worse, diverting fleets to low-demand zones will increase wait times in dense neighborhoods, where the vast majority of low-income urban residents live. This policy robs Peter to pay Paul, imposing steep costs on most low-income riders to deliver negligible benefits to a small subset, while crowding out far more efficient targeted public transit subsidies for low-demand areas that do not raise costs for other vulnerable groups. The proposed rule does not reduce class inequity—it exacerbates it, by making mobility more expensive and less reliable for the largest group of low-income riders to serve a tiny, more politically visible cohort, while eliminating the competitive pressure that keeps robotaxi prices accessible for everyone.
