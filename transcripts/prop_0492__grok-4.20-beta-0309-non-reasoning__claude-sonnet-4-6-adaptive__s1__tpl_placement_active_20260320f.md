[P1.2] Demand-side tools—carbon pricing, performance standards, public procurement, and targeted tax credits for buyers—pull innovation toward what markets actually need. Producer subsidies, by contrast, lock in today's champions, slowing the very progress they claim to buy.

[P1.4] The cost difference is stark. Every dollar spent on producer subsidies must first pass through political allocation and bureaucratic oversight, then through firms that may have weaker incentives to cut prices once the check clears. Demand-side measures put the incentive directly on the customer: the lower the price or the better the performance, the more the policy pays out. This aligns private profit with public goals far more tightly.
[P1.5] Electric-vehicle purchase incentives, for instance, have driven rapid scale-up across multiple suppliers without requiring governments to forecast which battery chemistry or which company would win. The market sorted it.
