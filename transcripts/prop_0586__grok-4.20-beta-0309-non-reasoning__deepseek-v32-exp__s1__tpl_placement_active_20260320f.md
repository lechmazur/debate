[P1.1] Sectoral bargaining and wage boards offer a proven, flexible path to lifting pay for low-wage workers while avoiding the blunt employment losses that accompany large nationwide minimum-wage hikes. Instead of imposing a single floor that ignores industry realities, these mechanisms let workers and employers in each sector negotiate wages and standards tailored to local costs, productivity, and competitive pressures. The result is higher earnings concentrated where they are most needed, with far smaller risks of disemployment.
[P1.2] The core mechanism is simple and powerful: wage boards or sectoral councils bring together unions, employers, and sometimes public representatives to set binding minimum standards for an industry. This spreads gains across competitors so no single firm is undercut, reducing the incentive to cut hours or staff. A fast-food chain in a high-cost city can absorb a sector-specific increase more readily than a uniform $15 mandate that hits rural diners and urban franchises alike. Employment effects stay muted because the wage path reflects actual market conditions rather than political fiat.
[P1.3] Contrast that with across-the-board increases. When the minimum wage jumps sharply everywhere, marginal employers in low-productivity sectors have few options: they shed workers, cut hours, or close. Decades of evidence show these disemployment effects are real, if sometimes modest in aggregate; they fall hardest on the very people the policy aims to help—young, less-educated, and minority workers. Sectoral bargaining sidesteps that trap by letting high-wage sectors move faster while giving lower-wage sectors breathing room, producing larger average gains with smaller job losses.
[P1.4] The approach also encourages ongoing dialogue rather than periodic political fights. Once established, boards can adjust standards gradually as conditions change, smoothing the path for both workers and businesses. This adaptability is precisely what large, infrequent minimum-wage leaps lack. By letting wages rise where markets can bear it and moderating them where they cannot, sectoral systems deliver more pay with fewer pink slips.
[P1.5] That is the superior bargain for low-wage Americans.
