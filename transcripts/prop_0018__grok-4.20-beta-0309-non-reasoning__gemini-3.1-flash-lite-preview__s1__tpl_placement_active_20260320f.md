[P1.1] Cities should impose congestion pricing in central districts even when drivers strongly oppose it, because the policy's benefits to mobility, economic vitality, and environmental quality decisively outweigh the temporary inconvenience of those who drive. Congestion is a classic tragedy of the commons: each additional car in the core slows everyone else, wastes fuel, and raises the cost of doing business for the entire city. Pricing internalizes that cost, shifting marginal trips to off-peak hours, public transit, or remote destinations. The result is faster travel times for the remaining drivers, more reliable bus and delivery schedules, and less idling pollution.
[P1.2] Experience from Stockholm, Singapore, and London shows that once implemented, congestion pricing quickly gains public acceptance as people experience freer movement and cleaner air. Drivers' initial opposition is understandable but ultimately self-defeating; they are objecting to paying for a scarce resource whose price has been artificially held at zero. The status quo forces everyone, including bus riders, cyclists, emergency vehicles, and the drivers themselves, to pay in the far more expensive currency of lost time and increased emissions.
[P1.3] Opponents may frame this as elitist or regressive, yet the policy can be designed with rebates, transit improvements, or income-sensitive discounts that protect lower-income residents while still preserving the incentive structure. The alternative is continued gridlock that harms the very workers who rely on efficient central-district commerce. A city that refuses to price congestion chooses paralysis over progress, subsidizing delay at everyone's expense. The principled stance is therefore clear: enact the charge, return the revenue to mobility-enhancing uses, and let the improved outcomes answer the critics.
[P1.4] Drivers' strong opposition does not confer a veto over policies that make the city function better for all.
