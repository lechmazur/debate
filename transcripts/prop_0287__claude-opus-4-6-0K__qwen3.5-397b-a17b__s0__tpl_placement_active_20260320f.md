[P1.1] Life tenure for constitutional court justices was designed for a world of shorter lifespans and smaller government footprints. Today it produces democratic pathologies that fixed terms would cure—without meaningfully threatening judicial independence.
[P1.2] **The democratic legitimacy gains are enormous.** When a single justice serves for thirty-five or forty years, the composition of the court becomes hostage to actuarial luck. Presidents who happen to preside over vacancies wield outsized influence; those who don't are erased from constitutional development. This randomness severs the link between electoral outcomes and the judiciary's direction. Fixed, staggered terms—say, eighteen years with one vacancy per presidential term—would regularize appointments so that each election carries roughly equal constitutional weight.
[P1.3] Every president shapes the court; every electorate's voice is heard. That is a direct, structural improvement to democratic legitimacy.
[P1.4] **The independence costs are minimal and manageable.** Independence does not require life tenure. Germany's Federal Constitutional Court uses twelve-year, non-renewable terms. Justices there are widely regarded as independent and have struck down major government initiatives, including surveillance laws and EU treaty provisions. [P1.5] The empirical track record across democracies shows that fixed terms and robust independence coexist comfortably.
[P1.6] **Life tenure itself creates independence problems.** The stakes of each appointment become so high that confirmation battles turn into political warfare, which paradoxically politicizes the judiciary far more than regular, predictable turnover would. Strategic retirement—justices timing departures to ensure ideological successors—is itself a compromise of independence, because it makes justices behave as political actors rather than neutral arbiters.
[P1.7] The proposition asks a comparative question: does the democratic legitimacy gain exceed the independence cost? The gain is systemic and structural. The cost is speculative and contradicted by real-world experience. Fixed terms don't just improve the balance—they improve both sides of the ledger simultaneously, strengthening legitimacy while reducing the perverse incentives life tenure creates.
