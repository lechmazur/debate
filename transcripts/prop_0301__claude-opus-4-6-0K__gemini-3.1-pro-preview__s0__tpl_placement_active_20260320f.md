[P1.1] Public money carries public obligations. That principle is the heart of this debate. When governments fund religious schools, they are redirecting taxpayer dollars—dollars collected from citizens of every faith and none—toward institutions that may teach creationism instead of biology, exclude LGBTQ students, or skip civic education entirely. The proposition does not ban religious schools or strip them of funding.
[P1.2] It simply says: if you want public money, meet public standards.
[P1.3] Three arguments anchor this case.
[P1.4] **First, accountability to taxpayers demands equivalent standards.** No one disputes that public schools must teach an approved curriculum, accept all students, and submit to oversight. Religious schools that receive equivalent funding but dodge equivalent obligations create a two-tier system where some children get a publicly guaranteed education and others get whatever a particular institution decides to offer. Parents remain free to choose religious schooling—but the public purse should not subsidize an education that fails to prepare students for citizenship or the labor market.
[P1.5] **Second, children have independent rights that override institutional autonomy.** A child enrolled in a government-funded school deserves competent science instruction, honest civic education, and freedom from discriminatory expulsion. Religious freedom protects belief; it does not entitle institutions to public funds while denying children the knowledge and equal treatment that society has decided every young person deserves. The standards in this proposition protect the very people least able to protect themselves.
[P1.6] **Third, conditional funding already works and is constitutionally routine.** Governments impose conditions on hospitals, universities, and nonprofits that receive public funds—fire safety codes, anti-fraud rules, non-discrimination clauses. Religious organizations already comply with many such conditions without losing their religious character. Requiring curricular and civic benchmarks is no different in kind. The fear that standards will destroy religious identity is empirically unfounded: many faith-based schools worldwide meet national curricula while maintaining vibrant religious missions.
[P1.7] The CON side must explain why religious schools deserve a unique exemption from the accountability every other publicly funded institution accepts. Until they do, the proposition stands as a straightforward application of democratic fairness.
