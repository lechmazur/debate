[P1.1] Your friends are not Facebook's property. Yet today, if you leave a major platform, you forfeit years of curated connections, messages, and community history. The platform does not merely host your social life; it holds it hostage. Governments should break this lock by mandating interoperable, user-portable messaging and social graph access for qualified third parties, because voluntary industry reform has repeatedly failed and market concentration keeps deepening.
[P1.2] The principle is unambiguous: your relationships belong to you, not to a corporation. We already enforce this logic for phone numbers, banking data, and email. You can switch carriers without losing your contacts or call history. Social media should operate under the same standard.
[P1.3] When users control their social graph, competition shifts from who can trap the most people to who can offer the best experience.
[P1.4] Without portability, network effects become impenetrable moats. A startup with superior privacy or cleaner design cannot gain traction if joining requires abandoning everyone you know. Interoperability dissolves that barrier. New entrants gain a fighting chance, incumbents must innovate to retain users, and the market rewards quality instead of inertia, surveillance, and dark patterns.
[P1.5] Email proves the model works. Because providers use open protocols, a ProtonMail user writes to a Gmail user seamlessly. If Gmail blocked outside messages, adoption would crumble. Dominant social platforms do exactly that with messaging and friend lists, suppressing alternatives you never get to try and forcing you to stay for fear of social isolation.
[P1.6] Qualified third-party access answers security fears without sacrificing freedom. Governments can mandate end-to-end encryption, audit eligibility, and revoke bad actors. The alternative is the status quo: unaccountable monopolies monetizing a captive audience by walling off human connection. Portable data returns power to the user where it belongs.

[C1.6] "Qualified" does enormous work here, but qualification is a process, not a guarantee. Neither outcome serves users.
[C1.7] Finally, interoperability mandates freeze architecture. Platforms cannot iterate on privacy models, encryption schemes, or trust-and-safety systems when they must maintain stable third-party APIs.
