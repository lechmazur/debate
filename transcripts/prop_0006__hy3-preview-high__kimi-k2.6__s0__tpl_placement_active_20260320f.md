[P1.1] The core unfairness of current gig wage calculations is a deliberate loophole: platforms exclude the hours workers spend tethered to their apps waiting for tasks, even when that time is entirely controlled by the platform. This exclusion is not a neutral accounting choice, but a calculated strategy to suppress labor costs while maintaining the appearance of competitive per-task pay. Minimum wage laws exist to ensure workers are paid for all time their labor is under an employer’s direction. When a driver stays logged into a ride-hail app to receive ride requests, or a delivery worker keeps a courier app open to accept orders, they are not free to take other work, leave their assigned zone, or disengage without losing access to income.
[P1.2] That is controlled, compensable work time.
[P1.3] Opponents will frame this waiting period as “flexible downtime,” but that misrepresents how these platforms operate. Most gig workers cannot opt out of active waiting without forfeiting the ability to earn at all: logging off means no task offers, and many platforms penalize workers for low availability metrics. This is not hypothetical: many gig workers report that waiting time makes up half or more of their total app time on slow days, eroding their take-home pay to below legal minimums.
[P1.4] A worker who spends three hours on an app, 90 minutes completing deliveries, and 90 minutes waiting for the next order is currently paid only for the task time. That shrinks their effective hourly wage well below minimum wage, even if their per-task rate looks adequate on paper.
[P1.5] Platforms reap direct benefits from having workers on standby: faster response times for customers, higher retention of users, and more reliable service. They should bear the cost of that standby time, not shift it entirely to workers. Counting active app waiting time for minimum wage compliance closes an artificial gap that lets platforms skirt basic labor standards while profiting from workers’ tied-up time.

[C1.1] The proposition asks us to rewrite the fundamental bargain of gig work. Task-based platforms do not hire workers by the hour; they offer piece-rate opportunities that workers accept precisely because they can log on and off at will. Treating every minute the app is open as compensable time collapses that distinction and turns millions of independent contractors into de facto hourly employees without the corresponding benefits package, merely the compliance costs.
[C1.2] PRO's portrait of "captive" waiting ignores how gig labor actually functions. Drivers and couriers routinely run multiple apps simultaneously, cherry-picking the best offer while declining others. A delivery rider waiting for a task is typically waiting on several platforms at once. Which single company owes her a wage for that shared minute?
[C1.3] The claim that waiting time is platform-controlled evaporates once you recognize that workers already multi-home, using so-called standby to comparison-shop rather than serving any one master.
[C1.4] If platforms must pay for waiting time, they will behave exactly like employers trying to minimize idle payroll. They will cap the number of workers allowed online, require scheduled shifts, and blacklist zones where demand is thin. The suburban driver who logs in hoping to catch the dinner rush will find the app locked because the company cannot afford to pay him to wait. The very flexibility PRO claims to protect would be the first casualty.
[C1.5] Finally, wage bills do not exist in a vacuum. Platforms facing standby obligations will not simply absorb the cost; they will cut per-task rates to maintain unit economics. A courier who currently earns well during three busy hours and endures two slow ones will instead receive a flattened, lower rate across the board. Counting waiting time toward minimum wage does not conjure new money into workers' pockets; it restructures pay in ways that reduce peak earnings and eliminate the freedom that made gig work attractive in the first place.
