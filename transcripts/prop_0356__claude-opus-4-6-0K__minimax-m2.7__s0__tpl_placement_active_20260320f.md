[P1.1] Continuous in-car surveillance—tracking every brake, every turn, every trip at 2 a.m.—represents one of the most intimate monitoring regimes any commercial actor has ever deployed against ordinary consumers. The proposition does not ban it. It demands only that drivers opt in on genuinely fair terms. That modest guardrail is essential for three reasons.
[P1.2] **First, the privacy intrusion is extraordinary and deserves affirmative consent.** Telematics devices and smartphone apps record location, speed, acceleration, braking patterns, time of travel, and route choice in continuous streams. Aggregated, this data reveals where you work, worship, seek medical care, and whom you visit. No other retail product routinely generates a comparable dossier. When a surveillance burden is this deep, the baseline rule should be informed, voluntary participation—not silent enrollment buried in page forty of a renewal packet.
[P1.3] **Second, without an opt-in requirement, market dynamics coerce participation.** Insurers can set the standard premium punitively high and frame surveillance as a "discount," making refusal financially irrational. That is not genuine choice; it is a penalty for exercising a privacy preference. Genuinely fair terms mean the non-surveillance price must reflect ordinary actuarial risk, not an inflated anchor designed to manufacture consent. The proposition addresses exactly this structural coercion.
[P1.4] **Third, the data collected creates downstream harms that drivers cannot control.** Behavioral profiles can be sold to data brokers, subpoenaed in litigation, breached in cyberattacks, or used to deny future coverage. Once continuous surveillance is normalized as a default, drivers lose leverage to negotiate data-use limits. An opt-in regime forces insurers to compete on the quality and fairness of their surveillance terms, giving consumers real bargaining power before data collection begins.
[P1.5] The CON side must defend letting insurers impose pervasive monitoring without meaningful consent. That position treats drivers as risk-generating objects to be measured, not autonomous people with rights over their own information. A genuine opt-in on fair terms is the minimum standard a just insurance market should meet.
