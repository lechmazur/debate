[P1.1] Nobody gets their property rights criminalised just because someone else might misuse the same tool. That is the baseline every decent legal system operates on. We do not ban kitchen knives because some people stab others. We do not ban locked garage doors because someone might store stolen goods inside.
[P1.2] We punish the actual crime, not the neutral tool that lets people exercise basic control over their own lives.
[P1.3] Self-custody wallets are just that: a digital locked box that only the holder holds the key to. They do not enable crime any more than a physical safe does. What enables crime is the deliberate choice to use them for illegal acts. The opposite rule—that neutral tools can be banned by default unless an individual proves they have a good use—breaks every presumption of innocence built over centuries of liberal law.
[P1.4] Consider the human cost of the alternative. If self-custody is restricted by default, every ordinary person loses the ability to avoid having their life savings frozen, drained by exchange hacks, or seized on unproven administrative suspicion. [P1.5] This proposition draws the only just line: state power may not touch an individual's wallet unless authorities can demonstrate, with actual evidence, that this specific wallet is tied to specific criminal conduct. That is not a loophole for criminals. That is the minimum standard for freedom in any society that does not treat every citizen as a suspect by default.
