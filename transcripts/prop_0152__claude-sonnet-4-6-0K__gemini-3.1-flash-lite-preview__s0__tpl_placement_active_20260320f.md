[P1.1] The central question is simple: does treating drug possession as a criminal matter save lives and reduce crime, or does it make both worse? The evidence from every jurisdiction that has tried decriminalization points firmly in one direction.
[P1.2] Start with the mechanism. Criminalization does not eliminate drug use—it drives it underground. When users fear arrest, they don't seek help; they use alone, they use quickly, and they use in dirty conditions. That fear kills people.
[P1.3] Decriminalization removes the legal threat that keeps someone from calling emergency services when a friend is overdosing. That single change—removing the disincentive to call 911—demonstrably saves lives where it has been implemented.

In the years that followed, drug-related deaths dropped sharply, HIV infections among users fell substantially, and incarceration of nonviolent drug offenders collapsed—freeing police and court resources for violent crime. Usage rates did not spike.
[P1.5] This directly contradicts the core assumption underlying punitive enforcement: that criminal penalties deter drug use in meaningful numbers.
[P1.6] The downstream crime point is equally important. Most acquisitive crime connected to drug use—theft, burglary, petty fraud—flows from addiction that goes untreated, not from decriminalization. When someone can access treatment without risking arrest, addiction rates fall. When addiction falls, so does the desperation-driven crime that funds it.
[P1.7] Criminalization, by contrast, creates additional crimes: criminal records that destroy employment prospects, which increase poverty, which increase recidivism. The punitive model manufactures the very downstream harm it claims to prevent.
[P1.8] The opposing case rests on the assumption that harsher penalties deter. But drug dependence is a medical condition, not a rational cost-benefit calculation. Deterrence theory simply does not apply the way it might for other offenses.
