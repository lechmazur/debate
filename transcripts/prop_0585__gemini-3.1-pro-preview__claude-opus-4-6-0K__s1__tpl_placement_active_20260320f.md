[P1.1] To achieve a reliable, decarbonized grid, we must prioritize moving power across space rather than shifting it across time. Investing primarily in transmission expansion and regional market integration is far more effective than subsidizing long-duration energy storage (LDES) for three fundamental reasons.
[P1.2] First, transmission leverages geographic diversity to solve intermittency. Weather systems are massive but localized. When wind generation drops in the Midwest, solar generation is peaking in the Southwest. By connecting disparate regions, a robust transmission network dynamically balances supply and demand.
[P1.3] Moving electricity across high-voltage lines is highly efficient. In contrast, LDES relies on converting electricity into another medium—like compressed air, thermal mass, or novel battery chemistries—and back again. These conversions suffer severe round-trip efficiency losses, often wasting 30 to 50 percent of the original energy. We should not subsidize the inefficient storage of clean power when we can simply deliver it directly to where it is needed.
[P1.4] Second, transmission targets the actual bottleneck to decarbonization: grid congestion. Today, thousands of gigawatts of cheap, clean wind and solar projects are stalled in interconnection queues. They are not waiting for breakthrough storage technology; they are waiting for the physical wires to carry their power to market. Subsidizing LDES does nothing to clear this backlog.
[P1.5] Building transmission directly relieves this congestion, eliminating the curtailment of existing renewables and accelerating the deployment of new ones.
[P1.6] Third, regional market integration fundamentally reduces the total backup infrastructure required to run the grid. When neighboring grid operators integrate, they pool their reserve margins. A broader geographic footprint experiences smaller aggregate fluctuations in peak demand and renewable output. This pooling of risk means the system requires drastically less backup power overall to maintain reliability during extreme weather events.
[P1.7] LDES technologies—excluding geographically constrained pumped hydro—remain largely pre-commercial, expensive, and speculative. Funneling primary investments into LDES subsidies risks stranding capital on technologies that may never scale. Conversely, transmission and regional markets rely on mature technologies and established economics. They represent the most immediate, efficient, and reliable backbone for a decarbonized energy future.
