[P1.1] Copyright law has always been a bargain: society grants creators temporary monopoly rights in exchange for genuine human creative contribution. That bargain breaks down entirely when the "creator" is software.
[P1.2] The proposition asks for something modest and precise: copyright protection for AI-generated output should require documented evidence of substantial human creative control over the final expression. This is not a ban on AI tools. It is a demand that the human contribution be real, traceable, and meaningful—not a post-hoc rationalization slapped on a machine's work.
[P1.3] Here is why this matters. Copyright confers enormous market power. Whoever holds it can exclude competitors, demand licensing fees, and suppress derivative uses. When a corporation deploys a generative model, prompts it with a single sentence, and claims ownership of the thousands-word output, that corporation obtains monopoly power without any of the creative labor copyright was designed to reward.
[P1.4] The downstream effect is that the public domain—the common cultural heritage that feeds future creativity—gets raided without justification. Society loses; only the prompt-writer's employer wins.
[P1.5] The principle of human authorship is not a technicality. US courts have historically grounded copyright in human creative expression, and the Copyright Office has consistently declined to register works produced without human authorship. Those decisions reflect a coherent view: the purpose of copyright is to incentivize human creative risk-taking. A language model takes no risks, makes no expressive choices in any morally meaningful sense, and receives no incentive from the prospect of royalties.
[P1.6] The practical test the proposition demands—documented substantial creative control—is workable. If a human makes genuine decisions about structure, language, and expression, they can show that. What fails the test is exactly what should fail: a company claiming ownership of text it essentially ordered from an automated system.
[P1.7] The real question is whether we allow copyright to become a tool for laundering machine output into monopoly rights. The answer must be no.
