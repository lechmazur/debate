[P1.1] Hidden fees are not a quirk of vacation rentals. They are a design choice—a deliberate friction point that extracts money from consumers after they have already committed emotionally and practically to a booking. Requiring platforms to surface cleaning fees, resort fees, and house rules before the checkout or booking-request step is not a bureaucratic nicety. It is the minimum standard of honesty that every other consumer marketplace already meets.
[P1.2] Consider how the problem actually works. A traveler searches for a beach house, finds one listed at $120 a night, and spends thirty minutes comparing it, reading reviews, imagining the trip. She clicks to book. Only then does a $200 cleaning fee, a $75 resort fee, and a rule prohibiting the two dogs she planned to bring appear.
[P1.3] At that stage, behavioral inertia—what researchers call the sunk-cost effect in decision-making—has already done its work. Many people proceed. That is exactly why platforms tolerate this structure: late disclosure converts hesitation into purchase.
[P1.4] This is a problem with three distinct dimensions. First, it is a fairness problem: consumers cannot make genuine price comparisons when key cost components are buried. A host charging $80 a night with a $250 cleaning fee is more expensive than one charging $150 with none, but the listing search makes the opposite appear true.
[P1.5] Second, it is a consent problem: house rules create real contractual obligations—no parties, no smoking, mandatory checkout at 8 a.m.—that users should evaluate before, not after, they are locked into a request. Third, it is a market-efficiency problem: when prices are obscure, competition on price weakens, and that benefits incumbent platforms at the expense of both guests and hosts who compete on genuine value.
[P1.6] The fix the proposition requires is simple and technically trivial. Platforms already possess this data. The only barrier to showing it early is that showing it early costs conversions. That interest—the platform's revenue optimization—is not a reason to exempt them from honest dealing.
