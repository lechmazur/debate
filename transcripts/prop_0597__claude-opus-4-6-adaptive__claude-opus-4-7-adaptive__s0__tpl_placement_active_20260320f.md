[P1.1] A child born with Tay-Sachs disease will, in almost every case, lose motor function, go blind, and die before age five. There is no cure. A child who inherits two copies of the sickle cell mutation faces a lifetime of pain crises, organ damage, and shortened life expectancy. [P1.2] The proposition asks a narrow question: once safety thresholds are met, should international rules *permit* heritable genome editing for these diseases? Not mandate it. Not open the door to enhancement. Permit it, for serious conditions, after rigorous safety criteria are satisfied.
[P1.3] Opposing this means telling families that even when the science is demonstrably safe, the international community should still forbid them from preventing devastating genetic disease in their children.
[P1.4] Three pillars support this case.
[P1.5] **First, the moral weight of preventable suffering.** When we possess a tool that can prevent a child from inheriting a fatal or severely debilitating condition, withholding that tool demands extraordinary justification. [P1.6] **Second, the proposition is self-limiting.** Every concern about runaway enhancement or premature application is addressed by the structure of the motion itself. [P1.7] **Third, a blanket ban is worse than regulated permission.** Prohibition doesn't eliminate demand—it drives editing underground or into poorly regulated jurisdictions. [P1.8] The CON side must explain why indefinite prohibition remains justified even after the science is proven safe—why the abstract fear of a slippery slope outweighs the concrete reality of children suffering from preventable disease.
