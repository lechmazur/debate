[P1.1] Major app-store operators like Apple and Google control both the platform and compete on it, which creates an irreversible conflict of interest that systematically disadvantages third-party developers. This structural conflict manifests in concrete, verifiable ways: Apple’s App Store pre-installs its own apps on every iPhone, gives them preferential placement in search results, and mandates that competitors use Apple’s in-app payment system with commissions up to 30%, while Apple’s own services face none of these constraints.
[P1.2] Meanwhile, Google bundles its suite of apps with Android, makes them non-removable defaults, and uses its control over the Play Store to promote its own services above competitors. These aren’t isolated incidents but consistent patterns that create an uneven playing field. The economic harm is substantial and multi-faceted: rivals must spend exorbitantly on marketing just to offset the platform’s built-in advantages, innovation stalls as developers divert resources from product improvements to navigating opaque restrictions, and consumers pay higher prices while facing fewer meaningful choices.
[P1.3] The scale of this distortion affects entire industries: app developers, publishers, and service providers all operate under the shadow of arbitrary platform rules. Behavioral remedies have repeatedly failed because app-store operators can always devise new, subtle ways to advantage their own offerings that are difficult to detect and even harder to litigate. Financial penalties are meaningless deterrents for trillion-dollar companies. Historical examples across industries demonstrate that structural separation is the only reliable solution to inherent conflicts: in telecommunications, we separated network operators from equipment manufacturers; in finance, we split commercial and investment banking with the Glass-Steagall Act.
[P1.4] The same principle must apply here. Without structural separation, the current system endures—a market where the gatekeeper writes the rules, owns the infrastructure, and fields its own team, ensuring that the notion of fair competition remains a carefully maintained fiction that serves the gatekeeper’s interests above all.
