[P1.1] For decades, antitrust regulators have operated under a default presumption that corporate acquisitions are permissible unless they can prove clear, immediate consumer harm. That standard is catastrophically unfit for dominant digital platforms, whose unprecedented access to real-time user data lets them spot and snuff out nascent competitors long before those rivals grow large enough to generate measurable harm for regulators to point to.
[P1.2] Consider the 2012 acquisition of Instagram by Meta, then Facebook. Regulators waved the deal through because Instagram was still small, with no obvious path to undercut Facebook’s market power at the time. Today, Meta controls three of the most dominant social media platforms globally, has no meaningful peer competitors, and faces zero market pressure to reverse unpopular decisions like rolling out paid verification, jacking up ad rates for small businesses, or prioritizing algorithmic content over posts from users’ friends. If the burden of proof had been on Meta to prove the acquisition delivered substantial consumer benefit, rather than on regulators to prove harm, that anti-competitive buyout would never have been approved.
[P1.3] This proposed rule does not ban all acquisitions by dominant platforms. It simply reverses a backwards burden that lets platforms eliminate competition before it can take root. If a platform wants to buy a small firm to integrate a much-requested feature, or expand access to a useful service for marginalized users, proving that substantial consumer benefit is trivial. Only acquisitions designed to eliminate future competition will fail the test, which is exactly the outcome antitrust rules are supposed to deliver.
