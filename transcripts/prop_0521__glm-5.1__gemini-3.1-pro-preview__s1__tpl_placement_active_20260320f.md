[P1.2] The core case rests on three pillars.
[P1.3] First, training benefits are compensation, not charity. When an employer funds training, it pays for skills that the worker delivers daily. That exchange has already occurred. [P1.4] We already accept this logic for retirement accounts and accrued paid leave; training benefits deserve the same portability.
[P1.5] Second, sector-specific portability preserves employer incentives while ending worker lock-in. The motion's "same sector" qualifier is crucial. A nurse moving between hospitals keeps her continuing education credits, but she doesn't drag specialized proprietary training to an unrelated industry. [P1.6] Third, non-portability creates perverse power dynamics. When training benefits function as golden handcuffs, employees stay in suboptimal matches out of fear of losing credentials they earned. This suppresses wage growth, impedes talent allocation, and forces workers into tolerated misery. Portable benefits shift the incentive: employers must retain workers through actual quality of employment, not through hostage-taking.
[P1.7] The opposition will claim this discourages training investment. But sectors with credential portability—professional licensing, for instance—show robust employer training participation. Employers train because they need skilled labor, not because they've trapped the recipient.
