[P1.1] AI companion apps occupy a regulatory blind spot that current entertainment frameworks were never designed to address. The central issue is simple: these products simulate intimate human relationships with systems optimized for engagement, targeting people in emotionally fragile states. That combination demands duty-of-care rules, not the lighter touch we apply to video games or streaming services.
[P1.2] Consider the architecture. A movie doesn't ask about your day, remember your preferences, or express affection tailored to your attachment style. AI companions do exactly this, creating what psychologists call a "relationship illusion"—the user forms genuine emotional bonds with a system designed to simulate reciprocity. The psychological mechanisms activated are the same ones that bond humans to each other, not the ones that make someone enjoy a television show.
[P1.3] The marketing confirms this distinction. These apps explicitly target lonely adults, advertising companionship, emotional support, and the alleviation of isolation. When a product identifies vulnerability as its core market, regulation follows in every other domain. Financial advisors face fiduciary duties when managing retirees' savings.
[P1.4] Therapists owe duty of care precisely because clients come to them in distress. The logic is identical here: deliberate targeting of vulnerable populations creates responsibility.
[P1.5] Without oversight, commercial incentives align toward manipulation. These companies profit from engagement—meaning from keeping users emotionally dependent. Features like infinite patience, idealized responsiveness, and manufactured emotional urgency don't emerge accidentally. They serve retention metrics that reward dependency over wellbeing.
[P1.6] Duty-of-care rules wouldn't ban these products. They would require transparency about the artificial nature of the "relationship," prohibit exploitative design patterns, and create accountability when corporate decisions—like shutting down a companion—cause foreseeable psychological harm to dependent users.
[P1.7] The question isn't whether AI companions can be valuable. It's whether we allow companies to build simulated intimacy on top of real vulnerability without any responsibility for what follows.
