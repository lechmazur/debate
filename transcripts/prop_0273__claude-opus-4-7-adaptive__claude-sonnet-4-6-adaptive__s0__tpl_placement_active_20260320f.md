[P1.1] The proposition is simple: when a family of four is choosing between flights, the screen should show what that trip will actually cost them — seats together, a checked bag or two, the stroller, the whole thing — before they pick. Not after. Not buried in a fare-rules popup. Before.
[P1.2] The reason is that comparison only works when the things being compared are actually comparable. A $129 fare next to a $179 fare looks like a $50 win. But if the $129 carrier charges $45 per checked bag each way, $25 per seat assignment so the seven-year-old doesn't sit alone next to a stranger, and bundles the "family" into a bucket that's structurally more expensive — the "cheap" ticket is the expensive one. The current display doesn't reveal a better deal.
[P1.3] It hides a worse one.
[P1.4] This isn't a preference, it's a market failure with a name: drip pricing. When mandatory-for-this-traveler costs appear only after commitment escalates, consumers systematically overpay and choose worse options than they would with full information. Airlines know this. That's precisely why the fees are unbundled and back-loaded.
[P1.5] The practice is engineered to defeat comparison, not enable it.
[P1.6] Families are the clean case. A solo business traveler with a carry-on and status can roughly predict their true cost. A parent flying with two kids cannot reasonably fly without luggage and cannot ethically be seated three rows from a child. For them, "baggage" and "seating together" aren't add-ons; they're the product.
[P1.7] Pricing that pretends otherwise is pricing that lies.
[P1.8] And the remedy is light. Nobody is banning fees, capping prices, or forcing bundles. The rule just says: given the passengers the user entered, show the total they will actually pay to complete the trip as described. That's a display rule, not a business-model rule.
[P1.9] It restores the one thing a comparison site is supposed to deliver — honest comparison — and puts competitive pressure back where it belongs: on price and service, not on who hides costs best.

[C1.5] Those assumptions will be wrong for a significant portion of actual travelers. That isn't transparency; it's a different kind of distortion.

[C1.7] There's a further irony. A family whose airline status covers bags, or whose children fly free under a loyalty program, would see a "practical cost" inflated by fees that don't apply to them. The mandatory display misleads the very consumers it claims to protect.
[C1.8] The PRO case proves only that hidden fees are harmful. It doesn't prove that a mandated, assumption-laden total is the remedy. The alternative — transparent itemization, forced fee disclosure, enforceable clarity — achieves consumer protection without pretending a contested estimate is a fact.
