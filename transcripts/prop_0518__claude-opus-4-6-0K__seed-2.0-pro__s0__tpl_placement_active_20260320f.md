[P1.1] Noncompete clauses function as a tax on worker mobility, and like most taxes, they should be presumed harmful unless the government has a compelling reason to permit them. The proposition asks whether the default rule should be broad restriction with narrow exceptions, or broad employer exceptions with narrow restrictions. The answer is clear: restrict first, carve out only where genuinely necessary.
[P1.2] Start with the labor market distortion. When workers cannot move to competitors, they lose bargaining power even if they never try to leave. Employers who know their employees are locked in have less incentive to raise wages, improve conditions, or invest in retention through merit. Empirical patterns from states that have limited noncompetes—California being the most prominent—suggest these jurisdictions see higher wage growth, more startup formation, and greater innovation diffusion.
[P1.3] Silicon Valley did not thrive because its employers hoarded talent through legal threats; it thrived because talent circulated freely, carrying knowledge and ambition across firms.
[P1.4] Second, the breadth problem. Noncompetes are not just imposed on senior executives with genuine trade secrets. They are routinely applied to sandwich makers, warehouse workers, hair stylists, and camp counselors—people who possess no proprietary information worth protecting. Broad employer exceptions entrench this pattern because employers draft noncompetes as boilerplate, and low-wage workers lack the legal sophistication or resources to challenge them.
[P1.5] A default restriction forces employers to justify the clause rather than forcing workers to fight it.
[P1.6] Third, existing legal tools already protect legitimate employer interests without noncompetes. Trade secret statutes, nondisclosure agreements, and nonsolicitation clauses all target the specific harms employers cite—misappropriation of proprietary data, poaching of client relationships—without the collateral damage of freezing a worker out of their entire occupation for a year or more.
[P1.7] The CON side must show that broad employer exceptions produce better outcomes than targeted alternatives. They cannot, because the mechanism of a noncompete is inherently overbroad: it restricts the worker, not the secret. Governments should restrict these clauses for nearly all workers and reserve exceptions only for genuinely extraordinary circumstances.
