[P1.1] Children under fourteen lack the neurological architecture to form criminal intent in any meaningful sense. Neuroscience demonstrates that the prefrontal cortex—responsible for impulse control, risk assessment, and understanding long-term consequences—remains structurally immature throughout early adolescence. Treating a ten-year-old as a fully punishable offender therefore mistakes childhood impulsivity for moral depravity, collapsing the vital distinction between a tragic mistake and a culpable crime. When legal systems pretend that a primary school child possesses the same cognitive machinery as an adult, they substitute vengeance for justice.
[P1.2] The proposition targets jurisdictions that permit criminal prosecution of children as young as seven or ten, treating them as miniature adults before they can legally consent to medical treatment or sign contracts. These ages reflect historical anachronisms, not contemporary understanding of human development. When states incarcerate pre-pubescent children alongside hardened offenders or subject them to criminal records that permanently foreclose educational and employment opportunities, they sacrifice rehabilitation for retribution without achieving deterrence. A child who cannot comprehend the permanence of death cannot fully grasp the permanence of imprisonment, rendering punitive sanctions cognitively meaningless.
[P1.3] Raising the floor to fourteen aligns criminal responsibility with the onset of reasoned moral agency while preserving welfare-based interventions for younger offenders. Child protection systems—focused on family support, education, and therapeutic intervention—address the root causes of delinquency more effectively than courts and prisons. International consensus has increasingly coalesced around fourteen as the minimum threshold for criminal responsibility, recognizing that younger children require guidance and structure rather than punishment. This standard reflects biological reality rather than leniency.
[P1.4] The alternative—maintaining very low ages—forces legal systems to perform theatrical cruelty: staging trials children cannot comprehend, issuing sentences they cannot contextualize, and manufacturing criminal identities during formative years. Fourteen marks the boundary where cognitive capacity intersects with sufficient educational maturity, allowing sanctions to function as meaningful behavioral corrections rather than destructive labels that perpetuate cycles of marginalization. Countries clinging to younger thresholds should join the evolving global standard, prioritizing child welfare and proven rehabilitation over archaic retribution.

[C1.1] The motion rests on a false premise that is never examined: that a low age of criminal responsibility means treating children as "fully punishable offenders." In most jurisdictions with ages of seven or ten, juvenile justice systems impose radically different consequences from adult courts—supervision orders, therapeutic interventions, family conferencing. The proposition attacks a caricature. Real children in real systems are not miniature adults before the law; they are placed in specialized frameworks precisely because legislators recognize their developmental difference.
[C1.2] Raising the threshold to fourteen does not improve those frameworks; it removes children from any accountability structure entirely.
[C1.3] That removal matters enormously in serious cases. Imagine an eleven-year-old who deliberately and repeatedly abuses a younger sibling over months. A welfare referral may help, but it carries no teeth, imposes no structured consequence, and sends no clear signal about the gravity of what occurred. The child's autonomy to continue harming is essentially unchecked until age fourteen.
[C1.4] Welfare systems are under-resourced, voluntary at the margins, and can be evaded by resistant families. A formal legal process—even a gentle juvenile one—creates compulsory engagement, mandatory review, and meaningful oversight. Abolishing that mechanism wholesale leaves victims without vindication and vulnerable children without intervention they cannot refuse.
[C1.5] The neuroscience argument proves far too much. If prefrontal immaturity negates criminal capacity at ten, it substantially compromises it at fourteen and fifteen as well. Yet PRO endorses responsibility at fourteen. The science cannot simultaneously be decisive below fourteen and irrelevant above it.
[C1.6] What actually varies is degree of understanding, not its binary presence or absence. Most children above seven grasp that hurting someone is wrong; that is the minimum cognition criminal law has always required, a standard preserved by doctrines like doli incapax that already screen out children who genuinely lack that understanding.
[C1.7] The real question is never fourteen versus seven. It is whether legal accountability—calibrated, child-centered, and rehabilitative—serves children better than wholesale exclusion. The answer is yes, and the motion's blunt mandate forfeits that nuance entirely.
