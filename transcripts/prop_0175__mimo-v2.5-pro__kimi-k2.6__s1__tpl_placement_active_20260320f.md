Without sanitation standards, blood-borne pathogens travel through shared combs and unwashed hands. The consumer cannot inspect a braider’s sterilization practices during a brief consultation, and the resulting infections—staph, fungal damage, traction alopecia—impose costs on public health systems, not just the individual.
[C1.3] Suggesting that braiders need no oversight because no harm is “documented” is like claiming restaurant inspections are useless because food poisoning is underreported.
[C1.4] Interior design operates at the same boundary between aesthetics and safety. Designers specify floor coverings, wall finishes, and furniture layouts in commercial spaces subject to occupancy limits, fire codes, and ADA accessibility requirements. A corridor narrowed by poor planning blocks egress; a specified textile with undisclosed flammability accelerates a fire; a non-compliant countertop height in a public restroom invites injury and liability. The designer’s mistake is not merely “bad taste” visited on the client—it is borne by employees, customers, and first responders.
[C1.5] The proposition’s narrative of regulatory capture ignores the structural problem these licenses solve: radical information asymmetry. Clients cannot independently verify that a braider disinfects tools or that a designer understands egress law. Licensing is a coarse but necessary filter. [C1.6] That incumbents sometimes support the rules does not erase the risks the rules address; it means experienced professionals have seen what untrained competitors cost the public.
[C1.7] We should not mistake invisible risk for nonexistent risk. These requirements are genuine safeguards because the dangers are real, even when they are not dramatic.
