[P1.1] Banning compensated surrogacy does not stop it; it merely drives the practice into legal shadows where surrogates are least protected. Where commercial surrogacy is criminalized, prospective parents travel abroad or enter unenforceable private arrangements, leaving carriers without guaranteed medical coverage, independent legal counsel, or fair compensation. The black market is where exploitation actually flourishes: unscreened intermediaries, coercive contracts, and zero regulatory oversight. Women in poverty bear the worst of these harms.
[P1.2] Prohibition is not a shield; it is a surrender to the very abuses its supporters claim to oppose.
[P1.3] Legalization with robust guardrails changes the incentives and the power balance entirely. A regulated framework mandates psychological screening, separate legal representation for the surrogate, enforceable compensation schedules, and the absolute right to withdraw consent at any stage without financial penalty. It caps reasonable payments to prevent bidding wars, requires contracts in the surrogate’s native language, and subjects agencies to strict licensing and regular inspection. Regulation does not invite abuse; it is the only mechanism capable of preventing it systematically and consistently.

Capable adults should be free to make informed reproductive choices, including carrying a child for compensation under conditions of dignity and legal security. For infertile couples and same-sex parents, surrogacy is often the only path to genetic parenthood. Outlawing the practice bars them from that possibility while paternalistically denying women the agency to use their reproductive capacity on freely negotiated, transparent terms.
[P1.5] The central question in this debate is whether exploitation is better addressed by driving surrogacy underground or by bringing it into the light with enforceable rights, medical safeguards, and strong protections. The answer is clear.

[C1.2] Regulation has failed wherever implemented. The "right to withdraw" meant nothing to a woman whose income, housing, and medical care all depended on staying in the clinic's good graces. Thailand and Mexico show similar patterns: protections exist on paper while power imbalances render them meaningless in practice.
[C1.3] The deeper problem is commodification itself. Compensated surrogacy treats children as commissioned products and pregnancy as a rental service. [C1.4] Legalization also expands the market, drawing more women into exploitation's orbit. Commercial surrogacy is a profit-driven industry that requires a steady supply of gestational carriers. Regulatory approval signals legitimacy, attracts investment capital, and normalizes the practice—inevitably targeting those with the fewest alternatives.
[C1.5] PRO frames this as bodily autonomy. But autonomy requires genuine freedom, not the illusion of choice shaped by desperation and commercial marketing. We prohibit organ sales precisely because financial pressure undermines consent. The same logic applies here.
[C1.6] The exploitation is not a bug that regulation can fix—it is inherent to the transaction itself.
