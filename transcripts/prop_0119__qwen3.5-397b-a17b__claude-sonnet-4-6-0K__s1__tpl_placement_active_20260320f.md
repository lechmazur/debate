[C1.1] The proposition sounds reasonable until you examine what it actually asks for: a legal or normative presumption that teenagers deserve expanding privacy and movement *within families*, as a matter of right, regardless of individual circumstance. That framing is where the case collapses.
[C1.2] Parental oversight isn't an affront to personhood. It's the mechanism through which families absorb risk on behalf of people who are, by measurable neurological and experiential reality, still developing the judgment that makes genuine autonomy safe. The prefrontal cortex—governing impulse control, risk assessment, and long-term planning—is not fully developed until the mid-twenties. That's not a cultural prejudice; it's a structural fact about the human brain.
[C1.3] Rights frameworks that ignore this aren't progressive; they're naive.
[C1.4] The proposition's core argument is that monitoring prevents skill-building. But consider the alternative trajectory. A fifteen-year-old who is genuinely free from all parental oversight in communication and movement doesn't become more competent—they become more exposed. Predators, peer pressure networks, and black-market drug supply chains don't wait politely for teenagers to develop good judgment before presenting opportunities.
[C1.5] The notion that privacy itself builds wisdom assumes a benign environment that does not universally exist.
[C1.6] The proposition also treats "monitoring" as a monolith of control and contempt. But most parental oversight isn't surveillance for its own sake—it's love operationalized. A parent tracking a new driver's location isn't treating them as a prisoner; they're managing a statistically dangerous period. First-year drivers have substantially elevated crash rates.
[C1.7] Oversight during that window isn't infantilizing; it's statistically justified.
[C1.8] The real question isn't whether teens should grow toward independence—everyone agrees on that. The question is whether families should be *required*, by social or legal norm, to cede monitoring as a *right* rather than earn trust incrementally and contextually. The answer is no: context, family knowledge, and individual maturity must govern that process, not an abstract entitlement.
