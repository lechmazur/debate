[P1.1] Representative democracy is struggling with legitimacy crises, partisan gridlock, and elite capture. Citizens’ assemblies chosen by sortition—randomly selecting a representative cross-section—offer a powerful corrective by enabling informed, inclusive deliberation. But advisory roles leave them toothless; politicians routinely ignore inconvenient recommendations. To genuinely renew democracy, these assemblies must have binding decision-making power on specific policy questions.
[P1.2] Binding power ensures the people’s voice directly shapes outcomes. Sortition guarantees descriptive representation: ordinary people from all walks of life, not just the politically connected or financially powerful. After thorough learning and debate, their collective judgment reflects the common good more authentically than partisan institutions. When such a body reaches a verdict, it deserves implementation, not sidelining.
[P1.3] Moreover, binding authority breaks political deadlock. On existential issues like climate change or electoral reform, elected officials often duck hard choices due to short-term electoral fears. A citizens’ assembly, insulated from reelection pressures, can make principled, long-term decisions. Making those decisions binding guarantees action rather than endless delay and dilution.
[P1.4] Advisory models are demonstrably weak. The French Citizens’ Convention on Climate proposed ambitious measures, but the government weakened or ignored key proposals. Similarly, many advisory assemblies see their work shelved. Binding power prevents such evasion, ensuring deliberative conclusions become law.
[P1.5] This is not about replacing parliaments but complementing them on specific, thorny issues where representative politics has failed. With clear mandates, legal safeguards, and transparent processes, binding citizens’ assemblies can restore trust and deliver robust policy. For democratic renewal, we must move beyond advice to real authority.
