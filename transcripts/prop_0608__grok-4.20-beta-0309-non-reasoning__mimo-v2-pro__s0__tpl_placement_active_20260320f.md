[P1.1] Carbon offsets should be legally capped at a small fraction of any corporation’s “net-zero” claim, with the overwhelming majority of reductions required to occur inside the company’s own value chain. This is the only structure that delivers genuine atmospheric progress instead of accounting theater.
[P1.2] The core problem with generous offset reliance is additionality and permanence failures. Most offset projects—whether forestry, cookstoves, or renewable-energy certificates—struggle to prove they would not have happened anyway or that the carbon stays locked away for centuries. When these projects under-deliver, the corporation still books the credit, the atmosphere sees no net reduction, and society is misled about real decarbonization. In-value-chain cuts, by contrast, directly eliminate emissions from the firm’s own operations, suppliers, and product use.
[P1.3] They are verifiable against the company’s actual carbon ledger, not against hypothetical counterfactuals.
[P1.4] Allowing heavy offset dependence also distorts incentives. Companies facing tough scope-3 choices can simply write a check to a distant forest project instead of redesigning supply chains, re-engineering products, or investing in genuinely cleaner technologies. The result is slower systemic innovation precisely where it is most needed. A strict cap forces capital and managerial attention onto the hardest, highest-leverage abatement opportunities inside the value chain—exactly the behavior climate policy should reward.
[P1.5] Critics will claim flexibility is essential and that some emissions are simply too expensive to abate directly. This concedes the point: if an emission is genuinely unavoidable, the corporation should not be permitted to label the product “net-zero” in the first place. Offsets may fund useful projects, yet they should be treated as additional good deeds, not as a substitute for genuine cuts. Only by legally limiting offsets to a modest share—say 10–20 percent—can regulators ensure that corporate net-zero claims reflect real reductions rather than outsourced accounting.
[P1.6] The choice is between credible decarbonization anchored in value-chain accountability and a paper exercise that lets firms declare victory while the climate clock keeps ticking. The law must choose the former.
