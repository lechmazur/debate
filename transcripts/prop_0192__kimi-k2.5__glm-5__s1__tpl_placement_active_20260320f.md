[P1.1] Legal personhood has never been about biology alone. Corporations hold rights without consciousness; infants possess them before rationality. The decisive factor is the capacity for interests—for suffering, autonomy, and social bond. Great apes, cetaceans, and elephants possess these capacities in degrees that render their current legal status as property not merely unjust, but intellectually incoherent.
[P1.2] The cognitive evidence is overwhelming and uncontroversial. Elephants grieve their dead, return to bones years later, and suffer neurological damage from solitary confinement. Great apes recognize themselves in mirrors, use tools with cultural transmission across generations, and experience clinical depression in captivity. Cetaceans display signature whistles functioning as names, engage in cooperative problem-solving with distinct pod dialects, and demonstrate long-term social memory.
[P1.3] These are not mechanical behaviors but markers of selfhood complex enough to generate interests that the law cannot ignore.
[P1.4] Currently, these interests receive zero legal consideration. A chimpanzee can be confined in isolation for decades for entertainment; an elephant can be broken through domination for tourism revenue; an orca can be separated from pod and offspring indefinitely for aquarium profits. Property status makes this not just possible but profitable. Basic rights against captivity would erect a legal firewall against such exploitation, recognizing that confining a being with autobiographical consciousness constitutes a distinct harm beyond physical pain—an annihilation of the social and mental life that constitutes their very existence.
[P1.5] Extending personhood to these specific taxa is neither speculative nor boundless. It targets animals with demonstrated self-awareness, long-term memory, and emotional architecture comparable to human children. The law already draws arbitrary lines; this motion simply corrects one by aligning legal status with moral reality. To deny them protection is to assert that species membership alone justifies suffering—a claim that collapses under its own weight when we confront beings who know they are imprisoned.

[C1.1] The proposition's central error is conceptual: it confuses moral consideration with legal personhood. These are not interchangeable categories. Personhood denotes membership in a community of reciprocal rights and duties—a framework that presupposes moral agency, the capacity to bear responsibilities, and the ability to participate in legal relations. Great apes, cetaceans, and elephants cannot hold duties, cannot be held legally accountable, and cannot participate in the social contract that makes rights meaningful.
[C1.2] The comparison to corporations fails—corporations are legal instruments through which humans act; they exist to facilitate human enterprise and can be dissolved, fined, and regulated because they remain accountable to human-controlled governance. The comparison to infants fails differently—infants are rights-bearers precisely because they are developing into full moral agents, and their rights are inseparable from human family structures and intergenerational obligation.
[C1.3] The proposition asks us to extend a legal category designed for beings capable of moral reasoning to beings who, however intelligent, remain outside that framework. This is not speciesism—it is recognition that the legal system presupposes agents who can understand rules, modify behavior accordingly, and answer for violations. An elephant that kills a human cannot be tried for murder. A dolphin that injures another cannot be sued.
[C1.4] These are not bugs in the system but reflections of a fundamental difference in kind.
[C1.5] We can oppose cruelty without granting personhood. Animal welfare legislation already prohibits wanton suffering; where it fails, the remedy is stricter enforcement and expanded protections—not a conceptual revolution that severs rights from responsibilities. The motion's approach creates incoherence: it grants protections that presuppose moral standing to beings who cannot hold standing, while providing no principled stopping point. If self-awareness and suffering ground personhood, what of dogs, pigs, or corvids?
[C1.6] The line-drawing problem reveals that the proposed criterion cannot bear the weight assigned to it. Personhood is the wrong tool for protecting animals; it degrades a legal concept that structures human society while offering animals protections that welfare reform could provide more effectively.
