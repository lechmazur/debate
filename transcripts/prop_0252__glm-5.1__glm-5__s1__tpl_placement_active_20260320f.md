[P1.1] Unpaid internships outside genuinely educational contexts are a loophole that transforms exploitation into résumé lines. Governments should heavily restrict them because they distort labor markets, deepen inequality, and undermine the very educational mission they claim to serve.
[P1.2] The core problem is economic: when a worker produces value for an employer, that labor has a price. Calling the arrangement an "internship" doesn't magically dissolve the moral and practical obligation to pay for work performed. A marketing intern drafting social media posts, a policy intern researching legislation, or a design intern creating client deliverables are all doing real work. The employer benefits concretely—often saving thousands in wages—while the intern receives "experience" that functions as a euphemism for uncompensated labor.
[P1.3] This system rations opportunity by wealth. Students from affluent families can afford to spend a summer without income; students who must support themselves or contribute to household expenses cannot. The result is a class filter posing as meritocracy. Industries that rely heavily on unpaid internships—media, politics, the arts, policy—become ghettos of privilege, locking out talented people who lack independent financial support.
[P1.4] The proposition explicitly preserves genuinely educational placements: clinical rotations in nursing and medicine, student teaching, supervised practicums where the primary purpose is the intern's learning rather than the employer's production. These placements have structured mentorship, clear curricula, and institutional oversight. The distinction matters. When an intern is primarily learning, the arrangement resembles education.
[P1.5] When an intern is primarily producing, the arrangement resembles wage theft with better branding.
[P1.6] Heavy restriction pushes employers toward a simple, fair choice: if the work is worth having done, it's worth paying for. If it isn't, the role shouldn't exist. This clarity eliminates the gray zone where exploitation hides behind credentialism and forces industries to invest in the talent pipeline they claim to value.
[P1.7] The status quo subsidizes employers at the expense of workers and equality. Restricting unpaid internships corrects that imbalance.
