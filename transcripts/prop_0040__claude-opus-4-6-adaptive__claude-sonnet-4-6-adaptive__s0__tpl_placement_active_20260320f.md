[P1.1] When you buy a carton of milk, the expiration date is printed on the package. When you buy a car battery, the warranty period is on the label. Yet when you spend hundreds of dollars on a smart thermostat, a security camera, or a phone, you have no idea whether the manufacturer will support it for five years or fifteen months. That information gap is the core market failure this proposal fixes.
[P1.2] Smart devices are uniquely dependent on ongoing software support. Unlike a hammer or a toaster, a smart device that loses security updates becomes a liability—vulnerable to exploits, incompatible with evolving standards, and potentially bricked entirely when a cloud service shuts down. The functional lifespan of the product is inseparable from the manufacturer's commitment to maintain it. Consumers deserve to know that commitment before they pay.
[P1.3] Right now, manufacturers benefit from deliberate ambiguity. They can advertise features that depend on future support while making no binding promise to provide it. This creates a lemon problem: companies that invest in long-term support cannot effectively differentiate themselves from those planning to abandon a product line in two years. A mandatory on-package label corrects this by making support duration visible, comparable, and enforceable at point of sale—exactly where purchase decisions happen.
[P1.4] The environmental stakes sharpen the case. Tens of millions of functionally intact devices enter the waste stream every year not because their hardware failed but because their software support ended. A visible support guarantee creates competitive pressure to extend lifespans, directly reducing electronic waste. It also lets consumers factor total cost of ownership into their decisions rather than being surprised by premature obsolescence.
[P1.5] This is not a novel regulatory mechanism. Energy efficiency labels, nutrition facts, and warranty disclosures all rest on the same principle: when a product characteristic materially affects value and consumers cannot observe it unaided, disclosure is justified. Software support duration is precisely that kind of hidden, material characteristic.
[P1.6] The question is simple: should consumers know what they're buying? The answer should be yes.

[C1.1] The proposition sounds intuitive until you examine what it actually demands: a *guaranteed* support lifespan, printed on *physical packaging*, before purchase. Each of those three elements is a serious design flaw, and together they make this mandate more likely to harm consumers than help them.
[C1.2] Start with the word "guaranteed." Guarantees create legal liability. Any manufacturer printing a support commitment on a box will do exactly what liability-conscious companies always do—minimize exposure. They will not promise five years if they can promise two.
[C1.3] They will not promise two years if they can promise eighteen months. The result is a floor that quickly becomes a ceiling, because no rational legal team approves exceeding what the label specifies. Mandatory disclosure of this kind does not reveal honest ambitions; it produces the lowest defensible number. That number is what the market then accepts as the standard.
[C1.4] Now consider the physical packaging requirement. A smart device can sit on a retailer's shelf for a year before you buy it. The moment the device ships from the factory, that printed date begins aging. If the label says "supported until 2027" and you're buying in 2026, the guarantee is already nearly worthless—but the packaging gives you no way to know that.
[C1.5] Digital disclosure, a QR code linked to a live support page, or a regulatory database would provide accurate, current information. Physical packaging cannot. PRO is solving an information problem with a mechanism that freezes information in amber.
[C1.6] Finally, the practical burden falls disproportionately on smaller manufacturers, startups, and open-source hardware companies. A startup cannot confidently commit to a legally binding five-year support window at launch. The effect is to entrench incumbents who can absorb liability, while blocking entrants who might otherwise compete on innovation and longevity.
[C1.7] The core issue—consumers lacking support information—is real. But a mandate requiring static, liability-minimizing guarantees on cardboard boxes is precisely the wrong remedy. It creates new market distortions while solving none of the original problem.
