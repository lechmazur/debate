[P1.1] The case for universal pre-K rests on three planks that, taken together, clear the cost bar by a wide margin.
[P1.2] First, the developmental window is real. Between ages three and five, children build the executive function, vocabulary, and social regulation that schooling later assumes they already have. A child who enters kindergarten unable to sit in a group, follow two-step instructions, or recognize letters spends first grade catching up instead of learning. High-quality pre-K closes a meaningful share of that gap before the race begins.
[P1.3] This is not speculation; it's why kindergarten readiness scores reliably predict third-grade reading, which reliably predicts high school completion.
[P1.4] Second, the long-run evidence points the same direction. The well-known intensive programs — Perry, Abecedarian — showed large effects on graduation, earnings, and reduced criminal justice involvement. Critics will say those were boutique. Fine: look at scaled programs like Boston's public pre-K and Tulsa's, which have shown durable gains in school progress and behavior, and at state-level studies finding higher graduation rates for pre-K attendees.
[P1.5] The "fadeout" of test-score advantages is real on narrow measures, but the life outcomes — attendance, grade retention, special-ed placement, graduation — persist. Those are exactly the outcomes that drive public cost.
[P1.6] Third, the fiscal math works even on conservative assumptions. Pre-K reduces special education placement, grade repetition, and child-care subsidies, and it raises maternal labor force participation immediately. Add modest long-run earnings and crime effects and mainstream cost-benefit estimates land between roughly 3-to-1 and 7-to-1. You do not need the high end to justify the program; you need it to beat alternative uses of the same dollar, and few social investments clear that bar as consistently.
[P1.7] Universality matters specifically because targeted programs miss eligible families, stigmatize enrollment, and lose the peer-effect benefits that mixed classrooms provide. The motion isn't whether pre-K is perfect — it's whether the gains justify the cost. On readiness, life outcomes, and dollars returned, they do.
