[P1.1] Algorithmic pricing that assigns different prices to different customers for the same product exploits personal data to capture more revenue than uniform pricing would allow. Two individuals might access the identical item online, yet one pays a premium because the algorithm detects they are less price-sensitive through patterns in their search history or device type. The good itself remains unchanged, but the cost does not. This creates a transaction where the buyer has no way to know if they received the standard rate or a targeted markup designed solely around their profile.
[P1.2] Such differentiation undermines the fairness embedded in competitive markets, where equal products should command equal prices absent genuine differences in cost or quality. Instead, these systems create secret tiers based on inferred characteristics, leaving buyers unaware that their willingness to pay has been calculated and acted upon. This opacity prevents informed choice and rewards sellers for gathering ever more intrusive data.
[P1.3] Disclosure of the practice would restore some balance by informing consumers that prices are not fixed but personalized, enabling them to seek better deals or limit data sharing. When the differential treatment stems from non-market factors like demographics or past behavior unrelated to the transaction, banning it for effectively identical products becomes the appropriate safeguard against exploitation.
[P1.4] Proponents of unrestricted use argue it enhances efficiency, yet this overlooks how it distorts consumer behavior and reduces overall welfare by discouraging purchases from those facing inflated quotes. Markets function best when participants operate with symmetric information; algorithmic personalization deliberately breaks that symmetry in the seller’s favor. This form of discrimination succeeds only because consumers cannot detect it, turning supposed efficiency into an advantage that undermines market competition itself.
[P1.5] The practice also risks broader societal effects, such as reinforcing inequalities by charging more to certain groups based on proxies for race or location. To prevent these outcomes, disclosure or prohibition should be the default position rather than allowing unchecked deployment.

[C1.1] The motion fails because it treats algorithmic price differences as inherently abusive when, in practice, they are often how firms cut prices, fill unused capacity, and serve buyers who would otherwise get no deal at all. Airlines, hotels, ride-hailing, coupons, loyalty rewards, off-peak rates, and abandoned-cart discounts all reflect this basic logic. A rule that says such pricing should usually be disclosed or banned does not just hit “predatory markups”; it threatens the discounts too.
[C1.2] The fairness standard in PRO’s case is wrong. “Equal product, equal price” is not how healthy markets work. Healthy markets work when consumers can compare alternatives and sellers compete for business. Two people already pay different prices for the same coffee because one used a coupon, one bought at rush hour, one joined a loyalty program, one ordered through a different channel. The product is effectively the same; the transaction is not.
[C1.3] Algorithms mostly make that process faster and more precise. If PRO’s principle were taken seriously, it would condemn a huge share of ordinary competitive discounting.
[C1.4] Disclosure is not the easy compromise PRO suggests. Usually it collapses into useless boilerplate—“prices may vary”—that informs nobody, or it forces firms to reveal pricing strategy in ways that make markets easier to game and sometimes easier to coordinate around. The real question is not whether a price was individualized; it is whether the firm lied, hid fees, or used unlawful discrimination. Those harms should be regulated directly.
[C1.5] A broad default of disclosure or prohibition is a blunt instrument aimed at the wrong target.
[C1.6] And “usually” is doing all the work here. There are plainly bad cases: deception, illegal proxy discrimination, exploitative emergency pricing. The right response is narrow rules against abuse, not a general presumption that algorithmic price differences are suspicious enough to disclose or ban.

[P5.3] Competitive markets function because participants can adjust to one another’s information; here, the adjustment runs only one way.
