When you buy an ebook, you purchase a revocable, non-transferable license to view content on a specific platform. [C1.2] Mandating transferability dismantles the licensing model entirely. If a digital library passes to heirs like a physical house, publishers lose control over distribution. A single purchased copy could circulate indefinitely within a family tree, eliminating future sales. To offset this revenue loss, platforms would raise initial purchase costs dramatically or shift entirely to subscription models where nothing is owned at all.
[C1.3] The consumer loses either way.
[C1.4] Furthermore, enforcement creates impossible security burdens. How does a platform verify death without exposing accounts to fraud? If inheritance is guaranteed, bad actors will exploit death certificates to hijack valuable libraries. [C1.5] Finally, this mandate ignores existing solutions. Most major platforms already offer legacy contacts or account memorialization tools. The problem isn't a lack of legal guarantees; it's a lack of consumer awareness. Law should encourage transparent planning tools, not force physical property law into digital licensing.
[C1.6] We cannot legislate away the fundamental nature of software licensing without breaking the market that supplies it. Preserving the licensing model ensures content remains affordable during life, rather than protecting a fictional estate value after death. The law should respect the contract signed at purchase, not rewrite it for heirs who never agreed to the terms.
