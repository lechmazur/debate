[P1.1] The case for legalizing compensated surrogacy rests on a simple truth: prohibition fails everyone it claims to protect.
[P1.2] When governments ban compensated surrogacy, they do not stop it. They drive it underground. Black markets have no contract enforcement, no health standards, no vetting of intended parents, and no recourse when something goes wrong. The coercion and exploitation opponents fear become more likely, not less, because no regulator is watching.
[P1.3] A surrogate in an illegal arrangement cannot go to authorities when a clinic cuts corners or when intended parents threaten to withhold payment. She has no legal standing, no protection, no voice. Prohibition does not shield vulnerable women—it silences them.
[P1.4] Legalization with strong protections transforms this dynamic. Contracts become enforceable. Medical standards apply. Independent legal counsel can be required.
[P1.5] Cooling-off periods ensure decisions are not rushed. Psychological screening can identify coercion risks. Payment structures can prevent withholding tactics. These protections exist only when the state acknowledges the practice and builds guardrails.
[P1.6] Bodily autonomy demands that women be trusted to make decisions about their own reproductive labor. We allow women to donate eggs, to participate in clinical trials for pay, to work physically demanding jobs during pregnancy. Surrogacy involves significant effort and risk; compensation acknowledges that reality rather than exploiting it. The question is not whether money changes hands, but whether the woman has genuine alternatives and genuine choice.
[P1.7] Finally, intended parents deserve a legal path to family formation. Infertility affects millions. Same-sex couples cannot conceive without assistance. A regulated system offers them hope without requiring them to exploit desperate women in unregulated arrangements abroad—precisely what prohibition encourages.
[P1.8] Prohibition produces shadow markets and silent victims. Legalization produces transparency, accountability, and genuine protection. The choice is clear.

Enforcement means either compelling a woman to surrender the child she has borne or binding her to invasive medical decisions she may no longer wish to make.
[C1.5] Strong protections cannot resolve this; they merely launder the coercion through legal language.
[C1.6] Prohibition is not the enemy of vulnerable women; the market is. Banning compensated surrogacy does not eliminate every underground arrangement, but it suppresses demand and denies the practice state legitimacy. Legalization would expand the industry, normalize the commodification of reproductive capacity, and call it protection. That is not reform.
[C1.7] That is institutionalized exploitation with better paperwork.
