[P1.1] Shifting to presumed consent delivers consistent, measurable increases in transplant rates by correcting for the status quo bias that stops willing donors from registering. Across dozens of regions that have made the switch, donation rates rise by 30 to 100 percent within five years, enough to cut waitlist deaths for kidney, heart, and liver transplants by a quarter or more in most jurisdictions. The mechanism is simple: roughly 7 in 10 adults report wanting to donate their organs, but fewer than 3 in 10 take the active step to register under opt-in systems.
[P1.2] Flipping the default captures that untapped willingness without forcing anyone to participate.
[P1.3] There is no increase in ethical violations, because opt-out processes are low-effort, universally accessible, and designed to capture active objection easily. No general-use presumed consent policy strips people of the right to exclude themselves from donation, and all include explicit carveouts for religious or personal objection. No one is counted as a donor unless they have failed to take a simple, widely advertised step to opt out, so there is no coercion of people who object to donation.
[P1.4] Far from increasing family distress, presumed consent reduces the burden on grieving families. Under opt-in, families facing sudden, traumatic loss are often asked to make an unguided choice about donation with no clear signal of their loved one’s wishes, a major source of guilt and conflict. Under presumed consent, families only face that question if their loved one actively opted out, or if they have overwhelming personal objections that trigger standard, universal family veto provisions. No family is ever forced to proceed with donation against their explicit wishes, and the default removes the pressure of guessing what their loved one would have wanted for the large majority of people who support donation.
