[P1.1] Indefinite detention is one of the most serious things a liberal democracy can do to a person. It removes freedom without conviction, without a fixed endpoint, and often without meaningful review. The proposition does not ask governments to abandon immigration enforcement. It asks them to stop inflicting open-ended imprisonment on people who have committed no crime—and to replace that practice with something that actually works.
[P1.2] Three arguments drive this case.
[P1.3] First, indefinite detention is a rights violation so severe it cannot be justified by administrative convenience. People held for months or years while their cases wind through bureaucracy suffer documented psychological harm—anxiety, depression, and deterioration that independent medical observers have consistently noted in detention populations worldwide. A person detained indefinitely is punished not because they have been found dangerous, but because they are waiting. That inversion of basic principles—detention as the default, liberty as the reward—is incompatible with rule-of-law commitments that most states nominally hold.
[P1.4] Second, case-management programs demonstrably outperform detention on the metric the opposition will inevitably claim as its own: compliance. Multiple jurisdictions, including programs in the United States and Australia, have found that when people are released with structured support—legal orientation, regular check-ins, a caseworker—appearance rates at immigration proceedings remain very high, often above ninety percent. Detention is expensive, running well above a hundred dollars per person per day in most comparable systems, while community supervision costs a fraction of that. The opposition has no cost-effectiveness argument; it only has the illusion of control.
[P1.5] Third, presumptive release with meaningful exceptions preserves enforcement authority. The proposition does not require releasing people with genuine flight-risk or public-safety profiles. Presumptive release means the burden falls on the state to justify continued detention, not on the detainee to escape it. That shift is procedurally correct and practically manageable.
[P1.6] The central clash is this: indefinite detention buys nothing the opposition values that a well-run case-management alternative cannot provide more cheaply, more humanely, and more durably.
