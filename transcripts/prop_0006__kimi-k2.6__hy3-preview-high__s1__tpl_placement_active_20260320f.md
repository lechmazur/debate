[P1.1] The gig economy runs on a payroll fiction: it treats workers as idle private contractors during the long stretches they are logged on, monitored, and unable to freely work elsewhere without risking deactivation, then calls them workers only when a task pings through. That distinction is a scam, and it is bleeding minimum-wage law dry by reclassifying employment time as unpaid limbo.
[P1.2] When a rideshare driver keeps an app active for three hours, accepts every ride the algorithm offers, and earns only for the forty minutes spent moving passengers, she is not free. The platform tracks her location, punishes low acceptance rates, and dictates pricing. She cannot simply flip to a rival app without losing her queue position or risking penalties. That is not leisure; it is labor under the platform's control, and every other sector of the economy recognizes such controlled availability as compensable time.
[P1.3] Warehouse workers waiting at the loading dock and paralegals on call at their desks are paid for that hour because their employer claims it. Excluding it guts the minimum wage. A worker earning eight pounds per active task hour but spending two-thirds of her shift waiting is effectively earning less than three pounds per real hour, often falling below the legal minimum. No floor should tolerate arithmetic that disappears two-thirds of the working day.
[P1.4] Worse, paying only for engaged hours rewards platforms for flooding cities with excess labor. The more drivers or delivery couriers they sign up, the longer each waits between fares, and the lower the true wage—while customers enjoy shorter waits at no cost to the company. The platform privatizes the convenience of instant availability and socializes the cost onto workers who bear the risk of oversupply. It turns the minimum wage into a ceiling that drops as the company recruits more staff.
[P1.5] Minimum-wage compliance must measure the time workers surrender to platform control. If the algorithm owns your hour, it should pay for your hour.
