[C1.1] Retribution is not revenge. It is the principle that punishment must be deserved and proportional to the wrong committed. Without it, criminal punishment loses its moral foundation entirely—and rehabilitation, stripped of that foundation, becomes something deeply troubling.
[C1.2] Consider what happens when rehabilitation displaces retribution as the primary aim. A person convicted of a serious assault could, in principle, be released quickly if a treatment program certifies them "rehabilitated." Conversely, someone convicted of a minor theft who proves resistant to programming could be held indefinitely because they haven't yet been "fixed." This is not a hypothetical nightmare—indeterminate sentencing regimes built on rehabilitative logic produced exactly these outcomes in the mid-twentieth century, and the backlash was swift and justified.
[C1.3] Retribution constrains punishment: you cannot punish beyond what the offense deserves. Remove that constraint, and you hand the state a blank check dressed in therapeutic language.
[C1.4] Now take the hardest cases. A wealthy executive embezzles millions, causing widespread suffering, but poses essentially zero recidivism risk—perhaps they've already aged out of opportunity, or the offense was situational. Pure rehabilitation logic says there is little to rehabilitate, so minimal punishment is warranted. Every moral intuition revolts.
[C1.5] The community's sense that serious wrongdoing demands a serious response is not primitive emotion; it is the bedrock of a legal order people can trust and respect. When punishment visibly fails to match the gravity of offenses, public confidence in the justice system erodes, and vigilante impulses fill the gap.
[C1.6] Retribution also uniquely honors victims. It says: what happened to you mattered, and the person who did it will answer for it in proportion. Rehabilitation centers the offender's future. Victims become incidental—relevant only insofar as their suffering explains a treatment need.
[C1.7] Finally, the empirical case for rehabilitation is far weaker than PRO suggests. Effective programming exists for some offenders, under some conditions—but scaling it reliably has proven enormously difficult. Retribution, by contrast, requires no speculative program success to deliver its core promise: justice proportional to wrongdoing, applied consistently under law.
