[P1.1] Ransomware is not a technical glitch; it is a business model. Attackers do not breach hospitals, schools, and power grids for sport. They do it because someone, somewhere, will pay. The global ransomware economy survives on a single pipeline: money flowing from victims to criminals.
[P1.2] If that pipeline runs dry, the model dies.
[P1.3] Right now we face a brutal collective-action problem. Any single victim who pays a ransom acts rationally in the moment: a modest wire transfer buys back patient records or restores a factory floor faster than rebuilding from scratch. But every payment trains, funds, and incentivizes the next wave of attacks against another city, another clinic, another school. Voluntary restraint is useless when neighbors keep feeding the beast.
[P1.4] Only a universal prohibition can break this cycle, because it removes the revenue expectation that drives the entire market.
[P1.5] The proposition pairs that prohibition with robust state-funded victim support, and this combination is what makes the policy both effective and humane. Attackers, meanwhile, learn that jurisdictions under this policy are unreliable sources of cash, forcing them to hunt elsewhere.
[P1.6] Critics will argue that bans merely push payments underground. But driving a market underground raises transaction costs, increases risk for criminals, and fragments the trust that lets ransomware gangs scale. It deters the opportunistic operators who currently attack indiscriminately. Even if sophisticated gangs persist, shrinking the total addressable market shrinks their global footprint and raises their cost of doing business.
[P1.7] A government that both starves the supply chain and cushions its own citizens attacks the incentive structure at its root.
[P1.8] We should not fund our own predators. A payment ban, backed by real victim support, is the only way to stop underwriting global cyber extortion and start dismantling it.
