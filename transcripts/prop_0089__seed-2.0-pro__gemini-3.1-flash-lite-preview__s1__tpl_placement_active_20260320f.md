[P1.1] A working character actor takes a 3-day guest spot on a network procedural in 2005, signs the standard take-it-or-leave-it boilerplate contract offered to every non-lead performer, and gets paid roughly $1,200 for their work. Twenty years later, they open a streaming app to find their voice perfectly cloned via AI to dub 12 episodes of a low-budget spinoff of that show, and then see the same voice used in a campaign ad for a political candidate they despise. They have no recourse, because the 20-year-old contract included a broad line about assigning all performance rights in perpetuity.
[P1.2] That is the inevitable outcome of relying on boilerplate for AI dubbing, and it is fundamentally unjust. Standard industry contracts were written for tangible, foreseeable uses of recorded performance: syndication, DVD releases, edited cuts for broadcast. They never contemplated the ability to perfectly replicate a performer’s unique vocal identity for unlimited, unforeseen uses with zero additional input or compensation. The vast majority of working performers have no leverage to rewrite these boilerplate clauses; they either sign the standard form or walk away from the job entirely, so there is no meaningful negotiation happening to begin with.
[P1.3] An opt-in requirement for AI voice dubbing imposes minimal burden on studios: they only need to ask for additional consent when they want to use a performer’s voice for AI cloning for uses outside the scope of the original production. It protects performers’ basic autonomy over their most recognizable professional assets, while still letting studios negotiate access to those rights for new use cases fairly, with compensation tied to the actual value of the new work.
