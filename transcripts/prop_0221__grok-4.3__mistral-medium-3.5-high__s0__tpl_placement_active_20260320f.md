[P1.1] High-dosage tutoring produces larger reading and math gains per dollar because it concentrates repeated, personalized instruction on the students whose deficits drive most achievement shortfalls. Three sessions per week create consistent feedback cycles that address specific skill gaps in phonics or number sense before they compound, allowing measurable progress within months for struggling readers and mathematicians.
[P1.2] Class-size reduction spreads the same money across every student in the room. An extra teacher lowers the pupil count from twenty-five to twenty, but that incremental attention still follows the group pace. Stronger students advance with less need, while the lowest performers receive only marginally more time with the same curriculum and limited diagnostic focus. The marginal dollar therefore buys broad but shallow coverage rather than deep remediation where it counts.
[P1.3] Tutoring scales more efficiently under fixed budgets. Funds can purchase part-time specialists or trained paraprofessionals who deliver targeted sessions to multiple small groups in the same facilities, adjusting intensity to diagnosed needs. Class-size cuts require permanent new full-time teachers, additional classroom space, and benefits for the entire enrollment. That structure locks resources into uniform staffing even after initial gains plateau for most children.
[P1.4] Early-grade pilots that redirected staffing dollars into daily tutoring showed faster closure of reading and math gaps than equivalent per-pupil investments in smaller classes. The difference arises because tutoring multiplies effective instructional minutes for the students who otherwise fall farthest behind, rather than adding small increments to a mixed-ability setting. This targeting mechanism gives tutoring the stronger return on each incremental dollar.
