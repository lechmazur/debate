[C1.1] The proposition sounds unobjectionable until you ask what "basic accessibility standards" actually means in code. WCAG alone contains dozens of success criteria across three conformance levels, and automated scanners routinely disagree with each other and with trained human auditors on the same page. A contrast ratio, an ARIA label, a focus order — reasonable experts reach different verdicts. Building a fine regime on top of that ambiguity doesn't create clarity; it creates a lottery where enforcement depends on who files the complaint and which tool they ran.
[C1.2] That matters because fines don't land evenly. A multinational absorbs them as a cost of doing business and keeps shipping. The entities that actually buckle under fine regimes are municipal websites, school portals, small clinics, community nonprofits, and the long tail of small operators running public-facing services on shoestring budgets. The US experience with web-accessibility litigation is instructive: a cottage industry of serial demand letters emerged, and the rational response from many small operators was to strip features, geoblock, or take services offline.
[C1.3] That is a net loss for disabled users, not a gain.
[C1.4] Third, "meaningful fines" is simply the wrong tool for the stated goal. If we actually want more accessible services, the interventions that change engineering behavior are procurement mandates (government refuses to buy inaccessible software), required remediation plans with binding timelines, public conformance reporting, and safe-harbor windows that reward fast fixes. These redirect money into engineering hours. Fines redirect it to treasuries and plaintiffs' lawyers — not a dollar of which necessarily reaches a disabled user or repairs one broken form.
[C1.5] PRO's restaurant analogy actually cuts against them. Health codes are enforced by trained inspectors against bright-line rules, not by private complaints interpreting a living technical standard. The digital equivalent is inspection and mandated remediation, not punitive fines.
[C1.6] The goal is access. The question is which policy delivers more of it. A fine-first regime predictably delivers less.
