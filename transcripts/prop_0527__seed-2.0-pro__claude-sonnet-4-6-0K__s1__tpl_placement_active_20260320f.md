[P1.1] Antimicrobial resistance kills roughly 1.2 million people annually today, with forecasts of that figure rising tenfold by mid-century if no new classes of antibiotics reach market. For decades, pharmaceutical firms have abandoned antibiotic R&D because traditional revenue models cannot justify the cost: effective new antibiotics are deliberately prescribed sparingly to slow resistance, meaning low sales volume that can never recoup R&D investment at low per-unit prices. Advance market commitments solve this market failure by guaranteeing developers a pre-negotiated, high per-unit price for a fixed volume of doses upfront, as soon as the drug meets strict efficacy and safety thresholds for priority resistant pathogens, eliminating all commercial risk for companies that deliver working products.
[P1.2] Critics will argue high unit prices are too costly, but that framing ignores the far larger cost of doing nothing. A single course of a new antibiotic priced at $1,000 is exponentially cheaper than the average $50,000 cost of treating a patient with an untreatable resistant infection, which often requires weeks of intensive care, multiple failed interventions, and carries a 50% or higher mortality rate. The high price is also temporary: once the pre-agreed AMC purchase volume is fulfilled, governments can negotiate steep price drops, mandate generic licensing for low-income markets, or use bulk purchasing to bring costs down for all subsequent doses.
[P1.3] There is no viable alternative path to get life-saving antibiotics to market fast enough to avoid the worst projected death tolls from resistance, and the temporary high per-unit cost is a trivial tradeoff for millions of lives saved.

[C1.6] Prize funds and publicly funded research pipelines offer additional alternatives that don't require accepting permanently inflated pricing.
