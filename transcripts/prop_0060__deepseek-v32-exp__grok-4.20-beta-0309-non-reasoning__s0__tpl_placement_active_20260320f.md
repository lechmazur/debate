[C1.1] The proposition claims a net productivity increase from working from home for many knowledge workers. This is false. Remove them and measured productivity erodes, even if superficial metrics appear better at first.
[C1.2] Collaboration collapses without physical proximity. Spontaneous problem-solving, quick clarifications, and serendipitous idea exchange happen in hallways and break rooms, not scheduled video calls. A developer stuck on a bug for ninety minutes at home might have resolved it in ten with a colleague glancing at their screen. That lost time compounds across teams, turning individual “focus” into collective stagnation.
[C1.3] Self-reported flexibility masks hidden costs. Workers who claim peak-hour autonomy still answer Slack at night, blur work-life boundaries, and suffer decision fatigue from constant context-switching between domestic and professional demands. Minor illnesses may keep someone online, but they also lower cognitive performance while present. Burnout statistics for remote workers have climbed precisely because the off-switch disappears.
[C1.4] Managerial oversight shrinks. When output is hard to observe directly, many knowledge workers substitute visible activity for real results: longer emails, performative status updates, and padded reports. Studies of remote call-center and software teams consistently show output per hour declining after the honeymoon period once social pressure and mutual monitoring vanish. The “many” in the proposition excludes the junior staff, the creative fields, and the cross-functional roles that actually move organizations forward; they require the office’s invisible scaffolding.
[C1.5] The office ritual is not empty attendance. It is the architecture that converts individual effort into organizational productivity. Remote work trades visible presence for invisible losses that the proposition simply ignores.
