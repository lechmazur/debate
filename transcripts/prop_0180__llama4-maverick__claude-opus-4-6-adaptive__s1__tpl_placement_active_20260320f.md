[C1.1] The proposition rests on a seductive but deeply misleading comparison: that doing less costs less and somehow delivers more. In reality, rewilding abandoned agricultural land faces serious biological, economic, and temporal obstacles that make it far less reliable than the rosy picture suggests.
[C1.2] First, speed matters enormously. We face a narrow window to draw down atmospheric carbon. Natural succession on former cropland is painfully slow—degraded soils, absent seed banks, and missing mycorrhizal networks mean that many abandoned fields stall in early successional scrub for decades before meaningful tree cover establishes. Active reforestation, by contrast, can establish canopy cover and accelerate carbon uptake within years rather than generations.
[C1.3] When we discount future sequestration against the urgency of the climate crisis, that time lag devastates rewilding's per-dollar value.
[C1.4] Second, the claim that rewilding is cheap ignores enormous hidden costs. Abandoned farmland doesn't simply recover on its own. Invasive species like kudzu in the American Southeast or Japanese knotweed across Europe aggressively colonize disturbed land, requiring sustained and expensive management. Rewilding also demands land acquisition or permanent easements, monitoring infrastructure, and often active reintroduction of keystone species or seed sources.
[C1.5] Once you account for these real expenditures, the cost gap with reforestation narrows dramatically.
[C1.6] Third, the biodiversity argument actually cuts against rewilding in many European contexts. Some of Europe's richest biodiversity exists in semi-natural grasslands and meadows maintained by centuries of low-intensity agriculture. Allowing these landscapes to succeed into closed-canopy forest would eliminate habitat for hundreds of specialist plant, insect, and bird species. Rewilding is not automatically a biodiversity gain—it can be a biodiversity trade, swapping one valuable community for another.
[C1.7] Finally, the proposition compares rewilding against a caricature. Modern reforestation programs increasingly plant diverse native species mixes, incorporate natural regeneration buffers, and target degraded land specifically chosen for ecological recovery. Comparing rewilding to outdated monoculture plantations misrepresents the actual alternative.
[C1.8] The proposition needs rewilding to be cheap, fast, and ecologically superior. It is none of these things reliably, and that unreliability is precisely what makes active management worth the investment.
