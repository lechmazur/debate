[P1.1] Dominant digital platforms don't acquire companies at random. They buy firms that threaten their market position, capture emerging technologies before competitors can, and absorb potential disruptors into their ecosystems. The question isn't whether some acquisitions might be benign—it's whether the current presumption of innocence serves competition policy when the acquiring party holds overwhelming informational and market advantages.
[P1.2] The burden of proof should rest with dominant platforms because they alone know their strategic motivations. When Facebook bought Instagram, internal communications later revealed concern about Instagram's competitive threat to Facebook's photo-sharing dominance. That information wasn't publicly available at the time of review. The platform knew; regulators didn't.
[P1.3] This asymmetry repeats across the digital economy. A reversal of presumption corrects for this structural information gap.
[P1.4] Digital markets create unique entrenchment dynamics. Network effects, data accumulation, and platform control generate winner-take-most outcomes. Once a platform achieves dominance, organic competition becomes extraordinarily difficult. Acquisitions become the primary mechanism by which potential challengers exit the market rather than challenge it.
[P1.5] When every successful startup's endgame is acquisition by a dominant incumbent, the competitive process itself corrodes.
[P1.6] The current framework asks regulators to prove future competitive harm from acquisitions that are structurally designed to obscure it. This is nearly impossible. By the time anti-competitive effects manifest, the acquired firm no longer exists as an independent competitor. The evidence trail goes cold.
[P1.7] A presumption of anti-competitiveness doesn't ban acquisitions—it requires dominant platforms to demonstrate concrete consumer benefits before approval. Lower prices, genuine innovation, improved services, or efficiencies that would otherwise be unrealized. If the acquisition serves consumers, the platform can prove it. They have the data, the projections, and the internal analyses.
[P1.8] The status quo treats billion-dollar acquisitions by trillion-dollar companies with the same presumptive innocence as a local hardware store merging with its neighbor. This formal equality ignores substantive power differences. Antitrust law exists to protect competition, not to protect acquirers' convenience. Reversing the presumption aligns enforcement with economic reality.
