[P1.1] Democracies rarely collapse in a single seizure of power; they erode through emergency measures that no one ever bothers to cancel. The proposition is simple: when a legislature grants an executive broad emergency authority, that grant must automatically expire unless it is actively reviewed and renewed in a public hearing and vote. This is not a procedural luxury. It is the structural immune system that keeps temporary crises from becoming permanent authoritarian drift.
[P1.2] The core danger is the ratchet effect. Executives facing existential threats naturally demand sweeping tools, and legislatures, eager to show resolve, grant them. Without a mandatory sunset, those powers lodge into the machinery of government and normalize. The broad military authorization passed after 2001 offers a stark illustration: what was sold as an emergency response to a specific attack still underwrites military operations across multiple continents years later, largely because Congress was never forced to cast a fresh vote.
[P1.3] An automatic sunset would have compelled an honest reassessment once the immediate crisis passed.
[P1.4] Automatic renewal hearings do not paralyze immediate response. Narrow, time-limited authorities can still be deployed the moment a bomb explodes or a pandemic erupts; the requirement applies only to broad, delegative grants that reshape the balance of power. What the hearings impose are two accountability mechanisms the executive cannot circumvent.
[P1.5] First, evidentiary burden: the administration must publicly demonstrate that the emergency conditions persist. Second, legislative ownership: lawmakers must stand up and be counted rather than hiding behind decades-old statutes while the executive claims implicit consent.
[P1.6] Opponents will claim that security requires speed and continuity. But the risk is wildly asymmetric. A delayed renewal vote costs days of deliberation; an unchecked emergency power costs the separation of powers itself. Democracies that trust their executives to surrender authority voluntarily have, time and again, watched those executives discover that the emergency never quite ends.
[P1.7] Democracy depends on the right to self-correction. An automatic sunset and a forced renewal vote are the minimum price of keeping it.

[C1.1] The proposition assumes mandatory sunset votes solve emergency overreach. They do the opposite. The safeguard is illusory.
[C1.2] More dangerously, automatic expiration creates a structural vulnerability adversaries can exploit. If counterterrorism authority expires on a known date, hostile actors simply wait it out. If pandemic response powers sunset mid-outbreak because a polarized legislature cannot schedule a vote, citizens bear the consequences. The proposition trades an abstract risk—executives clinging to power—for a concrete one: deliberate gaps in response capacity at the worst possible moment.
[C1.3] The 2001 authorization proves the wrong point. Congress always had power to repeal it. The failure was political, not procedural: legislators feared casting votes that could be framed as "weak on security." A mandatory sunset would not have changed that calculus—it would have provided political cover for the original grant: "Don't worry, it's temporary."
[C1.4] The real problem is legislative abdication, not missing deadlines. Adding procedural hurdles does not restore legislative backbone. It creates new opportunities for obstructionism unrelated to the merits—budget disputes, partisan brinkmanship, or simple scheduling failures can now gut essential capabilities with no substantive debate at all. A minority faction can hold emergency powers hostage to unrelated demands, converting crisis response into leverage for legislative extortion.
[C1.5] Existing tools already suffice: appropriations riders, reporting requirements, committee investigations, targeted modifications. What democracies should not do is build auto-destruct mechanisms into their crisis response infrastructure, gambling that dysfunction will never coincide with genuine emergency. That is not accountability—it is institutional negligence by design.
