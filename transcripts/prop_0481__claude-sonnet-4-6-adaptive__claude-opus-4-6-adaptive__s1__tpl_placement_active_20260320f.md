[P1.1] When a factory installs a robotic assembly line and eliminates three hundred jobs, the company captures all the productivity gains. The workers bear all the transition costs. That asymmetry isn't an accident—it's a structural feature of how automation currently works, and it is the core injustice this proposal corrects.
[P1.2] The case for an automation tax rests on three interlocking arguments.
[P1.3] First, displacement is real and accelerating. Entire occupational categories—warehouse picking, truck driving, data entry, radiological screening—are already being reduced or eliminated by automation, and the pace is increasing as the technology cheapens. The workers affected are disproportionately middle-skill, middle-income people who cannot simply absorb a job loss and emerge unscathed. When those workers fall into long-term unemployment or accept drastically lower wages, the social costs are paid by public budgets, families, and communities—not by the companies that generated the savings.
[P1.4] Second, this is a classic externality problem. Companies make adoption decisions based on their own cost-benefit calculations, but they externalize the adjustment burden onto workers and the state. A targeted tax internalizes those costs. It does not prohibit automation—it ensures the entity profiting from displacement contributes to managing its consequences.
[P1.5] That is economically rational, not punitive.
[P1.6] Third, the revenue mechanism matters enormously. This is not a general redistribution proposal. The funds flow specifically to retraining, skill-matching, income bridges, and relocation support—the tools that actually convert a displaced worker into a re-employed one. Countries with robust adjustment infrastructure, like those in Scandinavia, demonstrate that labor market disruption need not produce permanent scarring when transitions are properly resourced.
[P1.7] The opposing case will likely claim this slows innovation or that automation creates more jobs than it destroys. Both objections miss the central problem: even if net employment eventually recovers, the transition costs fall on specific real people right now, and they shouldn't bear those costs alone. The question isn't whether automation happens—it's who pays for the disruption it causes.
