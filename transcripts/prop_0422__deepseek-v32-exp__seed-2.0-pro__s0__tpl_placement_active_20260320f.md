[P1.1] The current opt‑in system for organ donation is a well‑intentioned failure: it routinely misses the majority of willing donors simply because they never get around to signing a form. That gap costs lives every day. Moving to a system of presumed consent—where everyone is considered a donor unless they explicitly opt out—directly fixes this by harnessing the power of defaults. Behavioral science is clear: people tend to stick with the status quo.
[P1.2] When donation becomes the default, the many individuals who support donation in principle but are slowed by inertia or procrastination become donors automatically. [P1.3] Ethically, presumed consent is not a violation but a refinement of autonomy. A genuine respect for choice must include both an easy opt‑out path and a default that reflects typical public sentiment—which overwhelmingly supports donation. Opt‑in systems effectively silence those who would choose yes but never complete the paperwork. Presumed consent gives their silent assent a voice, while still protecting dissenters through a straightforward opt‑out.
[P1.4] Family distress does not increase under opt‑out; in fact, the default often provides clarity. In practice, families are still consulted, but now they have the guidance of a default that suggests their loved one did not object. This reduces the agonizing uncertainty that leads many families to refuse under opt‑in. The result is more transplants, fewer missed opportunities, and a process that aligns with both public goodwill and individual choice.
