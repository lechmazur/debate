[P1.1] When a large employer tells thousands of workers they must return to the office, "culture" is doing a lot of work. It's meant to end the conversation rather than start it. And that is precisely the problem.
[P1.2] The proposition asks something modest: that large employers articulate *why* a specific role requires in-person presence. Not that they prove it mathematically. Not that a regulator overrides their decision. Just that they say something coherent and role-specific rather than retreating to an unfalsifiable assertion about vibes.
[P1.3] "Culture" is unfalsifiable by design. No one can measure it, challenge it, or prove a mandate failed to serve it. That makes it a perfect rhetorical shield for decisions driven by something else entirely—real-estate commitments already on the books, a desire to thin headcount through attrition, or the instinct that visible employees feel more controlled. Several well-publicized mandates from large technology and financial firms came shortly after expensive office lease renewals or hiring freezes.
[P1.4] The timing was suggestive. The justification was always culture.
[P1.5] Role-specific reasoning forces honesty. An employer who must explain why a data analyst needs to sit in a particular building will quickly discover that some roles genuinely require proximity—client-facing work, collaborative prototyping, roles dependent on physical equipment—and others plainly do not. Requiring that distinction doesn't constrain legitimate business judgment; it *reveals* whether business judgment is actually at work.
[P1.6] The costs being imposed are not trivial. Workers rearranged their lives based on remote arrangements: they moved further away, accepted lower salaries in exchange for flexibility, and structured childcare accordingly. A mandate that reverses all of that deserves more than "it's better for the energy of the team."
[P1.7] Large employers have the resources and the legal sophistication to articulate these reasons. The only thing this standard removes is the option to avoid accountability altogether. If the business case is real, stating it should be easy. If it isn't easy, that tells us everything.
