[P1.3] These are not minor inconveniences; they are the primary trade barriers of the digital economy.
[P1.4] Mutual-recognition deals solve this directly. By agreeing that each partner's regulatory standards are equivalent, trade blocs unlock professional mobility and digital market access without requiring full regulatory harmonization. This respects sovereignty while eliminating the duplication that throttles services trade.
[P1.5] The economic case is also a distributional one. Tariff cuts primarily benefit goods exporters and multinational supply chains. [P1.6] Finally, mutual recognition builds institutional trust that enables future cooperation. When regulators learn to rely on each other's standards, the foundation deepens for broader integration. Tariff cuts are transactional; recognition deals are relational.
[P1.7] The motion asks what trade blocs should prioritize. When the remaining barriers are regulatory, when the growth sectors are services, and when the constituencies most in need are professionals and digital entrepreneurs rather than established manufacturers, the answer is clear: mutual recognition first.
