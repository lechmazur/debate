[P1.1] Most users open food delivery apps assuming the quoted total is the cheapest available way to get their chosen meal, but that assumption is rarely correct. Ordinary user behavior shows most only check final totals at checkout, after investing time building their order, making them far more likely to accept unexpected fees than abandon their cart. When an app’s final price—including all service fees, delivery charges, and surge markups—is materially higher than the cost of picking up the same order from the independent restaurant directly, or ordering via the restaurant’s own platform, the app profits off deliberate information asymmetry.
[P1.2] The proposition fixes this: it does not ban app fees, mandate price matching, or force promotion of all competitors. It only requires a simple disclosure of the nearest lower-fee independent pickup or direct-order alternative when the app’s own total is materially higher.
[P1.3] This mandate is technically trivial for apps to implement. They already track user geolocation, maintain databases of nearby independent restaurants, and calculate final checkout totals for every order. Apps already surface similar "pickup discount" prompts for their own services; extending that to independent alternatives requires no new engineering. Adding a small, unobtrusive prompt noting a cheaper nearby option adds no meaningful operational cost.
[P1.4] Opponents will claim this is anti-competitive, but it is the reverse: it levels a playing field apps have tilted in their favor via opaque, layered pricing that hides true costs from users until checkout.
[P1.5] It also delivers direct benefits to the two groups most harmed by current app pricing practices. Price-sensitive consumers avoid paying material markups they never knowingly agreed to. Independent restaurants, forced to either pass app commission costs to customers or absorb them as lost revenue, get a low-barrier way to steer users to direct orders that preserve their thin margins that would otherwise go to app middlemen. No valid consumer protection or market fairness argument justifies opposing this minimal transparency requirement.
