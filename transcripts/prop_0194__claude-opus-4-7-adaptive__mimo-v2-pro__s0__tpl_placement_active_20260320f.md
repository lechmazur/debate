[P1.1] Democracies have a structural bug: the people most affected by long-horizon decisions—children, the unborn, those who will inherit the climate, the debt, the nuclear waste, the exhausted aquifer—cannot vote. Politicians optimize for the next election; voters discount the far future; lobbyists fill the silence. The result is systematic, predictable harm to people who have no seat at the table. A future-generations commissioner with binding veto power over a narrow class of decisions is the minimum institutional fix that matches the size of the problem.
[P1.2] Notice what I am not defending. Not a philosopher-king. Not veto over budgets, wars, or rights. [P1.3] Triggered, reviewable, and overridable by a supermajority. This is not exotic. Courts already veto democratic majorities to protect constitutional minorities. Central banks already bind fiscal politicians to long-run stability.
[P1.4] Wales has a Future Generations Commissioner; Hungary had an ombudsman for future generations; Finland has a parliamentary committee. PRO simply argues the advisory version is too weak for decisions that cannot be undone.
[P1.5] Why binding? Because advice without teeth loses every collision with a concentrated present-day interest. That is the whole lesson of climate policy: we have known for decades, we have commissioned the reports, and we still subsidize fossil fuels because no one in the room represents 2075. A veto changes the default.
[P1.6] It forces the government to either adjust the policy or assemble a supermajority and own the intergenerational cost publicly. That is more democratic accountability, not less—it surfaces the tradeoff instead of hiding it behind discount rates.
[P1.7] The central clash is simple. CON must argue either that the status quo already protects future people—visibly false—or that democratic legitimacy requires ignoring them. PRO says legitimacy requires representing everyone a decision binds. A scoped, overridable veto is how you do that.
