[P1.2] Certified genetic counselors play a crucial role in interpreting complex genetic data, providing context, and helping individuals understand their risks and the implications of their results. A genetic counselor can facilitate this understanding and support informed decision-making.
[P1.3] Moreover, direct-to-consumer genetic testing companies often lack the infrastructure to provide comprehensive support for the complex health information they generate. By integrating certified genetic counseling into the process, these companies can enhance the value of their services while protecting consumers from potential harm.

If consumers still misinterpret results, the problem isn’t the absence of a counselor; it’s the consumer’s choice to ignore or misunderstand the information provided.
[C1.2] Mandating counseling before results are released treats adults like children, stripping them of agency over their own health data.
[C1.3] The claim that genetic counselors are essential for "informed decision-making" ignores how most consumers actually use these tests. Forcing a counseling session before revealing, say, a slightly elevated risk of Alzheimer’s would create needless anxiety for those who simply want to know their ancestry or carrier status for recessive traits. Counseling should be an *option*—not a requirement—because the vast majority of results don’t demand immediate medical action.
[C1.4] The proposition’s one-size-fits-all approach wastes resources on low-risk scenarios while doing nothing to address the real issue: the quality of the information provided upfront.
[C1.5] Finally, the proposition assumes genetic counselors are a scalable, unbiased solution. They’re not. Wait times for counseling can stretch for months, and not all counselors interpret risk the same way. A mandate would either create bottlenecks—delaying results indefinitely—or force companies to hire counselors at scale, driving up costs and pricing out lower-income consumers.
[C1.6] If the goal is truly to protect consumers, the focus should be on improving the clarity and accessibility of the results themselves, not adding an unnecessary middleman that most users don’t need or want.
