[C1.1] The motion sounds righteous and accomplishes almost nothing. Worse, it makes the problem it claims to solve harder to fix.
[C1.2] **Start with the market.** Zero-days and intrusion software are produced globally—by Israeli, Italian, Indian, Russian, Chinese, and increasingly Gulf-based vendors, plus a thriving grey market of independent brokers. If "democratic governments" ban exports to non-democracies, the buyers do not give up surveillance; they simply buy from suppliers who answer to Beijing, Moscow, or no one. The ban removes the one category of vendor that can be subpoenaed, sanctioned, audited, or pressured. PRO's plan trades regulated supply for unregulated supply and calls that progress.
[C1.3] **Second, the line PRO draws is a fiction.** "Non-democratic" is not a switch. Is Turkey in or out? Hungary? The Philippines?
[C1.4] Tunisia? Mexico—PRO's own example—is a flawed democracy that has used Pegasus both against journalists *and* to capture cartel leaders who murder journalists. A binary export ban erases exactly the case-by-case judgment that responsible export control requires. Licensing regimes with end-use monitoring, human-rights conditions, and revocation powers do more good than a categorical prohibition, because they retain leverage over the buyer.
[C1.5] **Third, legitimate uses get nuked alongside abuses.** Counterterrorism cooperation, child-exploitation investigations, hostage recovery, and tracking transnational criminals routinely involve states that are not Denmark. If Jordan's intelligence service helps disrupt an ISIS plot aimed at a European capital, the tool that enabled the intercept may be the very one PRO wants forbidden. The motion would degrade shared security to register a symbolic objection.
[C1.6] **Fourth, the comparison to arms control cuts the other way.** We don't ban rifles to every non-democracy; we license, condition, and monitor. The same logic applies here, with one addition: software diffuses faster than steel. Prohibition is uniquely ineffective against code.
[C1.7] The choice is not "arm tyrants or don't." It is regulated leverage versus a feel-good ban that cedes the field.
