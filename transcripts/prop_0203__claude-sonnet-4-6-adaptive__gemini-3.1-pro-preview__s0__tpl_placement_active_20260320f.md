[P1.1] The central question is simple: should democratic nations retain the right to prevent their own capital from building the military-technological capacity of authoritarian rivals? The answer is obviously yes, and the only serious debate is about implementation details, not the principle.
[P1.2] Semiconductors and advanced AI are not ordinary commercial goods. They are the foundational infrastructure of modern warfare—enabling autonomous weapons, signals intelligence, missile guidance, and surveillance at scale. When a pension fund or venture firm invests in an authoritarian state's leading AI lab or chip designer, it is not merely seeking returns. It is transferring capital, expertise, and validation to institutions that operate under state direction and whose outputs will be available to the military regardless of any contractual assurances.
[P1.3] Export controls on physical goods already reflect this logic. Most democracies accept that you cannot freely sell advanced chips to adversaries. But capital moves faster than chips, and it is far less visible. [P1.4] Outbound investment screening closes a gap that export controls leave wide open.
[P1.5] Critics will say this distorts markets or harms returns. Both claims are true in a narrow sense and irrelevant in a broader one. Extending the same logic outward is consistent, not radical.
[P1.6] The asymmetry matters too. Authoritarian rivals do not permit equivalent inflows into their own strategic sectors. They benefit from open capital markets while maintaining closed ones. Screening corrects that asymmetry rather than creating one.
[P1.7] The proposition doesn't demand a ban. It demands scrutiny. That is a modest, proportionate ask given the stakes—and the burden falls entirely on those who would argue that democratic capital should flow without condition into the weapons programs of authoritarian states.
