[P1.1] Capital is not neutral. When a democratic country's investment flows into the strategic semiconductor and AI sectors of an authoritarian rival, it does more than fund a business—it transfers know-how, validates markets, and builds the supply chains that power repression and military modernization. Screening that outbound investment is not economic isolationism; it is elementary self-defense.
[P1.2] Semiconductors and AI are the foundational inputs of 21st-century hard power. Advanced chips enable precision-guided munitions, hypersonic missile guidance, and real-time battlefield targeting. AI drives mass surveillance, facial recognition, and autonomous weapons. An authoritarian state that builds indigenous capacity in these sectors, subsidized or de-risked by foreign capital, becomes a more dangerous adversary and a more effective oppressor.
[P1.3] The track record is not speculative: China's surveillance apparatus in Xinjiang runs on domestically produced AI trained on architectures Western firms helped commercialize. Its military-civil fusion doctrine explicitly targets dual-use private-sector technology for state absorption.
[P1.4] We already accept screening on the inbound side. Every major democracy reviews foreign acquisitions of domestic strategic assets through bodies like CFIUS. Export controls restrict the shipment of sensitive chips and equipment. Outbound investment is the glaring gap—the one channel through which capital, expertise, and tacit knowledge can still flow to the same destinations we otherwise restrict.
[P1.5] A firm blocked from selling a chip to a restricted entity can instead invest in that entity's local competitor, achieving the same result. Without screening, the other two guardrails are easily circumvented.
[P1.6] Screening is not banning. It means governments review where capital goes, attach conditions where warranted, and block only transactions that credibly threaten security. The scope is narrow—strategic sectors, authoritarian rivals—not all trade with all partners.
[P1.7] The core question: can democracies afford to let private profit finance the strategic capabilities of regimes that weaponize those capabilities against them? The answer is no. Screening outbound investment closes the last open door.
