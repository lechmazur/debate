[P1.1] Religious liberty is real, and it deserves real protection. But the scope of that protection has to match the reason we grant it. We carve out exemptions because a church choosing its priests, a seminary teaching doctrine, or a synagogue selecting its rabbi involves the state in questions it has no business deciding. That rationale is powerful, but it is also specific.
[P1.2] It does not stretch to cover every activity a religious organization happens to run.
[P1.3] The principle is simple: the further an activity sits from worship, doctrine, and the formation of the faith community, the weaker the case for overriding someone else's civil rights. Hiring a minister is core. Selecting who teaches catechism is core. Deciding who may take communion is core.
[P1.4] Running a commercial hospital that serves the general public, a university that takes federal money and enrolls all comers, a nursing home, a thrift store, a contracted social service agency — these are commercial and civic activities. They compete in the same market as secular actors, draw on the same public infrastructure, and affect employees and customers who never signed up for the institution's theology.
[P1.5] Tying exemptions tightly to core functions protects religion better, not worse. Sweeping exemptions invite backlash, erode public trust, and pressure courts to narrow doctrines like ministerial exception when they get stretched to cover janitors and accountants. A clear line — core religious functions get strong protection, broad commercial activity follows the same rules as everyone else — is more stable and more defensible.
[P1.6] It also protects third parties. A gay nurse fired from a public hospital, a pregnant teacher dismissed from a taxpayer-funded school, a Black couple turned away from a church-run banquet hall open to the public — these are not abstract costs. Anti-discrimination law exists because these harms are concrete and cumulative.
[P1.7] Religious mission deserves breathing room. It does not deserve a commercial blank check. Draw the line at core function, and both liberty and equality survive.

A Jewish social service agency does not suspend its values when placing foster children. These are not profit-maximizing enterprises that happen to be owned by churches—they are expressions of religious commitment in action.
[C1.3] The hospital is the mission. The school is the ministry.
[C1.4] PRO's framework would force a brutal choice: abandon these vocations or abandon your convictions. When a faith-based adoption agency must choose between closing its doors and compromising its beliefs, religious liberty loses either way. The community loses too—these agencies often serve populations others overlook.
[C1.5] The "commercial" label misleads. Charging tuition does not make a religious school secular. Accepting Medicaid does not transform a nursing home into a mere business. If anything, entering the public square while maintaining religious identity strengthens pluralism—it demonstrates that faith communities contribute to civic life rather than retreating from it.
[C1.6] The distinction PRO demands—core versus commercial—is judicially unworkable. Courts have struggled for decades to define "ministerial" and "religious." Now we would have them sort each activity by its theological weight? The inevitable result is government entanglement in exactly the doctrinal questions the First Amendment was meant to avoid.
[C1.7] Yes, third parties matter. But they are not helpless. Patients choose hospitals. Students choose colleges.
[C1.8] Employees choose employers. The real question is whether religious institutions can bring their whole identity into public service, or whether they must become functionally secular to participate at all.
[C1.9] PRO offers a choice that is no choice. Exemptions limited to "core functions" are exemptions that stop at the sanctuary door—and that is no protection worth having.
