[P1.1] When a rideshare driver logs into the app at rush hour, she is not free. She cannot accept a shift at the restaurant down the street or run her own errands; she is physically captive to an algorithm that might beep at any moment, with penalties if she declines. Under current rules, she is paid only when a passenger sits in her car, meaning the platform extracts hours of unpaid labor simply by existing. This is not flexibility; it is a structural shift of business risk onto workers who earn less than minimum wage once waiting time is counted.
[P1.2] The platform exercises precisely the kind of control that triggers wage obligations. It dictates her location, monitors her acceptance rate, and disciplines her for logging out during "busy" periods. Courts have long recognized that on-call workers deserve compensation when tethered to employer demands, yet gig platforms claim exemption by pretending waiting time is leisure. This legal fiction ignores the economic reality: the worker bears the cost of maintaining a fleet, fuel, and availability while the platform pockets the surplus value of instant labor access without paying for the inventory.
[P1.3] Calculating wages only during "active" tasks creates a perverse incentive. Platforms can flood markets with drivers to ensure sub-second delivery times for customers while externalizing the cost of that labor surplus onto workers who wait unpaid. A courier logged in for eight hours but delivering for only three effectively subsidizes the platform's convenience guarantee. Minimum wage laws exist precisely to prevent employers from forcing workers to donate unpaid time to make business operations possible.
[P1.4] Including active waiting time is the only mechanism that closes this loophole and ensures gig work pays survival wages rather than extracting private profit from public poverty.

[C1.1] Gig platforms are digital marketplaces, not conventional employers. The motion asks us to treat the seconds between matches as compensated labor, but that confuses being available for work with actually working. When a plumber carries a pager or a food truck owner parks and waits for customers, we do not demand their suppliers pay them minimum wage for the lull. We recognize that independent contractors bear the risk of uneven demand in exchange for the freedom to set their own terms.
[C1.2] The proposition would erase that boundary by legislative fiat.
[C1.3] The flexibility that defines gig work depends precisely on not paying for idle time. If platforms must calculate minimum wage across every minute a worker keeps an app open, they will inevitably restrict log-ins to scheduled shifts, cap driver supply during slow hours, or abandon thin markets entirely. Workers who currently earn supplemental income by toggling the app on between childcare or classes will find the door closed. The very workers the motion claims to help will lose the autonomy that makes the gig model attractive compared with rigid hourly employment.
[C1.4] Moreover, app presence is not the same as economic captivity. A rideshare driver waiting for a fare can simultaneously run delivery apps, listen to podcasts, or stop for coffee. The platform does not control her location; she chooses where to position herself based on demand patterns she can observe. Conflating this voluntary availability with directed on-call time ignores the reality that gig workers multi-app precisely because no single platform exercises the exclusivity that defines an employment relationship.
[C1.5] Finally, the proposed rule would create its own perverse incentives. Platforms would flood markets less, yes—but by locking workers out. They would ration app access to avoid paying for downtime, turning an open marketplace into a zero-sum shift-bidding war. The motion does not secure wages; it replaces a flexible earning opportunity with a scarce, regulated one.
