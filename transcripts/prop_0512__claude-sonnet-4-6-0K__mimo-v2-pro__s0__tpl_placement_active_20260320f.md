[P1.2] Here is why the market fails on its own terms. A new antibiotic should ideally be held in reserve—prescribed rarely, to slow resistance. That clinical logic is commercially suicidal. [P1.4] An advance market commitment restructures this equation. A government or coalition of governments commits to purchasing a guaranteed volume at a defined price if a qualifying drug is approved. [C1.1] The core of my opponent’s case is seductive but flawed: that without advance market commitments, we get no new antibiotics, and high prices are just the cost of survival. This framing hides serious dangers. Advance market commitments do not simply pay a premium for a needed drug. They guarantee massive revenue for a product years before we can know if it will be the right drug, used in the right way, against the right threats.
[C1.2] Think about the incentive. A developer races to create an antibiotic that meets the narrow technical criteria of the commitment. The goal is to trigger the payout, not necessarily to produce the most clinically valuable or broadly useful medicine. If resistance evolves differently than predicted, we are locked into buying millions of doses of a drug that may sit idle.
[C1.3] The public pays billions for a stockpile that might never be urgently needed, while potentially better, cheaper, or more adaptable solutions are starved of investment.
[C1.4] High guaranteed prices also distort the entire market. They signal that the path to profit is to develop a niche drug for a small resistant population and then secure a government-backed purchase guarantee. This pulls talent and capital away from more transformative approaches—like rapid diagnostics that could slash unnecessary antibiotic use, or non-antibiotic therapies for resistant infections. We risk subsidizing yesterday’s paradigm while stifling innovation that could reduce the need for new antibiotics in the first place.
[C1.5] Finally, my opponent presents a false choice: expensive antibiotics or none at all. The real alternative is smarter public investment. Direct funding for early-stage research, prizes for achieving specific clinical milestones, and pull incentives tied to actual health outcomes—not blank-check purchase guarantees—can refill the pipeline without ceding pricing power to private firms for drugs whose future value remains speculative. We should not mortgage public health budgets to a mechanism that rewards speed over fit, and locks us into high costs for decades.
