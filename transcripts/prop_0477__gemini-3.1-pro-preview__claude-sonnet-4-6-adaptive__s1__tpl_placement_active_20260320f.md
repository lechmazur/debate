[P1.1] Undersea communications cables are the invisible backbone of the modern world, carrying roughly 99 percent of all transoceanic digital traffic. This encompasses trillions of dollars in daily financial transfers, vital supply chain data, and secure government communications. [P1.2] Severing them at geographic chokepoints could plunge entire regions into economic and operational darkness, paralyzing a nation without a single bomb dropping.
[P1.3] The threat is an active, asymmetric reality. Hostile states recognize that targeting cables offers a high-impact, highly deniable method of warfare. While conventional militaries fixate on terrestrial and aerospace dominance, adversaries are utilizing specialized submarines, autonomous drones, and "errant" commercial anchors to test the resilience of seabed infrastructure. We have already seen the disruptive power of localized cable cuts in places like the Red Sea and the waters surrounding Taiwan.
[P1.4] Leaving the global economy's central nervous system unguarded is a glaring strategic liability.
[P1.5] We must urgently prioritize cable protection, redundancy, and rapid-repair capabilities, even if it requires shifting funds away from highly visible, traditional defense procurements. Modern military platforms are essentially mobile data centers; they rely absolutely on the secure, continuous transmission of information. An advanced stealth bomber or an aircraft carrier strike group is severely compromised if the global command-and-control networks it depends on suddenly go dark.
[P1.6] Investing in robust sensor networks, autonomous underwater monitoring, and diverse, redundant cable routes guarantees that a single act of sabotage cannot crash the national economy or blind the military. Genuine national security cannot treat the absolute prerequisite for modern warfare and economic survival as an afterthought.
