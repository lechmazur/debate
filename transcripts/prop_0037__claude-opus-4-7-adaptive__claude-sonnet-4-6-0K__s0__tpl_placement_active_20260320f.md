[P1.1] Congestion pricing is a toll on a problem we should be eliminating. It treats the private car in a dense city center as a legitimate default that we merely tax. A ban treats it as what it actually is: a spatially impossible way to move millions of people through streets that were mostly laid out before cars existed. That framing shift is the heart of the case.
[P1.2] Three reasons the ban wins over pricing alone.
[P1.3] First, geometry. A single car carries on average roughly one and a half people and consumes an enormous footprint of road and curb per passenger. A bus, a bike lane, or a wider sidewalk moves an order of magnitude more people through the same slice of street. Pricing nudges the margin; it still lets the car own the space.
[P1.4] A ban reallocates the space itself — to buses that finally move at bus speed, to protected cycling, to trees, to outdoor commerce. You cannot get those gains by charging a fee and leaving four lanes of traffic in place.
[P1.5] Second, equity. Congestion pricing is, by design, a system where the wealthy keep driving and the poor are priced off roads they already barely use — while still breathing the exhaust. A ban applies equally: the banker and the bartender both take the metro. Exemptions for disability access, deliveries, emergency vehicles, and residents with genuine need handle the hard cases without letting ability-to-pay decide who gets clean air.
[P1.6] Third, the evidence from partial closures. Pedestrianized cores in places like central Copenhagen, the Madrid low-emission zone, and the progressive closures around the Seine and Paris's rue de Rivoli show the same pattern: predicted traffic chaos fails to materialize, retail revenue rises, air quality improves, and the space becomes a destination rather than a thoroughfare. Traffic evaporates; it does not simply displace.
[P1.7] Pricing is a compromise with a vehicle that doesn't belong there. If the goal is livable, breathable, efficient central districts, stop metering the harm and remove it.
