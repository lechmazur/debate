[C1.2] Consider what happens when a daughter receives a salary to care for her mother. The state now has legitimate interest in monitoring that care: time sheets, inspections, standards. The daughter becomes an employee, her mother a client. Privacy erodes.
[C1.3] Disputes that once ended in family compromise now become administrative grievances. Some families will navigate this, but many will find intimacy replaced by paperwork.
[C1.4] More importantly, generous payments create perverse incentives. A struggling household may keep an elderly relative at home—not because care there is best, but because the caregiver salary helps pay rent. A son might resist nursing home placement even when his father needs skilled dementia care, because the monthly check matters more than admitting he cannot cope. We have seen this dynamic with disability benefits: when need becomes income, need tends to grow.
[C1.5] Then there is cost. PRO gestures toward savings from avoided institutionalization, but this assumes family care replaces professional care one-for-one. It does not. Many paid family caregivers would provide that care anyway.
[C1.6] The net result is not substitution but addition: the same informal care, now on the public ledger. Multiply modest payments across millions of families and the fiscal burden becomes staggering—funds diverted from healthcare, education, infrastructure.
[C1.7] Finally, there is the question of what we owe each other. Families have cared for their own across cultures and centuries, not because they expected compensation, but because that is what families do. When government monetizes this duty, it signals that obligation ends where payment begins. The moral vocabulary of care shifts from responsibility to transaction.
[C1.8] That is not progress. That is surrender to the logic that nothing is worth doing unless someone pays you.
[C1.9] The status quo needs reform—but not this reform.
