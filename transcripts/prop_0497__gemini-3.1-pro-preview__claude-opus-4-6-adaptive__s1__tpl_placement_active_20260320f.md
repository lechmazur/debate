[P1.1] Hyperscale data centers are unprecedented resource sinkholes. Facilities spanning millions of square feet demand hundreds of megawatts of continuous power and millions of gallons of water daily for cooling. Offering limitless tax incentives to the world’s most profitable tech corporations to build these facilities—without forcing them to pay for the massive infrastructure strain they cause—is an economic trap. We must cap these incentives unless local grid and water costs are fully internalized.
[P1.2] When governments hand out decades-long property and sales tax exemptions, they deliberately starve the local tax base. Simultaneously, the arrival of a hyperscaler forces massive upgrades to local electrical grids and municipal water systems. If the data center does not internalize these costs upfront, the financial burden inevitably falls on ordinary utility ratepayers. Local residents and small businesses end up subsidizing the infrastructure expansions of trillion-dollar tech giants.
[P1.3] This functions as a massive, regressive wealth transfer.
[P1.4] Capping tax incentives unless these costs are internalized enforces basic economic accountability. If a tech company wants to build a water-intensive facility in a drought-prone region, or a power-hungry server farm on a strained electrical grid, they must bear the true cost of that decision. Forcing the internalization of these costs drives innovation. It pushes companies to invest in closed-loop cooling systems, build dedicated renewable energy assets, and upgrade transmission lines at their own expense, rather than socializing their operational costs while privatizing their profits.
[P1.5] Furthermore, the supposed economic benefits of data centers rarely justify blank-check subsidies. Unlike manufacturing hubs, hyperscale data centers are highly automated. They create temporary construction jobs but employ remarkably few permanent workers relative to their massive physical and environmental footprint.
[P1.6] By making tax incentives conditional on internalizing grid and water costs, governments protect utility ratepayers from footing the bill for corporate infrastructure, prevent local resource depletion, and ensure that the digital economy grows without cannibalizing the host communities.
