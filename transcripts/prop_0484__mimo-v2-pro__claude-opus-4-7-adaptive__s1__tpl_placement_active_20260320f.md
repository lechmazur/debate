[P1.1] The climate crisis is, at its core, a crisis of survival for small island developing states. Their very existence is threatened by rising seas, intensified storms, and ecological collapse. Yet, in the international forums meant to address this emergency, their influence is dictated by outdated metrics—population and economic output—that have no bearing on the existential stakes at hand. This must change.
[P1.2] Weighted voting proportional to existential risk directly aligns the process with the problem. Climate negotiations are not primarily about economic trade or geopolitical might; they are about preventing catastrophic, irreversible outcomes. The nations facing annihilation possess the most urgent moral claim and the clearest understanding of the consequences of failure. Granting them a voting share based on their risk ensures that the decision-making table is shaped by the reality of the crisis, not by historical emissions or GDP.
[P1.3] The current system perpetuates a profound injustice. Small island states collectively emit a negligible fraction of global greenhouse gases, yet they bear the gravest threats. For instance, nations like Tuvalu and the Marshall Islands are already grappling with land loss and saltwater intrusion, issues that demand immediate and ambitious global action. Under population- or GDP-based voting, their critical priorities are routinely marginalized in favor of the economic interests of larger emitters.
[P1.4] This structural imbalance leads to weaker agreements, as seen in the incrementalism of past COP outcomes, which consistently fall short of the 1.5°C target essential for island survival.
[P1.5] This proposal is not radical; it applies a logic already accepted in other global institutions. The World Bank and International Monetary Fund allocate voting power based on financial contribution, acknowledging that those with the greatest economic stake should have a proportional voice. Here, the stake is not financial—it is the survival of peoples, cultures, and sovereign territories. Weighting power by existential risk is a necessary correction to ensure climate governance is both effective and just.
[P1.6] Therefore, adopting weighted voting by existential risk is the only way to center climate negotiations on their true purpose: safeguarding the most vulnerable from oblivion.
