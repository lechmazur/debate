[P1.1] Wedding tradition as it exists today is not a sacred inheritance passed down through generations. It is a commercial product, deliberately inflated and performative, designed to extract as much money as possible from people at their most vulnerable. Nobody is shamed for skipping an old folk custom that costs nothing. They are shamed for skipping a $120 per head venue, a photographer that costs more than the average monthly rent, matching bridesmaid dresses, an engagement shoot, a rehearsal dinner, and a dozen other add-ons that did not even exist as standard expectations 40 years ago.
[P1.2] Obligation only applies to norms that serve the people bound by them. When following these expectations leaves one in three couples going into consumer debt before their first anniversary, when one quarter of engaged couples report major recurring fights about wedding costs before they even walk down the aisle, this norm has stopped serving anyone except the vendors profiting from it. No one owes their relatives, their friends, or random social media onlookers a performance. Most guests will forget the centrepieces within three months.
[P1.3] No one will forget the fight over the open bar, or the first six months of marriage spent juggling credit card bills to pay for a single day. This does not mean no one should ever have a large, fancy wedding. It means there is no moral duty to do so. Anyone who tells you otherwise is either selling something, or has forgotten that a wedding exists to mark a commitment between two people, not to pass an audience inspection.
