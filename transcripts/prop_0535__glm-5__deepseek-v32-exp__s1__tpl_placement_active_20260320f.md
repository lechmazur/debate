[P1.1] The fundamental question is not whether education has value, but which policy lever actually moves long-run earnings for the population in question. Paid youth apprenticeships and employer-linked pathways outperform college subsidies on every metric that matters: completion, debt avoidance, skill alignment, and labor market connection.
[P1.2] Start with completion. Roughly 40% of students who enroll in four-year programs never graduate. They exit with debt, incomplete credentials, and no clear pathway forward. Apprenticeships invert this dynamic—participants earn while they learn, and dropping out still leaves them with partial training and work experience rather than a balance sheet hole.
[P1.3] A policy that pushes more people into a system where failure is common and costly is not an earnings strategy.
[P1.4] Second, labor market signaling. College degrees increasingly function as sorting mechanisms rather than skill validators. Employers hire degree holders not because the curriculum prepared them, but because completion signals persistence and general ability. Apprenticeships embed the signal and the substance: graduates emerge with documented experience, industry credentials, and employer relationships that translate directly into job offers.
[P1.5] Third, opportunity cost compounds. A college student foregoes four years of earnings while accumulating debt. An apprentice earns from day one. By the time a college graduate enters the workforce, the apprentice has four additional years of experience, earnings, and retirement contributions—and no debt service dragging on monthly budgets.
[P1.6] The college earnings premium exists, but it concentrates among those who already complete degrees in high-return fields. Subsidizing enrollment expands the pool of marginal entrants—the students least likely to finish and most likely to struggle in the labor market. Apprenticeships reach a different population: those who would benefit most from structured pathways and who currently have no good option.
[P1.7] Long-run earnings depend on completing training, entering work without debt, and building skills employers actually need. Apprenticeships deliver all three. College subsidies gamble on a system where far too many lose.
