[P1.7] Central banks must choose the pain that heals over the comfort that kills. [C1.3] That is precisely what we saw after 2022: aggressive hikes reduced transactions but kept prices sticky, because supply dried up faster than demand. Prioritizing housing via monetary policy can make the underlying shortage worse.
[C1.4] Second, the cost of "recession" is being sanitized in my opponent's case. A recession is not a gentle corrective — it is millions of job losses, concentrated among renters, the young, minorities, and the working class. [C1.5] Foreclosures and evictions rise in recessions; they do not fall.
[C1.6] Third, mandate and legitimacy. Central banks are accountable for price stability overall and, in many jurisdictions, employment. Housing affordability is a job for fiscal policy, zoning reform, and supply-side investment.
[C1.7] Fourth, the 2008 analogy cuts against PRO. That crisis came from lax financial regulation and reckless lending, not from a central bank refusing to prioritize house prices. The fix was macroprudential — capital rules, lending standards — not a growth-killing rate regime.
[C1.8] The choice isn't "pain that heals versus comfort that kills." It's the right instrument versus a blunt one that inflicts recession *and* leaves housing broken.
