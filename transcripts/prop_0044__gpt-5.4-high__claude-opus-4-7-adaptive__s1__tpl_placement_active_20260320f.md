[C1.1] The proposition sounds like simple honesty, but the mechanism it mandates will actively mislead consumers, raise prices, and entrench the largest manufacturers. Three reasons.
[C1.2] First, a *guaranteed* number printed on a box is a legal commitment, and legal commitments get priced conservatively. Smart devices depend on third parties the manufacturer does not control: chipset vendors issuing security patches, cloud providers, app stores, carrier networks, OS makers. A doorbell company cannot truly guarantee that AWS pricing, a Qualcomm patch cadence, or Android API changes will let them support a device for seven years. So they will print the number their lawyers can defend — two years, maybe three — even when they realistically intend to support it far longer.
[C1.3] The label becomes a floor that drags expectations *down*, not up. Consumers throw away perfectly supported devices because the box told them to.
[C1.4] Second, packaging is a frozen medium for a moving target. Support policies change: companies get acquired, extend updates after public pressure, or pivot strategy. A printed guarantee cannot be revised once cartons ship. The honest, dynamic alternatives — a QR code linking to a live support commitment, a registry like the EU is already building, point-of-sale disclosure online — give *better* information than ink on cardboard, and they update when reality updates.
[C1.5] Third, the cost falls hardest where PRO claims to help. Liability insurance, legal review, and conservative warranty reserves are trivial for Apple and Samsung and crushing for the small IoT startup making a niche sensor or an open-source-friendly router. The rule hands incumbents a moat and thins out exactly the competitive pressure that produces longer-lived, repairable devices.
[C1.6] The real question is not "should buyers know support length?" — of course they should — but "is a printed, binding number on the box the right instrument?" It is the worst instrument: rigid where the problem is fluid, punitive where the market is fragile, and misleading where it claims to clarify.
