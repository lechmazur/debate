[P1.1] Automatic fiscal stabilizers exist because markets and legislatures cannot be trusted to respond quickly or rationally to economic crises. When a recession strikes, unemployment rises and incomes fall - and that is precisely when the countervailing mechanisms embedded in the tax code and social safety net should activate automatically, injecting demand into the economy without the paralysis of political deliberation.
[P1.2] The proposition's real test is whether debt concerns should override these automatic responses. They should not, for three reasons.
[P1.3] First, recessions are temporary shocks, and suppressing stabilizers to manage debt ratios during a downturn is self-defeating. Forcing austerity when the economy is already contracting destroys the tax base, lengthens the recession, and ultimately worsens debt-to-GDP ratios more than allowing stabilizers to function would. The historical record is consistent: Italy and Spain tried pro-cyclical consolidation during the post-2008 European crisis and their debt ratios kept rising anyway, because shrinking the denominator matters little when the numerator shrinks faster.
[P1.4] Second, the fiscal multiplier is highest during downturns. When factories sit idle and workers wait on benches, every dollar of government support converts into genuine economic activity rather than crowding out private spending. The same dollar deployed during a boom might be wasted or offset; deployed during a recession, it cascades through the economy, preserving employment and tax revenue that would otherwise be lost.
[P1.5] Third, creditor confidence in advanced economies with reserve currency status is robust enough to absorb temporary debt increases. The United States, Japan, and the eurozone have all maintained or restored borrowing access even at elevated debt ratios, because markets recognize that a government can service debt it continues to grow, but cannot service debt attached to an economy in freefall.
[P1.6] The motion asks whether stabilizers should expand during downturns despite elevated debt. The answer is yes, because the alternative - letting debt optics dictate policy when multipliers are highest - sacrifices real economic output for a nominal accounting fiction.
