[C1.2] **The proposition solves a problem that voluntary programs have already addressed better.** Major insurers already run opt-in wellness programs—think of the discount structures offered through fitness tracking where participants voluntarily share step counts or activity data in exchange for premium reductions. These programs are popular precisely because they reward healthy behavior without compelling disclosure. The proposition claims to want opt-in with strict limits, but we already have opt-in. What the proposition actually adds is the word "prohibited"—a blanket ban on any use outside a narrow, government-defined channel.
[C1.3] That prohibition destroys the flexible, competitive market for data-sharing arrangements that currently benefits millions of willing participants.
[C1.4] **The "strict limits" do the real damage.** Who defines them? Regulators writing rigid rules will inevitably lag behind rapidly evolving wearable technology. A framework locked into today's categories—step counts, heart rate—cannot accommodate tomorrow's innovations in continuous glucose monitoring, fall detection for elderly policyholders, or early atrial fibrillation screening. Each beneficial use case would require a new regulatory carve-out, creating delays that cost lives and savings alike.
[C1.5] **Privacy concerns are real but misplaced here.** The appropriate response to data privacy risks is comprehensive data protection law—governing collection, storage, breach liability, and algorithmic transparency across all industries. Singling out insurers with a sector-specific prohibition doesn't protect consumers from tech companies, data brokers, or employers accessing the same wearable streams. It merely handicaps one industry's ability to reward healthy customers while leaving every other privacy threat untouched.
[C1.6] The proposition offers the illusion of protection while imposing concrete costs: higher premiums for healthy people, frozen innovation, and a regulatory apparatus that cannot keep pace. Consumers deserve better than a prohibition dressed up as empowerment.
