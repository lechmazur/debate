[P1.1] University expansion on a mass scale has devalued degrees faster than it has created roles that demand them. As enrollment surged, the supply of graduates exceeded the growth in professional and managerial positions, prompting employers to treat bachelor's degrees as a minimum screening device for jobs previously open to high-school leavers. This process converts education into a positional good rather than an absolute skill enhancer. In effect, the same occupational ladder now requires climbing an extra rung of formal certification.
[P1.2] The mechanism works through simple supply dynamics. When the proportion of degree holders rises rapidly, employers can require the credential for entry without expanding the number of such positions or increasing compensation. Many administrative, retail management, and technical support roles now list bachelor's requirements where experience or vocational training sufficed before. Graduates in these positions rarely apply field-specific knowledge, indicating the degree functions as a barrier rather than a capability builder.
[P1.3] Over time, this dynamic pushes the required credential higher, turning master's degrees into the new baseline for roles that once accepted undergraduates.
[P1.4] Genuine opportunity would manifest as increased mobility into better-paid work or new industries that value the additional human capital. Expansion has instead generated underemployment, with the share of recent graduates in roles below their qualification level rising across cohorts. This pattern appears because expansion increased the number of people competing for existing desirable jobs without generating new demand for those skills at scale. This outcome concentrates the benefits among those who secure spots at selective institutions while diluting returns for the expanded base of attendees.
[P1.5] The result is a system that demands more years and resources to reach the same relative position in the job market. Mass expansion amplified this credential competition without a corresponding increase in the absolute opportunities available to the population as a whole. The expansion therefore expanded access to the signal while shrinking its value for most participants.
