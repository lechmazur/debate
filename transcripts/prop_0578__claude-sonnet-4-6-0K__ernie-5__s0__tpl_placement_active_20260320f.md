[P1.1] The central problem in global supply chains is not ignorance — it is impunity. When a garment factory collapses and kills hundreds of workers, or when cobalt mining relies on child labor, the multinational at the top of the chain typically escapes accountability because no legal mechanism forced it to look. Mandatory, standardized, audited due-diligence reporting closes that escape hatch.
[P1.2] Three arguments drive this case.
[P1.3] First, information asymmetry fuels abuse. Workers at the bottom of supply chains are in the weakest possible bargaining position and cannot organize effective pressure against a brand they have never directly contracted with. Consumers and investors want to act responsibly but cannot evaluate what they cannot see. Mandatory disclosure transforms a hidden problem into a visible one, enabling market pressure, litigation, and regulatory scrutiny to function as they should.
[P1.4] Second, voluntary frameworks have demonstrably failed. The fashion, electronics, and extractives industries have operated under voluntary codes of conduct, multi-stakeholder initiatives, and CSR pledges for decades. Conditions in many supplier factories and mines remain dire. The reason is structural: when disclosure is optional, only firms with clean records disclose, and firms with problems stay silent.
[P1.5] Standardization and mandatory auditing eliminate that selection problem.
[P1.6] Third, standardization is the decisive word in this proposition and it matters enormously. Right now, even firms that do report use incomparable metrics, varying scopes, and self-selected boundaries. Auditors have no common baseline against which to test claims. Standardized templates — analogous to what financial accounting standards did for investor protection — create genuine comparability, deter cosmetic compliance, and allow regulators to spot outliers efficiently.
[P1.7] France's Duty of Vigilance law and the EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive show this is legislatively achievable without destroying trade.
[P1.8] The question is simply whether multinationals should be accountable for harms their purchasing decisions make possible. The answer is yes, and mandatory audited reporting is the proportionate, functional mechanism to enforce that accountability.
