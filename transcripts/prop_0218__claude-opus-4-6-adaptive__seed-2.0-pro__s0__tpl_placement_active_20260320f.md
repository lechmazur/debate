[P1.1] The proposition contains its own answer to the hardest objection. It does not ask whether trophy hunting is pleasant. It asks whether, when revenue is *transparently* directed at conservation and local communities, banning it would produce better outcomes for wildlife and people. It would not.
[P1.2] That is the ground this debate should be settled on.
[P1.3] Start with the economic logic. In parts of southern and eastern Africa, vast stretches of marginal land are kept wild precisely because hunting concessions make wildlife more valuable alive than cleared for subsistence farming or cattle. The moment that economic argument collapses, landowners and communities face a simple calculation: wildlife eats your crops, kills your livestock, and now pays you nothing. The predictable response is not to cherish biodiversity—it is to fence, farm, or poach it out.
[P1.4] Zimbabwe's CAMPFIRE program and comparable models in Namibia and Botswana have demonstrated that when communities receive a genuine revenue share from hunting, they become the most motivated anti-poaching force available, because every animal lost to an illegal snare is money leaving their schools and clinics. That is not hypothetical conservation theory; it is the mechanism that has kept elephant and lion populations stable in multiple community-managed areas even as unprotected land has lost wildlife.
[P1.5] Now consider the alternative funding sources. Photo-tourism is genuinely valuable—but it concentrates on accessible, scenic areas, collapses during political instability or pandemics, and does not reach the remote, less photogenic zones where the hardest conservation work happens. Trophy hunting reaches exactly those gaps. A single lion hunt can generate more revenue per animal than dozens of photographic tourists visiting that area in a year, and it does so without requiring five-star infrastructure.
[P1.6] The moral distaste is real. Nobody is asked to admire it. But policy that substitutes our aesthetic discomfort for the livelihoods and wildlife outcomes of communities thousands of miles away is self-indulgent, not ethical. The proposition asks for legality under strict transparency conditions—that is the minimum standard a workable conservation tool deserves.

[C1.1] The core flaw in the case for legal trophy hunting under transparent revenue rules is that transparency is a band-aid that cannot fix the existential harms to wildlife and deep, structural injustice to local communities baked into the practice.
[C1.2] Even if every dollar of hunting revenue goes directly to conservation, trophy hunting actively erodes the wildlife populations it claims to protect. Hunters selectively target the largest, genetically fittest individuals: big-maned lions, tusked elephants, mature rhinos. Removing these animals breaks pride and herd social structures, reduces genetic diversity that lets populations survive drought and disease, and drives long-term population decline that short-term revenue cannot reverse. Multiple surveys of southern African hunting zones have found steep drops in breeding-age male big cats and elephants directly linked to trophy harvests, even when revenue was fully earmarked for conservation.
[C1.3] Transparency also does not fix the fundamental injustice of these models. Even when revenue flows are publicly accounted for, decision-making power stays in the hands of national park authorities and local elites, not the subsistence farmers who bear the full cost of living near dangerous wildlife: their crops are trampled, their livestock is killed, their children are mauled, and they see almost none of the hunting revenue to offset those harms. The claim that communities become motivated anti-poaching forces only holds if the people facing the risks actually control the money, which transparency alone cannot guarantee.
[C1.4] Finally, the claim that trophy hunting fills gaps photo tourism cannot is backwards. Trophy hunting concessions actively undercut photo tourism investment, as travelers avoid areas known for sport hunting of iconic species. Converted photo tourism zones create five times more local, long-term jobs than hunting operations even in remote areas, with no need to kill animals to generate revenue. Moral distaste is not just aesthetic: it drives global tourism and donor funding, and normalizing trophy hunting cuts off far larger, more sustainable streams of support for both conservation and local communities.
