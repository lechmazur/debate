[P1.1] Advance directives exist for one reason: a competent person anticipates losing the capacity to speak for themselves and wants their voice to persist. The moment we treat those documents as merely advisory—easily overridden by whatever preferences emerge once cognition fades—we hollow out the very instrument people relied on to protect themselves.
[P1.2] The core principle is precedent autonomy. When someone writes a directive while fully rational, they exercise the same self-governance we routinely honor in medical decisions. That choice doesn't become less authoritative because the person later cannot reaffirm it; it becomes *more* necessary, because the person can no longer defend it. If I stipulate today that I do not want prolonged tube feeding if I develop advanced dementia, that stipulation carries the weight of myreflective, informed self—the self most capable of weighing tradeoffs and identifying what makes my life worth living.
[P1.3] Dementia does not produce authentic value change. It produces cognitive erosion. Confusion, agitation, or momentary appetite are not evidence that someone has rationally reconsidered their life's meaning. A person with advanced dementia who appears content in the moment may also appear distressed five minutes later; their preferences fluctuate with neurology, not reflection.
[P1.4] Treating those fluctuating states as authoritative over a carefully considered prior statement replaces autonomy with caprice.
[P1.5] The proposition already includes a genuine safeguard: if there is *clear evidence* that the person's values materially changed—say, a documented conversation years after the directive where the person, still competent, expressly revised their wishes—then the directive yields. That threshold is appropriate because it distinguishes between authentic reconsideration and mere symptom.
[P1.6] Weakening advance directives does not protect vulnerable people; it abandons them to whatever momentary impulse their damaged brain produces, overriding the values they built their lives around. The competent self, not the fragmented one, deserves the final word.
