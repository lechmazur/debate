[P1.1] The central question is simple: when public money funds a university, who does it serve? The answer must be the public—and the public is poorly served when graduates cannot find work in their fields while employers in critical sectors go begging for qualified people.
[P1.2] Across most developed economies, genuine shortages exist in healthcare, engineering, cybersecurity, skilled trades, and data science. These are not fashionable preferences—they represent documented structural gaps that inflate costs, slow infrastructure, and undermine public services. Meanwhile, some programs at public universities consistently produce graduates at rates far exceeding any plausible demand, leaving students with debt and dim prospects. That outcome is not neutral.
[P1.3] It is a policy failure that universities currently have no incentive to correct because enrollment, not employment, drives their revenue.
[P1.4] Enrollment caps do not eliminate programs or ban disciplines. They introduce proportionality. A public university can still teach philosophy, fine arts, or communications—it simply cannot enroll unlimited cohorts in those programs when resources are finite and returns are demonstrably low. Redirecting marginal seats and funding toward high-shortage fields means more nurses, more engineers, more cybersecurity analysts at a direct cost to no student's intellectual freedom.
[P1.5] Students who want low-employment fields can still pursue them; they simply do so in cohorts matched to realistic demand.
[P1.6] The alternative—unlimited enrollment with no accountability to outcomes—privatizes benefit while socializing cost. Students bear the debt, taxpayers fund the subsidy, and employers in critical industries pay premium wages chasing a shortage that university policy helped create. That is a bad equilibrium and this proposition fixes it by aligning institutional incentives with national need.
[P1.7] Critics will claim this threatens academic freedom or disadvantages humanities. Neither holds. Academic freedom governs what is taught inside a classroom, not how many seats a government subsidizes. And redirecting marginal funding toward shortage fields does not abolish the humanities—it right-sizes them.
[P1.8] The burden on CON is to explain why unlimited public funding with no accountability to employment outcomes is a defensible use of taxpayer money.
