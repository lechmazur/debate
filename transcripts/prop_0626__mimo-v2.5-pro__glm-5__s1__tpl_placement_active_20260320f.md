[P1.1] The case for banning fully autonomous lethal weapons rests on a single, devastating insight: these systems would fundamentally sever the link between the exercise of lethal force and human moral responsibility. That link is the foundation of both international humanitarian law and basic ethical accountability.
[P1.2] **First, accountability collapses.** When an autonomous weapon kills the wrong person—a civilian, a surrendering combatant, a child carrying a toy—who bears responsibility? Not the machine, which has no moral agency. Not the commander, who didn't choose that specific target. Not the programmer, who wrote general code years before the incident.
[P1.3] The International Committee of the Red Cross has identified this as a genuine legal void, not a theoretical puzzle. Every system of law that governs the use of force assumes a human decision-maker who can be held accountable. Autonomous lethal systems don't just strain that assumption; they break it.
[P1.4] **Second, these systems cannot comply with the core principles of the laws of war.** Distinction requires judging whether someone is a combatant or a civilian—often based on subtle behavioral cues, context, and cultural knowledge that current and foreseeable AI cannot reliably process. Proportionality demands weighing an anticipated military advantage against expected civilian harm, which is inherently a moral and contextual judgment, not a computational one. Precaution requires creative judgment about how to minimize harm while achieving legitimate objectives. Delegating these assessments to machines doesn't just risk errors—it guarantees that the legal framework meant to protect civilians becomes ornamental.
[P1.5] **Third, autonomous weapons lower the political threshold for using force.** When nations can wage war without risking their own soldiers, the calculus of conflict shifts dangerously. Military action becomes cheaper, politically easier, and more tempting. We should be making war harder to start, not engineering a technological shortcut around the political costs that restrain it.
[P1.6] This ban is not about opposing military technology. It is about drawing a clear, enforceable line: the decision to take a human life must remain in human hands.
