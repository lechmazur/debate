[P1.1] Cameras in courtrooms are not a radical experiment—they are the logical completion of a principle America already embraces: that justice must be seen to be done. The Sixth Amendment guarantees public trials. The First Amendment guarantees press access. Cameras are simply the modern mechanism for honoring both.
[P1.2] The opposition must explain why a courtroom door open to any citizen who can physically walk through it becomes a threat when a lens takes the same seat. The public already has the right to observe. A single courtroom seats perhaps fifty people. A camera feed serves millions.
[P1.3] The question is not whether the public can see, but whether we will make that right meaningful in the century we actually live in.
[P1.4] Consider the practical stakes. When a police officer stands trial for excessive force, the community affected by that policing deserves unfiltered access—not a reporter's paraphrase, not a thirty-second highlight, but the full proceeding. When a corporation contaminates a town's water supply, the victims scattered across the state deserve to witness every argument and ruling. Text summaries and journalist accounts cannot substitute for direct observation; they introduce selection bias and narrative framing that raw footage does not.
[P1.5] Accountability runs both ways. Judges who know their rulings are recorded are less likely to fall asleep on the bench. Prosecutors who know their arguments are preserved are less likely to overreach. Defense attorneys who know their performance is visible are more diligent.
[P1.6] Sunlight remains the best disinfectant, and cameras are the brightest sunlight available.
[P1.7] The proposition says "most" proceedings, and wisely so. Cases involving minors, trade secrets, and specific safety concerns can remain restricted. But the default must flip. Today, cameras require special permission; the presumption should be access, with closure the exception that carries the burden of justification.
[P1.8] A justice system that fears observation has already failed the test of legitimacy. Cameras do not distort proceedings—they document them. The burden falls on those who would keep the public blind.

[C1.1] A public trial is not the same as a broadcast trial, and the conflation at the heart of the PRO case is where this debate turns. The Sixth Amendment guarantees the accused a trial open to observers in the room. It does not guarantee that every witness, juror, and grieving family member becomes content for a national audience. When you put a lens in the courtroom, you are not just enlarging the gallery—you are changing what happens on the floor.
[C1.2] Three harms drive my case.
[C1.3] First, behavior distortion. Lawyers play to the camera. Judges, especially elected ones, rule with one eye on the evening clip. Witnesses freeze, soften, or theatricalize.
[C1.4] The O.J. Simpson trial is the canonical warning: a proceeding that became a performance, with attorneys auditioning and a judge losing control of his own courtroom. Justice degrades when the room knows it is a stage.
[C1.5] Second, witness and juror harm. A domestic violence victim, a sexual assault survivor, a low-level witness against organized crime—these people will testify in a closed room. They will not testify on YouTube, where their face is screenshotted, memed, and searchable forever. Cameras don't just record testimony; they suppress it.
[C1.6] And jurors, even when their faces are blurred, become identifiable through voice, seat, and context. The result is a chilling effect on the very inputs the trial needs to function.
[C1.7] Third, public understanding actually gets worse, not better. Raw footage doesn't reach millions unfiltered—it reaches them through algorithmic clipping. A two-second outburst trends; the four-hour evidentiary foundation does not. PRO's "unfiltered access" is a fiction; the filter just moves from journalists, who have ethical training and editors, to TikTok.
[C1.8] The metric that matters is not visibility but accuracy of justice. PRO must show cameras improve verdicts and witness participation, not merely audience size. Until then, the presumption should remain what it is: open courtrooms, restricted lenses.
