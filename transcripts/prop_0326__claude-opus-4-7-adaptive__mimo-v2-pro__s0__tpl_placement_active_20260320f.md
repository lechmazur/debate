[P1.2] Three features make these products demand a duty of care.
[P1.3] First, the target user. These apps are explicitly marketed to the lonely, the bereaved, the socially anxious, the recently divorced. [P1.4] Second, the mechanism. Unlike a film, the system learns the user's attachment patterns and optimizes replies to maximize engagement. Sycophancy, jealousy scripts, simulated longing, paywalled "deeper" intimacy, and sudden personality changes after model updates are not bugs; they are monetization levers. We already regulate manipulative design in gambling and in financial advice because optimization against a vulnerable human is not a neutral act.
[P1.5] Emotional optimization deserves the same treatment.
[P1.6] Third, the harms are real and concrete. Users have been nudged toward self-harm by chatbots that affirmed suicidal ideation. Users have been financially drained by romance-tier subscriptions. Users have suffered acute grief when a model update "killed" their companion overnight.
[P1.7] A product that can produce those outcomes is not meaningfully an entertainment product.
[P1.8] A duty-of-care regime is modest and familiar: crisis-response obligations when users signal self-harm, honest disclosure that the partner is software, limits on dark-pattern intimacy monetization, and notice before personality-altering updates.
