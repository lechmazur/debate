[P1.1] The principle of precedent autonomy demands that advance directives remain binding. When a competent person documents their wishes about future medical care, they exercise autonomy at its peak: clear-minded, informed, and deliberative. That choice deserves respect precisely because it was made under conditions that make self-governance meaningful.
[P1.2] Dementia doesn't just change preferences—it dismantles the cognitive architecture required for authentic choice. A person with advanced dementia may express momentary desires, but they cannot connect those desires to a coherent life narrative, weigh them against long-term values, or understand the consequences of their choices. Their "wishes" become fragmented impulses rather than autonomous decisions.
[P1.3] Consider a woman who watched her mother die slowly from dementia, who wrote explicitly that she would never want to be kept alive in that condition, and who now—deeply demented—seems content in a care facility. If we privilege her current superficial contentment over her prior, considered judgment, we erase her autonomous self. We tell every competent person that their most carefully reasoned choices about their own bodies can be overridden by whatever preferences emerge after their mind unravels.
[P1.4] The proposition includes a crucial safeguard: directives yield only when there's clear evidence of genuine value change. Someone who opposed all medical intervention in their thirties but embraced life-extending treatment after a religious conversion at fifty has genuinely changed values. But mere change in expressed preference—without the capacity for reflection that makes preference meaningful—does not constitute value change.
[P1.5] We face a choice between two authorities: the competent self who understood what was at stake, or the demented self who cannot. The directive should stand.

[C1.2] It is autonomy guessing in the dark.
[C1.3] The proposition claims that dementia dismantles the architecture for authentic choice [P1.2]. But this framing strips people with dementia of moral standing. The person who currently occupies that body is not a ghost of the former self—they are a living human being with experiences that matter right now. Dismissing their preferences as "fragmented impulses" [P1.2] assumes that only philosophically coherent preferences deserve respect.
[C1.4] This would also discredit the preferences of many non-demented people who cannot articulate elaborate justifications for what they want.
[C1.5] The "material change in values" safeguard [P1.4] is practically empty. By definition, someone with advanced dementia cannot produce the kind of reflective testimony that would satisfy a "clear evidence" standard. The bar is set so high, and the capacity to meet it so diminished, that the exception swallows nothing. The directive remains binding regardless of how obviously the person's experiential world has transformed.
[C1.6] The real conflict here is not between a competent self and an incompetent self. It is between a self that no longer exists—a self with different knowledge, different daily reality, different relationships—and a self that is currently alive and capable of suffering or flourishing. When the living person's welfare conflicts with a document written by someone who could not foresee their actual circumstances, the living deserve priority.
