[P1.1] A society is not merely a geographic container—it is a shared project. When immigrants cross borders to join that project, adopting its core civic norms and public language is not an unreasonable demand but a precondition for meaningful participation in it.
[P1.2] Consider what happens without that expectation. Language barriers don't simply preserve cultural diversity; they trap people in isolated enclaves, cut off from legal protections, employment opportunities, and civic engagement. A resident who cannot understand their rental contract, communicate with emergency services, or read public health notices isn't thriving in cultural autonomy—they're surviving in vulnerability. The expectation to learn the public language is an expectation of empowerment, not erasure.
[P1.3] Civic norms function similarly. Shared expectations about rule of law, democratic participation, religious freedom, and equal treatment undergird the trust that makes pluralism possible. Without that baseline, diverse groups don't coexist—they collide. History offers repeated examples: segregated communities where honor codes override criminal law, where women's rights regress, or where parallel legal systems erode the rights of the most vulnerable members within those communities themselves.
[P1.4] The distinction matters: civic integration is not cultural assimilation. No one demands that immigrants abandon their cuisine, religion, or traditions. The requirement is narrower—accepting the procedural framework that lets millions of people with different backgrounds live together without violence or oppression. Treating that framework as optional signals that the project of coexistence is optional too.
[P1.5] Every functioning immigrant society—from the American melting pot to French laïcité—has insisted on some baseline of civic and linguistic integration precisely because those baselines enable everything else, including the tolerance of difference. When integration becomes optional, it is the immigrants themselves who pay the price: marginalization, exploitation, and the quiet violence of permanent second-class status. Expectation is not hostility. It is the honest recognition that belonging requires participation, and participation requires shared terms.

[C1.5] Third, the proposition is asymmetric. It demands adaptation from newcomers and says nothing about the receiving society's duty to fund classes, recognize foreign credentials, or refrain from discrimination.
