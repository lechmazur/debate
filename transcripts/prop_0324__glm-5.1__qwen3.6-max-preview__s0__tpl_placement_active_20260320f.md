[P1.1] Place identity is not a logo. It is not a momentary spectacle. It is the accumulated story a community tells about itself, repeated until it becomes truth. That distinction is the entire debate.
[P1.2] Creator-led local festivals and recurring community events build place identity from the inside out. Mega-events impose it from the outside in—and then leave.
[P1.3] Consider what makes a place identifiable. Edinburgh is not defined by a single concert that happened once; it is defined by a festival culture that has grown over decades—driven by local artists, local venues, local audiences who keep reinventing it. The community owns the story, so the story sticks. When a city hosts a one-off mega-event, the identity it projects is rented, not owned.
[P1.4] The branding consultants fly in, the cameras roll, the sponsors pack up, and the city is left with infrastructure it may not need and a narrative it did not write.
[P1.5] Recurring events create feedback loops. A community invests in its own festival; the festival rewards that investment with social capital, local spending, and civic pride; the next year, participation deepens. One-off events extract: they consume public subsidies, occupy public space, demand security resources, and return a brief spike in external attention that fades as soon as the next city hosts its own spectacle. Place identity requires repetition, memory, and ownership—all casualties of the mega-event model.
[P1.6] Creator-led events also reflect authenticity. When local artists, chefs, musicians, and organizers shape programming, the event becomes a mirror of the place. Mega-events, designed for global audiences, flatten local distinctiveness into something universally palatable. The very thing that could make a place singular gets sanded away.
[P1.7] Building place identity is a long game. Public institutions should invest in the people who stay, the traditions that deepen, and the stories that compound. One-off prestige is expensive and disposable. Local, recurring, creator-driven culture is where identity actually lives.
