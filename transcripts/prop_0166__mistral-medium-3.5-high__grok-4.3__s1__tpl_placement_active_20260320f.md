[P1.1] De-extinction is a transformative conservation tool, not a distraction. Reviving the woolly mammoth to restore the Arctic mammoth steppe ecosystem could lock vast amounts of carbon in permafrost, reflect sunlight through snow compaction, and re-establish a self-sustaining cold-resistant ecosystem—ecosystem services that far outweigh the impact of protecting any single endangered species today. The thylacine’s return to Tasmania could suppress invasive predators like cats and foxes while controlling overgrazing herbivores such as wallabies, healing an ecosystem still scarred by its absence nearly a century after its extinction.
[P1.2] These are not speculative benefits but direct applications of proven ecological principles like trophic cascades and keystone species dynamics, as seen in successful rewilding projects worldwide like the reintroduction of wolves to Yellowstone.
[P1.3] The diversion argument fails on facts and values. Current conservation budgets often flow disproportionately toward charismatic megafauna while critical but less glamorous species and habitats go underfunded. De-extinction, by contrast, explicitly targets the restoration of lost ecological functions and roles. For example, the same gene editing tools used in de-extinction research are already being deployed to save existing species from inbreeding depression, disease susceptibility, and habitat loss.
[P1.4] Ethically and ecologically, the case is unanswerable and urgent. Humanity bears direct responsibility for numerous extinctions through direct persecution, hunting, habitat destruction, and the introduction of invasive species, from the thylacine wiped out by government bounties to the mammoth driven out by human pressure. Restoring them is a form of ecological justice that acknowledges our historical responsibility—that we have a duty to repair.
[P1.5] It is a false dichotomy to pose a choice between past and present. In reality, de-extinction makes conservation stronger for both current and future generations. We are not stealing from Peter to pay Paul; we are giving conservation a new, comprehensive toolkit to address both historical and contemporary global biodiversity loss.

[C1.2] Technical uncertainties compound the waste. [C2.2] The examples of adapted gene-editing for Hawaiian honeycreepers or improved breeding for black-footed ferrets do not rescue the case. Those applications are already advancing through targeted conservation funding; routing primary research through an extinct species merely layers on higher failure risk and longer development cycles. [C2.3] The Yellowstone wolf analogy fails for the same reason. Wolves were an extant species returned after a brief absence into a landscape whose prey base and competitors had not fully shifted. Revived mammoths would enter an Arctic already altered by permafrost thaw and novel plant communities, with no tested mechanism guaranteeing snow compaction sufficient to offset carbon release. Any captive infrastructure required during the uncertain acclimation phase would lock in ongoing public maintenance costs long after the initial diversion.
[C2.4] The deeper problem is one of priority. When governments endorse resurrection as a core strategy, they implicitly devalue the prevention of extinctions that are still avoidable. Every dollar and permit issued for thylacine revival reinforces the view that repairing the past is more compelling than shielding what remains, leaving current species to compete for the residual resources.
