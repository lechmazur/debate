[P1.1] Recurring water shortages require structural solutions, not temporary, arbitrary mandates. Implementing sharply rising, tiered pricing for heavy water users is overwhelmingly superior to blunt universal restrictions because it ensures true equity, provides superior efficiency, and funds long-term resilience.
[P1.2] First, tiered pricing is the only genuinely equitable approach. A well-designed pricing model keeps the first tier of water—enough for drinking, cooking, and basic hygiene—cheap or even free. The sharply rising costs apply exclusively to the luxury tier: filling private swimming pools, maintaining massive turf lawns, or running inefficient private fountains. Conversely, blunt universal restrictions—like limiting watering to specific days or mandating flat percentage cuts—are fundamentally regressive.
[P1.3] They punish a large family living in a dense apartment just as harshly as a wealthy estate owner. A flat restriction treats all gallons as equal; tiered pricing correctly recognizes that survival water and luxury water are not the same.
[P1.4] Second, price signals enforce themselves and preserve individual flexibility. Universal restrictions force municipalities to deploy "water cops" to patrol neighborhoods for illegal lawn watering, a system that is expensive, invasive, and easily evaded. Sharply rising prices, however, guarantee compliance directly through the water meter. Furthermore, pricing empowers individuals to decide exactly *how* to conserve.
[P1.5] A household can choose whether to install high-efficiency appliances, capture rainwater, or let the lawn go brown. The financial penalty simply ensures that if they choose to consume heavily, they pay the true environmental cost of that scarcity.
[P1.6] Third, and crucially, pricing helps solve the root problem. Universal restrictions merely ration a shrinking pie. Sharply rising prices on heavy users generate necessary revenue from those most responsible for the strain. This capital can be directly reinvested into fixing leaky municipal pipes, subsidizing water-efficient appliances for low-income residents, or upgrading water recycling infrastructure.
[P1.7] By transitioning to steep, tiered pricing, we stop treating chronic water scarcity as a temporary emergency and start managing it with a system that protects the vulnerable, penalizes waste, and funds our future water security.

That is the frame this debate has to live in. And against that frame, sharply rising prices fail on the two things that actually matter—speed and certainty.
[C1.2] Prices work through billing cycles. A household sees the damage six weeks later, argues with the utility, maybe adjusts next quarter. Restrictions work tomorrow morning: no sprinklers Tuesday, car washes closed, cooling towers capped. When the reservoir is the clock, you need a lever that moves gallons this week, not a financial nudge that resolves after the crisis.
[C1.3] Second, pricing fails precisely where PRO says it succeeds: the wealthy heavy user. A steep tier is a deterrent to a middle-class family and a rounding error to an estate owner. The predictable outcome of "sharply rising prices" is that green lawns become a visible marker of wealth while everyone else cuts back. That is not equity—that is privatizing a shared resource during a shared emergency.
[C1.4] Universal restrictions, by contrast, say the pool stays empty whether you are a billionaire or not. Shared sacrifice is both fairer and politically sustainable.
[C1.5] Third, "heavy user" is not a synonym for "wasteful rich person." It frequently means a household of seven, a dialysis patient, a renter behind a single master meter with no control over the building's fixtures, a daycare, a small laundromat. Sharply rising prices punish household size and medical need. Restrictions target the actual behaviors that waste water—ornamental turf, daytime irrigation, decorative fountains—without taxing biology.
[C1.6] Pricing may belong in the long-run toolkit. But the motion is about recurring shortages, where cities need guaranteed, visible, equal cuts. On that terrain, blunt beats clever.
