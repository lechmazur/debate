[P1.1] The primary purpose of housing is to provide shelter and stability for communities, not to serve as a low-risk, high-yield asset class for private equity. In tight real estate markets, restricting large institutional investors from buying single-family homes is an essential defense of the local middle class.
[P1.2] First, institutional investors fundamentally distort local markets. This is not a level playing field. Mega-corporations enter constrained markets with structural advantages that ordinary families cannot overcome: algorithmic targeting, billions in pooled capital, and the ability to make instant, all-cash offers sight unseen. Crucially, these entities rarely increase the local housing supply by building new stock.
[P1.3] Instead, they outbid residents for existing starter homes. This artificial demand restricts supply, drives up prices, and systematically crowds out first-time buyers.
[P1.4] Second, allowing Wall Street to hoard single-family homes converts community wealth creation into corporate wealth extraction. For the vast majority of families, owning a home is the single greatest engine for building generational wealth and achieving economic mobility. Every time a large investor converts a residential property into a permanent rental unit, that wealth-building vehicle is stripped from the community. Its equity is funneled upward to distant shareholders, transforming neighborhoods of invested stakeholders into populations of perpetual, rent-burdened tenants.
[P1.5] Finally, institutional ownership degrades the living experience itself. Operating as absentee landlords driven by quarterly earnings, large investors inevitably optimize for maximum financial yield. In practice, this translates to aggressive annual rent hikes, deferred maintenance, automated eviction protocols, and exorbitant fee bundling. The result is community destabilization and diminished neighborhood cohesion.
[P1.6] Targeted municipal restrictions on these entities do not harm the housing market; they correct a severe market failure. By limiting institutional purchases where inventory is already starved, cities can restore a fair competitive landscape, allowing local wages rather than global capital to dictate local housing prices. Protecting single-family homes ensures that the ladder of economic mobility remains intact, rather than being pulled up by Wall Street.
