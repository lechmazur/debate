[P1.1] The affordability crisis in advanced economies is, at its core, a scarcity crisis. Decades of restrictive zoning, lengthy permitting processes, and hyper-local veto points have choked off housing supply in the very cities where economic opportunity is greatest. When demand rises and construction is legally capped, prices must rise. No tenant protection statute can repeal that arithmetic, and no eviction moratorium builds a bedroom that did not previously exist.
[P1.2] Rent control and expanded tenant protections treat the symptom while worsening the disease. They may shield incumbent tenants from immediate displacement, but they do so by suppressing the return on new housing investment and discouraging landlords from maintaining or leasing units. The result is predictable: conversions to owner-occupied condos, deferred maintenance, and a shrinking pipeline of new homes. The beneficiaries are a narrow, already-housed cohort; the costs are paid by future renters, young workers, and newcomers who face fewer vacancies and higher shadow prices.
[P1.3] Streamlining permitting and zoning attacks the root cause directly. By legalizing density near transit, allowing accessory dwellings, and replacing multi-year discretionary reviews with clear, as-of-right rules, cities unlock construction that matches demographic need. Tokyo offers a ready model: its flexible, national zoning standards have allowed housing stock to expand steadily with population, keeping rents far lower than in comparably wealthy but restrictive Western capitals. Faster approvals cut financing costs and construction risk, which translates directly into lower asking rents without any public subsidy or market distortion.
[P1.4] The contrast is stark. One approach grows the housing stock and lowers costs through abundance. The other rations existing homes, degrades quality, and pushes pain onto the excluded—especially younger households and mobile workers who never secured a protected lease. If affordability means more people in more homes at sustainably lower prices, supply-side reform is the only strategy that scales across an entire metropolitan economy.

[C1.1] The notion that streamlining permitting and zoning to expand housing supply increases affordability more effectively than rent control and tenant protections assumes that greater quantity will naturally depress prices for everyone. Yet in advanced economies, new supply often caters to the highest bidders and financial investors rather than easing costs broadly. Speculative demand absorbs additional units without lowering rents in the older stock that most households rely upon, and land prices adjust upward to capture the regulatory relief.
[C1.2] Rent control paired with tenant protections offers a superior path because it intervenes directly on pricing and security for existing residences. By capping increases and limiting arbitrary displacements, these measures keep housing within reach for current tenants and reduce the churning that forces people into higher-cost alternatives. This approach does not require waiting for building permits or market responses; it stabilizes the affordability of the current inventory immediately and encourages upkeep rather than the deferred maintenance sometimes seen when controls are absent.
[C1.3] The supply expansion strategy also risks exacerbating inequality by prioritizing growth in high-value segments. Developers under looser zoning tend to favor luxury or mixed-use projects with strong returns, leaving gaps for affordable family housing. Tenant protections fill those gaps by maintaining diversity in occupied units and preventing the sorting of households by income that pure market expansion can produce.
[C1.4] In practice, regions that have pursued aggressive upzoning still confront affordability challenges because overall demand pressures, including from global capital, outpace the benefits of local supply increases. Rent regulations, applied consistently, have allowed cities to retain broader access to reasonable housing costs even as economies expand, demonstrating that protecting the terms of occupancy can be more decisive for affordability outcomes than merely adding dwellings.
