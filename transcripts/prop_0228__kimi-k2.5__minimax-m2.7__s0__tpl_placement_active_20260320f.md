[P1.1] The gig economy has built a business model on regulatory arbitrage: extracting labor discipline without assuming labor responsibility. Platforms like Uber, Deliveroo, and Grab exercise granular algorithmic control—setting prices, dictating routes, monitoring acceptance rates, penalizing "deviations"—yet classify workers as independent contractors to evade minimum wage laws, social insurance contributions, and safety protections. This is not innovation; it is a transfer of risk from capital to labor.
[P1.2] The proposition does not abolish genuine freelancing. Narrow exemptions protect true independents—graphic designers who set their own rates, writers who choose their clients, consultants who control their methods. The target is the false freelancer: the driver who cannot negotiate fares, the rider who wears a uniform and faces termination for rejecting orders, the cleaner dispatched through an app that monitors every minute. These workers wear the economic reality of employment; only the legal fiction differs.
[P1.3] The flexibility argument collapses under scrutiny. Employment status does not mandate rigid schedules—part-time, temporary, and zero-hour contracts exist precisely to accommodate variable demand while preserving baseline protections. The "flexibility" platforms offer is one-way: workers absorb income volatility, vehicle depreciation, and accident liability while platforms capture the surplus. When a delivery cyclist fractures a limb, society pays through emergency care and disability support while the platform books another rider, externalizing costs that proper employment status would internalize.
[P1.4] Default employee status with narrow exemptions restores the burden of proof to where it belongs. Platforms must demonstrate genuine independence to avoid employer obligations, rather than forcing individual workers to litigate their classification against billion-dollar legal teams in courts they cannot afford. This corrects a market distortion that socializes risk while privatizing profit, ensuring that competitive advantage comes from efficiency, not from evading the social contract.

[C1.1] The flexibility argument is real, and it is not mere corporate spin. Millions of gig workers have actively chosen this model precisely because it lets them set their own hours, work around caring responsibilities, supplement low wages elsewhere, or ease into retirement. These are not victims in need of rescue; they are adults making rational calculations about what matters most to them. For many, the ability to log on at 6am or 6pm, to take a day off without permission, to work for multiple platforms simultaneously, is genuinely worth more than the social insurance package that employment status would provide.
[C1.2] Mandatory reclassification destroys exactly this. When platforms become legally responsible for workers as employees, they must control scheduling, enforce availability windows, and manage exclusivity to protect their investment in labor costs. The result is not the same job with better protections. It is a different job—one with fixed shifts, minimum hour requirements, and the very rigidity gig workers fled.
[C1.3] Platforms like Uber and Deliveroo have already warned, credibly, that they would shift to narrower, contracted workforces rather than employing everyone at the current scale. The workers who lose their positions entirely will be those at the margins: the least skilled, the oldest, those with the fewest alternatives.
[C1.4] Beyond workforce size, reclassification imposes costs that ripple outward. Platforms facing higher labor expenses will automate more aggressively, investing in autonomous delivery and driverless vehicles sooner than they otherwise would. Prices for on-demand services will rise, cutting into demand and further shrinking work availability. Small and emerging platforms will be hit hardest, reducing competition and consolidating the market among a few large players with the capital to absorb employment costs.
[C1.5] The answer to genuine exploitation is targeted enforcement, not a blunt reclassification that treats all gig workers as though they face the same risk. Minimum earnings floors, portable benefits, and platform contribution mandates can address real harms without dismantling a working model that millions of workers have deliberately chosen.
