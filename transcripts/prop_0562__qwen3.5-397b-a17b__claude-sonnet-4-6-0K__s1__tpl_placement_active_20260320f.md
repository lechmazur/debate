[C1.1] The proposition rests on a seductive but incomplete model: fix the pull factor, eliminate irregular migration. There are three fundamental problems with that logic, and each one matters decisively here.
[C1.2] First, legal pathways do not substitute for irregular migration—they frequently coexist with and even stimulate it. Migration economists call this the "migration hump": when a country expands legal access, it raises visibility and awareness of destination opportunities, increasing overall flows rather than simply redirecting them. Seasonal programs specifically attract workers who then overstay, convert to informal employment, or use legal entry as a first step toward permanent undocumented residence. The American H-2A agricultural visa program demonstrates this plainly—it has expanded substantially over the past two decades, yet irregular agricultural labor has persisted alongside it because the legal program is slow, narrow, and employer-controlled.
[C1.3] Legal pathways supplement the irregular market; they do not replace it.
[C1.4] Second, the proposition ignores the supply-push dimension of migration entirely. Many irregular migrants are not responding to job advertisements—they are fleeing violence, environmental collapse, or family collapse. No seasonal farmworker visa addresses a Honduran family fleeing gang coercion. Pathways calibrated to labor demand are structurally blind to distress migration, which comprises a substantial portion of irregular flows.
[C1.5] If your instrument doesn't target the phenomenon, it cannot reduce it more effectively than the alternative.
[C1.6] Third, border enforcement is not the static, rerouting mechanism the proposition caricatures. Modern enforcement includes deportation as deterrence, document verification systems, worksite enforcement, and international cooperation agreements—measures that directly attack the infrastructure of irregular migration. These work in concert, not in isolation.
[C1.7] The proposition frames this as pathways versus walls. The real question is: which instrument more directly reduces irregular presence? Pathways depend on irregular migrants voluntarily choosing legal channels that may not fit their situation. Enforcement removes the option.
[C1.8] The mechanism is more direct, and the comparison favors enforcement decisively.
