[P1.1] When police uploaded the Golden State Killer’s DNA to a genealogy site, they solved a forty-year-old murder—but opened a surveillance backdoor that now threatens millions who never consented to genetic exposure. The proposition demands strict prohibition not because justice for victims should be abandoned, but because commercial genealogy databases create a form of biometric dragnet that is inherently indiscriminate, irrevocable, and structurally biased against the very communities most vulnerable to state overreach.
[P1.2] The fundamental flaw is familial bleed. When a distant cousin uploads their genome to trace ancestry, they unilaterally expose the biometric signatures of hundreds of relatives—parents, siblings, estranged family members—who never swabbed a cheek, clicked “agree,” or imagined their DNA catalogued in a police file. Unlike banking records or location data, genetic material cannot be retracted, encrypted, or rotated. Law enforcement leverages this spillover to construct dragnets that would require individual warrants if conducted through blood draws or hospital records, effectively bypassing Fourth Amendment protections by commercial proxy while offering users no recourse to withdraw their relatives’ involuntary participation.
[P1.3] These databases also encode profound demographic asymmetry. Consumer genomics skews heavily toward wealthy, white populations. Permitting police access creates a two-tiered system where certain family trees are transparent to the state while minority communities remain genetically invisible, introducing confirmation bias and false match risks that compound existing patterns of over-policing and wrongful suspicion.
[P1.4] Finally, “cold case” is a convenient misdirection. Once the infrastructure exists for law enforcement to query commercial servers, scope creeps inevitably—from homicide to petty theft, from suspect identification to predictive screening. Genetic data cannot be sanitized or updated after exposure; it persists indefinitely, vulnerable to breaches, political misuse, and future technological exploitation. The promise of narrow use is a fiction; strict prohibition is the only architectural choice that preserves bodily autonomy against irrevocable biometric surveillance and the slow normalization of genetic policing.
