[P1.2] Start with the human cost. When Russia vetoed Security Council action on Syria repeatedly throughout the 2010s, the Council did not merely fail to act—it actively signaled to the Assad regime that mass atrocity would face no coordinated international response. Similar dynamics have played out with China shielding Myanmar and the United States shielding allies from accountability.
[P1.3] Every such veto tells victimized populations that the international order was not built for them.
[P1.4] Now consider the withdrawal threat. CON will argue that losing a major power collapses the institution. A Security Council without Russia's veto but also without Russia's membership could have authorized humanitarian intervention in Syria.
[P1.5] That Council would be imperfect, but it would be more legitimate and more capable than what we have now.
[P1.6] Moreover, withdrawal threats are likely overstated. Major powers benefit enormously from Security Council membership—it confers prestige, intelligence access, and agenda-setting influence. Threatening to leave is a negotiating posture, not a credible commitment. [P1.7] The question is not whether abolishing the veto creates risk. It does. The question is whether the guaranteed ongoing cost of the veto—measured in blocked resolutions, unanswered atrocities, and eroded institutional legitimacy—is acceptable. It is not.
