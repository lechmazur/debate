[P1.1] Democracy's legitimacy rests on a simple promise: government reflects the will of the governed. That promise breaks when large shares of citizens stay home. In low-turnout elections, the resulting government represents only the motivated, the organized, and the well-resourced—not the public as a whole. Mandatory voting with proportional civil penalties fixes this by making representative outcomes a structural guarantee rather than a lucky coincidence.
[P1.2] Consider who abstains under voluntary systems: the poor, the young, the mobile, the marginalized. Their absence distorts policy priorities. Politicians rationally ignore constituencies that do not vote, no matter how badly those constituencies need representation. The cycle is self-reinforcing—disengagement breeds neglect, which breeds further disengagement.
[P1.3] A modest penalty for non-participation severs this cycle at precisely the right point, pulling marginal voters into the process and forcing the political system to account for them.
[P1.4] Critics will cry coercion, but stable democracies already impose civic obligations: jury duty, tax filing, compulsory education laws. Each carries penalties for non-compliance. We accept these burdens because their social benefits vastly outweigh the minor inconvenience they impose on individuals. A proportional civil penalty—a small fine, a modest administrative fee—belongs in the same category.
[P1.5] Nobody is forced to vote for any particular candidate; a deliberate spoiler ballot or a formal abstention option satisfies the mandate. The requirement is to show up, not to endorse.
[P1.6] The real coercion is the status quo: a system that silences millions by treating their absence as consent. When turnout drops below sixty percent, winning coalitions can be built on a third of the electorate. That is not self-government; that is minority rule dressed in democratic clothing.
[P1.7] Mandatory participation also starves voter suppression of its weapon. If voting is legally required, the state must make voting accessible or the mandate becomes unenforceable. The penalty's proportionality ensures enforcement remains reasonable, and the representative outcome justifies the modest burden.
