[P1.1] The kafala system is not merely a visa protocol; it is an institutionalized power imbalance that reduces migrant workers to property of their sponsors. By tying legal residency exclusively to employer consent, Gulf states grant sponsors unilateral control over a worker's ability to earn, stay, or leave. This structure incentivizes exploitation because the cost of complaining is deportation. When a worker's right to exist in the country depends entirely on their boss's signature, labor rights become theoretical rather than real.
[P1.2] Consider the mechanism of abuse. Under kafala, a worker facing wage theft or unsafe conditions cannot simply quit. Leaving the job means losing legal status, rendering them vulnerable to detention or expulsion. This trap forces millions to endure violations they would otherwise escape.
[P1.3] Portable, worker-held visas break this chokehold. They decouple residency from employment, ensuring that reporting abuse does not equate to self-deportation. This change transforms the worker from a captive asset into a rights-bearing participant in the economy.
[P1.4] Economically, the current model is inefficient. It locks labor into specific firms regardless of productivity or fit. If a construction worker is underutilized or a nurse is mismatched, kafala prevents correction. Portable visas allow labor to flow where it is most valued, boosting overall economic output.
[P1.5] Employers would compete for talent through better wages and conditions rather than retaining staff through legal coercion. A free labor market reduces turnover costs and incentivizes training, whereas trapped workers have no reason to invest in loyalty.
[P1.6] Ultimately, this reform is about aligning Gulf labor markets with modern human rights standards. No worker should need permission to escape abuse. Portable visas do not eliminate borders or contracts; they simply restore the basic freedom to change employers without losing legal status. This shift pressures employers to improve conditions voluntarily, knowing workers can leave.
[P1.7] It replaces coercion with competition, protecting dignity while sustaining the labor flow Gulf economies require.
