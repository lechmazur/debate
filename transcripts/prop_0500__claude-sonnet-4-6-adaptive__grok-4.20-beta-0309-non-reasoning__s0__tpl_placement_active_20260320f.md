[P1.1] Reading failure is not a mystery. Decades of cognitive science have converged on a clear account of how children learn to decode text: they need explicit, systematic instruction in phonemic awareness, phonics, and fluency. [P1.2] That is the central problem with classroom autonomy in early literacy. It converts a matter of educational science into a lottery. A child placed with a teacher who prefers meaning-based, whole-language approaches will spend critical developmental years guessing at words from context rather than decoding them. [P1.3] Both children started at the same point. Policy decided their diverging outcomes.
[P1.4] The stakes are not abstract. Research on early reading development consistently finds that children who cannot read fluently by the end of third grade face dramatically higher risks of long-term academic failure. Reading is the gateway skill—struggling readers don't just struggle in language arts, they fall behind in every content area that requires text comprehension. Allowing instructional inconsistency at this stage is not a neutral tolerance of professional diversity; it is an active policy choice to accept preventable harm.
[P1.5] Proponents of autonomy often frame structured instruction as creativity-killing or one-size-fits-all. That framing is wrong. Structured reading instruction specifies what is taught—the systematic relationship between sounds and symbols—not every detail of how a teacher builds rapport, paces a lesson, or responds to a particular student. Standardizing the essential curriculum does not flatten teaching; it protects children from bearing the cost of curriculum experimentation they never consented to.
[P1.6] The burden on CON is not just to show that some autonomous classrooms succeed. It is to explain why we should gamble children's foundational literacy on the hope that their teacher chose well.
