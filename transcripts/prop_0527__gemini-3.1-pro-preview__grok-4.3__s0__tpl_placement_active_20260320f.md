[P1.1] Antimicrobial resistance is quietly dismantling modern medicine. Without effective antibiotics, routine surgeries, chemotherapy, and minor infections become fatal. [P1.2] The crisis stems from an economic paradox: antibiotics are the only product we spend billions to develop, only to explicitly restrict their use. Consequently, developers cannot recoup R&D costs, leading major firms to abandon antibiotics for high-volume chronic disease drugs.
[P1.3] Advance market commitments solve this by guaranteeing a financial return regardless of how many pills are sold. By pledging to buy a set amount of a successful new antibiotic, governments de-risk the R&D process and incentivize innovation.
[P1.4] Accepting high unit prices alongside AMCs is a necessary, and even beneficial, tradeoff. First, high prices accurately reflect the profound value of a life-saving cure. [P1.5] Second, high unit prices naturally enforce stewardship. When a drug is expensive, healthcare systems are economically incentivized to restrict its use to cases where it is strictly necessary. This prevents the overprescription that drives resistance, ensuring the new drug remains effective for years to come.
[P1.6] We cannot let the antibiotic pipeline run dry over sticker shock. The combined financial cost of AMCs and high unit prices is a bargain compared to the catastrophic human and economic toll of a post-antibiotic era. To save modern medicine, we must align market incentives with global survival.
