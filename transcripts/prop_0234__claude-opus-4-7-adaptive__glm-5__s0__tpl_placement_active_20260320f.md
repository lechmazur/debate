[P1.1] Pay secrecy is not a neutral default. It is an active subsidy to whichever party already has the most information — and inside a large firm, that is never the employee. When a company knows every salary and every promotion rate but the worker knows only their own, bargaining is not a negotiation; it's a guess against a house that sees all the cards. Mandated disclosure of pay bands and promotion rates by role simply levels the table.
[P1.2] Three reasons this proposition should carry the day.
[P1.3] First, it fixes a market failure. Labor markets only clear efficiently when both sides can price the job. Without bands, workers systematically underestimate their options, stay too long in underpaid roles, and misallocate their careers. Disclosure turns vague folklore ("engineers here top out around six figures") into something a person can actually plan a life around.
[P1.4] Second, it is the single most effective tool against discrimination that doesn't require proving intent. You don't need to find a smoking-gun email to see that women in a given role are promoted at half the rate of men, or that a pay band is being applied asymmetrically. Jurisdictions that have moved toward pay transparency — several U.S. states, the EU's recent pay transparency directive — have done so precisely because decades of anti-discrimination litigation proved that secrecy is where bias hides.
[P1.5] Sunlight is cheaper than lawsuits.
[P1.6] Third, the cost CON will invoke — "managerial flexibility" — is mostly the flexibility to pay two people differently for the same work without having to explain why. That is not a business efficiency worth protecting; that is the thing the motion is trying to fix. Real flexibility — rewarding performance, paying for scarce skills, adjusting for geography — survives perfectly well inside a published band with a stated promotion criterion. Companies like Buffer and Whole Foods have run fully transparent pay for years without collapsing.
[P1.7] The burden on CON is to show that opacity produces something workers and markets actually want. It doesn't.

[C1.1] The proposition asks us to mandate disclosure of pay bands and promotion rates by role, explicitly accepting reduced managerial flexibility as a tolerable cost. That tradeoff is far steeper than PRO admits—and the benefits far more uncertain.
[C1.2] First, managerial flexibility is not merely the freedom to conceal discrimination. It is the ability to retain a critical employee facing a competing offer without restructuring an entire job family's published band. It is the capacity to reward a high performer who took on stretch work without triggering resentment from peers who see only the outcome, not the input. When a company cannot adjust pay responsively because every move becomes a public event, the result is not fairness—it is rigidity.
[C1.3] Rigid systems reward tenure and politics over contribution.
[C1.4] Second, mandated disclosure does not produce the clarity PRO promises. Companies game disclosure regimes. Create fifteen overlapping job titles instead of five. Narrow bands to points.
[C1.5] Publish promotion rates that reflect bureaucratic reorganizations rather than genuine advancement. The information PRO treats as a simple corrective—pay bands and promotion rates—is structurally manipulable. The mandate produces the appearance of transparency without its substance, while imposing compliance costs and inviting litigation over definitional disputes.
[C1.6] Third, this approach ignores that many workers do not want their compensation public. High performers often prefer discretion; they understand that transparency about their pay attracts competitors and destabilizes team dynamics. The mandate treats workers as a monolith demanding sunlight, when in practice preferences diverge sharply. Some of the most successful transparency experiments PRO cites—Buffer, Whole Foods—emerged from voluntary adoption by organizations with specific cultures.
[C1.7] That voluntariness matters. A mandate imposes one model on every large employer regardless of whether it fits.
[C1.8] The real question is not whether opacity serves workers, but whether a uniform disclosure mandate produces better outcomes than allowing firms and workers to negotiate transparency on terms that fit their contexts. PRO assumes away the tradeoffs. They are real, and they cut against this policy.
