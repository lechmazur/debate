[P1.1] Every parent who has booked a flight knows the quiet dread: will my three-year-old be seated next to me, or will the airline assign them to a middle seat twelve rows away unless I pay yet another fee? This is not a hypothetical—it is a routine experience across commercial aviation. The proposition is simple: when an adult travels with a young child, the operator must seat them together, and must not charge a premium for doing so.
[P1.2] The core argument rests on three pillars.
[P1.3] First, safety. During turbulence, decompression, or emergency evacuation, a separated child cannot follow safety instructions independently. Flight attendants are trained to assist, but they cannot substitute for a parent during a rapid evacuation with 150 passengers scrambling for exits. The child separated from their adult becomes a hazard to themselves and to everyone around them.
[P1.4] Charging a seat-selection fee to avoid this scenario is not revenue management—it is the monetization of a safety risk.
[P1.5] Second, the fee structure is coercive, not market-based. A family that declines to pay the advance seat-selection surcharge is not "choosing" to sit apart from their toddler; they are being pressured into paying more for what should be the default. The transaction lacks meaningful consent because the alternative—leaving a young child unsupervised in a metal tube at 35,000 feet—is not a real option.
[P1.6] Third, the policy discriminates against families by income. A business traveler expensing a seat upgrade is unaffected. A parent saving for months to visit grandparents faces a binary: pay more or accept an unsafe arrangement. Public conveyance, whether airplane or train, operates under licensing and infrastructure granted by the public.
[P1.7] With that privilege comes the obligation not to extract surcharges from parents for keeping their own children safe.
[P1.8] The proposition is not radical. It simply says: seat families together, and do not penalize them for it.

[C1.3] Second, aircraft seating is zero-sum. When a flight boards at ninety percent capacity or higher, adjacent seats may already be occupied by passengers who selected them weeks in advance, hold tight connections, or have medical requirements. Forcing a family together does not summon new space into existence; it displaces someone else. Gate agents would be trapped between a federal mandate and furious passengers with legitimate claims to their assigned spots, turning boarding into a litigation-prone lottery where agents must play musical chairs with a full manifest.
[C1.4] Third, the rule as written is operationally absurd. What qualifies as a "young child"? Three years? Eight?
[C1.5] Twelve? One adult traveling with three children on a sold-out narrowbody cannot occupy four consecutive seats if no four consecutive seats exist. The airline cannot magic away capacity constraints, yet under this rule every failed adjacency becomes a potential regulatory violation.
[C1.6] Airlines already accommodate families where operationally possible because goodwill is commercially rational. Replacing that discretion with a rigid command ignores scarcity, punishes travelers without children, and pretends that legislative fiat alters a fully booked cabin. Some problems are not solved by prohibiting prices; they are merely disguised and dumped onto everyone else.
