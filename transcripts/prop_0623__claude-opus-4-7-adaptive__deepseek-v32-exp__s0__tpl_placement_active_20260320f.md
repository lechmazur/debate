[P1.2] Consider fast food, warehousing, home care, janitorial work, hotel cleaning. These sectors share three features. Workers are dispersed across thousands of small employers. Turnover is high.

[C1.1] The PRO case correctly identifies a problem—fragmented low-wage sectors—but prescribes a cure worse than the disease. Sectoral bargaining creates systemic rigidity that stifles innovation, entrenches incumbents, and ultimately harms the very workers it aims to help.
[C1.2] First, it ignores critical diversity within industries. A one-size-fits-all wage and standard floor for “restaurants” would crush the thin margins of a family-owned diner while barely affecting a national chain. This forces consolidation, destroying small businesses and reducing consumer choice. The dynamic competition that drives quality and adaptation is replaced by a static, negotiated standard.
[C1.3] Second, it institutionalizes inertia. The very boards PRO praises are notoriously slow, political, and prone to capture by the largest firms and oldest unions. They freeze outdated job classifications and work rules, blocking the adoption of new technologies and business models. Think of the rigidity that plagued European economies in the 1970s and 80s—sectoral bargaining was a core cause of their unemployment crises and inability to adapt to globalization.
[C1.4] Finally, it undermines enterprise-level initiative. Why would a employer invest in premium training or superior working conditions if they cannot differentiate themselves on cost? The PRO’s vision of competition shifting solely to “productivity and innovation” is naive; in reality, competition on cost is essential for consumer welfare and market entry. Removing it doesn’t elevate standards universally—it risks making entire industries uncompetitive, leading to job loss or automation outpacing job creation.
[C1.5] The goal should be empowering workers with flexibility and portability of benefits, not locking entire sectors into a centrally bargained straitjacket. The path forward is through portable benefits systems and competitive, firm-level bargaining where possible, not a return to mid-century corporatism.

[P2.7] High-road employers gain, not lose.
