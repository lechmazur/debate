[P1.1] Red-flag laws rest on a single, unavoidable premise: when someone poses an imminent threat, waiting for a criminal conviction can mean waiting until people are dead. That premise is not controversial—it is the entire basis for emergency protective orders, for involuntary psychiatric holds, for the warrantless search doctrine in exigent circumstances. Imminent danger justifies accelerated action across virtually every area of law. Firearms are no different, and they should be no different.
[P1.2] The proposition does not ask courts to strip rights permanently. It asks them to act on credible evidence of imminent risk before a tragedy occurs, then return the firearms once the crisis passes or the owner demonstrates they are safe. That is not radical. It is the same logic that allows courts to temporarily remove a child from a dangerous parent, temporarily bar contact with a stalking victim, or temporarily hospitalize someone who is actively suicidal.
[P1.3] Temporary interventions are how the law buys time to assess danger without permanently prejudging anyone.
[P1.4] Consider what "imminent" actually requires. It is not suspicion, not a bad feeling, not a neighbor's grudge. It means specific, credible evidence that violence is likely and soon—a pattern of escalating threats, a recent act of violence, a breakdown combined with access to weapons and a stated plan. Courts already make these judgments in domestic violence cases, and they do so under the same due-process framework the proposition contemplates.
[P1.5] The opposition will argue this creates a risk of abuse, that people will weaponize these laws to harass gun owners. That risk exists, but it does not outweigh the alternative: knowing inaction. Every year, shooters leave trails of warning signs that nobody had the legal power to act on until it was too late. Red-flag laws exist precisely because the status quo—waiting for a crime to be committed before removing weapons—produced corpses.
[P1.6] The question is not whether temporary firearm removal is ever dangerous. The question is whether it is justified when imminent harm is credibly demonstrated. The evidence says yes, and the lives saved will prove it.

[C1.1] Red-flag laws do not merely accelerate justice; they replace it with administrative pre-crime punishment. The proposition invites courts to seize property and suspend constitutional rights based on civil evidentiary standards—lower than probable cause, often decided ex parte, with the accused typically excluded from the initial hearing. This is not "buying time"; it is bypassing the criminal justice system entirely while pretending the protection is temporary.
[C1.2] The fiction of temporariness collapses under scrutiny. Restoration requires the owner to prove their innocence to reclaim their property—a direct inversion of the presumption of innocence. The burden shifts to the respondent to demonstrate they are "safe," often without appointed counsel, while police custody fees and legal costs mount. For most working owners, this creates a permanent forfeiture dressed in temporary language.
[C1.3] The "imminent risk" standard the PRO describes sounds rigorous, but statutory language betrays its elasticity. "Credible evidence" frequently encompasses hearsay, social media posts, or testimony from estranged partners in custody battles. Unlike emergency psychiatric holds, which require medical professionals and immediate danger to self, red-flag petitions can be filed by neighbors or colleagues with no expertise, triggering armed confiscation based on subjective fear rather than objective threat.
[C1.4] Existing law already handles imminent violence without shredding due process. Terroristic threat statutes allow arrest and detention when someone communicates intent to kill. Emergency mental health commitments remove dangerous individuals—not just their tools—from circulation. These mechanisms preserve the distinction between punishment for crime and prevention of crime; red-flag laws erase it.
[C1.5] When we allow the state to confiscate weapons before conviction, before arrest, often without notice, we transform the right to bear arms into a privilege contingent on judicial mood rather than criminal behavior. The harm is not theoretical; it is the normalization of civil confiscation of constitutional rights. That precedent endangers far more than the Second Amendment. It threatens the foundation that liberty requires proof of guilt, not proof of safety.
