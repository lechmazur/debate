[P1.1] Tax exemption is a subsidy. Every dollar a religious organization avoids paying in taxes is a dollar the public treasury forgoes, a dollar every other taxpayer implicitly covers. That arrangement rests on a social compact: we exempt churches, mosques, synagogues, and temples because they serve their communities spiritually and charitably, filling gaps the state cannot fill. The moment an organization abandons that mission to operate as an overt partisan political actor, it has broken the compact—and there is no principled reason the public should keep subsidizing the breach.
[P1.2] The distinction matters because it is already built into existing law. The Johnson Amendment in the United States has long prohibited tax-exempt organizations from endorsing or opposing candidates precisely because lawmakers recognized that tax-privileged money must not flow into elections. The principle is clear: public benefit justifies public subsidy; partisan advantage does not. When a congregation's leadership instructs members whom to vote for, coordinates with campaigns, funnels donations toward candidate support, or becomes effectively an arm of a political party, it is no longer primarily a spiritual or charitable institution.
[P1.3] It is a political organization wearing religious clothing.
[P1.4] Critics will argue this threatens religious liberty. That argument inverts the actual dynamic. No one proposes telling a church what to believe, what moral positions to hold, or what social teachings to preach. The proposition concerns behavior, not belief.
[P1.5] A pastor is free to say that a particular policy violates scripture. The line is crossed when the institution directs electoral outcomes using funds the public has subsidized. Political campaigns do not receive tax exemptions; political action committees do not. An institution that functions as one should not receive exemption either.
[P1.6] The stakes are concrete. If overt partisan activity costs nothing, tax-exempt status becomes an almost unlimited campaign finance loophole. The integrity of both the charitable sector and democratic elections depends on that loophole being closed. That is the center of this debate, and it is where the proposition wins.
