[P1.1] De-extinction projects deserve government funding and permission because restoring species such as the woolly mammoth can revive critical ecological processes that directly counteract the pressures now facing many endangered animals. Woolly mammoths historically grazed and trampled the Arctic tundra, which kept it as productive grassland instead of moss-dominated terrain that traps heat and accelerates permafrost melting. Small herds of engineered mammoths could recreate these grazing patterns, reducing carbon emissions from thawing ground and providing a buffer against the rapid habitat changes that drive species toward extinction today.
[P1.2] This approach extends equally to the thylacine in Australia. Without this top predator, certain marsupial populations have surged, leading to overbrowsing and soil erosion that damages the very ecosystems conservation groups work to preserve. Reintroducing the thylacine would reinstate natural controls, easing the load on current protection programs rather than competing with them.
[P1.3] Opponents correctly note that every dollar directed toward de-extinction could instead support anti-poaching or habitat restoration for living species. Far from shrinking the conservation pie, the visibility of de-extinction efforts draws new sources of funding and inspires the next generation of biologists to tackle both past and present losses.
[P1.4] Beyond practical gains, governments hold a unique duty to correct extinctions they enabled through past policies or inaction. Permitting these revivals establishes that biodiversity loss is not an irreversible endpoint, which encourages more ambitious protection measures for species still hanging on. Refusing support because it might divert attention ultimately accepts a smaller, less dynamic natural world that offers future generations fewer options for ecological stability.
