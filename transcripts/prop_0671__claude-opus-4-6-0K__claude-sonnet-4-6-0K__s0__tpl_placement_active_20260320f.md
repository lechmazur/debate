[P1.1] Young children learn differently than older students, and our policies should reflect that difference rather than ignore it. The proposition asks for something modest: prioritize paper-based learning in the earliest grades, then make one-to-one tablets available later. This isn't technophobia—it's developmental sequencing.
[P1.2] Three reasons this policy wins.
[P1.3] **First, handwriting and tactile engagement produce superior learning outcomes in young children.** When a five-year-old forms a letter by hand, they engage fine motor pathways, spatial reasoning, and memory encoding simultaneously. Research in cognitive psychology consistently finds that handwriting improves letter recognition, reading acquisition, and retention compared to typing or tapping. The physical act of writing slows cognition in a productive way—it forces the young brain to process rather than passively consume. Replacing that with swiping a screen during the critical window for literacy development is trading depth for convenience.
[P1.4] **Second, screens in early grades undermine the executive function skills children are just beginning to develop.** Self-regulation, sustained attention, and impulse control are fragile capacities in five-to-eight-year-olds. Tablets are engineered for engagement—bright colors, instant feedback, swipeable interfaces. That design competes directly with the patience and frustration tolerance that paper-based tasks cultivate. A child who learns to sit with a pencil and work through difficulty is building cognitive infrastructure that pays dividends for every subject later.
[P1.5] Introducing devices before that infrastructure is stable risks creating attentional habits that are hard to reverse.
[P1.6] **Third, the policy is proportionate and flexible.** It doesn't ban technology from schools. It sequences it. Older students who have developed literacy foundations, attention regulation, and the ability to use devices as tools rather than entertainment platforms are far better positioned to benefit from tablets. This is the same logic behind age-gating any powerful tool: you teach fundamentals first, then hand over the sophisticated instrument.
[P1.7] The burden on my opponent is to explain why the earliest grades—ages five through eight—specifically need screens when the developmental evidence points away from them and the policy still delivers technology later.
