[P1.1] Cities facing overtourism must protect their fragile heritage sites and museums by granting residents priority booking or lower prices during peak seasons. This is not favoritism but a necessary corrective that sustains both the sites themselves and the social contract that makes preservation politically possible.
[P1.2] The core mechanism is straightforward: when tourist demand overwhelms capacity, locals are priced and queued out of their own cultural assets. Venice's Piazza San Marco, Barcelona's Sagrada Família, and Machu Picchu's timed entries show the same pattern. Residents encounter hour-long lines or prohibitive seasonal rates for places their taxes help maintain. Over time this breeds resentment that manifests as anti-tourist protests, vandalism, or political pressure to close sites entirely.
[P1.3] Priority access reverses that dynamic. It reminds citizens that these treasures remain theirs first, turning them into stewards rather than bystanders.
[P1.4] The policy also improves site management. Locals who visit regularly tend to follow norms, stay shorter, and complain about damage quickly. Tourists, by contrast, often treat the experience as a checklist item, concentrating pressure at peak hours. Giving residents earlier or cheaper slots spreads usage more intelligently without reducing total revenue.
[P1.5] The marginal tourist who pays full price still funds conservation; the marginal local who returns keeps the place alive as a living culture rather than a photo backdrop.
[P1.6] Opponents will call this discrimination. Yet cities already discriminate by time, by price, and by nationality in dozens of ways. What matters is whether the discrimination serves the long-term viability of the heritage itself. When residents feel excluded from their own monuments, support for maintenance budgets and protective regulations erodes.
[P1.7] When they feel ownership, that support strengthens. The evidence from cities that have tried resident passes, from Amsterdam's museum cards to Kyoto's experiments, shows higher local engagement and fewer backlash incidents.
[P1.8] The proposition therefore offers the decisive path: protect the residents' stake, preserve the sites' integrity, and keep tourism sustainable. Without it, fragile heritage does not survive the crowds it attracts.
