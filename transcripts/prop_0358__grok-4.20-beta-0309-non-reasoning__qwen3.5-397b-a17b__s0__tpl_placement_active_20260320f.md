[P1.1] Cities should prioritize lower default urban speed limits and street redesign over harsher post-crash penalties because prevention through physical reality works far more reliably than punishment after the fact. Traffic deaths are overwhelmingly caused by the simple physics of speed: every mile per hour above 20 dramatically shortens a driver’s reaction window and multiplies the energy in a collision. A pedestrian struck at 30 mph faces roughly twice the fatality risk of one struck at 20 mph. Lowering default limits and redesigning streets to enforce them through narrower lanes, tighter radii, and visual narrowing directly cuts that energy before any crash occurs.
[P1.2] Harsher penalties, by contrast, operate only after a mistake has already become a tragedy. They rely on perfect detection, consistent prosecution, and the assumption that drivers rationally weigh distant legal consequences against immediate traffic conditions. In practice, many fatal errors involve momentary lapses, not calculated risk-taking. A driver who never intends to speed or run a red light still kills when streets are engineered for high velocity.
[P1.3] Deterrence cannot fix a built environment that makes safe driving unnatural.
[P1.4] Street redesign and lowered limits change the environment for every user on every trip. Protected bike lanes, curb extensions, and narrower travel lanes make speeding physically difficult and visibility easier. Cities that have implemented 20 mph or 30 km/h zones with traffic calming have seen sustained drops in serious injuries without relying on police to hand out tickets. The effect is immediate, universal, and self-enforcing.
[P1.5] Penalties, however, are patchy, expensive to administer, and easily gamed by those with resources or luck.
[P1.6] The choice is therefore between a strategy that reduces kinetic energy at the source versus one that merely increases the cost of failure after the damage is done. Prevention through design scales better, affects all road users equally, and addresses the root physical causes of urban traffic deaths. Harsher penalties treat the symptom while leaving the hazardous conditions untouched. Cities serious about cutting fatalities must first make crashes less lethal, not merely make survivors more afraid.
