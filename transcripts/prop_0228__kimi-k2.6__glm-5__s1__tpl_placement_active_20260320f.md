[P1.1] A delivery rider who cannot set his own price, cannot reject an algorithm’s dispatch without penalty, and can be deactivated instantly by an app he does not control is not an entrepreneur. He is a worker stripped of the wages, insurance, and collective bargaining power that employment law was built to guarantee. The proposition simply closes the chasm between legal fiction and economic reality.
[P1.2] The gig economy’s central innovation is not logistical genius; it is regulatory arbitrage. Platforms extract billions in revenue while treating the core costs of labor—vehicles, fuel, insurance, illness, downtime, and pensions—as personal burdens to be borne by the worker alone. When that rider crashes or retires without savings, the bill lands on public healthcare and social services, meaning taxpayers subsidize corporate profit. Genuine freelancers, those with diverse clients who set their own rates, choose their own methods, and answer to no single boss, are shielded by the narrow exemption this motion explicitly preserves.

[P1.4] Opponents will claim that reclassification destroys flexibility. That is a false dichotomy. Part-time, temporary, and zero-hour contracts already prove that flexibility and employee status can coexist perfectly well. What the current model destroys is security and decent pay.
[P1.5] Without reform, we cement a two-tier labor market where basic protections become a luxury reserved for a shrinking traditional workforce, while everyone else shoulders the risks for shareholder returns. That race to the bottom hurts not only riders and drivers but also the brick-and-mortar competitors who actually employ their staff. European and Asian governments should acknowledge what these workers already live: if an algorithm governs your schedule, prices, and discipline, you are entitled to the wages, leave, and safeguards of a real job.
