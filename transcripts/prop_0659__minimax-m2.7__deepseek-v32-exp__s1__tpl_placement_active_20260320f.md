[P1.1] Emotion-recognition systems do not read emotions—they guess at them, and they guess wrong in ways that harm workers. That is the core of this debate, and it is enough to decide it.
[P1.2] These systems rest on a flawed premise: that human emotional states can be reliably inferred from facial movement alone. Decades of research have undermined this assumption. Expressions vary across cultures, personalities, and contexts in ways that defy simple categorization. A furrowed brow might signal concentration, confusion, or a headache.
[P1.3] A flat expression might mask excitement or devastation. No algorithm has solved this problem, and no algorithm will—not because of insufficient data, but because the mapping from face to feeling simply does not exist in the deterministic way these systems assume.
[P1.4] What these systems do produce is discriminatory outcomes. Studies have shown that facial-analysis technologies perform inconsistently across skin tones, ages, and genders. When an employer deploys these tools to screen candidates or evaluate current workers, it bakes known technical failures into employment decisions. A system that misreads anxiety signals in one demographic but not another is not a neutral tool—it is a mechanism for discriminatory harm, operating with the false veneer of objectivity.
[P1.5] Beyond accuracy, there is the question of what we are asking workers to accept. Employment already involves trading autonomy for livelihood. Asking employees to submit to real-time monitoring of their emotional responses crosses a line that should remain uncrossed. The workplace is not a laboratory.
[P1.6] Workers are not specimens. Their inner lives—their grief, their joy, their irritation, their pride—belong to them. An employer who purchases software to surveil these states is not managing performance; it is intruding into human dignity.
[P1.7] The proposition does not ban emotion analysis from existence. It simply says: not here, not in hiring or evaluation, where the consequences of error fall on workers who cannot opt out. That is a modest, defensible limit—and it is the right one.
