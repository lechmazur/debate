[P1.1] The core question is simple: what actually changes what people eat? Labeling changes what people know. Reformulation mandates change what people eat. And diet-related disease is caused by what people eat.
[P1.2] Consider the mechanism. A reformulation mandate—say, a binding cap on sodium per serving in commercial bread—alters every loaf on every shelf in every store. It reaches the person who never reads a label, the person shopping exhausted after a night shift, the child whose parents didn't attend a nutrition seminar. It operates at the point of production, not the point of decision-making, which means it doesn't depend on attention, literacy, motivation, or willpower.
[P1.3] It changes the default.
[P1.4] Labeling and education campaigns, by contrast, require a long causal chain: the consumer must notice the label, understand it, care about it, and then act on it consistently over years. At every link, people drop out. Nutritional literacy is unevenly distributed. Time, stress, and poverty compress the space for deliberate choice.
[P1.5] The populations suffering most from diet-related disease—low-income communities, people with limited education—are precisely those least reached by informational interventions. Labeling doesn't close health gaps; it often widens them, because the already-health-conscious benefit most.
[P1.6] We have real-world evidence that reformulation works at scale. When several countries pushed aggressive sodium reduction targets for processed foods—through binding or quasi-binding industry agreements—average population salt intake fell measurably, and stroke and cardiovascular mortality declined in parallel. When jurisdictions effectively banned industrial trans fats, cardiovascular outcomes improved, and the effect was strongest in the most disadvantaged communities. These are not theoretical projections.
[P1.7] Meanwhile, decades of labeling policy—from nutrition facts panels to front-of-pack traffic lights—have coincided with rising obesity rates in virtually every country that adopted them. Information alone has never reversed a population-level dietary trend.
[P1.8] The proposition doesn't claim labeling is useless. It claims reformulation mandates improve population health *more*. Given that mandates reshape the food supply universally while labels rely on individual action under imperfect conditions, the comparison is decisive.
