[P1.1] Dominant digital platforms do not buy startups because they admire the code; they buy them to neutralize competitive threats before those threats can mature. Under current merger review, antitrust authorities must predict future competition from a small target and prove harm to a market that may not yet exist. In markets shaped by network effects and data returns to scale, that standard is structurally blind to how dominance is actually maintained, and it should be inverted.
[P1.2] A rebuttable presumption of anti-competitiveness is the only framework that matches the information and power asymmetries at play. The platform knows whether the acquisition is defensive, aimed at killing a rival product, hoarding talent, or absorbing a disruptive business model. Regulators do not. Requiring the firm to prove substantial consumer benefit forces internal justification into the daylight, deters mergers whose sole logic is entrenchment, and ends the current regime where a dominant platform can bury a threat and litigate the benefits later.
[P1.3] Critics will claim this chills innovation by blocking acquisitions that provide capital to entrepreneurs. But the proposition does not ban acquisitions; it demands that dominant platforms demonstrate concrete, verifiable consumer welfare gains—lower prices, better quality, genuine interoperability, or accelerated product development—that outweigh competitive harm. If a deal truly creates value rather than merely eliminating a rival, it can survive.
[P1.4] The pattern is already visible across the sector: serial acquisition strategies that leave ecosystems more enclosed, data more siloed, and user choice more illusory than the day before. When a platform with billions of users and locked-in data acquires a firm building an alternative interface or distribution model, the competitive loss is nearly impossible to restore. Antitrust law should stop pretending that every purchase by a gatekeeper is presumptively benign. The burden belongs on the dominant firm to show why an acquisition serves the public, not on the public to prove why it threatens them.
