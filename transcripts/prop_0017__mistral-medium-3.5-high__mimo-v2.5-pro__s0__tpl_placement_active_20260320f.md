[P1.1] Anti-encampment laws without genuine alternatives do not solve homelessness—they punish it. When a city destroys an encampment but offers no shelter, it criminalizes the act of having nowhere else to go. This is morally indefensible: no just society punishes people for their poverty. Courts have agreed, repeatedly striking down bans on outdoor sleeping when no indoor option exists, recognizing this as cruel and unusual punishment for the status of being homeless.
[P1.2] The practical failures are glaring and costly. Sweeps can cost cities $10,000 or more per person in police, sanitation, and court expenses, yet encampments typically reappear within days or weeks, often in more dangerous locations. These operations force vulnerable individuals away from services, community connections, and even their own possessions. The lack of storage is particularly devastating: when people lose tents, sleeping bags, identification, prescription medications, or work uniforms during sweeps, the already difficult climb out of homelessness becomes nearly impossible.
[P1.3] Services are equally vital, as chronic homelessness is often driven by untreated mental illness, addiction, or trauma—conditions that no fine, arrest, or jail sentence can cure. The evidence in favor of the proposition’s condition is overwhelming. Cities that prioritize permanent housing with wraparound services have achieved remarkable results: Houston reduced homelessness by 63% through such strategies, while Salt Lake City saw chronic homelessness drop by 91% after implementing a housing-first approach. Economically, the choice is clear: studies show that providing housing to the chronically homeless saves taxpayers between $3,000 and $30,000 per person per year by reducing emergency room visits, police calls, and jail stays.
[P1.4] Without genuine shelter, storage, and services, anti-encampment laws are not instruments of governance but tools of oppression that merely hide human suffering while failing to address the underlying crisis. The proposition’s standard is therefore not just morally necessary but pragmatically essential—the minimum requirement for any law claiming to balance order with justice.
