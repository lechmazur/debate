[P1.1] Coordinated harassment exploits a blind spot in content moderation: the gap between what any single post achieves and what a flood of posts achieves together. When ten thousand strangers show up at someone's door, each saying "just asking questions," the individual Transcript looks harmless. The experience for the target is siege.
[P1.2] Doxxing operates on the same logic. A post that says "Jane Smith lives at 123 Maple Street" is not a threat in the literal sense. But when that post circulates through a network organized around hostility toward Jane, the disclosure becomes an invitation and a weapon. The harm is in the coordination, not in any one sentence.
[P1.3] Platforms that evaluate each post in isolation will always miss this. They are reading the words while ignoring the orchestration. The result is predictable: bad actors learn to stay just inside the rules. They post addresses without fantasies of violence.
[P1.4] They pile on with mocking memes and dehumanizing rhetoric, none of which individually triggers review. The architecture of the attack—its timing, its targeting, its escalation route through Telegram or Discord before converging on the target's mentions—does the real work. A policy that only bans explicit threats functionally licenses coordinated harassment so long as participants phrase things carefully.
[P1.5] Immediate suspension addresses the only dimension that matters in a crisis: speed. Coordinated campaigns compound. Each hour the accounts stay active, more participants join, the target's personal data spreads further, and the psychological damage deepens. Waiting for someone to cross the line into an explicit threat means waiting until the harm has already metastasized.
[P1.6] The rule of law does not require us to ignore conspiracy. Criminal law routinely treats coordinated action—conspiracy, racketeering, gang enhancement—as more dangerous than isolated acts, even when no single act would qualify as a crime standing alone. Platform moderation should follow the same logic. Coordinated harassment and doxxing are the conspiracy charges of online harm: the pattern itself is the offense, and suspension is the proportionate response.
