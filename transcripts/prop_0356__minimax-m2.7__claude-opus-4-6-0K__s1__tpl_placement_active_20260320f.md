[P1.1] Continuous in-car surveillance is not simply a pricing tool—it is a permanent, intimate observer riding shotgun every time you start your engine. Every hard brake, every late-night trip, every passenger becomes data. The proposition before us asks a straightforward question: should insurers be permitted to monetise that surveillance unless drivers have genuinely fair choice about it? My answer is no, and here is why.
[P1.2] First, meaningful consent in this context is largely fictional. Insurance contracts are not negotiated; they are take-it-or-leave-it. Insurers possess specialised actuarial knowledge, vast datasets, and teams of analysts who can extract signals from driving behaviour that most drivers would never recognise or understand. When the party with superior information dictates the terms of its own power, genuine opt-in does not exist.
[P1.3] Drivers may technically check a box, but they cannot meaningfully evaluate what they are surrendering.
[P1.4] Second, continuous surveillance creates chilling effects that distort behaviour in ways that have nothing to do with actual risk. Drivers aware they are monitored may avoid driving at night—even when safe—because late trips look suspicious in an algorithm's model. People with medical conditions that require flexibility, shift workers, parents with irregular schedules all pay a hidden price that no opt-in disclosure adequately quantifies.
[P1.5] Third, the data does not stay contained. Behavioural profiles assembled from driving patterns can be sold, leaked, or combined with other datasets to build portraits of individuals that affect employment, credit, or social profiling—uses drivers never anticipated when they clicked accept.
[P1.6] Finally, the asymmetry is irreconcilable without structural safeguards. Insurers profit from granular behaviour data; drivers bear the costs of surveillance, discrimination, and privacy erosion. The proposition does not ban the technology—it demands fair terms. That is not radical.
[P1.7] It is the minimum a just market requires.
