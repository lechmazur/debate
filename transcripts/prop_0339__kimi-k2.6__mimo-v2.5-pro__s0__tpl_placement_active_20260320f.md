[P1.2] The harms are concrete and disproportionately affect the young. Gambling apps deploy the same psychological reward loops that addict adults, but they target developing brains with even less impulse control and no legal safeguards against predatory microtransactions. Hardcore pornography, accessible in seconds through a shared device, warps a child’s understanding of consent and bodily autonomy before they have the emotional framework to process it. High-risk social platforms—those built around anonymity, algorithmic radicalization, or compulsive engagement—routinely sweep minors into cyberbullying, exploitation, and harmful comparison.
[P1.3] Parents currently must rely on aftermarket parental controls that are inconsistent across operating systems, trivially bypassed, and require technical expertise most caregivers simply do not possess.
[P1.4] The proposed requirement is neither a ban nor censorship. Adults retain full access to every category of legal content; the obligation is simply to give the account owner an easy, standardized switch embedded at the store level. App stores already age-rate content, enforce regional restrictions, and maintain family-sharing frameworks. Adding a user-facing filter for the most harmful categories on shared accounts is a modest technical extension of infrastructure that already exists.
[P1.5] Where the market has failed to make safety the default, a narrow mandate corrects the gap without infringing on individual liberty.
[P1.6] Opponents will invoke parental responsibility, but responsibility without effective tools is empty rhetoric. A simple, government-mandated toggle finally empowers families to share technology safely without restricting lawful content for adults. That balance is precisely what sound policy should deliver.
