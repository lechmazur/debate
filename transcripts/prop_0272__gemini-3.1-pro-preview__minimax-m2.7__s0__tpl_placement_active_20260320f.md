[P1.2] Implementing long, fixed, non-renewable terms solves this democratic crisis. By guaranteeing regular, predictable vacancies—such as one every two years—the appointment process becomes a standardized feature of the democratic cycle. No single election or administration can radically hijack the court due to a random cluster of vacancies.
[P1.3] Instead, the court’s composition gradually and predictably evolves to reflect the sustained democratic will of the populace over time. This ends the incentive for politicians to appoint dangerously inexperienced partisans simply to secure demographic dominance for a half-century. It restores the legitimacy of the court by tying it to the nation's long-term democratic choices, rather than the accidents of history.
[P1.4] Critically, this massive gain in democratic legitimacy does not come at the expense of judicial independence. The core requirement for an independent judiciary is that judges are insulated from political retaliation and cannot be removed or punished for unpopular rulings. A lengthy, guaranteed, non-renewable term provides the exact same insulation as life tenure. If a justice cannot be fired and cannot be reappointed, they have absolutely no incentive to pander to the executive, the legislature, or public opinion.
[P1.5] They are entirely free to rule strictly on the law.
[P1.6] The opposition will rely on the false premise that only a lifelong appointment can shield a judge's conscience. But judicial independence is guaranteed by the unalterable security of the appointment, not its eternity. The gains to public trust are immense; the damage to independence is zero.
