[P1.1] Long-term care is one of the most financially catastrophic risks an ordinary person faces, and the current system—built on family responsibility, means testing, and private savings—fails to manage it. That failure is predictable, not incidental, and government-built universal systems are the principled and practical remedy.
[P1.2] The core problem is that long-term care risk is genuinely uninsurable at the individual level. You cannot know whether you will need two months of support or twelve years. Private insurance markets have largely retreated from this space because adverse selection and unpredictable claim tails make sustainable pricing nearly impossible. The result is that most people arrive at old age either underinsured or entirely uninsured against a cost that can easily consume a lifetime of accumulated savings.
[P1.3] Means testing merely codifies this: it requires people to exhaust their assets before receiving help, punishing prudent saving and forcing deliberate impoverishment as the price of assistance.
[P1.4] Relying on families compounds the injustice. The burden falls disproportionately on women, who more often reduce work hours, forgo promotions, and sacrifice retirement savings of their own to provide unpaid care. This is not a private arrangement freely chosen across a level playing field; it is an externalization of social cost onto the most available caregiver. Universal systems—those operating in the Nordic countries, Japan, Germany, and the Netherlands—demonstrate that pooling this risk publicly makes the math work.
[P1.5] Large mandatory pools spread tail risk across entire populations, dramatically reducing per-capita cost volatility and eliminating the adverse-selection spiral that kills private markets.
[P1.6] The comparison to universal health care is instructive. We do not ask individuals to save privately against the full cost of a heart attack; we recognize catastrophic, unpredictable medical risk as a social problem requiring collective solutions. Long-term care presents exactly the same structure. Moral consistency demands we treat it the same way.
[P1.7] The question is not whether pooling this risk is efficient—it plainly is. The question is whether we do it fairly, across everyone, or leave the unlucky to fall.
