[P1.1] Warning labels on ultra-processed foods and sugary drinks succeed where consumer choice campaigns fail because they address the root problem: people cannot choose what they do not understand at the point of decision.
[P1.2] Consider the parallel with tobacco. Decades of public education campaigns against smoking produced modest results. But when governments mandated graphic, prominent warning labels on cigarette packages, smoking rates dropped meaningfully and persistently across countries that adopted them. The mechanism is straightforward—warnings intervene at the exact moment of choice, when motivation to seek out and process information is lowest.
[P1.3] Consumer campaigns assume people will seek health information, internalize it, and recall it in a grocery store aisle surrounded by packaging designed to trigger impulse purchases. That assumption is wrong.
[P1.4] Ultra-processed foods and sugary drinks are engineered by teams of food scientists to maximize cravings and override satiety cues. The industry spends billions on marketing, shelf placement, and packaging psychology. Asking governments to fight this with poster campaigns and educational websites is bringing a pamphlet to an arms race. A prominent warning label—a bold mark on the package saying this product is linked to obesity, diabetes, or heart disease—creates a counterweight at the moment it matters.
[P1.5] Chile's experience is instructive. After implementing mandatory front-of-package warning labels on unhealthy foods, purchases of products carrying those warnings dropped significantly. The labels worked precisely because they were immediate, unavoidable, and required no prior engagement with public health messaging.
[P1.6] Consumer choice campaigns have a role, but they are the supporting actor, not the lead. They reach people selectively, require sustained attention, and operate at a psychological distance from actual purchasing behavior. Warning labels are democratic—they reach every single person who picks up the product, regardless of education, language, or whether they have ever seen a government nutrition campaign.
[P1.7] The question is not whether to inform consumers. It is whether to inform them at the only moment that matters.

The more fundamental problem is label fatigue. When warnings are everywhere, they become wallpaper. This is well-documented in pharmaceutical and alcohol labeling contexts.
[C1.7] Prominent warnings work when they're rare and striking—tobacco benefited from being early. Food labels on hundreds of product categories will not reproduce that effect.
