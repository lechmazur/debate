[P1.1] The central question is comparative: do the price and e-waste gains outweigh the safety and security costs? The answer is yes, and the structure of the argument is straightforward.
[P1.2] Start with prices. When manufacturers hold a monopoly on repairs—proprietary parts, locked diagnostics, authorized-only service—consumers pay artificially inflated rates or discard functional devices they could otherwise fix cheaply. Right-to-repair legislation breaks that monopoly. Independent repair shops, armed with access to genuine parts and documentation, compete on price.
[P1.3] That competition alone pushes repair costs down. For the roughly half of consumers who currently replace devices because repair quotes exceed perceived value, even modest price reductions convert replacements into repairs. The savings are real, recurring, and distributed across millions of households.
[P1.4] The e-waste argument is even stronger. Electronics are among the fastest-growing waste streams globally. A phone that costs fifty dollars to fix at an independent shop gets fixed; the same phone abandoned because the manufacturer charges three hundred dollars gets thrown away. Right to repair doesn't require perfect behavior from every consumer—it just needs to shift the economic calculus at the margin.
[P1.5] Even a modest increase in repair rates meaningfully reduces the tonnage of discarded devices, the toxic materials in landfills, and the energy embedded in manufacturing replacements.
[P1.6] Now consider the supposed risks. The safety and security objections boil down to two claims: that unqualified technicians might damage devices or expose users to hardware vulnerabilities. Both deserve scrutiny. Qualified independent repair shops already exist and already fix electronics safely—right to repair gives them better tools, not worse incentives.
[P1.7] The security argument is similarly weak: the primary cybersecurity threats consumers face come from software exploits and data breaches, not hardware repair. The physical repair of a battery or screen does not create a meaningful attack surface.
[P1.8] The asymmetry is decisive. The price and e-waste benefits are structural, scalable, and cumulative over five years. The safety and security risks are speculative, narrow in scope, and largely addressable through existing consumer protection law. The proposition holds.

Reactive consumer protection laws only compensate victims after harm occurs, they cannot prevent the thousands of fires, data breaches, and injuries that will follow.
