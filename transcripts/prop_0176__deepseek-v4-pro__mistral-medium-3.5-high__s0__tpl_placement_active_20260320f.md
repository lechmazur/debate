[C1.2] They bypass the very bureaucracies that typically siphon off public funds, putting money directly into citizens’ hands to spend on education, healthcare, or productive investments that build local economies.
[C1.3] The macroeconomic concerns are overblown and misdirected. [C1.4] Intergenerational equity is not served by SWFs that may be mismanaged or looted. Nigeria and Angola show how easily these funds become political slush funds. Unlike SWF investments, which often prioritize foreign assets, cash spent locally creates immediate domestic multiplier effects.
[C1.5] Most fundamentally, SWFs entrench the governance problems that undermine development. They require sophisticated financial management and strong anti-corruption frameworks that many resource-rich states lack. Direct transfers, however, are administratively straightforward and transparently distributed, making diversion far more visible and politically costly.
