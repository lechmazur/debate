[P1.1] Blanket pet bans are a sledgehammer used on a problem that calls for a scalpel. The harm landlords actually fear — chewed baseboards, stained carpets, noise complaints, the rare bite — is concrete, bounded, and insurable. The policy response should match that profile. Standardized pet insurance or a dedicated damage deposit does exactly this: it internalizes the risk to the pet owner, guarantees the landlord a clean recovery path, and stops punishing the 90% of responsible owners for hypothetical sins of the 10%.
[P1.2] Three reasons this should be the default.
[P1.3] First, welfare and fairness. Pets are not luxuries; for elderly tenants, single adults, and families, they are core to mental health and daily life. A blanket ban means renters routinely surrender animals to shelters, lie on applications, or stay trapped in unsuitable housing. When a cheap, standardized bond solves the landlord's actual exposure, refusing it isn't prudence — it's preference dressed up as policy.
[P1.4] Second, the economics already favor this. Pet damage in a typical unit is small relative to a one-month deposit, and insurance products in this space exist precisely because the loss distribution is predictable. Risk that is predictable and bounded is exactly the kind of risk markets price well. A blanket ban over-insures the landlord at the tenant's full expense; a standardized deposit prices the risk at its true level.
[P1.5] Third, the default matters. "Allowed unless the building can justify otherwise" flips the burden onto the party with information and market power, and it lets genuine exceptions — a tiny converted Victorian, a documented allergy cluster — still opt out. PRO is not arguing every building must accept a Great Dane; PRO is arguing the starting point should be yes-with-skin-in-the-game, not no.
[P1.6] The clash, then, is simple. CON must show that bounded, insurable, individualized risk justifies a categorical prohibition on a basic feature of tenant life. That's a heavy lift, and the insurance mechanism is precisely what makes it unnecessary.
