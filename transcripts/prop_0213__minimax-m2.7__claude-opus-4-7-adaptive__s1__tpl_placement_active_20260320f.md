[P1.1] # PRO Opening Speech
[P1.2] The proposition before us asks a fundamental question: should societies emerging from mass atrocity face their past or bury it? I will argue they must choose truth.
[P1.3] Consider what happened in Argentina after its dictatorship. Rather than granting immunity, the 1984 Nunca Más report documented over nine hundred disappearances, named perpetrators, and gave families answers. The result was not renewed bloodshed—it was a democratic consolidation that has lasted four decades. Chile's 1990 Rettig Commission followed a similar path.
[P1.4] These societies discovered that false stability through silence is borrowed time. Perpetrators remain in power, victims remain silent, and the underlying conditions that produced atrocity remain intact.
[P1.5] Amnesties trade a present calm for a deferred reckoning. They are not peace—they are suppression. And suppression only works until it doesn't.
[P1.6] Truth commissions work differently. Public testimony creates an official record that cannot be revised or denied. It names names. It establishes what happened.
[P1.7] This record serves multiple functions: it honors victims by validating their suffering, it deters future perpetrators by demonstrating that crimes will be documented rather than forgotten, and it equips democratic institutions with the knowledge to prevent recurrence.
[P1.8] The case for truth commissions is ultimately a case about what stability actually means. Amnesties often preserve the power structures that produced the conflict in the first place. Truth commissions break those structures open. South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission forced confessions that otherwise would have remained secrets—and in doing so, it made the machinery of repression visible and therefore preventable.
[P1.9] My opponents will argue that truth commissions destabilize fragile transitions. But the instability that matters is not the discomfort of testimony—it is the instability of unfinished justice. The societies that have tried amnesties as shortcuts to peace have repeatedly discovered that shortcuts through injustice lead to dead ends.
