[P1.2] That information gap is the problem this proposition corrects.
[P1.3] The ask is modest: when the platform's all-in price is materially higher than a nearby pickup or direct-order alternative, show the user that option. Not a mandate to use it. Not a ban on convenience fees. A disclosure.
[P1.4] The user still chooses.
[P1.5] Three reasons this is right.
[P1.6] First, transparency is the baseline of fair markets. We require financial products, prescription drugs, and energy tariffs to disclose material cost differences. Food platforms occupy the same structural role—an intermediary with far more information than the consumer. Mandatory disclosure simply levels the information playing field.
[P1.7] Second, the harm is real and concentrated. Delivery fees disproportionately burden lower-income consumers who rely on apps because they lack time or transport, not because they're indifferent to price. A ten-dollar markup on a twenty-dollar meal is a fifty-percent surcharge on people least able to absorb it. Disclosure gives them the exit they don't currently know exists.
[P1.8] Third, the "materially higher" threshold already does the proportionality work. The proposition doesn't require disclosure for marginal differences—only when the gap is substantial. Platforms retain their revenue when convenience genuinely justifies the premium; they lose only their ability to exploit ignorance when it doesn't.
