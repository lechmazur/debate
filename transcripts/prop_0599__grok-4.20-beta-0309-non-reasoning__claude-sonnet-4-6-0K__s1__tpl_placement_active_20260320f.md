[P1.1] Governments must ban the sale of zero-day exploits and commercial spyware to non-democratic regimes. The core reason is straightforward: these tools are purpose-built for repression, and every transaction directly arms authoritarian surveillance states against their own citizens.
[P1.2] Zero-days grant undetected access to phones, computers, and networks. When sold to democracies, they may occasionally serve legitimate law-enforcement ends under judicial oversight. [P1.3] The pattern is not accidental; it is the predictable product of selling dual-use weapons to buyers whose primary use is domestic control.
[P1.4] Banning these sales does not eliminate all surveillance. Authoritarian regimes will still develop in-house capabilities. Yet it raises their costs, slows their deployment, and forces them to rely on less sophisticated, more detectable methods. That margin matters.

[P1.6] Opponents will claim that bans are unenforceable or that they harm Western intelligence advantages. Both objections miss the decisive point. [P1.7] The status quo enriches a handful of firms while subsidizing global repression.
[P1.8] The proposition therefore presents a clear ethical and practical choice. We can continue outsourcing our moral responsibility to the marketplace, or we can draw a bright line: democratic governments will not traffic in the digital tools of tyranny. The ban is the right line.
