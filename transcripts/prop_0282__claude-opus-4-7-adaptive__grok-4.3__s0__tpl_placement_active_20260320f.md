[P1.3] My case rests on three points.
[P1.4] First, the harm is real, repeated, and one-sided. [P1.5] Second, the existing guardrails are obviously insufficient. Defamation law protects the living; the dead cannot sue. Privacy torts are weak against "matters of public record." Industry self-regulation amounts to a content warning card.

[P1.7] Third, stronger rules are tractable, not utopian. Notification and consultation requirements for surviving family before dramatization. [C1.1] True-crime entertainment often advances public knowledge rather than merely trading on grief. Cases that reach popular formats frequently highlight investigative failures, procedural errors, or overlooked suspects, prompting fresh tips, reopened files, or external pressure on police departments that had moved on. [C1.2] Legal constraints centered on family consent would hand private parties veto power over public events. A murder or abduction that occurred in a public space or affected community safety is not private property once it enters the record. Requiring approval before dramatization creates selective silence: stories involving wealthy or litigious relatives might vanish while others persist, turning coverage into a privilege rather than a societal tool.
[C1.3] Existing remedies already address clear abuses. Defamation suits, rights of publicity for the living, and contract negotiations when families choose to participate cover verifiable harms without new legislation that must then define “exploitation” across thousands of cases each year. Adding layers of mandated consultation or revenue sharing invites endless disputes over whose account of the crime is definitive.
[C1.4] Market incentives also restrain the worst practices more effectively than anticipated rules. Audiences quickly abandon productions that fabricate events or cross into obvious sadism; sustained demand rewards accuracy and context. Families who object retain the ability to speak publicly, file complaints, or boycott—outcomes that have altered later seasons and distribution choices without statutory intervention.
[C1.5] Broader speech protections matter more than perfect victim comfort. Once government can regulate entertainment about real crimes on ethical grounds, the same logic extends to other disputed histories. The burden lies on the proposition to show why current tort and market mechanisms have failed so comprehensively that fresh legal machinery is required.

[C2.2] The distinction between journalism and entertainment collapses under scrutiny. Treating those sequences as a separate category simply creates a licensing hurdle that smaller outlets cannot clear.
[C2.3] Market response already incorporates family objections without statute. When relatives publicly criticize a production, the resulting backlash shapes distribution, sequels, and future casting decisions, as audiences reward projects that appear less callous. This feedback loop operates faster than any regulatory process and preserves the incentive to cover the case in the first place.
[C2.4] Existing remedies are not inert. Public statements, press appearances, and organized campaigns by families routinely shift narratives and reduce the commercial value of the most sensational retellings. Creating a new cause of action for "exploitation" would instead empower the most vocal relatives to shape the historical record while leaving quieter families with no additional benefit.
[C2.5] The claim that current law leaves victims defenseless overlooks that no property right in public events exists precisely because society retains an interest in examining how crimes occur and how systems respond. Adding consultation mandates does not close a loophole; it transfers the power to decide which crimes receive sustained attention from producers and viewers to private parties who hold no special qualification on accuracy or public value.
