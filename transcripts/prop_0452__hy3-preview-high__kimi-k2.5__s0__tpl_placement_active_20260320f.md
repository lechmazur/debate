[P1.1] India’s demographic dividend is a non-negotiable deadline. Over 10 million people enter the workforce annually, most with only basic formal education. Manufacturing is the only sector capable of creating millions of stable, middle-income jobs at that scale: services demand advanced degrees, agriculture is already overstretched with low-productivity labor. Rapid manufacturing growth is not a preference, it is an existential requirement to avoid mass unemployment, social instability, and a wasted generation.
[P1.2] Reliable, affordable power is the backbone of that growth. India’s grid cannot run 24/7 export-oriented factories on intermittent renewables alone, not when grid-scale battery storage remains prohibitively expensive for mass use, and new nuclear capacity takes over a decade to deploy. Existing coal assets are already capitalized, operate at moderate capacity today, and supply the cheapest baseload power available—far cheaper than new gas plants or utility-scale battery arrays. Retiring them early would spike industrial power costs by 2-3x, or force constant outages that make Indian manufacturers uncompetitive against peers in Southeast Asia and China.
[P1.3] Delaying coal retirement is not a rejection of climate goals, but a pragmatic sequencing of priorities. India’s current coal fleet is its most efficient; replacing it early with imported gas or subsidized renewables would drain foreign exchange, raise production costs, and stall the manufacturing boom entirely. Compare this to peer developing economies: Vietnam’s rapid manufacturing expansion relied on expanded coal capacity to keep power costs low, and it only began retiring coal assets once export earnings from factories funded large-scale solar rollout.
[P1.4] The tax revenue and export earnings from rapid manufacturing will fund faster renewable adoption later, once storage technology matures and costs fall. You cannot build a green transition on a foundation of mass unemployment and stagnant growth. Prioritize manufacturing now, keep existing coal running to power it, and transition cleanly once the economy can afford to.
