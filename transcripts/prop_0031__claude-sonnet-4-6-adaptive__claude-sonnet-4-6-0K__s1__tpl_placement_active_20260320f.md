[P1.2] That inversion is the entire debate.
[P1.3] Density bonuses sound like reform but function like gatekeeping. When the underlying zoning still permits only a single house by right, adding a conditional bonus does almost nothing to actual housing production. Developers, small builders, and homeowners respond to what they can build by right, not what they might be permitted to build after navigating a discretionary process. Minneapolis abolished single-family zoning citywide and saw permit applications accelerate because the legal uncertainty evaporated.
[P1.4] Bonuses don't remove that uncertainty—they institutionalize it.
[P1.5] The equity argument is equally decisive. Single-family-only zoning has historically served to keep lower-income households and communities of color out of high-opportunity neighborhoods. A full ban means a small landlord can build a backyard cottage or convert a garage unit without hiring a land-use attorney.
[P1.6] CON will argue that local character, infrastructure capacity, or community input requires preserving some single-family districts. [P1.7] The choice is structural. Bonuses keep the ceiling low and call it progress. A ban removes the ceiling. Only one of those actually solves the problem.
