[P1.1] The United States cannot decarbonize without a grid that moves clean power from where it is generated to where it is needed. This veto power is not democracy; it is a structural bottleneck that sacrifices the national interest to parochial convenience. Permitting reform must break this logjam by prioritizing speed over local control, because the climate clock is running and the costs of delay are measured in gigatons of carbon, not just dollars.
[P1.2] Local vetoes create a tragedy of the commons. A single county can block a 500-mile line that would deliver wind power from the Plains to East Coast cities, yet that county bears none of the reliability or climate consequences of its decision. The result? Projects like the Grain Belt Express, which would carry 4,000 megawatts of wind energy, have spent over a decade tangled in lawsuits and rejections by counties that represent a fraction of the beneficiaries.

[P1.4] Speed does not mean recklessness. Federal siting authority can still require environmental reviews, route alternatives, and community compensation. But it must set a firm deadline: if a project meets baseline criteria, it moves forward. The alternative is paralysis.
[P1.5] Germany’s Energiewende shows what happens when local vetoes dominate: transmission expansion lags years behind schedule, forcing costly workarounds like curtailing renewable generation. We cannot afford that outcome. The Inflation Reduction Act has unleashed a wave of clean energy projects, but without transmission, they will sit idle. Prioritizing speed is not about ignoring communities; it is about recognizing that the scale of the climate crisis demands a grid built at the speed of necessity, not the pace of town halls.
