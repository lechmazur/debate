[P1.1] Farmland is not a neutral asset class. It is the physical substrate of our food supply, and the social substrate of rural communities. When we let it be treated like any other yield-bearing security, we get predictable pathologies — and the case for a cap follows directly from those pathologies.
[P1.2] First, price distortion. When pension funds, sovereign wealth vehicles, and private equity chase farmland as an inflation hedge, they bid against working farmers who have to make the land pay for itself through actual production. A young farmer borrowing at commercial rates cannot outbid an institution that is satisfied with a 3% real return plus appreciation. The result is a one-way ratchet: land leaves the hands of operators and accumulates in portfolios.
[P1.3] A cap preserves a competitive bidding pool in which farming families can still win.
[P1.4] Second, resilience. Food-system resilience depends on diversity — diverse operators, diverse crops, diverse tenure arrangements. Institutional owners rationally push toward monoculture cash crops, standardized leases, and absentee management, because that is what scales inside a fund. A countryside of 10,000 owner-operators fails gracefully; a countryside owned by a dozen funds fails all at once when those funds rebalance, get margin-called, or decide almonds beat alfalfa this decade.
[P1.5] The 2008 financial crisis should have taught us what happens when a critical asset class is concentrated in leveraged hands.
[P1.6] Third, community. Absentee institutional ownership hollows out rural towns — fewer local input purchases, fewer kids in schools, fewer property taxes reinvested locally, weaker stewardship of soil and water because the manager's horizon is the fund's horizon, not a generation's. This is not romanticism; it is observable wherever tenancy flips to distant ownership.
[P1.7] A cap is a narrow, targeted tool. It does not ban investment, ban leasing, or freeze markets. It simply says: beyond some threshold, the food system stops being a vehicle for financial accumulation. That is a conservative, pro-market intervention to keep a strategic sector from capture.
[P1.8] The burden is on CON to explain why that guardrail should not exist.
