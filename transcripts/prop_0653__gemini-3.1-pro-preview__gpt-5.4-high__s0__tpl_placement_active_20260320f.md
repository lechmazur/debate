[P1.1] Workplace evaluation must be based on what employees actually do, not what a machine guesses they might do. The right to organize is protected by law, but AI tools designed to detect "unionization likelihood" allow companies to bypass these protections. By analyzing communication patterns, badge swipes, or social interactions, AI can flag potential organizers before a formal union drive ever begins. Management can then quietly sideline, transfer, or terminate these individuals under the cover of ordinary business operations.
[P1.3] The same dynamic applies to predicting a worker's chance of quitting. If an algorithm labels an employee a "flight risk," the rational corporate response is to deny them promotions, mentorship, and critical projects. [P1.4] Second, predicting future productivity creates toxic self-fulfilling prophecies built on flawed proxies. AI cannot measure human potential; it relies on historical data and behavioral correlations that frequently encode bias. If a model predicts an individual will have low future productivity—perhaps penalizing a worker for taking legally protected medical leave or having caregiving responsibilities that limit after-hours emails—management inevitably anchors on that negative prediction. The worker is subsequently micromanaged, subjected to heightened scrutiny, and denied developmental opportunities.
[P1.5] Deprived of support, their performance inevitably drops, conveniently "proving" the algorithm right.
[P1.6] We cannot allow the workplace to become a predictive panopticon where workers are judged and penalized for statistical shadows of their future selves.
