[P1.1] Conventional age restrictions treat tobacco like alcohol: set a minimum age, enforce point-of-sale checks, and hope for the best. But this framing misunderstands the problem. The vast majority of lifelong smokers begin before they turn twenty-one. Age restrictions address the very window where compliance is lowest, peer pressure is highest, and enforcement is weakest.
[P1.2] Once a person crosses the legal threshold, the restriction vanishes entirely. You get addicted at twenty-one and legally purchase cigarettes for decades, generating enormous public health costs along the way. The restriction doesn't fail because it sets the wrong age—it fails because it creates a binary cliff that does nothing for anyone already past it.
[P1.3] A generational ban replaces that cliff with a ramp. It says: if you were born after a certain year, tobacco will never be legally sold to you, at any age. New Zealand pioneered this approach, proposing to raise the purchasing age by one year, every year, until an entire generation grows up without legal access. This doesn't criminalize existing smokers or confiscate anyone's supply.
[P1.4] It simply ensures that the pool of people for whom tobacco is a legal purchase shrinks steadily, year after year.
[P1.5] The logic is straightforward. Tobacco is uniquely lethal among consumer products legally sold to adults. Roughly half of long-term users die from tobacco-related causes. Unlike alcohol, where moderate consumption poses limited risk, there is no safe level of regular smoking.
[P1.6] When a product kills one in two of its habitual users, standard age-gating is an absurdly gentle intervention. We don't apply the same regulatory logic to anything else with that lethality profile.
[P1.7] Generational bans also attack the social infrastructure that sustains smoking. As each cohort ages out, the normality of seeing peers smoke, bumming cigarettes at a party, or purchasing tobacco as a casual habit erodes. Fewer smokers in a cohort means fewer people modeling the behavior for the next generation. This is a self-reinforcing cycle in the right direction.
[P1.8] Age restrictions leave the door open forever. Generational bans close it, one birth year at a time.
