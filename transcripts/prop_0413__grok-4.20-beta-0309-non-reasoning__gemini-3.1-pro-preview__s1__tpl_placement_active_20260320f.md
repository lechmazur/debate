[P1.1] Cities should invest more in low-cost social infrastructure such as libraries, recreation centers, and late-hour public spaces than in app-based loneliness interventions because durable, place-based institutions create genuine belonging while apps deliver fleeting distraction that often deepens isolation.
[P1.2] Physical spaces work through repeated, low-stakes encounters that build trust organically. A patron returning weekly to the same library branch sees the same staff and neighbors; those micro-interactions compound into familiarity and eventual friendship. Recreation centers host leagues, classes, and open gym hours that force people into shared activity rather than solitary scrolling. Late-hour public spaces—lit parks with benches, 24-hour reading rooms, or community cafés—solve the precise timing problem of modern loneliness: evenings and weekends when people feel most cut off.
[P1.3] These venues require no download, no subscription, no digital literacy. They are inherently democratic.
[P1.4] App-based interventions, by contrast, privatize the solution. They route connection through screens, algorithms, and corporate incentives that reward engagement over outcome. Users swipe, match, then ghost; the platform profits from the churn. The mechanism is isolating by design: people remain in private bedrooms performing sociality rather than inhabiting a shared public world.
[P1.5] When the app fails to deliver real relief, the user’s sense of personal deficiency grows, reinforcing the very loneliness it promised to fix.
[P1.6] Cost seals the case. Renovating or extending hours at existing libraries and centers is dramatically cheaper than subsidizing app licenses, data plans, or perpetual software updates for an entire population. The infrastructure already exists; the marginal dollar stretches further when spent on staffing, lighting, and programming than on venture-backed digital products whose business models collapse when public funding dries up.
[P1.7] The proposition therefore presents a clear choice: invest in the civic skeleton that holds communities together across decades, or outsource belonging to tools that monetize its absence. Physical infrastructure wins on efficacy, equity, durability, and cost. Cities that choose apps over bricks are choosing theater over remedy.
