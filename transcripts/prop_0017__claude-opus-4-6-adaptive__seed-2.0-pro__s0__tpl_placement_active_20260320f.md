[P1.1] When someone tells you that your medical choice violates your dignity, notice what has just happened: a third party has decided they understand your body and your suffering better than you do. That substitution is not a defense of dignity—it is its erasure.
[P1.2] The motion asks about *personal* medical choices. That word does all the philosophical work. The moment a medical decision is personal, the only dignitary judgment that is morally relevant is the patient's own. When dignity is invoked *against* a patient's autonomous choice, it ceases to be a first-person moral claim and becomes a vehicle for external control.
[P1.3] The structure of that argument is paternalism, whatever it calls itself.
[P1.4] Consider what dignity appeals actually do in contested medical cases. They have been used to deny assisted dying to patients in documented, irreversible suffering—telling them that a death they are actively choosing is beneath them. They have been used to justify forced psychiatric treatment against patients' explicitly stated wishes. In an earlier era, they underwrote compulsory sterilizations, with institutions sincerely claiming they were preserving social dignity.
[P1.5] In each case, the person invoking dignity was not the person bearing the consequence. That should disturb us.
[P1.6] Bodily autonomy, by contrast, locates moral authority where the stakes actually land—in the body that bears the pain, the side effects, the recovery, or the dying. It is not merely one value to weigh against dignity; it is the precondition for dignity having any personal meaning at all. A conception of dignity that can be enforced against your will over your own body is just domination with better vocabulary.
[P1.7] The decisive comparison is this: bodily autonomy gives the individual the final word on decisions that are irreducibly theirs. Dignity appeals give that word to someone else. In personal medical choices, the first is a principle. The second is a pretext.

When dignity is correctly understood as the inherent worth of the individual, it sets the non-negotiable floor for all medical choices, not a secondary value to be weighed against autonomy.
[C1.4] Bodily autonomy is a valuable tool to protect dignity, but it can never carry more moral weight than the value it exists to serve.
