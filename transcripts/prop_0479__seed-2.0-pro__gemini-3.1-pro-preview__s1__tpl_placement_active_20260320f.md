[P1.1] Right now, major corporations routinely label themselves “net-zero” without cutting a single ton of their own emissions, buying cheap, often fraudulent carbon offsets to cover 100% of their footprint. A major airline might pay pennies per ton for credits tied to a forest that was never slated for clearing, while expanding its fleet of fossil fuel-powered planes and passing off green branding to eco-conscious customers. This isn’t just misleading—it actively slows the global transition away from fossil fuels, because companies face no pressure to make the permanent, systemic changes required to hit global climate targets.
[P1.2] In-value-chain emission cuts are the only reliable, high-integrity path to decarbonization. When a food producer switches its delivery fleet to electric vehicles, or a steel manufacturer invests in green hydrogen furnaces, those reductions are permanent, verifiable, and drive spillover benefits: they bring down the cost of green technology for smaller firms, create local green jobs, and eliminate the risk of reversal that plagues most offset projects, from forest fires that wipe out preserved land to double-counted credits sold to multiple buyers.
[P1.3] Capping offsets to a small share of allowed emissions for net-zero claims strikes the exact right balance. It lets companies use offsets for the narrow slice of emissions that are truly technically or commercially unavoidable today, while forcing them to prioritize the core, in-house cuts that are non-negotiable for long-term climate progress. There is no other way to stop widespread greenwashing while keeping net-zero claims credible for consumers, investors, and regulators alike.

[C1.3] This legal cap ignores the economic reality of decarbonization. For a tech company, cutting in-house emissions is straightforward. For aviation, shipping, or heavy manufacturing, the necessary technologies—like commercial-scale green hydrogen—are decades away from economic viability. If we legally cap offset use to a "small share," these hard-to-abate companies will not magically invent zero-emission fleets overnight.
[C1.4] Instead, they will simply abandon their net-zero pledges. PRO’s rigid limit turns "net-zero" from a pragmatic tool for immediate climate action into an impossible purity test that drives corporate money entirely out of the fight.
[C1.5] Furthermore, PRO ignores the role offsets play in global climate equity. The carbon market is one of the only mechanisms successfully funneling billions of dollars in private capital directly to the Global South. When corporations buy high-integrity offsets, they finance tropical reforestation, shut down coal plants early, and build renewable infrastructure in developing economies—projects that desperately lack other funding sources. A legal limit on offsets chokes off this vital financial lifeline.
[C1.6] We must ruthlessly police offset fraud, but restricting the volume of legitimate offsets a company can buy is fundamentally counterproductive. Capping offset use delays total global emissions reductions, destroys voluntary corporate climate action, and defunds critical green infrastructure in the developing world.
