[P1.1] Standardized IQ-style testing operates on a flawed premise: that a few hours of abstract puzzles can quantify human potential. They capture a dangerously narrow band of cognitive ability, yet wield disproportionate power over life trajectories. When schools and employers rely on these scores as primary gatekeepers, they systematically exclude capable individuals whose strengths lie outside the tested spectrum. The convenience of a single number never justifies such high-stakes exclusion.
[P1.2] Real-world success demands far more than pattern recognition and working memory. It requires creativity, ethical reasoning, social collaboration, and resilience in the face of ambiguity. None of these critical competencies are measured by a cognitive battery. A student might score average on fluid reasoning but possess exceptional artistic vision or leadership drive.
[P1.3] By sorting them into lower tracks based on a narrow metric, the system squanders talent that a broader assessment would reveal. In employment, a high scoring candidate might solve logic puzzles quickly but fail to navigate team dynamics or adapt to shifting market needs.
[P1.4] Furthermore, the outsized role of these tests reinforces existing inequalities rather than meritocracy. Performance correlates heavily with socioeconomic status, reflecting access to preparation resources rather than innate capacity. When a single number determines university admission or hiring eligibility, it codifies privilege under the guise of objectivity. Organizations mistake familiarity with test formats for raw intelligence, confusing privilege with potential.
[P1.5] The convenience of a standardized score does not justify the collateral damage of missed potential and disparity.
[P1.6] The specific slice measured by IQ-style tests is too thin to bear the weight of major life decisions. Efficient sorting requires multidimensional evaluation that values practical intelligence alongside abstract reasoning. Until screening mechanisms account for creative problem-solving and emotional regulation, their current dominance remains unjustified. The cost of false negatives—talented people rejected—is too high to maintain the status quo.
[P1.7] We must demand assessments that match the complexity of the roles they fill.
