A limited right to explanation means something modest but vital: the person affected can learn the main reasons for the decision, the kinds of data that mattered, and how to challenge a material mistake. [P1.2] It requires enough intelligibility for accountability.
[P1.3] That matters because automated systems do not just make decisions faster; they make hidden errors and hidden bias harder to detect at scale. A patient can be deprioritized because the system uses past spending as a stand-in for need, punishing people who historically received less care.
[P1.4] Without an explanation, the person cannot correct bad data, cannot show unfair proxy use, and cannot even tell whether the law was violated. Rights against discrimination and arbitrariness become ornamental if the reason for the denial is sealed inside a black box.
