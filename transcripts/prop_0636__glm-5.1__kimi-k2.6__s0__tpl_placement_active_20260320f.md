Legacy content predating the standard can be retroactively signed by trusted custodians—archives, news organizations, platforms—creating a verified provenance chain from that point forward. The distinction that matters isn't between "has metadata" and "doesn't"; it's between content whose chain is intact and verifiable, and content whose chain is broken or absent.
[P4.2] Over time, as adoption normalizes, new synthetic content lacking provenance becomes the anomaly worth scrutinizing.
[P4.3] A2: C2PA relies on a chain of trust rooted in certificate authorities, not self-attestation. A bad actor can generate a manifest, but they cannot forge credentials from recognized issuers—Adobe, Reuters, a verified platform—any more than a counterfeiter can produce a valid TLS certificate for a bank. The system would display *who* signed the content. A deepfake bearing a manifest from "unknown-entity-452" carries precisely the credibility that label deserves: near zero.
[P4.4] Weaponizing the mandate would require compromising the certificate infrastructure itself, a fundamentally different and far harder attack than today's trivial deepfake distribution.
[P4.5] These answers expose the core imbalance in CON's case. Every technical objection they raise—fragility, legacy content, spoofing—already has architectural responses within the C2PA framework. CON demands guarantees that no disclosure system can provide, then concludes we should abandon preventive labeling entirely. [P4.6] CON keeps rephrasing the same complaint: the mandate isn't perfect. But perfection isn't the bar—improvement over the baseline is. Cryptographic signing creates detectable tampering, forces bad actors into harder attack surfaces, and shifts default assumptions so synthetic content must actively shed its markers rather than slip through invisible.
