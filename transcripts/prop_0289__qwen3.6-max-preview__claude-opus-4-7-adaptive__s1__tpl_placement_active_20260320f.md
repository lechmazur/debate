[P1.1] Robotaxis are not merely private luxury services; they are emerging transit infrastructure that operates on publicly maintained roads, leverages publicly funded mapping data, and benefits from regulatory exemptions. When a technology scales to replace or supplement municipal mobility, equitable access stops being optional and becomes a baseline condition of operation. Requiring fleets to maintain reasonably similar reliability in low-income and low-demand neighborhoods prevents the automation of transit inequality.
[P1.2] The mandate does not demand identical profitability or force operators into ruinous routes. It requires comparable reliability—meaning wait times and cancellation rates that do not systematically strand residents who lack car ownership. This mirrors established utility frameworks: telecommunications, electricity, and legacy transit networks all rely on internal cross-subsidization to guarantee baseline service. Fleets can meet this standard through predictive rebalancing, zone-based pricing adjustments, and integrating low-demand trips into existing deadhead routes.
[P1.3] The operational friction is manageable; the alternative is cementing a two-tier mobility system where algorithmic efficiency becomes a proxy for digital redlining.
[P1.4] Unchecked margin optimization will inevitably bypass communities that need reliable transport most, worsening job access, healthcare attendance, and local commerce. The opposition will claim that service obligations crush margins and delay rollout. Yet robotaxi companies already depend on public rights-of-way, municipal data streams, and liability frameworks shaped by regulators. That public footprint creates a reciprocal duty.
[P1.5] If fleets are permitted to cherry-pick only lucrative downtown corridors, they externalize their infrastructure costs while privatizing the gains. A reliability mandate simply internalizes that social contract. It ensures autonomous mobility scales as inclusive infrastructure, not as a gated service that leaves vulnerable neighborhoods further behind.

[C1.1] The proposition sounds humane, but it gets the sequencing exactly backwards and misreads what robotaxis actually are.
[C1.2] First, the utility analogy collapses on contact. Electricity and landline telephony justified universal service because they were natural monopolies with captive customers and guaranteed rate-of-return pricing. Robotaxis are a competitive, pre-profit, capital-burning service competing with buses, bikes, used cars, Uber, and walking. Cross-subsidy works when you control the market.
[C1.3] Mandate it on a fragile new entrant and you don't get equity—you get withdrawal. The likely response to a strict reliability parity rule is not heroic deployment in underserved zip codes; it's the operator declining to launch in that city at all, or pulling back to one wealthy enclave they can fully cover. Low-income riders end up with nothing instead of something.
[C1.4] Second, "reasonably similar reliability" hides enormous cost asymmetry. Low-density neighborhoods mean longer deadhead miles, fewer chained trips, and worse vehicle utilization. Forcing parity isn't trimming margin—it's mandating loss-making operations during the exact phase when scaling depends on reinvested revenue. Every dollar spent satisfying parity in year two is a dollar not spent on the fleet expansion that would organically reach those neighborhoods in year four.
[C1.5] Third, there is a better tool already available: targeted public subsidy. If a city wants robotaxi coverage in a low-demand neighborhood, it can contract for it, voucher riders, or partner with the operator the way transit agencies contract paratransit. That directly serves the equity goal, makes the public cost visible and democratically accountable, and doesn't smuggle a tax through a reliability metric. PRO's framing pretends the choice is mandate or redlining.
[C1.6] The real choice is mandate versus subsidy—and subsidy is honest, scalable, and doesn't risk service collapse.
[C1.7] Finally, the "public footprint" argument proves too much. Every business uses roads. That cannot, by itself, generate a duty to operate at a loss in specified geographies.
[C1.8] The path to coverage is growth and procurement, not parity decrees.
