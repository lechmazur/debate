[P1.1] Cultural extinction is irreversible. When a language dies, millennia of ecological knowledge, medical practices, and unique philosophical frameworks vanish forever. Treating preservation as a private luxury rather than a public responsibility commits us to a grotesque lottery where survival depends on whether donors find a culture photogenic enough to fund, or whether its speakers live near wealthy universities.
[P1.2] Markets and charities fail here because cultural preservation is a classic public good. The benefits—linguistic diversity, historical continuity, collective human knowledge—accrue to everyone, yet private actors cannot capture these returns. Without public funding, we get a hierarchy of memory: well-connected communities with slick crowdfunding campaigns survive while isolated indigenous groups with urgent needs but weak networks disappear. This is not preservation; it is aesthetic gatekeeping dressed as philanthropy, systematically privileging visible minorities over marginalized ones.
[P1.3] Standing public funds solve this through democratic accountability and institutional scale. Unlike project-based charity that forces communities to reapply annually with ever-shifting criteria, endowment-style financing permits systematic, decades-long documentation. It funds not just recordings but living transmission—trained teachers, childcare, ceremonial spaces—alongside climate-controlled archives that outlast political cycles. Charity fluctuates with economic downturns and trending hashtags; extinction does not pause for recessions.
[P1.4] Outsourcing this duty to private actors also reinforces colonial power dynamics. It treats minority communities as supplicants begging for cultural survival rather than citizens entitled to public resources. When governments fund preservation as core infrastructure—like roads or libraries—they recognize that linguistic diversity strengthens the polity itself, enriching education, medicine, and legal systems for all. A Māori language revitalization program funded as public education produces fluent professionals; one funded by sporadic grants produces anxious grant-writers.
[P1.5] The question is not whether we can afford these funds, but whether we can afford to gamble our collective memory on the whims of charity.

They do not have to submit to state audits, conform to majoritarian educational standards, or fear devastating funding reversals when a newly elected administration inevitably shifts its political priorities.
