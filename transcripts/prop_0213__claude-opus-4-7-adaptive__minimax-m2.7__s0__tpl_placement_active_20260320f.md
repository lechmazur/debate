[P1.1] Broad amnesties don't buy stability; they rent it, and the interest compounds. When a society tells victims "forget what happened to your daughter so the generals will leave quietly," it builds the new order on a fault line. Truth commissions with public testimony are the better default because they do three things amnesty cannot.
[P1.2] First, they establish a shared factual record. Authoritarian regimes and armed factions survive on competing myths — the disappeared were "subversives," the massacre was "crossfire." Public testimony, cross-referenced and entered into the official record, strips those myths of deniability. Once a society has heard mothers describe what was done in its name, future demagogues cannot easily rebuild the lie.
[P1.3] South Africa's TRC and Argentina's investigations, whatever their limits, made denial socially expensive in a way Spain's pacto del olvido never did.
[P1.4] Second, they restore moral standing to victims. Amnesty-for-silence is not neutral; it is a second injustice, in which the state tells the tortured that their suffering is a bargaining chip the powerful have spent. Public testimony reverses that. It treats victims as citizens with a claim on the public record, which is the minimum condition for them to re-enter political life as equals rather than as wounds the state wants closed.
[P1.5] Third, they discipline future power. Elites calculating whether to repress next time weigh the odds of exposure. A norm of public truth-telling raises that expected cost; a norm of amnesty lowers it. Amnesty teaches the next strongman that if he kills enough people, he'll be too dangerous to prosecute — exactly the lesson we do not want taught.
[P1.6] Note what PRO is not claiming. Not that every commission succeeds, not that prosecutions must always follow, not that transitions are risk-free. The motion says "usually prefer," and the comparison is against broad amnesties that trade silence for stability. That trade is a bad one: it purchases short-term quiet by mortgaging legitimacy, deterrence, and the dignity of the very citizens the new order needs.
