[P1.1] Signing a residential lease is often the largest financial commitment an individual makes annually, yet it remains one of the most opaque transactions in the modern market. Prospective tenants view a staged unit and are expected to commit to twelve months of residence without any substantive knowledge of the living environment’s actual history. This imbalance of information is inherently predatory.
[P1.2] Landlords possess exclusive, longitudinal data regarding their properties. They know if the unit above hosts revolving short-term rental guests who turn the hallway into a nightclub. They know if the lobby is a magnet for package thieves, rendering mail delivery impossible. They know if the walls are thin or the ventilation systems vibrate, causing chronic noise disturbances.
[P1.3] When a landlord conceals these persistent, property-specific issues, they are not merely marketing an apartment; they are selling a set of expectations that they know, with certainty, cannot be met.
[P1.4] Requiring disclosure is not about policing every minor grievance; it is about establishing a basic standard for "quiet enjoyment," a principle already central to contract law. If a building suffers from recurring party complaints or systemic theft, that is not a fleeting inconvenience—it is a material defect in the service being sold. Forcing tenants to discover these problems only after moving in creates immense personal and financial hardship. It traps them in a cycle of anxiety, wasted money, and the legal quagmire of lease-breaking, which often carries heavy, unfair penalties.
[P1.5] Critics might argue that such mandates are burdensome, but disclosure is a simple administrative task. It requires only the honest documentation of persistent problems the landlord already knows exist. If a landlord is confident in their property, a disclosure statement poses no threat. If they fear a disclosure would lose them a tenant, then that disclosure is precisely the information the tenant needs to avoid a fraudulent transaction.
[P1.6] We must treat housing with the transparency consumers demand in every other significant market.
