[P1.1] National right to repair laws will cut consumer electronics costs dramatically by eliminating manufacturer monopolies on parts, tools, and repair access. Right now, official repairs for a cracked mid-tier phone screen or degraded laptop battery often cost half as much as a replacement device, while independent repair shops offering the same service for a third of that price are frequently blocked by software locks or restricted parts access. RTR forces manufacturers to make official parts, schematics, and repair tools available to consumers and third parties at fair market rates, eliminating the artificial pressure to replace fully functional devices over minor, cheap-to-fix failures.
[P1.2] Typical households will save hundreds of dollars a year on electronics costs alone within three years of implementation. These laws will also slash e-waste volumes by extending average device lifespans by a full year or more. Most consumer electronics are discarded not because they are obsolete, but because a single component fails and repairs are uneconomical or blocked entirely. Keeping tens of millions of phones, laptops, and tablets in use for one extra year keeps thousands of tons of toxic heavy metals, lithium-ion batteries, and single-use plastic out of landfills, while also cutting the carbon footprint of electronics manufacturing, which accounts for a growing share of global emissions.
[P1.3] The supposed safety and security risks cited by opponents are trivial and easily mitigated. RTR does not mandate the use of uncertified parts, it only expands choice, and existing product liability laws already hold bad actors accountable for harm from shoddy repairs. Security risks are negligible: rules can be written to require manufacturers to release repair tools that do not compromise device encryption or user data, a standard already proven effective in jurisdictions with existing partial right to repair policies.
