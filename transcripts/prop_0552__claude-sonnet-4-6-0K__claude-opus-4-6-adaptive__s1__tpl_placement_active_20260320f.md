[P1.1] The global electronics industry generates tens of millions of tonnes of discarded devices every year, and a substantial fraction of that waste travels—legally or through deliberate mislabeling as "secondhand goods"—to communities in West Africa, South Asia, and Southeast Asia. What happens there is not recycling in any meaningful sense. Workers, often children, burn cable insulation to recover copper, dissolve circuit boards in open acid baths, and inhale fumes laden with lead, mercury, cadmium, and brominated flame retardants. The burden of wealthy countries' consumption falls on the lungs and bloodstreams of people who had no say in the transaction.
[P1.2] The core argument for a ban is straightforward: when disposal is profitable to export, domestic recyclers cannot compete, and the true environmental and health costs are simply transferred rather than solved. A ban changes that calculus. It forces the cost of responsible processing back onto the economy that generated the problem, creating real market pressure for certified recyclers, design-for-disassembly manufacturing, and producer take-back schemes. Every jurisdiction that has introduced extended producer responsibility—the EU's WEEE framework is the clearest example—has demonstrated that domestic processing is achievable when dumping is no longer the cheaper alternative.
[P1.3] Critics argue a ban would deprive lower-income countries of affordable electronics and economic activity. That argument conflates distinct things. Selling functional secondhand devices raises living standards; shipping end-of-life toxics for backyard burning does not. A well-drafted ban targets material that has reached end-of-life status, not functional reuse.
[P1.4] The distinction is enforceable through audit and certification.
[P1.5] The alternative—voluntary guidelines, informal "green" corridors, reliance on receiving countries to police imports—has had decades to work. The evidence is the ongoing existence of places like Agbogbloshie. Voluntary frameworks without export prohibition have failed structurally, not accidentally.
[P1.6] The proposition is a correction of a long-running injustice. The question is whether we continue externalizing costs onto communities with the least power to refuse.
