[P1.1] Political campaigns should not be allowed to peer inside voters’ heads. When a campaign infers that you are anxious, grieving, or temperamentally hostile, then serves you emotionally tailored ads designed to trigger precisely those vulnerabilities, it is no longer conducting politics—it is conducting psychological operations. The proposition bans exactly this: behavioral micro-targeting rooted in inferred psychological or emotional profiles.
[P1.2] The harm is structural, not merely aesthetic. Democracy depends on persuasion in a shared public sphere. A campaign might tell anxious voters that crime is collapsing while telling fearful voters in the same district that crime is spiking, not because either claim is central to its platform, but because the algorithm calculated each message would maximally activate the targeted psyche. This fragments the electorate into isolated emotional echo chambers and eliminates the common factual baseline that makes collective judgment possible.
[P1.3] Moreover, this form of targeting is inherently covert. Voters know when they see a billboard; they do not know when an ad is engineered for their specific inferred neuroticism or recent grief. Because the profiling is hidden, the manipulation cannot be resisted through ordinary critical scrutiny. The voter is outmatched by data scientists and clinical-scale psychographic models wielded by campaigns with enough money to buy opacity.
[P1.4] Opponents will likely claim this is just better tailoring of political speech. But there is a categorical difference between choosing to attend a rally and having your deepest emotional seams algorithmically mined so a campaign can stress or soothe you into compliance. One treats citizens as participants; the other treats them as instruments.
[P1.5] Prohibit the practice. Democracy requires citizens who are spoken to, not psychologically hacked.

[C1.3] A Spanish-language ad about family reunification "infers" emotional connection to immigration. Once you prohibit messaging keyed to inferred psychological states, you are not banning a creepy fringe practice; you are banning tailored political communication itself, and you are inviting regulators and courts to decide which inferences about voters' inner lives are permissible. That is a serious speech problem, not a footnote.
[C1.4] Second, the ban hits the wrong actors. Campaigns are the most regulated, most disclosed, most accountable speakers in the ecosystem. Prohibit them, and you do nothing to super PACs, foreign influence operations, partisan media, viral influencers, or churches and unions doing turnout. [C1.5] PRO's "psyops" concern actually gets worse.
[C1.6] Third, micro-targeting has democratic upsides PRO ignored. It is how underfunded challengers reach niche constituencies without buying $5 million Super Bowl ads. Ban it, and politics tilts further toward incumbents, billionaires, and broadcast-era name recognition.
[C1.7] Fourth, the real harms PRO names—covertness, contradictory messaging, manipulation of vulnerable people—are addressable without prohibition: ad transparency libraries, disclosure of targeting criteria, limits on sensitive data categories, platform rules against deceptive content. [C1.8] The clash is simple. PRO needs prohibition to be the only fix. It isn't, and it backfires.
