[P1.1] Blasphemy laws do something unusual in a legal system: they punish speech not because it harmed a person, but because it wounded an idea. Once a state accepts that premise, three failures follow—predictably, across very different cultures.
[P1.2] First, they cannot be neutrally enforced. Every religion contains claims that directly contradict every other religion. A Christian saying Muhammad is not a prophet blasphemes Islam; a Muslim denying the Trinity blasphemes Christianity; an atheist offends both. So blasphemy law in practice always collapses into majoritarian protection—it shields the dominant faith and criminalizes minorities and dissenters.
[P1.3] Pakistan's experience is the textbook case: the law is used overwhelmingly against Ahmadis, Christians, and Shia, and mere accusations have triggered lynchings. But you see milder versions of the same dynamic wherever these laws exist.
[P1.4] Second, they destroy the reform mechanism inside religions themselves. Every major tradition has been renewed by people its contemporaries called blasphemers—Luther, the early Christians under Rome, Sufi mystics, Reform Jews, liberal Muslim theologians today. Criminalizing offense freezes orthodoxy at whatever the current majority happens to believe and punishes the believers trying to push their own tradition forward. The supposed protection of the sacred is actually a protection of a particular clerical establishment.
[P1.5] Third, the "public order" justification inverts responsibility. It tells citizens: if you riot over a cartoon, we will punish the cartoonist. That is a standing invitation to the most easily offended faction to set the limits of everyone else's speech. The heckler's veto becomes law.
[P1.6] Any serious commitment to public order should punish the people throwing bricks, not the people drawing pictures.
[P1.7] The CON side will appeal to social cohesion and respect. But respect cannot be coerced into existence by prosecutors; what coercion produces is fear, self-censorship, and grievance. Majorities may genuinely want these laws. That is exactly when rights matter—when the vote would go the other way.
[P1.8] Abolish them.
