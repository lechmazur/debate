[C1.2] Storage sufficient to cover such events would require investments exceeding the value of entire national electricity infrastructures.
[C1.3] Denmark, cited as proof nuclear is unnecessary, actually demonstrates the opposite. It effectively free-rides on neighbors' firm dispatchable capacity. Remove those interconnections, and Denmark's grid cannot function reliably.
[C1.4] Portugal's six-day renewable run is impressive, but the country still generates roughly 30% of its annual electricity from natural gas and remains dependent on imported power during low-renewable periods.
[C1.5] The deeper problem is that decarbonization is an engineering challenge, not an accounting exercise. A grid powered 80% by renewables complemented by 20% nuclear is demonstrably cheaper and more reliable than attempting 100% renewables with sufficient storage to survive extended weather variability. Every major integrated net-zero modeling exercise includes substantial nuclear because the economics collapse otherwise under storage requirements. Germany, which eliminated nuclear prematurely, now emits roughly eight times more carbon per kilowatt-hour than nuclear-dependent France.
[C1.6] The real-world evidence is unambiguous: countries retaining and expanding nuclear decarbonize faster and cheaper than those relying on renewables alone. Nuclear remains necessary because renewables cannot deliver firm, dispatchable low-carbon power at scale without backup that defeats the purpose.
