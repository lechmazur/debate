[P1.1] Democracies face a structural problem with emergency powers: they expand during crises but rarely contract afterward. The proposition solves this by requiring automatic sunset hearings and renewal votes—not ending emergency powers, but forcing democratic deliberation about their continuation.
[P1.2] The historical pattern is clear and alarming. Emergency measures enacted after 9/11, from surveillance authorities to detention policies, were sold as temporary expedients yet persist decades later. The USA PATRIOT Act's provisions have been repeatedly renewed with minimal debate. Egypt's "temporary" emergency law lasted thirty-one years.
[P1.3] Hungary's COVID emergency powers became a vehicle for consolidating one-party rule. Each time, the logic is identical: grant broad authority now, trust that normal oversight will return later. It rarely does.
[P1.4] This isn't conspiracy—it's institutional self-interest. Executives naturally prefer broad discretion to legislative constraint. Once emergency powers exist, leaders face strong incentives to maintain them and weak incentives to surrender them. Without structural mechanisms forcing reconsideration, the default becomes perpetuation.
[P1.5] The proposition addresses this through a simple, elegant mechanism: automatic expiration dates with mandatory legislative hearings and votes for renewal. This doesn't handicap crisis response—executives can still act swiftly when genuine emergencies arise. But it ensures that six months or a year later, the legislature must publicly affirm continuation, the executive must justify the ongoing necessity, and citizens can hold their representatives accountable for that choice.
[P1.6] The alternative—trusting executives to voluntarily narrow their own powers or legislatures to proactively revoke them—ignores how political incentives actually work. Legislatures struggle to reclaim authority they've already surrendered. Executives don't surrender power they've grown accustomed to wielding.
[P1.7] Sunset mechanisms don't prejudge whether any particular emergency measure is justified. They simply guarantee that democracies cannot sleepwalk into permanent emergency governance. That isn't constraint on legitimate executive action—it's the minimum democratic hygiene required to ensure temporary powers remain genuinely temporary.
