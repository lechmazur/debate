[P1.1] Congestion pricing is a half-measure dressed up as bold policy. It manages car traffic; it doesn't transform cities. The proposition before us is that major cities should go further — banning private cars from central districts entirely. That position is correct, and here is why the rest of this debate should turn on three interlocking points.
[P1.2] First, the physical logic is decisive. Cars occupy enormous space relative to the people they move. A single traffic lane cycling private vehicles carries a fraction of the passengers that a bus lane, cycle track, or widened footway can move in the same footprint. Congestion pricing reduces the number of cars modestly; a ban removes the structural problem.
[P1.3] Oslo, Barcelona, and parts of central London have already pedestrianized significant zones, and the pattern is consistent: air quality improves, street injuries fall, and retail foot-traffic rises once people aren't navigating exhaust and fast-moving metal.
[P1.4] Second, congestion pricing is a wealth filter, not a solution. It keeps wealthy drivers on the road and prices out lower-income residents who may depend on a vehicle. A ban applied with proper alternatives — frequent buses, protected cycling, car-share hubs at the district boundary — distributes mobility more equitably. The charge just shuffles who suffers congestion; the ban restructures how the space works for everyone.
[P1.5] Third, climate and public-health targets require structural change, not price signals alone. Urban transport is a significant emissions source, and incremental demand suppression through tolls has repeatedly proven insufficient to hit stated targets. A ban creates the irreversible infrastructure investment signal that buses, trams, and cycling networks need to become genuinely reliable.
[P1.6] The opposition will argue congestion pricing is more flexible and politically realistic. That is the argument for incrementalism when the problem is structural. The question is not what is easiest to pass today, but what cities actually need. The answer is a ban, backed by transformed public transport.
