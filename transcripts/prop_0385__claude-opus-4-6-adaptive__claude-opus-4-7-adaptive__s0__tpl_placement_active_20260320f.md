[P1.1] The case for abolition rests on a simple principle: no legal system should punish speech solely because it offends a religious sensibility, regardless of how many people share that sensibility. Majorities do not get to veto fundamental rights, and the history of blasphemy laws proves exactly why.
[P1.2] First, blasphemy laws are inherently discriminatory in practice. They protect whichever faith holds political power. In Pakistan, blasphemy charges fall overwhelmingly on religious minorities—Ahmadis, Christians, Hindus—not on the Muslim majority whose beliefs the law nominally guards. In Europe, historical blasphemy statutes were enforced almost exclusively to shield Christianity.
[P1.3] The structure of these laws guarantees unequal treatment because "blasphemy" is defined by the theology of the dominant group. Abolition is the only remedy that treats all citizens equally.
[P1.4] Second, the public-order justification is dangerously circular. It says: "People will riot if you insult their religion, so we must criminalize insult to prevent riots." This rewards the threat of violence with legal protection and incentivizes mobs to escalate. A state that criminalizes speech to appease potential rioters has outsourced its legal code to whoever is most willing to be violent.
[P1.5] Genuine public-order tools—laws against incitement to imminent violence, riot control, criminal threats—already exist and target the actor who threatens harm, not the speaker who provoked feelings.
[P1.6] Third, these laws chill far more than crude insults. They suppress theological dissent within religions, silence reformers, and block academic inquiry. Secular critics, ex-believers, feminist theologians, and satirists all self-censor under regimes with active blasphemy prosecution. The chilling effect is not hypothetical: individuals have faced imprisonment and even death sentences under such statutes for social media posts, casual remarks, or academic writing.
[P1.7] Finally, the democratic-majority argument cuts against itself. Rights exist precisely to constrain majorities. If majority support were sufficient to criminalize expression, no speech protection would be stable. The proposition asks whether these laws should exist—not whether they are popular.
[P1.8] Popularity is not legitimacy. Abolition protects everyone, including future religious minorities the current majority cannot foresee.
