[P1.1] The proposition asks something modest but important: when it is genuinely possible, build elder care around the home rather than defaulting to the institution. I want to defend that on three grounds—wellbeing, economics, and what "default" actually means.
[P1.2] First, wellbeing. Aging is, among other things, the slow loss of familiar things: routines, neighbors, a kitchen you know in the dark, a garden, a dog. Institutions, even good ones, replace that texture with linoleum and a shift schedule. The evidence we have on cognitive decline, depression, and even mortality after relocation into facilities is not flattering, and the mechanism is intuitive: dislocation accelerates frailty.
[P1.3] Home care preserves identity, which is the thing dementia and aging are already eroding.
[P1.4] Second, economics and capacity. Round-the-clock institutional beds are expensive—staffing, real estate, regulatory overhead—and most developed countries are facing a demographic wave that no realistic facility-building program can absorb. Supporting a spouse, an adult child, or a part-time aide in the existing home leverages housing stock and unpaid care that already exists. Even when public money pays for home aides, day programs, and home modifications, the per-person cost is typically a fraction of a nursing-home placement.
[P1.5] Defaulting to institutions is the expensive option, not the safe one.
[P1.6] Third, what "default" means. The motion is not "ban nursing homes." It is: when home care is feasible, organize the system to support it. That matters because today many systems do the opposite—funding flows easily to facilities and grudgingly to home aides, respite care, or caregiver leave.
[P1.7] Families end up institutionalizing a parent not because it is best, but because that is where the subsidy lives. Flipping the default forces the harder question to be asked case by case: what does this person actually need?
[P1.8] CON has to defend something stronger: that we should keep pushing older adults into facilities even when home care would work. That is the position that needs justifying, and I don't think it can be.
