[P1.1] GDP was invented in the 1930s as a wartime accounting tool. Simon Kuznets, who helped build it, warned explicitly that it should not be confused with welfare. Nearly a century later, governments still crown it king. The proposition asks us to correct that category error.
[P1.2] The case rests on three claims.
[P1.3] First, headline targets shape behavior. Treasuries, central banks, and cabinets optimize what the nightly news prints at the top. If the headline is GDP, every policy gets a free pass as long as the aggregate number goes up — even when the gains accrue to a narrow slice, even when they come from depleting forests, overworking nurses, or paying to clean up disasters GDP also counted on the way in. A headline target that rewards an oil spill twice (once for the drilling, once for the cleanup) is a broken compass.
[P1.4] Well-being indices — health, secure employment, education, environmental quality, social connection — measure what citizens actually asked government to deliver.
[P1.5] Second, well-being is not fuzzy anymore. New Zealand, Scotland, Wales, Finland, Iceland and Bhutan have all built practical dashboards. They combine objective indicators (life expectancy, child poverty, air quality, housing affordability) with survey-based life satisfaction. These are no less measurable than GDP, which itself is a model full of imputations, seasonal adjustments, and revisions years after the fact.
[P1.6] Third, the swap changes real decisions. A GDP-first government approves a motorway that boosts output and ignores the asthma ward downwind. A well-being-first government weighs both on the same dashboard and often picks differently — insulating homes instead of subsidizing fuel, funding mental health instead of another stadium. The point is not that growth is bad; it is that growth is an input, not the goal.
[P1.7] Treating the means as the end is how you get rich countries with falling life expectancy and collapsing trust.
[P1.8] Make the scoreboard measure what we actually want. The policies will follow.
