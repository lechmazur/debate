[C1.2] Start with who actually pays. Extended Producer Responsibility schemes don't make corporations absorb costs out of profit margins. Companies pass costs downstream. When a beverage producer faces a new per-unit recycling fee, the price of a twelve-pack rises.
[C1.3] The family buying store-brand soda—not the shareholders—bears that burden. Framing this as "making producers pay" obscures that consumers are the ones writing the check, while the administrative machinery to collect and redistribute those fees grows in the middle.
[C1.4] The design-incentive argument is similarly overstated. Producers already respond to market pressure to cut packaging costs; lighter, cheaper packaging reduces shipping expense and material spend. The idea that companies are currently choosing expensive, wasteful designs because they externalize disposal costs ignores how intensely competitive packaging decisions already are. Adding a recycling levy on top doesn't automatically translate into innovative sustainable materials—it often just adds a compliance cost that large incumbents can absorb while small competitors cannot.
[C1.5] The countries-with-higher-recycling-rates comparison [P1.4] mistakes correlation for causation. Nations with strong recycling cultures also have denser populations, shorter collection distances, different consumer habits, and robust public investment in infrastructure. Recycling rates reflect system design broadly, not producer-funding specifically. Cherry-picking bottle-return deposit schemes—which are narrow, product-specific programs quite unlike broadly shifting "much more" cost to producers—doesn't validate the sweeping proposition being argued here.
[C1.6] Finally, the proposition sets no principled limit. "Much more" is a rhetorical anchor that smuggles in unlimited expansion. At some threshold, the policy collapses recycling into a tax regime that funds general government rather than actual waste management.
[C1.7] The real question isn't who pays on paper. It's what system produces the best waste outcomes at the lowest total social cost. That requires nuance the proposition forecloses.
