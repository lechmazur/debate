[P1.1] The historic center of Venice now has fewer residents than cruise ship passengers on any given summer day. Barcelona's Gothic Quarter has become so inundated that locals organize protests against tourist buses. These are not accidents of geography—they are policy failures. When cities funnel millions of visitors into the same few square kilometers during peak months, they sacrifice livability, economic equity, and sustainability.
[P1.2] The resident displacement effect is documented across overtouristed cities worldwide. In tourism-concentrated districts, landlords convert long-term housing to short-term rentals, businesses shift from serving locals to serving visitors, and everyday services disappear. The result: residents who built these neighborhoods cannot afford to remain in them. This is not an inevitable consequence of tourism—it is the direct outcome of concentrating demand instead of distributing it.
[P1.3] Spreading tourism across neighborhoods and seasons directly addresses this harm. When visitor spending reaches outer districts, new businesses emerge serving both tourists and residents. When tourism extends into shoulder seasons, year-round employment replaces precarious seasonal work. Distributed tourism creates distributed benefits rather than concentrating gains among a few landlords and established operators in historic cores.
[P1.4] Infrastructure tells a similar story. Concentrated districts face sewage overflows, waste management crises, and accelerated heritage degradation from foot traffic that historic streets were never designed to handle. The same number of visitors, distributed thoughtfully, would strain no single area beyond capacity.
[P1.5] The objection that tourists want only famous attractions misunderstands the appeal. Visitors seek authentic experiences—and authenticity lives in working-class neighborhoods, local markets, and seasonal rhythms, not in overcrowded museum queues. Spreading demand improves the visitor experience while preserving what makes cities worth visiting.
[P1.6] Cities face a choice: manage tourism proactively through spatial and temporal distribution, or watch beloved districts become unlivable theme parks. The proposition is not anti-tourism—it is pro-city.
