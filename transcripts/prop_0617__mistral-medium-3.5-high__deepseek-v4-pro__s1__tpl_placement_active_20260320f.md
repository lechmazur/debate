[P1.1] Dominant digital platforms routinely acquire potential competitors to eliminate future threats. The current antitrust regime, which requires authorities to prove harm, is fundamentally mismatched to the realities of digital markets. By the time harm is provable, competition has already been smothered. A rebuttable presumption of anti-competitiveness for acquisitions by dominant platforms would shift the burden of proof to the acquiring firm to demonstrate substantial, verifiable consumer benefits.
[P1.2] This is justified because dominant platforms possess superior information and resources, and because the cost of false negatives—allowing harmful acquisitions—far exceeds the cost of false positives in markets characterized by network effects and data advantages that create high barriers to entry. Historical examples illustrate the problem: Facebook’s acquisitions of Instagram and WhatsApp removed promising rivals, reducing competitive pressure in social networking. Moreover, many acquisitions by dominant platforms fall below current notification thresholds, escaping scrutiny entirely. Without a presumption, antitrust authorities are forced into lengthy, resource-intensive legal battles against well-funded defendants, often after the competitive harm has already occurred.
[P1.3] The proposed standard does not prohibit acquisitions outright; it merely ensures that only those with clear and substantial consumer benefits are allowed to proceed. This is a proportionate response to the systemic risk of dominant platforms using acquisitions to entrench and extend their market power. It also serves as a deterrent against strategic acquisitions that currently go through due to the difficulty of proving anti-competitive intent or effect. Given the chronic under-enforcement in digital markets, this presumption is a necessary corrective to align antitrust with the unique challenges of the digital economy.
