[P1.1] Place identity is not a logo unveiled at a press conference. It is the accumulating weight of shared stories, rituals, and creative expressions that residents recognize as *theirs*. The question before us is which institutional investment strategy actually builds that identity—and the answer is clearly recurring, creator-led local events.
[P1.2] Three arguments.
[P1.3] **First, identity requires repetition.** A place becomes recognizable through traditions people can anticipate, return to, and pass down. New Orleans didn't become New Orleans because of a single spectacular occasion; it became New Orleans through generations of Mardi Gras, Jazz Fest, and second-line parades rooted in local creative communities. Recurring events create temporal landmarks that structure how residents experience belonging. A one-off mega-event, however dazzling, is a parenthesis—not a sentence in the city's ongoing story.
[P1.4] **Second, creator-led events anchor identity in authentic local culture rather than imported spectacle.** When festivals grow from the vision of local artists, musicians, food artisans, and cultural practitioners, the resulting identity is distinctive and hard to replicate. Mega-events like world expos or global sports tournaments import standardized formats, corporate sponsors, and international consultants. The host city becomes interchangeable staging. Edinburgh's Fringe—born from local performers who were excluded from the official festival—is now more synonymous with Edinburgh's identity than any single prestige event the city has hosted.
[P1.5] Creator leadership produces cultural specificity; corporate mega-event templates produce generic spectacle.
[P1.6] **Third, the economics favor sustainability.** Recurring community events distribute spending across years and local vendors. Mega-events concentrate spending in construction booms that leave behind underused venues and public debt. The identity benefit of a mega-event peaks during the event itself and decays rapidly; the identity benefit of a recurring festival compounds annually.
[P1.7] The CON side will likely point to visibility and global branding. But global attention that lasts two weeks is not place identity—it is advertising. Identity is what remains when the cameras leave. Public institutions should invest in what stays.
