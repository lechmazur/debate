[P1.1] Schools exist to ensure every graduate can function independently in society. [P1.2] The distinction between "core" and "optional" tracks what we consider essential versus enrichment. Literacy is mandatory because illiteracy cripples adult life; these life skills deserve identical status because their absence creates comparable vulnerability. A graduate who cannot comparison-shop for loans, patch drywall, or prepare food from raw ingredients faces predatory fees, expensive service calls, and chronic health costs that compound disadvantage across a lifetime.
[P1.3] Optional status produces systematic inequality. Affluent students absorb these skills through home osmosis or extracurriculars, while disadvantaged students graduate knowing how to analyze Shakespeare but not how to dispute a medical bill or cook dried beans. Schools explicitly exist to level such disparities; abandoning survival skills to the family lottery betrays that mission.
[P1.4] Embedding these as core guarantees a baseline: every student leaves able to feed themselves affordably, maintain their living space, and navigate financial systems without exploitation. That is the minimum threshold for an education claiming to prepare young people for life, not just for college admission.
