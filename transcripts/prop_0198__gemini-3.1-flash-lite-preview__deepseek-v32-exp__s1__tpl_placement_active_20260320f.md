[P1.1] The Constitution of the United States is clear: "The Congress shall have Power To lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises" and to regulate foreign commerce. This was a deliberate design choice. The Founders understood that trade policy is an extension of taxation and inherently political; it must be subject to the debate, compromise, and accountability of the legislative process, not the stroke of a pen in the Oval Office.
[P1.2] In recent decades, we have seen an alarming drift. Provisions intended for genuine national emergencies or wartime exigencies have been repurposed as convenient tools for broad-spectrum economic policy. When an administration bypasses Congress to impose sweeping tariffs, it does more than ignore the legislative branch; it destabilizes the economic foundation of the country. Businesses rely on certainty to invest, hire, and compete globally.
[P1.3] When tariff regimes can be fundamentally altered overnight by executive decree, that certainty vanishes. This creates a "lobbying-first" economy where industries compete not to innovate, but to extract favorable executive orders.
[P1.4] Furthermore, delegating this power creates a profound democratic deficit. Tariffs are taxes on consumers. They shift costs and redistribute wealth throughout the economy. When Congress imposes these costs, representatives must answer to their constituents for the price hikes, supply chain disruptions, or retaliatory trade wars that follow.
[P1.5] When the President does so unilaterally, that accountability is severed. The executive is insulated from the immediate political consequences of the economic pain they inflict, incentivizing rash policies that serve short-term political posturing over long-term national prosperity.
[P1.6] By reclaiming sole authority, Congress can restore the constitutional balance, demand rigorous economic evidence for tariff adjustments, and ensure that American trade policy is a product of democratic deliberation rather than executive whim. We must close the emergency loophole to ensure that the power to tax remains where it belongs: with the people’s representatives.
