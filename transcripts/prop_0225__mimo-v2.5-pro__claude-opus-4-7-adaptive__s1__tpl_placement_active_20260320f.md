[P1.1] Public universities exist to serve the public good. When taxpayer dollars fund degree programs whose graduates consistently cannot find work in their field, while critical national shortages go unaddressed, we have a misallocation problem demanding a policy response.
[P1.2] Every public dollar is a choice. When a state university expands enrollment in fields where most graduates end up underemployed or working outside their discipline, that dollar fails both the students it was meant to help and the society that provided it. The cost—to the economy, to public safety, to national competitiveness—is measurable and real.
[P1.3] This proposal does not eliminate any program. It calls for capping enrollment in low-employment fields and redirecting funding toward areas of demonstrated need. That is a calibration, not a shutdown. A university enrolling three hundred students in a field absorbing ten graduates per year can responsibly reduce intake while expanding programs where graduates are actively recruited.
[P1.4] Critics will invoke academic freedom and education's intrinsic value. These matter, but they do not require unlimited public subsidy at unlimited scale. A classics department thrives with thirty per cohort. It does not need funding for three hundred graduates into a market absorbing ten.
[P1.5] Academic inquiry continues; the subsidy simply becomes proportional to reality.
[P1.6] Universities have had decades to self-regulate and haven't. Administrative incentives reward enrollment volume, not graduate outcomes. Without external accountability, the pattern persists: students accumulate debt, taxpayers foot the bill, and employers cannot fill vacancies.
[P1.7] The status quo is not neutral. It actively harms students steered toward degrees with poor prospects—often from families who can least afford the financial setback. [C1.3] By the time a state agency identifies a "shortage," caps a humanities cohort, and pushes a freshman into the favored track, that student graduates four years later into conditions no one predicted. Caps lock in yesterday's forecast and call it strategy.
[C1.4] Second, "low-employment" is a manipulated metric. Graduates of philosophy, history, and English routinely out-earn many vocational majors by mid-career because employers buy writing, reasoning, and adaptability. [C1.5] Third, the proposal hits the wrong lever. America's nursing shortage is not caused by too many anthropology majors—it is caused by a shortage of nursing faculty, clinical placements, and licensure throughput. [C1.6] The redirection story is a non-sequitur dressed as efficiency.
[C1.7] Fourth, caps fall hardest on the students PRO claims to protect. Enrollment caps mean rationing, and rationed seats go disproportionately to applicants with polished applications and private resources. That is not opportunity; that is a tracked system with a public-good label.
[C1.8] The right response to mismatch is better information, expanded capacity in shortage fields, and student choice—not a planner with a clipboard deciding which minds the country needs.
