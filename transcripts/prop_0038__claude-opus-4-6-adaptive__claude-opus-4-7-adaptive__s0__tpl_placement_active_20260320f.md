[P1.5] **Equity demands action.** Noise exposure is not distributed randomly. Lower-income neighborhoods are disproportionately located near highways, rail yards, and industrial zones. [C1.3] **Second, the tools don't transfer.** Air pollution is a diffuse common-pool problem: particulates drift across a city, so only population-wide limits work. Noise is local, directional, and attenuates with distance. A jackhammer on one block is silent three blocks away. [C1.4] **Third, the binding-limits mechanism bites the wrong people.** Hard caps on construction noise don't make buildings quieter—they make them slower and more expensive, which in a housing crisis means fewer affordable units and longer displacement. The "equity" argument cuts the other way: aggressive enforcement has historically been used to push out exactly the bars, markets, and street vendors that serve working-class neighborhoods.
[C1.5] **Fourth, cities already regulate noise.** The real question is marginal: should we escalate to air-quality-grade urgency—emergency thresholds, automatic penalties, continuous binding monitoring? For a harm this localized and this variable, that machinery is overkill that will be captured by wealthy complainants and weaponized against construction and nightlife.
