Currently, many universities prioritize enrollment numbers, often at the expense of student success. By shifting the funding model, universities will be incentivized to focus on supporting students through to graduation and into successful careers.
[P1.2] This approach addresses the issue of institutions enrolling large numbers of students but failing to provide adequate support, leading to high dropout rates and significant student debt without corresponding increases in earnings potential. Universities that can demonstrate effective strategies for improving student outcomes will be rewarded with continued or increased funding, while those that fail to adapt will face financial consequences.
[P1.3] A key benefit of this system is that it encourages universities to be more responsive to the needs of the labor market. By linking funding to post-graduation earnings, institutions will be motivated to develop programs that are aligned with the demands of employers, thereby enhancing the employability of their graduates. [P1.4] Critics might argue that such a system could lead to universities prioritizing programs with immediate job prospects over those in the arts or humanities. However, this overlooks the fact that many of these fields still lead to viable careers, and universities can be incentivized to demonstrate the long-term value of their programs. By focusing on outcomes, we can create a more efficient and effective higher education system that benefits both students and taxpayers.

[C1.4] The institutions most serving disadvantaged populations—historically Black colleges, regional public universities, community colleges—would face funding cuts precisely because they take on the hardest work.
[C1.5] The earnings metric compounds these problems. [C1.6] Enrollment-based funding has flaws, but it preserves a crucial principle: universities should be accessible institutions serving diverse populations with varied goals.
