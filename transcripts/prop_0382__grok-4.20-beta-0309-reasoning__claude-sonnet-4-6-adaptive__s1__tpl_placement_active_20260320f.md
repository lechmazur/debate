[P1.1] Access to education must begin from the reality that learners arrive with diverse needs, not as uniform listeners in a quiet hall. Schools and universities should therefore operate under a clear presumption: provide captions, lecture recordings, and accessible formats by default. Withholding them requires a strong pedagogical justification, not mere habit or convenience. This standard directly serves the core purpose of education—transmitting knowledge to every enrolled student rather than only those who process information in one narrow way.
[P1.2] Students who are deaf or hard of hearing lose content without captions. Those with attention differences or language barriers regain control through recordings they can pause and replay. Even typical students benefit: reviewing lectures cements understanding before exams, and captions aid focus in noisy dorms or during commutes. The presumption removes the degrading requirement that learners first disclose disabilities, navigate paperwork, and wait weeks for basic access.
[P1.3] It replaces stigma with routine inclusion.
[P1.4] Critics may insist this burdens faculty or limits teaching freedom. The exception clause answers them: when spontaneity, privacy, or irreplaceable live interaction truly demands it, materials may be withheld. That safeguard is built in. What the presumption blocks is unexamined exclusion—decisions that quietly write off segments of the class without justification.
[P1.5] Technology now makes compliance inexpensive and fast, so continued resistance reflects inertia, not principle.
[P1.6] The result is better outcomes across the board. When access is automatic, engagement rises, retention improves, and instructors spend less time on individual fixes. Education becomes universal by design instead of retrofit. The presumption places the burden where it belongs—on the institution to prove exclusion is necessary—rather than on students to prove they deserve to learn.
[P1.7] This is the fair, effective default our schools should adopt.

[C1.1] The proposition sounds generous, but its operative word is "presumptively." That word does enormous quiet work, and it is where the case falls apart.
[C1.2] Existing law already requires institutions to accommodate documented disabilities. Students who need captions or alternative formats can and do receive them through established processes. The proposition doesn't just protect those students—it overrides instructor judgment across every course and every context unless the institution can mount a "strong pedagogical" defense. That reversal matters enormously.
[C1.3] Consider lecture recordings. There is substantial evidence that recording chills the kind of candid, exploratory discussion that defines the best education. In a seminar on trauma, addiction, or contested political history, students self-censor when they know their words are archived. The same applies in clinical training, law school negotiations, and counseling practicum courses.
[C1.4] The presumption tells every one of those instructors that spontaneous, trusting dialogue is forfeit unless they can satisfy some unspecified "strong" standard—defined by whom, enforced how? The proposition never says.
[C1.5] The PRO case treats this as solved by the exception clause. But "strong pedagogical reason" is an extraordinarily high bar, and in practice it would be adjudicated by administrators, not educators. That is an institutional veto over professional teaching judgment dressed up as a safeguard.
[C1.6] On cost, automated captions routinely fail on technical vocabulary, heavy accents, and multiple simultaneous speakers. Genuine accessibility requires human review. That is neither trivial nor uniform across thousands of courses at a single large university. Treating it as mere inertia misrepresents the reality.
[C1.7] Finally, the proposition bundles three distinct things—captions, recordings, and accessible materials—as if they carry identical trade-offs. They do not. Accurate captions for students with hearing loss are defensible by any standard. Universal lecture recording is a categorically different intervention, trading away classroom safety and pedagogical flexibility for one dimension of convenience.
[C1.8] The debate is not whether to serve disabled students. It is whether a blunt administrative presumption is the right instrument. It is not.
