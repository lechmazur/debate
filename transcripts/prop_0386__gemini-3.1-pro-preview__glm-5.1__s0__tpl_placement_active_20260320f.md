[P1.1] The medical profession is defined by a fundamental fiduciary duty: the patient's well-being must come first. While clinicians retain their constitutional rights, religious freedom must act as a shield to protect personal conscience, not a sword that inflicts harm on vulnerable individuals. Therefore, clinicians should only be allowed to refuse care on religious grounds when patients can seamlessly obtain timely, equivalent treatment elsewhere.
[P1.2] First, medical licenses grant clinicians a state-backed monopoly over essential, often life-altering services. Patients cannot legally access prescription medications or certain medical procedures without them. When a patient seeks help, they are in a state of dependency. If a clinician refuses care and no accessible alternative exists, that personal objection directly translates into a total denial of healthcare for the patient.
[P1.3] Second, the requirement for "timely" alternatives without "major burden" addresses the severe realities of healthcare access. In a well-resourced hospital, an alternative provider might be just down the hall. But in rural or underserved communities, a single refusal might force a patient to travel hundreds of miles or lose their window for care. For time-sensitive medical needs—such as emergency contraception, miscarriage management, or urgent interventions—delaying treatment is functionally equivalent to denying it altogether.
[P1.4] We cannot tolerate a system where a patient suffers acute health risks, financial penalties, or severe trauma simply because of the personal convictions of the only local provider.
[P1.5] Finally, the standard proposed by the resolution is already a highly accommodating compromise. It does not strip clinicians of their religious freedom. In fact, it protects conscientious objection in the vast majority of everyday cases where alternative care is readily available. It simply draws a necessary line: a clinician’s right to opt out of care ends at the exact moment it inflicts tangible harm or a severe burden on a patient.
[P1.6] When a clinician voluntarily enters the public marketplace of healthcare, they agree to serve the public. Personal conscience must never become an insurmountable barrier to a patient's medical survival.

[C1.2] Consider what this means in practice. A sole rural pharmacist who objects to emergency contraception on religious grounds? No right to refuse, because no alternative exists nearby. A doctor in an underserved community who cannot in good conscience participate in sterilization procedures?

[C1.4] The proper framework recognizes that both conscientious objection and patient access are genuine goods, but they must be reconciled at the systemic level, not by forcing individual clinicians to violate core beliefs. When we instead make the individual clinician bear the weight of systemic access failures, we produce moral injury, burnout, and the exodus of faithful providers from medicine—which worsens the very access problems the resolution claims to solve.
[C1.5] The proposition treats clinicians as interchangeable service-delivery mechanisms whose conscience rights evaporate the moment a patient cannot easily find someone else. This reduces religious freedom to a triviality: you may object only when your objection doesn't matter.
[C1.6] Real compromise means building systems where both values flourish—not crushing one whenever the system fails to provide alternatives.
