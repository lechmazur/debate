[P1.1] The gig economy runs on a fiction: that a worker glued to an app, denied the freedom to take other jobs or even log off without punishment, is somehow “off the clock.” That fiction allows platforms to collect the benefits of a ready workforce while pushing the cost of idle time entirely onto the worker. The motion simply says that if platforms exercise enough control to keep workers tethered to their apps, they must count that tethered time when checking whether pay meets the minimum wage.
[P1.2] Consider a delivery courier logged in for a full evening, subject to acceptance-rate quotas and priority algorithms that punish disappearing. During slow periods, she is not free in any meaningful sense. She cannot run another errand far from the restaurant cluster. She cannot take a competing job without risking deactivation.
[P1.3] She is waiting under the platform’s discipline, and that waiting is a direct input into the service the platform sells to customers. When a traditional employer requires a worker to remain on-site or on-call, labor law recognizes that constraint as compensable. Gig platforms have engineered a loophole by relocating the “site” to a smartphone, but the economic reality is identical.
[P1.4] The current model—paying only for “task time”—makes a mockery of minimum-wage guarantees. A platform can satisfy the letter of wage law while a worker spends half a shift earning nothing, effectively subsidizing the company’s surge capacity with unpaid labor. The proposition does not ask platforms to pay for sleep or hobbies; it asks them to pay for active waiting time on the app, the period when workers are available and constrained by platform rules. That is a floor, not a windfall.
[P1.5] Until platforms bear the cost of the labor they actually command, minimum-wage laws will protect a theory of work while failing the people who perform it.

[C1.1] The motion collapses the distinction between employment and independent contracting, treating a smartphone as if it were a factory time-clock. But gig workers are not captive employees; they are independent operators who voluntarily log in, log out, and simultaneously run multiple apps. A driver idling with Uber, Lyft, and DoorDash all open is not "tethered" to any single master—she is arbitraging opportunity costs across competing platforms. Forcing any one platform to pay for that time ignores that she earns from, or owes loyalty to, none exclusively.
[C1.2] It externalizes the cost of her multi-homing onto whichever platform happens to be open when the ping arrives.
[C1.3] Mandating minimum-wage compliance across waiting time would economically compel platforms to restrict labor supply to survive. If Uber must pay a driver minimum wage for every hour the app is open in a slow suburb, the rational response is to cap driver numbers, assign rigid shifts, or exit thin markets entirely. We have seen this dynamic before: when jurisdictions impose heavy payroll obligations on flexible work, platforms respond with scheduled blocks and waiting lists, converting flexible side-gigs into regimented part-time jobs with fewer total slots and higher barriers to entry.
[C1.4] The "active waiting" metric is also administratively unworkable. The line between waiting for a ping and browsing Netflix is invisible to auditors. Workers would rationally keep apps open during leisure hours to capture pay, forcing platforms to surveil phones or cut access, eroding the autonomy that defines gig work.
[C1.5] Most importantly, the proposition ignores the voluntary trade workers make. They exchange the security of guaranteed hourly wages for the freedom to reject fares, work sporadically around school or caregiving, and control their own schedules. Importing the wage-calculation methods of traditional employment destroys the very flexibility workers value, replacing it with a model where platforms must control worker behavior to justify costs—precisely the employment relationship the gig economy avoids.
[C1.6] If society wishes to bolster income, portable benefits or per-task supplements achieve that without dynamiting the structural flexibility that keeps millions in the labor market.
