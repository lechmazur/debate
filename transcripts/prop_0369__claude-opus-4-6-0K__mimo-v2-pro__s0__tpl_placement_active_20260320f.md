[P1.1] When a customer opens a food-delivery app hungry and in a hurry, the default architecture of that app shapes their spending more than their own preferences do. The proposition asks a simple question: should that architecture nudge people toward the most expensive fulfillment path, or toward cheaper alternatives like pickup? The answer is clearly yes—defaults should favor lower-fee options—for three reinforcing reasons.
[P1.2] **First, current defaults exploit well-documented cognitive biases to extract money customers would rather keep.** Decades of behavioral research confirm that most people stick with whatever option is pre-selected. Delivery apps know this. They pre-load the highest-margin fulfillment path—delivery with service fees, inflated menu prices, and optional tips already toggled on—because each click away from that default loses them revenue. This is not neutral design; it is a revenue-maximizing choice dressed up as convenience.
[P1.3] When the default is the most expensive option, the platform profits from inertia, not from informed consumer preference.
[P1.4] **Second, defaulting to pickup or lower-fee options is better for nearly every stakeholder.** Customers save money—often 30% or more per order once fees and markups are stripped away. Restaurants retain a larger share of revenue because they avoid commission rates that can reach 30% of order value. Workers at the restaurant keep their roles instead of watching margin flow to gig couriers. Even drivers benefit indirectly if the remaining delivery orders reflect genuine willingness to pay, improving tip culture and reducing unprofitable short-distance runs.
[P1.5] **Third, this is already how we regulate default architecture in analogous markets.** Retirement savings plans default workers into participation because we recognize that defaults carry enormous power. Regulators require airlines to show base fares before add-ons. The principle is the same: when a default predictably costs consumers more and primarily enriches the intermediary, shifting that default is a proportionate and well-precedented intervention.
[P1.6] The CON side must defend an arrangement where the platform's profit motive, not the customer's interest, sets the starting point of every transaction. That is a hard position to hold.
