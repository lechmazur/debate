[P1.1] The status quo fails both creators and competitive AI innovation. Right now, foundation model developers scrape billions of copyrighted texts, illustrations, photographs, and written works without consent or compensation. Most rightsholders, from independent graphic designers to midlist novelists to part-time stock photographers, cannot afford to sue deep-pocketed tech firms to recoup lost value, even when their work is a core input that makes model outputs commercially viable. Large rightsholders with dedicated legal teams can negotiate fragmented, bespoke deals, but smaller creators are locked out entirely, getting nothing for work that drives billions in annual AI revenue.
[P1.2] The status quo also cripples fair, accessible AI development. Small and open-source AI teams cannot afford the legal risk of potentially massive copyright damages, nor the administrative cost of negotiating separate licenses with thousands of individual rightsholders, ceding the market entirely to a handful of giant tech firms that can absorb those costs.
[P1.3] A compulsory licensing scheme solves both problems at once, using a proven model already deployed for music streaming, radio public performance, and cable broadcast retransmission. A neutral non-profit collective would set a standardized, scaled royalty rate based on model size and revenue, collect fees from model operators, and distribute payments proportionally to rightsholders whose works are included in training sets. Rightsholders get automatic, no-litigation compensation regardless of their size, while AI operators get full legal certainty to build and deploy models without the risk of ruinous copyright lawsuits, leveling the playing field for smaller developers and ensuring value flows to the people who create the core inputs AI depends on.

[C1.1] Compulsory licensing for AI training is a flawed solution to a misdiagnosed problem. The proposition assumes copyright law requires permission for every use, but fair use doctrine already permits transformative, non-expressive uses like AI training. Forcing payment where none is legally due creates artificial scarcity, raising costs for developers without compensating creators meaningfully. AI models don’t merely copy works—they analyze patterns to generate new content, a quintessential fair use.
[C1.2] Compulsory licensing treats this as equivalent to music retransmission, ignoring the fundamental difference between reproduction and transformation.
[C1.3] Consider the administrative burden: a neutral collective would need to track billions of works, identify rightsholders, and distribute royalties. Orphan works—millions of copyrighted pieces with unclear ownership—would create legal quagmires. Even with standardized rates, startups and open-source projects would face compliance costs rivaling those of litigation, cementing big tech’s dominance rather than leveling the playing field.
[C1.4] Worse, payments would be inequitable. The collective’s operational costs would consume a significant portion of fees, leaving crumbs for individual creators. A 2023 study found that music streaming’s compulsory licensing distributes less than 15% of revenue to artists; AI training would likely fare worse, with most funds going to intermediaries and large rightsholders. Moreover, proving which works influenced a model’s output is technically infeasible, making proportional distribution a fantasy.
[C1.5] Rightsholders would fight over scraps while the collective absorbs most revenue. Small creators—the very people PRO claims to help—would receive negligible checks, while the scheme legitimizes big tech’s scraping practices under a veneer of legality.
[C1.6] The real solution isn’t compulsory licensing—it’s clarifying that AI training is fair use. This promotes innovation without new bureaucratic layers, ensuring creators aren’t exploited while allowing technology to advance.
