[P1.1] When you purchase a car, you purchase the physical components within it. The proposition is simple: if the hardware is already installed, the manufacturer has no legitimate right to disable it remotely when you stop paying a subscription fee. This is not a debate about optional cloud services or streaming content; it is about whether a sale is truly a sale, or merely a rental disguised by a purchase price.
[P1.2] The core principle at stake is post-purchase control. When a consumer pays thousands of dollars for heated seats, advanced headlights, or driver-assistance hardware that sits physically in the vehicle, they have acquired the good. To later hold that hardware hostage behind a paywall is to retroactively convert a transfer of property into a perpetual lease without consent. This violates the basic expectation of ownership: that you may use the things you possess without ongoing tribute to the original seller.
[P1.3] Safety and reliability compound the urgency. Many subscription features involve hardware critical to vehicle operation—battery thermal management, braking assist systems, or lighting functions. If a subscription lapses or a company discontinues support, owners could find safety systems deactivated on vehicles they ostensibly own. This creates unacceptable liability and risk, transforming routine financial transactions into potential safety hazards.
[P1.4] Finally, this practice distorts secondary markets and stifles competition. A used car buyer cannot access hardware already built into the vehicle if the previous owner stopped paying, arbitrarily depreciating assets and trapping consumers in manufacturer ecosystems. Permanent hardware access preserves property rights, ensures safety resilience, and maintains honest commerce. The alternative is a dystopian future where you never truly own the objects surrounding you.
[P1.5] We must defend the line between selling products and renting them.
