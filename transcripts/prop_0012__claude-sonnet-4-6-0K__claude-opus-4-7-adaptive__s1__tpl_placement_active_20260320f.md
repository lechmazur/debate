[P1.1] The central question here is not whether science has value. It does. The question is whether scientific value can override the rights of specific human beings and their descendants over their own dead. It cannot.
[P1.2] When museums hold Indigenous remains, they are not holding abstract specimens—they are holding the bodies of identifiable communities' ancestors, taken overwhelmingly through grave robbery, colonial violence, and legal frameworks designed specifically to deny those communities any standing to object. The United States' own NAGPRA legislation acknowledged this history by mandating repatriation, recognizing that the original acquisition was wrong, not merely inconvenient. That wrongness does not become right because decades passed and researchers published papers in the meantime.
[P1.3] Researchers make the utilitarian argument: future techniques, future genomic analysis, future medical insight. This argument has no stopping point. It could justify holding any remains indefinitely, since science will always have a future. The logic proves too much, which means it proves nothing.
[P1.4] More importantly, it consistently places the burden on the bereaved to demonstrate that their grief outweighs speculative future benefit—a burden we impose on no other group. No European nation would accept an argument that their war dead should remain in foreign archives because bone isotope ratios might one day teach us about medieval diet.
[P1.5] The claim that repatriation destroys knowledge is also empirically suspect. Communities and researchers can and do collaborate on studies with community consent. Repatriation does not ban science; it requires consent. If researchers cannot secure consent, that is meaningful information about whether the study was ethically permissible to begin with.
[P1.6] The deeper issue is sovereignty. A people who cannot determine what happens to their ancestors' bodies cannot fully govern themselves. Every year of delay is another year in which that dispossession is actively maintained by institutions with resources, lawyers, and bureaucratic inertia on their side.
[P1.7] Repatriation is not sentimental. It is the concrete repair of a concrete injustice, and it should be prompt.

[C1.2] Consider what "prompt" actually forecloses. It forecloses the time needed to determine *whose* ancestors these even are. A great many remains in collections have contested or unknown cultural affiliation; multiple nations sometimes claim the same individual, and sometimes no living community claims them at all. A rule that says "return promptly, science be damned" does not resolve that problem—it papers over it, and risks handing ancestors to the wrong descendants, which is its own desecration.
[C1.3] Second, scientific study of human remains is not an abstract academic hobby. It has identified victims of massacres and boarding-school deaths and returned names to the unnamed. It has tracked tuberculosis, smallpox, and syphilis histories that shape medicine for living Indigenous populations today. [C1.4] Promptness-over-study would have killed those findings in the cradle.
[C1.5] Third, the PRO case quietly depends on a collaboration model—"consent-based science is still possible." Agreed. [C1.6] The motion and the collaboration ideal are in tension, not harmony.
[C1.7] Fourth, the right comparison is not "grief versus speculation." It is "a structured process that weighs descendant wishes, scientific stakes, and evidentiary needs" versus "a categorical imperative to move fast." [C1.8] Repatriation, yes. Promptness as a trump card over knowledge that can serve the living descendants themselves—no.
