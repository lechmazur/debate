[P1.1] A U.S. carbon border adjustment directly tackles the biggest flaw in a domestic-only strategy: carbon leakage. Strong subsidies and regulations can lower emissions at home, but if production simply shifts to countries with weaker standards, global emissions may stay the same or even rise. A border charge closes this loophole, ensuring that goods entering the U.S. market bear a cost for their carbon content regardless of where they are made.
[P1.2] This creates an immediate financial incentive for foreign producers to decarbonize. A U.S. version would amplify this, pushing major exporters like China and India to accelerate their own green transitions far faster than they would respond to U.S. domestic policies alone.
[P1.4] Thus, over the critical next five years, a border adjustment delivers greater global emissions cuts by both securing domestic reductions and actively driving decarbonization abroad—a double benefit that subsidies and regulations alone cannot match.

[C1.1] The proposition rests on a seductive but flawed premise: that a carbon border adjustment can be designed, implemented, and functioning effectively within five years. That timeline is the debate's central stress test, and the mechanism fails it badly.
[C1.2] Consider what implementation actually requires. A workable border adjustment must calculate the embedded carbon content of thousands of imported product categories, verify foreign producers' emissions data, survive WTO legal challenge, and withstand intense domestic political opposition from industries that rely on cheaper foreign inputs. The EU's carbon border mechanism took over a decade of negotiation and is still in a transitional phase with reporting-only requirements. The U.S. faces a more contentious political environment and a less cooperative trading relationship with its largest import sources.
[C1.3] Five years is not a reasonable deployment horizon—it is closer to the time needed just to finish the legislative and administrative architecture.
[C1.4] Meanwhile, domestic subsidies and regulations are already operational. The Inflation Reduction Act created real incentive structures—tax credits, manufacturing support, clean energy deployment—that are actively pulling investment into low-carbon sectors right now. These tools scale in real time, not in theory. Their global spillover through technology cost reduction is already measurable: as U.S. demand drives down costs for solar, batteries, and clean manufacturing, those technologies become cheaper for every country.
[C1.5] The PRO case treats carbon leakage as decisive, but the scale of leakage from U.S. domestic policy is far smaller than claimed. Most U.S. emissions reductions come from energy generation and transportation, sectors where production cannot simply migrate offshore. A steel mill cannot move to avoid American clean energy mandates; American power plants are not competing with Chinese ones.
[C1.6] The proposition must prove the border adjustment outperforms over the *next five years specifically*. A tool that spends most of that window in legal and administrative limbo while facing retaliatory trade measures cannot plausibly beat a functioning, already-deployed domestic program.
