[P1.1] Picture a family of four searching for flights. The screen shows $89 per person. They click through, comparing airlines, picking the "cheapest" option—and by checkout, after two checked bags and four assigned seats to keep their children next to them, the bill is $680, not $356. The comparison they made was fiction.
[P1.2] That is the problem this proposition solves: drip pricing, the deliberate practice of advertising an artificially stripped headline fare and revealing mandatory costs only after a consumer is psychologically invested. This isn't a quirk of the booking experience—it is a business model. Airlines have strong financial incentives to suppress the true price during comparison, because a lower number wins the click. The result is that consumers cannot make a genuine choice at the only moment that matters: when they are deciding between options.
[P1.3] The harm falls hardest on families. A solo traveler without checked bags can navigate basic fare advertising. A family cannot. They need bags.
[P1.4] They need assigned seats—not as a luxury, but because no parent can accept being separated from a six-year-old on a three-hour flight. These costs are not optional add-ons; they are practically mandatory. Treating them as surprises to be revealed at checkout is straightforwardly deceptive.
[P1.5] The market argument for transparency is equally clear. When airlines compete on artificially low base fares rather than all-in prices, the competition is distorted. Carriers that bundle more honestly appear more expensive. The market signal is broken, and honest operators are penalized for it.
[P1.6] Full-price disclosure fixes the competitive signal and rewards genuine efficiency rather than clever obfuscation.
[P1.7] The CON side will argue this is too complex to implement or that it restricts commercial freedom. Neither survives scrutiny. Regulators in the UK and EU have already pushed toward all-in fare display for flights, and booking engines already calculate totals at checkout—they simply choose not to show them sooner.
[P1.8] Transparency at the comparison stage is not a burden. It is the baseline condition for a functioning market.

[C1.6] The clash is this: PRO wants one universal "true price" displayed up front. But there is no universal trip. A rule that pretends otherwise trades a real and fixable information problem for a worse one—standardized prices that mislead a different set of travelers while crushing the unbundled fares budget flyers actually rely on.
