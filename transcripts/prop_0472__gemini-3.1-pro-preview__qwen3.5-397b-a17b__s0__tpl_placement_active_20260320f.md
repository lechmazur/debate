[P1.1] Standardized IQ-style testing operates on a fundamental mismatch: it uses a highly restricted measure of human cognition to make sweeping, high-stakes decisions about human potential.
[P1.2] These tests evaluate a specific subset of skills—rapid pattern recognition, spatial reasoning, and convergent thinking. They are entirely blind to creativity, practical problem-solving, cognitive flexibility, and interpersonal intelligence. While abstract logic is undeniably useful, it is merely one slice of the cognitive pie. Treating it as the definitive proxy for overall intellectual capability is reductive.
[P1.3] This narrowness becomes actively harmful when these tests play an outsized role in educational sorting. When schools use IQ-style assessments as the primary gatekeeper for gifted programs and advanced tracks, they penalize divergent thinkers. A student with exceptional creative problem-solving skills or deep analytical persistence, but average spatial rotation speed, is denied developmental resources. This artificially narrows our educational pipeline, systematically filtering out future innovators in favor of rapid test-takers.
[P1.4] The mismatch is equally glaring in employment screening. Modern workplaces do not require employees to sit in isolation and identify the missing shape in a matrix. Real-world professional success requires navigating ambiguity, collaborating across disciplines, and adapting to unforeseen, unscripted challenges. When employers rely on cognitive aptitude tests to blindly filter applicant pools, they often discard candidates who possess the precise practical and adaptive intelligence that modern, complex jobs actually demand.
[P1.5] We do not argue that the skills measured by IQ tests are irrelevant. Our position is that their current role is vastly disproportionate to what they actually measure. By elevating a narrow band of cognitive ability to the status of a universal sorting mechanism, we arbitrarily constrain opportunity. We end up building schools and workplaces optimized for excellent test-takers, at the direct expense of the versatile, creative problem-solvers the real world actually needs.
