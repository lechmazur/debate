[P1.1] The collapse of local journalism over the past two decades is the single most underrecognized driver of both political polarization and local public corruption, and targeted public funding with strict independent editorial firewalls fixes this crisis at far lower risk than the status quo already imposes. When local newsrooms close, ad-driven national partisan outlets fill the information gap for most voters.
[P1.2] Instead of coverage of local school board debates, zoning votes, or city contract awards, voters get fed national culture war talking points that frame all local issues as extensions of partisan combat. Towns that once voted across party lines for candidates who fixed potholes or funded parks now split along national partisan lines for every local race, even when those races have nothing to do with federal partisan priorities. That direct link between lost local news and higher local polarization holds across hundreds of U.S. communities.
[P1.3] Lost local news also removes the only consistent watchdog for low-level public corruption. Without a regular reporter attending city council meetings, small towns have seen millions in public funds embezzled, no-bid contracts awarded to elected officials’ family members, and environmental violations covered up for years before anyone notices. The strict editorial firewalls specified in the proposition eliminate the bulk of partisan capture risk: funding is allocated by independent, non-partisan boards with no authority to dictate editorial content, and outlets that take funding retain full control over their reporting.
[P1.4] The alternative status quo already has far higher partisan capture risk, with most surviving local outlets owned by out-of-state partisan conglomerates that force syndicated partisan content on readers instead of local reporting. The small, mitigable risk of any bad actor exploiting the funding system is trivial compared to the massive, ongoing harm of lost local journalism driving polarization and corruption every day.

[C1.1] The entire proposition rests on a single load-bearing assumption: that firewall governance actually works. Strip that assumption away and you have government-funded journalism with all the risks and none of the promised protections. The CON case begins there.
[C1.2] "Independent boards" are not independent. They are constituted through political processes—appointed by governors, legislative committees, or city councils. The people who design the funding architecture and choose the board members are the same people local journalism is supposed to hold accountable. That isn't a firewall; it's a permission slip with extra steps.
[C1.3] Whoever controls board composition controls, over time, which outlets receive funding, which get defunded, and which survive. Editorial independence doesn't last when the organization's budget depends on reappointment cycles.
[C1.4] Even without overt editorial interference, the funding relationship itself distorts coverage. Outlets dependent on public grants have powerful incentives toward self-censorship on the issues that matter most: public spending decisions, corruption by incumbent officials, the very people who hold the purse strings. The chilling effect doesn't require a phone call from a mayor. It operates through every editor's quiet calculation of what story might jeopardize next year's grant.
[C1.5] PRO claims private conglomerate ownership is just as bad. But private capture and government capture are categorically different. A partisan media chain can be sued, competed against, regulated, or simply ignored by readers who distrust it. Government capture converts public resources into incumbent protection, creates structural advantages for the party in power, and is extraordinarily difficult to reverse once entrenched.
[C1.6] Jurisdictions that have normalized public media funding—look at the pattern across Central and Eastern Europe—have found the mechanism nearly impossible to depoliticize once politicians discover its value.
[C1.7] Finally, audiences who already distrust government institutions will dismiss publicly funded outlets as state media on sight. That response doesn't reduce polarization. It adds a new axis to it: those who trust government news and those who call it propaganda. The firewall fixes nothing if the audience can't see it from the outside.
