[P1.1] Deep-sea mining proceeds without adequate environmental baselines because the International Seabed Authority has accepted contractor-supplied data that lacks independent verification and comprehensive coverage of the target zones. These baselines require systematic mapping of biodiversity, sediment stability, and water-column interactions across the full scale of proposed operations, yet most exploration data remains limited to narrow transects that miss rare species and ecosystem linkages.
[P1.2] Once collection vehicles disturb the seafloor, sediment plumes can travel tens of kilometers, smothering filter feeders and altering nutrient cycling in ways that current models cannot predict with confidence. Without pre-existing, independently confirmed reference points, regulators have no measurable standard to detect deviation or enforce restoration. This absence turns every mining license into an uncontrolled experiment whose results arrive after the habitat has already been removed.
[P1.3] The precautionary principle applies directly here because recovery timelines for nodule-associated communities exceed human planning horizons, and no restoration technique has demonstrated success at commercial scale. Pausing approvals until baselines exist therefore preserves the option to avoid harm rather than attempting to compensate for it later. Economic arguments for proceeding rely on projected mineral demand, yet those projections assume the marine environment can absorb the disturbance without systemic effects; absent verified baselines, that assumption rests on assertion rather than measurement.
[P1.4] Independent verification also prevents the conflicts of interest that arise when the same entities proposing extraction fund and interpret the environmental surveys. Until the Authority requires and secures such data from neutral scientific programs, any approval decision lacks the factual foundation needed to protect the common heritage of the seabed.
