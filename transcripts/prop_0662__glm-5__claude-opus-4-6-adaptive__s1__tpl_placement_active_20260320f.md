[P1.1] Automated video and personality assessments have spread through hiring with almost no regulatory guardrails. These tools ask candidates to record video responses analyzed by facial recognition software, or complete personality questionnaires scored by proprietary algorithms claiming to predict job performance. Governments should ban or heavily restrict them because they systematically discriminate, operate as unaccountable black boxes, and rest on pseudoscientific foundations that no job applicant should be subjected to.
[P1.2] The discrimination problem is severe and well-documented. Facial analysis algorithms consistently perform worse on darker skin tones and non-standard facial features. Voice recognition systems struggle with accents, dialects, and speech patterns common among immigrant communities. When hiring tools encode these technical failures, they systematically screen out qualified minority candidates while their employers remain blissfully unaware of the discrimination happening on their behalf.
[P1.3] A rejected applicant never learns that the algorithm docked points for their skin tone or speech cadence—how could they challenge a decision whose reasoning is concealed?
[P1.4] The opacity runs deeper. Vendors treat their scoring methodologies as trade secrets, meaning neither employers nor candidates can examine what traits are being measured or how they weight responses. A personality test might penalize introversion, neurodivergent communication styles, or cultural differences in self-presentation—all while claiming to assess "cultural fit" or "leadership potential." Without regulatory scrutiny, these tools operate as unchallengeable gatekeepers to employment.
[P1.5] Proponents claim efficiency, but efficiency for whom? Employers save time by offloading human judgment onto software, but candidates bear the cost of being treated as data points rather than people. The job market already tilts heavily toward employer power. Adding algorithmic screening compounds that imbalance with tools candidates cannot question, understand, or appeal.
[P1.6] No one should lose a job opportunity because an unvalidated algorithm flagged their facial expressions as "concerning" or their personality profile as "low potential." When hiring decisions affect livelihoods, they deserve human accountability—not black-box verdicts that cannot be contested. Governments must step in because market incentives push employers toward expedience over fairness.

[C1.2] Human hiring is catastrophically biased. Decades of research show that résumé screeners discriminate based on names, zip codes, and educational pedigree. If algorithmic tools are flawed, the baseline they replace is worse, and PRO never grapples with that comparison.
[C1.3] Automated assessments are improvable in ways human prejudice is not. When an algorithm exhibits demographic disparities, those disparities can be measured, audited, and corrected systematically. The very measurability PRO treats as damning is actually the strongest argument for these tools: you can detect and fix what you can quantify.
[C1.4] A ban does not protect vulnerable candidates—it locks them back into a system where a hiring manager's gut feeling is unchallengeable and invisible. [C1.5] Existing anti-discrimination law already applies to automated hiring tools. Employers using these systems are liable under disparate-impact doctrine. Illinois and New York City have enacted targeted transparency requirements—notice, consent, bias audits—without banning the technology. [C1.6] PRO's case conflates the worst possible implementation with the technology itself. The answer to bad algorithms is better regulation, not prohibition. Banning these tools surrenders the auditable, correctable future of hiring and retreats to the unaccountable past.
