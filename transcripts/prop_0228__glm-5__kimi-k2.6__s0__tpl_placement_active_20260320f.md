[P1.1] The gig economy has become a loophole masquerading as innovation. Companies like Uber, Deliveroo, and Grab have built business models on a simple premise: classify workers as contractors to avoid the costs of employment while maintaining near-total control over how work is performed. This is not genuine freelance work—it is employment stripped of its protections.
[P1.2] Consider the reality. A delivery driver receives her assignments through an algorithm, accepts them under threat of deactivation, works fixed hours determined by the platform, and has no meaningful ability to negotiate rates or build her own client base. She bears all the risks—vehicle maintenance, fuel, insurance, injury—while the platform captures the profit. If she gets sick, she has no paid leave.
[P1.3] If demand drops, her income collapses with no safety net. The platform calls her a partner; it treats her like an employee without the benefits.
[P1.4] This is not flexibility for workers—it is flexibility for capital. The current default lets companies shift costs onto individuals and, ultimately, onto public systems. When gig workers lack health insurance, emergency rooms absorb the cost. When they lack unemployment protection, social welfare systems fill the gap.
[P1.5] Taxpayers effectively subsidize corporate profit.
[P1.6] Reversing the default changes the calculus. Making employee status the norm forces companies to justify contractor classification rather than assume it. Narrow exemptions would still protect genuine freelancers—consultants, designers, professionals who truly set their own terms, work for multiple clients, and control their work. But the burden shifts to proving that independence exists in substance, not just in contract language.
[P1.7] The economic case is equally clear. Legitimate businesses that provide fair wages and benefits cannot compete against rivals who dodge those obligations by rebranding employees as contractors. This is a race to the bottom that erodes labor standards for everyone. Reclassification restores fair competition and ensures that the costs of doing business fall on businesses—not on vulnerable workers or the public systems that catch them when they fall.

[C1.1] Forced reclassification would not rescue gig workers; it would erase the jobs many of them depend on. The proposition imagines a workforce of disguised full-timers begging for stability, but reality is messier. Millions drive, deliver, and freelance precisely because they reject the rigid schedules, exclusivity clauses, and capped hours of traditional employment. Students fitting shifts around lectures, parents earning between school runs, and retirees topping up pensions are not exploited sweatshop laborers seeking a forty-hour contract.
[C1.2] They are economic agents choosing flexibility over predictability. Treating them as employees by default strips away that choice.
[C1.3] The economics are equally punishing. Employee status carries fixed costs—minimum wages, payroll taxes, benefits, dismissal protections—that do not vanish because a regulator relabels them. Platforms operating on thin margins respond predictably: they cap hours, restrict access, raise prices, automate, or exit markets entirely. When Spain imposed delivery-rider employment laws, some platforms simply shifted toward subcontracting or reduced service, leaving fewer earning opportunities, not better ones.
[C1.4] Others passed costs to consumers, shrinking demand. You cannot mandate prosperity by decree. If the cost of a ride or delivery doubles, demand collapses, and the work disappears.
[C1.5] The proposed "narrow exemptions" are a mirage. Drawing a bright line between a genuine freelancer and a disguised employee is a bureaucratic nightmare that invites endless litigation and chilling uncertainty. A consultant who uses a platform to find clients looks suspiciously like a driver who uses an app to find fares. A photographer setting her own rates and a cleaner setting hers through an algorithm share similar surfaces.
[C1.6] Rather than surgically correcting abuse, this rule would engulf entire sectors in reclassification.
[C1.7] There are smarter ways to address real harms: portable benefits, sectoral bargaining, or minimum earnings floors that preserve flexibility while adding security. The motion offers a sledgehammer where a scalpel is needed. We should reject it.
