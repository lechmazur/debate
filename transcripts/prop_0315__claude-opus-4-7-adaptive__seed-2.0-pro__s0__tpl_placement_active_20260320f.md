[P1.1] Officiating is the one part of modern sport that still runs on medieval epistemics: a robed authority pronounces, and fans are expected to accept the verdict on faith. Every other high-stakes institution we trust — courts, surgeries, aviation incidents, even algorithmic moderation — has moved toward recorded reasoning and after-action transparency. Sports leagues are the conspicuous holdout, and the proposition simply asks them to catch up for the narrow, high-leverage moments where a review has already happened and a decision has already been made.
[P1.2] Three reasons this wins.
[P1.3] First, legitimacy. When the NFL mic'd up referees to announce penalties, the game didn't collapse; it became more trusted. When the NHL started releasing situation-room explainers for disallowed goals, disputes shortened instead of lengthening. Showing the logic doesn't create controversy — hiding it does.
[P1.4] A fanbase that hears "no clear and obvious error, because the defender's arm is in a natural position" walks away grumbling but informed. A fanbase that hears nothing invents conspiracies.
[P1.5] Second, accountability and improvement. If the reasoning is public, bad reasoning gets corrected faster, and consistent reasoning gets reinforced. Coaches stop guessing what "completing the process of the catch" means this week. Players adjust behavior to an actual standard rather than a rumored one.
[P1.6] Opaque officiating is how you get the same disputed interpretation re-litigated for a decade.
[P1.7] Third, the costs CON will raise are manageable, not disqualifying. Audio can be lightly edited for slurs or medical info. Explanations can be written, not raw. Referees already speak on hot mics in rugby, cricket, and the NFL without psychological collapse.

[P1.9] The central clash is simple: does the sport belong to the institution that adjudicates it, or to the millions who invest in it? Transparency is the price of that investment. Pay it.
