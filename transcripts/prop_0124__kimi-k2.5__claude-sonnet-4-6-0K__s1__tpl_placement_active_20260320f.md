[P1.1] The right to determine the manner and timing of one's death is the final expression of bodily autonomy. When competent adults face terminal or degenerative conditions that produce genuinely unbearable suffering—pain that cannot be relieved by palliative care or that strips away identity and agency—state prohibitions on medical assistance do not protect life; they prolong torture. The question is not whether death is desirable, but whether the law may force conscious, rational individuals to endure suffering they find intolerable until biology mercifully fails.
[P1.2] Consider the concrete reality these patients face. A person with advanced ALS may remain fully aware while their body becomes a prison, breathing through tubes, unable to speak, swallow, or move, facing months or years of progressive suffocation while cognitively intact. Someone with advanced Huntington's disease may watch their personality deteriorate while physically incapacitated, knowing exactly what degradation comes next. Without medical assistance, their only alternatives are violent suicide, starvation, or continued existence in a state they experience as humiliating degradation.
[P1.3] The state currently mandates this trajectory, substituting its abstract valuation of biological function for the actual lived experience of the person enduring it.
[P1.4] Strict safeguards—multiple medical confirmations of terminal diagnosis, psychiatric evaluation to rule out reversible depression, mandatory waiting periods, and voluntariness checks—ensure that this right extends only to those with verified, irremediable conditions who consistently request it. These criteria distinguish legitimate autonomy from transient despair, creating a narrow exception for extreme cases rather than a broad license to die. Physicians already make life-and-death decisions regarding terminal sedation; this simply grants the patient the decisive voice.
[P1.5] Opposition claims about protecting vulnerable people ignore that the current prohibition actively harms the most vulnerable by denying them agency over their own bodies while privileging the comfort of bystanders who never face such choices in their own lives. We do not force medical treatment on competent adults; we should not force medical existence upon them either. Dignity demands that when medicine cannot cure the suffering, it may at least permit the escape.

[C1.2] Start with competence. Determining decision-making capacity in patients experiencing severe pain, existential dread, and depression is genuinely difficult. Physicians routinely disagree. Depression, which is both treatable and extraordinarily common in terminal illness, can systematically distort what a patient wants.
[C1.3] The proposition promises psychiatric evaluation will filter this out, but there is strong reason to doubt that guarantee holds under real-world conditions. When palliative care is unavailable, undertreated, or simply unfamiliar to the patient, the request for death may reflect a failure of care rather than a settled autonomous preference.
[C1.4] Now consider the systemic pressure that legalization creates. Once medical assistance in dying is available, it becomes one option on a menu. Patients who feel like burdens—financially, emotionally—may choose it not from pure autonomous desire but from an internalized belief that they are imposing. Evidence from jurisdictions that have expanded these frameworks suggests that criteria drift over time: initial narrow categories broaden, psychiatric suffering is added, disability becomes sufficient, waiting periods shorten.
[C1.5] The Netherlands and Belgium illustrate this pattern. The safeguards that seemed robust in the opening law do not hold their shape.
[C1.6] Third, there is a structural problem of incentives. Insurance systems, resource-constrained health systems, and even well-meaning families all have interests that do not perfectly align with the patient's. Legalization introduces a subtle but real force: it becomes cheaper to approve a death than to fund years of intensive palliative support. The proposition treats the safeguard framework as if it operates in a vacuum.
[C1.7] It does not.
[C1.8] This debate is ultimately about what happens when the framework fails—because frameworks do fail, and these failures are permanent. The burden on PRO is not to show the system works perfectly in ideal conditions. It is to show it is safe enough given realistic, imperfect ones.
