[P1.1] The central question is simple: when governments spend political capital and public money to accelerate batteries and clean technology, which instrument actually works? The answer is demand-side policy—purchase incentives, public procurement mandates, performance standards, and consumption tax credits—not large producer subsidies.
[P1.2] Here is why the mechanism matters. The cost reductions that have transformed solar panels and lithium-ion batteries over the past two decades came overwhelmingly from deployment at scale, not from subsidized factory construction. Each doubling of cumulative installed capacity drives predictable cost reductions through learning-by-doing. That learning happens when units are built, sold, and used—when real customers pull product through the market.
[P1.3] Producer subsidies can build capacity that sits idle or floods export markets; demand-side policy creates the actual volume that drives the learning curve.
[P1.4] Producer subsidies also carry a structural flaw: they require governments to choose winners before the market has sorted them. Picking a battery chemistry, a manufacturer, or a production method in advance means locking in today's technology and today's incumbents. Demand-side instruments are technology-neutral. A vehicle purchase credit rewards whatever EV customers choose to buy; a clean electricity standard rewards whatever generation technology proves cheapest to deploy.
[P1.5] Competition among producers remains intact, innovation pressure remains intact, and the government avoids becoming a venture capitalist with public funds.
[P1.6] There is also a political economy problem with producer subsidies that compounds over time. Firms receiving large direct subsidies build lobbying capacity to preserve them. The subsidy becomes self-perpetuating, divorced from performance. Demand-side policy ties government support to actual adoption outcomes—if the product doesn't sell, the credit doesn't flow.
[P1.7] Finally, producer subsidies invite retaliation. Large manufacturing grants and targeted tax relief for domestic producers trigger countervailing duties and trade disputes that fragment the very supply chains clean tech needs. Demand-side measures are far more legally durable in international trade frameworks.
[P1.8] The path to victory here runs through mechanism, incentive structure, and real-world performance. Demand-side policy wins on all three.
