[P1.1] Cinema-going is a habit, not a transaction. Once a week, once a month, a family says "let's go to the movies"—and the magic of that sentence is that it doesn't require a spreadsheet. You know roughly what it costs. You can decide on Thursday for Friday.

[P1.3] Aggressive dynamic pricing destroys exactly that frictionless quality. And cinema, more than almost any other entertainment, lives or dies on frictionlessness. It is already competing with a couch, a streaming subscription with near-zero marginal cost, and a hundred other free attention substitutes. Add price volatility on top, and the marginal moviegoer—the one whose habit you most need to protect—simply stops bothering.
[P1.4] They wait for streaming. The habit dies one Saturday at a time.
[P1.5] The damage is asymmetric. Either way the theater loses the one thing it has that Netflix doesn't: the communal, simultaneous, everyone-saw-it-this-weekend experience.
[P1.6] There is also a fairness point that matters for a mass cultural habit. Dynamic pricing systematically transfers the best seats and best showtimes to whoever is least price-sensitive. Cinema becomes stratified by willingness to pay in real time, and the working family that used to go on Saturday night becomes the family that goes Tuesday afternoon, or not at all. That is the literal mechanism by which a mass habit becomes a niche one.
[P1.7] Theaters can price differently by time, by format, by membership. What they should not do is turn the standard seat into an airline ticket. The medium cannot afford it.
