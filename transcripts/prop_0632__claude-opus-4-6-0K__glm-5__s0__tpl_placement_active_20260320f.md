[P1.1] Obesity is a chronic, relapsing disease that affects roughly 40 percent of American adults. It drives type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, sleep apnea, osteoarthritis, and multiple cancers. We have long treated its downstream consequences at enormous cost while refusing to cover the upstream intervention. The proposition corrects that incoherence—and does so with a built-in fiscal guardrail.
[P1.2] First, the clinical case is overwhelming. Newer GLP-1 receptor agonists and similar agents produce sustained weight loss of 15 to 20 percent or more of body weight in clinical trials. That magnitude of weight loss reliably reduces hemoglobin A1c, blood pressure, liver fat, and major adverse cardiovascular events. No lifestyle intervention alone achieves comparable results at a population level.
[P1.3] Denying coverage for these medications when a physician judges them clinically indicated is the equivalent of covering insulin for diabetes but refusing to cover the drug that could have prevented the diabetes in the first place.
[P1.4] Second, the economic logic favors coverage. Obesity-attributable medical spending in the United States runs into hundreds of billions of dollars annually. Bariatric surgery—already widely covered—costs tens of thousands of dollars per procedure. If anti-obesity medications avert even a fraction of downstream surgeries, hospitalizations, and chronic disease management, the net fiscal impact is favorable.
[P1.5] The proposition wisely specifies value-based pricing and outcomes-based contracts, meaning insurers pay less when a drug underperforms and manufacturers share the risk. This is not a blank check; it is the most disciplined form of pharmaceutical purchasing available.
[P1.6] Third, the equity dimension matters. Today, patients with employer-sponsored plans or personal wealth can access these medications; Medicaid and uninsured patients largely cannot. Mandating coverage with clinical criteria closes a gap that otherwise widens health disparities along income and racial lines.
[P1.7] The CON side must somehow argue that a proven treatment for a deadly chronic disease should remain optional for insurers to exclude—while those same insurers cover statins, antihypertensives, and every other downstream consequence of the very condition these drugs treat. That position is medically incoherent, economically short-sighted, and ethically untenable.
