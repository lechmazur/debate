[C1.1] The proposition sounds like consumer protection. In practice, it is a mandate that will make service worse for the very people PRO claims to help.
[C1.2] Start with the mechanics. "Fast path to a human" is not a free resource floating in the ether. It is a queue staffed by people who cost money. If you legally require every billing, cancellation, or safety inquiry to reach a human quickly, one of three things happens: companies hire enough agents to meet a legal standard and pass the cost through in higher prices and fees; they cut investment in the self-service tools that actually resolve most issues in seconds; or, most likely, they meet the letter of the rule with a thinly-staffed "human" line where the real wait balloons from ten minutes to an hour.
[C1.3] Legally compliant. Substantively worse.
[C1.4] Second, the category trick. "Safety complaints" and "billing disputes" are exactly the labels every savvy caller will select to skip the queue, because humans game incentives. The fast lane becomes the only lane, and genuine emergencies get buried behind password-reset traffic wearing a safety costume. The rule destroys the triage function that makes call centers work.
[C1.5] Third, the remedies already exist and they are stronger than a staffing mandate. Chargebacks reverse unauthorized charges without ever speaking to the company. State attorneys general, the FTC, and sector regulators handle cancellation traps and safety issues; the FTC's click-to-cancel direction and utility commission rules target the specific harms PRO names. If the concern is deceptive dark patterns, regulate the dark pattern.
[C1.6] Do not mandate a channel.
[C1.7] Fourth, bots are not uniformly the villain. For a deaf user, a night-shift worker, a non-native English speaker who reads better than they hear a rushed agent, text-based automation is the accessible option. A blanket "human on demand" rule treats a tool many users prefer as contraband.
[C1.8] Regulate outcomes—wrongful charges, safety response times—not seating charts.
