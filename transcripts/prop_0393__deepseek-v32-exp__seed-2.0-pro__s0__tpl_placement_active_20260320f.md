[P1.1] Pedestrian injuries from shared e-bikes and scooters ridden illegally on sidewalks are frequent and severe. Companies that deploy these vehicles profit from each ride, yet when riders injure pedestrians in clearly restricted zones, those companies often escape legal accountability. This must change: shared micro-mobility providers should bear more legal responsibility for such injuries.
[P1.2] Three reasons support this. First, only the companies have the systemic capacity to prevent harm at scale. They control vehicle technology (geofencing, speed limits), user agreements, and rider data. They could disable scooters on sidewalks, enforce slow zones, and penalize repeat offenders.
[P1.3] Without legal liability, they lack incentive to implement these costly safety measures.
[P1.4] Second, the current legal framework shields companies, creating a moral hazard. They benefit from aggressive expansion and rider convenience while externalizing the costs of injuries onto pedestrians and the public healthcare system. Increased responsibility would internalize these costs, aligning profit with safety.
[P1.5] Third, it is a matter of fairness. When a pedestrian is hit by a rented scooter, the victim should not bear the burden alone. The company that enabled the ride and profited from it must be held accountable. This principle is well-established in product liability and enterprise responsibility.
[P1.6] Opponents will shift blame to riders, but riders are often inexperienced, hurried, or impaired. Companies are repeat players with the resources and capability to design safety into the system. Holding them more responsible will drive innovation in safety features and operational controls, making sidewalks safer for everyone.

[C1.1] Shifting extra legal responsibility for rider-caused sidewalk injuries to shared e-bike and scooter companies rests on two false assumptions, and would cause far more harm than it prevents. First, it erases the direct, intentional negligence of the rider who chooses to break clearly posted rules banning sidewalk or restricted zone use. Every rider signs a user agreement explicitly prohibiting this conduct before unlocking a vehicle, and holds sole control over how and where they operate it. Holding the company liable for a rider’s deliberate choice to violate the law is no more logical than holding a car rental company liable when a renter runs a red light and hits a pedestrian.
[C1.2] PRO’s claim that companies can eliminate this harm via technology is wildly overstated. GPS geofencing is rarely precise enough to distinguish a sidewalk from an adjacent bike lane 3 feet away; forced automatic disabling of vehicles in those fuzzy zones would strand riders in the middle of busy roads, raising crash risks for both riders and drivers, while blanket slow zones push riders to use cheaper, unregulated personal e-scooters with zero safety controls at all. Worse, this policy creates a new, more dangerous moral hazard: riders will have zero incentive to avoid restricted zones if they know the company will bear all legal and financial responsibility for any harm they cause.
[C1.3] Current rules already require most providers to carry supplemental insurance to cover injury costs if a rider cannot pay, so expanding liability only raises ride prices for low-income users who rely on these services for affordable transit, or pushes providers to pull out of lower-income neighborhoods entirely to cut risk.
