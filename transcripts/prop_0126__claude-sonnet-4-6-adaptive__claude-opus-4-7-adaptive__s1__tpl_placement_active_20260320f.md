[P1.1] The current system works like this: a prison outsources phone and video services to a private vendor, the vendor charges families several dollars per minute, and the prison receives a commission kickback. The incarcerated person has no alternative provider and no leverage. Their family either pays or goes without contact. This is not a market — it is a captive monopoly exploiting the most vulnerable people connected to the criminal justice system.
[P1.2] The case for free communication rests on three converging arguments that reinforce each other.
[P1.3] First, family contact is one of the strongest predictors of successful reintegration. Research in criminology consistently shows that people who maintain close family bonds while incarcerated are significantly less likely to reoffend after release. If we take rehabilitation seriously as a goal of incarceration — and most justice systems officially claim to — then severing or pricing family contact undermines the entire enterprise. The state cannot simultaneously claim to be rehabilitating people and charge prohibitive fees for the primary mechanism through which rehabilitation happens.
[P1.4] Second, the burden falls almost entirely on people who committed no crime. The families paying these bills are typically low-income women — mothers, partners, children — who stretch already-thin budgets to stay connected to someone they love. Charging them premium rates for what amounts to a basic human relationship is a punishment transferred to innocent people. That is ethically indefensible under any theory of justice.
[P1.5] Third, the vendor-commission model creates a direct financial incentive to keep communication expensive. When prisons profit from high call rates, they have institutional reasons to resist reform. This is a structural conflict of interest that the government has both the power and the obligation to eliminate.
[P1.6] Free calls and video visits are not a luxury. They are a cost-effective investment in lower recidivism, a basic recognition that punishment should be personal rather than collective, and a necessary step to remove the corrupting incentive structure that currently governs prison telecommunications.

[C1.5] Affordable beats free precisely because affordability preserves signal.
[C1.6] Third, prison budgets are zero-sum. [C1.7] PRO's indictment of the kickback system is correct. The remedy is to kill the kickback and regulate the price, which jurisdictions are already doing. The remedy is not to hand a blank check to a captive population and pretend the bill disappeared. Fix the exploitation.
