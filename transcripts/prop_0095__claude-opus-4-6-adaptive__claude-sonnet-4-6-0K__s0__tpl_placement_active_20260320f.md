[P1.1] The question is simple: when a competent adult faces unbearable suffering with no prospect of relief, does the law serve them by forcing them to endure it? The answer must be no.
[P1.2] Three arguments carry this case.
[P1.3] **Autonomy is the foundation.** We already recognize that competent adults may refuse life-sustaining treatment, including ventilators and feeding tubes, and allow death to follow. The only principled distinction between that right and medical assistance in dying is one of method, not of moral logic. If a patient may choose the timing and manner of death by withdrawal, the law is not protecting life by denying the same choice to a patient who cannot simply disconnect a machine. Denying MAID to the latter patient is not neutrality—it is coercion dressed as compassion.
[P1.4] **Unbearable suffering is a concrete harm.** The proposition is not about existential fatigue or ordinary disability. It specifies terminal or degenerative conditions producing suffering that the patient themselves describes as unbearable. Palliative medicine has advanced enormously, but its practitioners are candid that some pain—particularly neuropathic pain, suffocation from motor neuron disease, or the total loss of bodily function—cannot always be adequately managed. To insist that the law must never permit MAID because palliative care is available is to deny the reality of patients for whom it is insufficient.
[P1.5] **Strict safeguards answer the abuse concern.** Jurisdictions that have operated MAID laws for years—the Netherlands, Belgium, Canada, Oregon—show no credible evidence of systematic coercion or demographic targeting of vulnerable groups. Safeguards such as independent assessments, mandatory waiting periods, and documented competency reviews are precisely what the proposition requires. The question is not whether safeguards can fail in theory; every medical intervention can fail. The question is whether well-designed safeguards make the law safe enough—and the evidence says yes.
[P1.6] The case for prohibition rests on preventing rare abuse at the cost of certain, ongoing suffering for many. That trade is morally indefensible.

Distinguishing genuine autonomous preference from suffering-induced despair is among the hardest diagnostic challenges in medicine. When the error costs someone their life, there is no correction.
[C1.4] **"Unbearable" is subjective and socially constructed.** What a patient experiences as unbearable is shaped by what their environment tells them they should expect—financial dependence, being a burden, inadequate social support. That is not autonomous choice; that is structural failure rerouted through a medical procedure. Legalizing MAID does not cure the conditions that make dying seem preferable to living; it makes dying the available option when those conditions are not fixed.
[C1.5] **Evidence from operating jurisdictions should concern, not reassure.** The scope of eligibility has expanded in every major jurisdiction after initial legalization—from terminal illness to chronic conditions, from physical to psychiatric suffering, and in some places toward minors. Each expansion was justified by the same autonomy logic the proposition advances. That pattern is not incidental; it is what the underlying argument logically demands. The safeguards the other side calls strict were also strict at the beginning.
[C1.6] The real question is not whether some individuals could benefit. It is whether a legal regime reliably produces benefit without systematically endangering the vulnerable. That question, the other side has not yet answered.
