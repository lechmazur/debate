[P1.1] The promise was simple and compelling: put a camera on every officer, and sunlight would do the rest. A decade into widespread deployment, the evidence tells a more complicated and ultimately disappointing story.
[P1.2] Start with the headline finding that advocates cannot easily dismiss. Several large randomized controlled trials—including one conducted across multiple Washington, D.C. precincts—found no statistically significant reduction in use-of-force incidents or civilian complaints when body cameras were introduced. These were not poorly designed studies; they are among the most rigorous evaluations in policing research. If cameras were delivering on their promise, this is where we would see it.
[P1.3] The reasons for the gap between promise and reality are structural, not incidental. Footage is only accountable if it is actually reviewed, preserved, and acted upon. In practice, departments control their own footage, decide what gets released, and frequently claim technical failures—cameras not activated, recordings corrupted, files unavailable—precisely when accountability matters most. The camera records what the officer points it at; it does not record the supervisor who decides to do nothing with the file afterward.
[P1.4] Prosecutorial outcomes reinforce the pattern. Even when footage clearly captures misconduct, convictions remain rare. Juries have proven reluctant to convict officers, and prosecutors remain structurally dependent on police cooperation. Video evidence has been introduced in high-profile cases and still failed to produce accountability, suggesting the bottleneck was never a lack of evidence in the first place.
[P1.5] Finally, the fiscal cost is substantial. Storage, maintenance, and litigation over footage access have cost cities tens of millions of dollars—resources that could fund community interventions with better-documented effects.
[P1.6] The proposition does not require cameras to be worthless. It requires only that they delivered less than advocates promised. Given the evidence, that bar is cleared. The gap between the reform narrative and the measurable reality is the heart of this debate.
