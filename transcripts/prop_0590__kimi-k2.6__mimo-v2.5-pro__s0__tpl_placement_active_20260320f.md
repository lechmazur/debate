[P1.1] The entire point of classifying an AI system as “high-risk” is that it can inflict serious, documented harm on real people. That harm happens in hospitals, courtrooms, and hiring departments—not in a Delaware boardroom or a Shenzhen server farm. Basing jurisdiction on where a provider happens to incorporate is therefore a recipe for a massive protection gap that foreign developers will drive straight through, leaving European citizens exposed.
[P1.2] Consider a firm headquartered outside the EU that deploys a biased credit-scoring algorithm across the German rental market. Under a headquarters-based rule, if that firm lacks a European subsidiary, Brussels effectively waves it through. EU consumers bear all the risk of automated discrimination, while EU-based competitors must shoulder the full compliance burden. That is not sensible regulation; it is an open invitation to regulatory arbitrage that punishes domestic innovators and lets foreign actors externalize their harms onto European soil.
[P1.3] The alternative is neither radical nor untested. The GDPR’s extra-territorial scope works precisely because it targets the processing of EU residents’ data, not the postcode of the company’s legal team. Product safety, environmental liability, and consumer protection all follow the same territorial logic: if you produce effects inside the jurisdiction, you answer to its standards. AI should be treated identically.
[P1.4] A hiring tool that sorts résumés in Lyon or a medical diagnostic used in Warsaw must comply with EU rules because the impact is European, regardless of where the model was trained or where the parent company files its taxes.
[P1.5] Adopting an impact-based standard also gives enforcement a coherent lever. Market access is the mechanism: any provider whose system shapes European lives, markets, or public services must meet European safeguards. That is how you build a rulebook that actually protects citizens, rather than one that merely reorganizes corporate letterheads while the danger seeps in from abroad.
