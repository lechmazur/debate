[P1.1] Clothing resale platforms extract billions in fees from luxury and streetwear markets while pushing the existential risk of counterfeits onto individual buyers who lack the expertise, capital, or leverage to verify authenticity. [P1.2] Authentication is a specialized craft requiring trained eyes, reference libraries, and forensic tools. A college student buying a single second-hand designer bag cannot reasonably inspect stitching density, hardware weight, or UV-reactive date codes against evolving manufacturer specifications. When platforms charge premiums for "verified" goods yet force buyers to bear the cost of authentication errors, they privatize the gains of trust while socializing the losses of fraud. This externalization creates a race to the bottom where verification is cosmetic rather than rigorous.
[P1.3] Risk should sit with the party best able to bear it. A single counterfeit purchase can devastate a buyer's monthly budget; for a platform, the same loss is a fractional cost amortized across millions of transactions and offset by insurance. Tort law consistently places liability on the superior risk-bearer who profits from the transaction and controls the verification process.
[P1.4] The current system reverses this principle, shielding the sophisticated intermediary while exposing the vulnerable consumer.
[P1.5] Shifting burden to platforms corrects perverse incentives. Under "buyer beware," platforms maximize volume by minimizing verification costs, knowing victims lack recourse. If platforms faced direct liability for fakes, authentication becomes a competitive advantage rather than a cost center. The result is a larger, more efficient market: buyers transact with confidence, prices reflect true scarcity rather than risk premiums, and counterfeiters face higher barriers when centralized gatekeepers have financial skin in the game.
