[C1.1] The proposition sound reasonable until you examine what digital assets actually are. The "bookshelf analogy" collapses immediately: a physical book is a rival good that one person possesses and can physically hand over. A digital license is a contractual permission to access content on specified terms—and those terms have always been non-transferable, a fact visible before purchase.
[C1.2] This isn't fine-print trickery. When you pay $9.99 for an ebook, you're not buying a book; you're paying for personal access at a fraction of what permanent, transferable ownership would cost. A transferable digital asset would be priced like a physical object—often ten or twenty times higher—because it retains resale value and can circulate indefinitely. Inheritance mandates would either force platforms to absorb massive uncompensated transfers or raise prices for every living consumer to subsidize posthumous ones.
[C1.3] The current pricing model for digital content depends on single-user, non-transferable licensing. Break that assumption, and the economics unravel.
[C1.4] Then there's the privacy catastrophe. Digital libraries don't exist in isolation—they're embedded in accounts containing search histories, private messages, reading habits, annotations, and payment details. A legal mandate to transfer "digital libraries" inevitably transfers the surrounding digital footprint. The deceased didn't consent to their next of kin reading their private correspondence or reviewing their browsing records.
[C1.5] Living correspondents who exchanged messages with the deceased certainly never agreed to disclosure. Inheritance law for physical property has no equivalent: opening a safety deposit box doesn't expose intimate data about third parties.
[C1.6] Finally, enforcement creates bureaucratic chaos. Who verifies next-of-kin status? What happens when family members dispute access? Must platforms maintain accounts in perpetuity for deceased users?
[C1.7] The compliance infrastructure required—and the liability exposure from wrongful disclosures—would be enormous, falling disproportionately on smaller platforms and creators.
[C1.8] The market is already producing voluntary solutions like family sharing and legacy contacts. Those respects platform economics and privacy while giving consumers options. A blanket mandate ignores what digital assets fundamentally are and what makes them affordable in the first place.
