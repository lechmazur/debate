The PRO case rests on a participation gap, but participation without adequate contribution is a hollow victory. Default rates, often set at 3% or less, are widely recognized as insufficient for a secure retirement. For a median earomer, such a low rate may generate only a fraction of what’s needed, leaving households functionally insecure despite technically "participating."
[C1.2] This locks people into a false sense of security and shields employers and policymakers from accountability for real income replacement.
[C1.3] Furthermore, automatic enrollment’s reach is inherently limited. It depends entirely on an employer-sponsored plan, leaving out the entire growing sector of gig workers, part-time employees, small-business staff, and the self-employed—precisely the populations with the most precarious retirement prospects. Increased tax deductions, by contrast, at least theoretically create incentives for anyone with taxable income to save, independent of their employment structure. The PRO claim that tax deductions only help the wealthy ignores that expanding deductions, particularly through refundable credits, can directly target lower-income households, putting cash in hand for those who choose to save.
[C1.4] Finally, the behavioral "nudge" of auto-enrollment can backfire. By establishing a low default as the socially endorsed norm, it may suppress further savings through a psychological anchor. Workers often interpret the default as expert-recommended, staying at 3% indefinitely, whereas a well-structured tax incentive can motivate incremental increases to savings rates. The real path to financial security isn’t funneling people into a default, low-saving trap; it’s empowering choice with meaningful, accessible incentives that reward greater personal investment in one’s future.
