[P1.1] Water shortages force a choice: ration by price, or ration by decree. The proposition says price — specifically sharply rising tiers that bite heavy users — should do most of the work. Here is why that wins.
[P1.2] First, targeting. Universal restrictions like "no outdoor watering Tuesdays and Thursdays" treat a retiree with three tomato plants the same as an estate with two acres of turf and a fountain. Heavy users are where the water actually is. A small number of premises typically account for a disproportionate share of municipal demand, especially through lawns, pools, and leaks.
[P1.3] A steep rising-block tariff — normal price for essential indoor use, then doubling and redoubling above thresholds — cuts precisely where cuts matter, and leaves cooking, drinking, and hygiene alone.
[P1.4] Second, information. A city manager cannot know which household's irrigation is vanity turf and which is a home daycare's laundry. Prices let each household decide what to give up first. That is why every drought economist's instinct is the same: blunt bans save the easy gallons; price signals find the hard ones.
[P1.5] Third, compliance and enforcement. Odd-even watering days require inspectors, neighbor complaints, and fines that rarely land. A tariff enforces itself on the bill. No patrol cars, no snitch lines, no arbitrary exemptions for politically connected golf courses.
[P1.6] Fourth, equity — which is where CON will push hardest. Rising blocks are progressive by construction: the first tier, sized to basic human need, can be held at or below current prices, even subsidized. The pain falls on discretionary consumption, which correlates with lot size, pool ownership, and income. Pair the tariff with a per-person essential-use allowance and a hardship rebate for large or medical households, and you protect the vulnerable better than a flat ban that punishes a family of six identically to a childless mansion.
[P1.7] Finally, revenue. The surcharge funds leak repair, efficiency rebates, and drought infrastructure. Bans raise nothing and often starve the utility exactly when it needs to invest.
