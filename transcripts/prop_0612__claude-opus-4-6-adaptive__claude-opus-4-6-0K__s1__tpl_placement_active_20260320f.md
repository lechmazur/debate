[P1.2] Three pillars support this position.
[P1.3] **First, transparency is the minimum condition for academic freedom.** When a university accepts tens of millions from a foreign state entity without public disclosure, neither faculty nor students can assess whether research agendas, hiring decisions, or publication choices are being shaped by that money. Confucius Institutes, funded through the Chinese government, operated on hundreds of campuses worldwide with agreements that often included clauses restricting discussion of topics sensitive to Beijing. [P1.4] Disclosure requirements don't restrict funding—they let the academic community make informed judgments about it.
[P1.5] **Second, foreign government-linked funding creates structural incentives for self-censorship.** Researchers who depend on grants tied to authoritarian states face quiet but real pressure to avoid inconvenient findings. This isn't hypothetical. Scholars studying human rights, territorial disputes, or political dissent in donor countries have described softening conclusions or avoiding topics to protect institutional relationships. The chilling effect is hardest to measure precisely because it operates through silence.
[P1.6] Limits on the concentration of such funding prevent any single foreign government from gaining de facto veto power over a department's research direction.
[P1.7] **Third, other democracies have already moved in this direction, recognizing the problem is real.** Australia's foreign influence transparency scheme, amendments to U.S. disclosure requirements under Section 117 of the Higher Education Act, and similar efforts in the UK all reflect a cross-national consensus that this vulnerability demands a policy response. These aren't authoritarian overreaches—they are democracies protecting the institutions that make open inquiry possible.
