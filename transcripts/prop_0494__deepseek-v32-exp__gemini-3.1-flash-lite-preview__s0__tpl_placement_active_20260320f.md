[P1.1] The traditional peer review system is overburdened, slow, and opaque because it relies on unpaid, anonymous labor. Paying reviewers recognizes the substantial skill and time required, turning a voluntary favor into a professional responsibility. This financial incentive directly improves quality: compensated reviewers are more likely to accept assignments promptly, engage deeply with the methodology, and deliver thorough, constructive critiques. The chronic delays and superficial reports that plague journals today stem from asking experts to donate scarce hours without reward.
[P1.2] Publishing reports alongside papers transforms a secretive process into a transparent one. Anonymity can shield careless or biased comments; open attribution encourages civility, rigor, and evidence-based criticism. Readers then see exactly how scholarly scrutiny shaped the final paper, building greater trust in published science. For instance, in fields like climate research or clinical trials, public reviews allow the community to evaluate the robustness of feedback and the authors’ responses, elevating collective understanding.
[P1.3] Together, payment and publication create a virtuous cycle. Compensation ensures reviewers are motivated to produce high-quality evaluations, while transparency holds them accountable and turns each review into a public educational resource. This reform directly targets the core failures of the current model—lack of incentive and accountability—making peer review more reliable, efficient, and trusted.

[C1.1] Peer review is a foundational act of scientific service, not a piecework task to be commodified. The proposal to pay reviewers and publish their reports fundamentally misunderstands the incentive structure of academia and risks institutionalizing mediocrity.
[C1.2] First, consider the impact of financial incentives. When you attach a fee to peer review, you shift the motivation from professional contribution to transactional speed. Reviewing is an intellectual exercise that requires deep, often slow contemplation. Paying per review inevitably creates perverse incentives: reviewers will prioritize volume over depth to maximize earnings.
[C1.3] We risk turning rigorous scrutiny into a "gig economy" model where speed is rewarded at the expense of thoroughness. Furthermore, journals will pass these new costs directly to researchers and institutions, ballooning already exorbitant article processing charges and further restricting access to knowledge.
[C1.4] Second, mandating the publication of reviews destroys the essential protection of anonymity. The goal of blind review is to allow for the ruthless, objective evaluation of ideas regardless of the author’s stature. If reviews are public, this protection evaporates. A PhD student or junior researcher reviewing a paper by a powerful, established professor will be terrified to provide the harsh, honest critique that the work might demand.
[C1.5] Fear of professional retribution—in hiring, grant reviews, or future collaborations—will lead to "polite" reviews that prioritize social harmony over scientific truth.
[C1.6] Anonymity and unpaid service are not bugs in the system; they are features that protect the integrity of the process. By removing these protections and introducing market dynamics into a collaborative pursuit of truth, this proposal would degrade the quality of scholarship and silence the very voices needed to hold science accountable. Peer review functions best as a community duty, not a commercial transaction. We should focus on supporting the academic ecosystem, not dismantling the safeguards that allow for honest, fearless critique.
