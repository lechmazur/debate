[P1.3] Indigenous children were removed to boarding schools. Wartime displacement scattered communities across continents. In each case, the same governments that permitted or caused these disruptions now leave recovery to the marketplace.
[P1.4] The market fails these communities in predictable ways. Private genealogy companies digitize records based on commercial demand. Slave manifests, Bureau of Indian Affairs documents, and displaced-persons registries serve smaller or less affluent user bases than, say, census records for populations with continuous documentation. The profit motive systematically under-serves precisely the communities whose histories were most violently interrupted.
[P1.5] Public digitization corrects this by treating these records as what they are: public goods with profound civic significance. Historical scholarship depends on broad access to primary sources. [P1.6] These functions justify public investment just as surely as we publicly fund archives, libraries, and court records.
[P1.7] There is also a preservation imperative. Many of these documents are deteriorating—fragile paper, decaying microfilm, records stored in underfunded local archives. Governments have the institutional capacity to coordinate large-scale digitization efforts that patchwork private investment cannot match. Every year of delay means permanent loss.
[P1.8] Finally, public stewardship offers stronger accountability for sensitive family data than corporate ownership. Private genealogy firms have faced legitimate scrutiny over data practices. A publicly governed repository with appropriate privacy protections serves both access and ethical responsibility.
