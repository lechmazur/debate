[P1.1] Unconsented, unregulated AI monitoring of remote workers inflicts irreversible harms to privacy, equity, and even productivity, making opt-in consent and strict, enforceable limits a necessary baseline for all workplaces.
[P1.2] First, the privacy costs of unregulated monitoring are untenable. These tools capture far more than work-related activity: AI video monitoring can pick up private medical care administered in a worker’s home, a family member’s confidential conversation, or religious observance happening just outside the intended camera frame, while keystroke monitoring captures personal passwords, private messages sent during a short break, or sensitive health information typed into a personal portal when a worker forgets to switch windows. Workers should not have to sacrifice the privacy of their home and loved ones as a non-negotiable condition of remote employment.
[P1.3] Opt-in requirements ensure this tradeoff is fair: companies that wish to use monitoring tools must offer tangible, proportional benefits to workers to opt in, whether that is a pay premium, formal flexibility accommodations, or explicit protections against data misuse, instead of forcing the burden of surveillance exclusively on low-wage workers who cannot afford to quit over unfair terms. Strict limits are equally critical, even for workers who do opt in: guardrails banning off-hours monitoring, biometric tracking of facial expressions or eye movement, and use of AI monitoring data as the sole cause for discipline eliminate the worst abuses of these tools, while still allowing companies to gain any legitimate productivity insights they claim the tools deliver.

[C1.2] Start with the consent structure itself. Remote employment is a voluntary contractual relationship. When a worker accepts a remote position, the terms of that role — including monitoring policies — are disclosed in offer letters, employee handbooks, and onboarding agreements. That is consent, functional and meaningful.
[C1.3] Layering a separate opt-in requirement on top doesn't add protection; it manufactures a fiction that the initial contract never happened, while simultaneously giving employers a perverse incentive to bury surveillance terms even deeper in legal boilerplate to qualify as "disclosed."
[C1.4] Worse, opt-in frameworks fragment workforces. If half a team opts in and half opts out, employers face unequal datasets, discriminatory discipline risk, and compliance nightmares. The predictable response is to make opt-in the implicit condition for promotion, preferred assignments, or continued remote status — precisely the coercion the proposition claims to prevent. Workers with the least leverage will still opt in, just under more pressure than before.
[C1.5] The phrase "strict limits" is the proposition's other trap. Strict by whose definition? Undefined limits handed to regulators invite shifting standards, jurisdictional conflicts, and compliance costs that fall hardest on smaller firms — pushing them toward cheaper, less careful monitoring vendors or simply terminating remote work arrangements entirely. The workers who lose flexible remote positions because of compliance burdens are not protected; they're casualties.
[C1.6] Meanwhile, genuine privacy violations — capturing family members' conversations, off-hours biometric tracking — are already actionable under employment law, data protection statutes, and tort law in most jurisdictions. The real problem is enforcement, not the absence of an opt-in layer that creates new coercive dynamics while doing little to discipline bad actors.
[C1.7] The proposition doesn't fix surveillance abuse. It restructures it.
