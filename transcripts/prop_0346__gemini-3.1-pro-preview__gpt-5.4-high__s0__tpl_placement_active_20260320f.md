[P1.1] The fundamental purpose of employment is to generate value, not to simulate busyness. In a remote or hybrid environment, evaluating workers based on outputs rather than constant activity monitoring is the only logically sound and economically viable approach to management.
[P1.2] First, activity monitoring relies on deeply flawed proxy metrics. Keystrokes, mouse movements, and active status indicators measure the appearance of work, not its substance. When companies measure presence, employees optimize for presence. This spawns "productivity theater"—workers actively interrupting their own deep, focused work to wiggle a mouse or send a superfluous message just to keep their status green.
[P1.3] Constant monitoring penalizes efficiency; a worker who brilliantly solves a complex problem in two hours looks "worse" to a tracking algorithm than a worker who struggles inefficiently for eight.
[P1.4] Second, an output-based model aligns employee performance with actual business goals. Companies do not profit from hours spent logged into a server; they profit from closed sales, shipped code, resolved customer tickets, and completed reports. By setting clear expectations and measuring these concrete deliverables, management focuses directly on what impacts the bottom line. It filters out the noise of how and when the work is done, accurately identifying high-performing employees based on the actual value they deliver.
[P1.5] Finally, constant surveillance actively degrades workplace culture. Digital monitoring signals a fundamental lack of trust, replacing intrinsic motivation with anxiety. Highly skilled professionals—the very talent companies are most desperate to retain—thrive on autonomy and will not tolerate being micromanaged by an algorithm. Pervasive monitoring accelerates burnout and drives away top performers who know their results should speak for themselves.
[P1.6] If a manager cannot tell whether an employee is doing a good job without looking at their screen time or keystroke logs, the problem is not the employee’s work ethic; it is the manager’s inability to define what success looks like.
