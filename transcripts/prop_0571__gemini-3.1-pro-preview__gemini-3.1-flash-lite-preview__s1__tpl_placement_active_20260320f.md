[P1.1] Sectoral bargaining and wage boards offer a precise scalpel for raising low-wage workers' incomes, avoiding the collateral damage caused by the sledgehammer of a large, across-the-board minimum wage increase. The defining advantage of wage boards is their granularity. They allow compensation to be tailored to the specific economic realities, profit margins, and labor dynamics of individual industries.
[P1.2] A sweeping, uniform minimum wage hike ignores the vast structural differences across the economy. A mandate that sets wages at a high, flat rate might be easily absorbed by capital-intensive industries or multinational corporations in wealthy urban centers. However, that exact same mandate risks triggering devastating layoffs, reduced hours, or accelerated automation in low-margin sectors like restaurants, agriculture, or rural retail. When employers face an unmanageable, one-size-fits-all labor cost shock, their immediate survival mechanism is shedding workers.
[P1.3] Sectoral bargaining eliminates this blind spot. Because wage boards bring together workers and employers from within a specific industry, negotiations are grounded in the actual economic constraints of that sector. Stakeholders can pinpoint the maximum sustainable wage an industry can bear without destroying jobs. They can also negotiate comprehensive packages—adjusting not just base pay, but scheduling, training, and benefits—creating a nuanced compensation structure that a blunt statutory minimum wage cannot achieve.
[P1.4] Furthermore, sectoral bargaining protects employment by leveling the competitive playing field. When wages are negotiated sector-wide, employers compete on productivity, innovation, and service quality rather than by exploiting a race to the bottom on labor costs. High-road employers are protected from being undercut by exploitative competitors, reducing the business failures and subsequent job losses that often accompany uniform wage shocks.
[P1.5] Ultimately, large across-the-board minimum wage hikes force a dangerous gamble, risking the very jobs they aim to improve by ignoring industry-specific breaking points. By calibrating pay increases to the specific carrying capacity of each industry, wage boards secure meaningful economic gains for low-wage workers without sacrificing their livelihoods in the process.

[C1.1] The proposition fundamentally misreads the mechanism of sectoral bargaining. It frames the system as a “surgical tool,” but in practice, it functions as a mechanism for cartelization. By inviting unions and industry representatives to set wages sector-wide, the government effectively deputizes these parties to insulate themselves from competition.
[C1.2] This creates two significant harms. First, it entrenches incumbents. Large firms and established unions have every incentive to set high wage floors that smaller, burgeoning competitors cannot afford. This creates a massive barrier to entry that destroys market dynamism.
[C1.3] Small businesses—the primary drivers of job creation—cannot negotiate at these tables. They are forced to comply with standards designed to suit the cost structures of industry giants. Consequently, instead of protecting all workers, these boards prioritize the interests of established players, stifling the innovation that actually drives long-term wage growth.
[C1.4] Second, the proposition assumes that wage boards can magically bypass the fundamental laws of supply and demand. Even if industry constraints are nominally considered, a wage board remains a vehicle for raising production costs. If a board mandates wages above market-clearing levels to satisfy bargaining demands, firms must inevitably respond by raising prices for consumers or cutting non-wage benefits. If prices rise, demand for that sector’s products drops, leading to the same employment losses the proposition claims to mitigate.
[C1.5] There is no escaping the economic reality that when labor costs are artificially inflated across an entire sector, the market will eventually correct through reduced headcount.
[C1.6] Finally, wage boards are a recipe for regulatory capture. A universal minimum wage is transparent, predictable, and democratically debated. Sectoral wage boards are opaque, prone to intense lobbying, and beholden to the most powerful interests in the room. They replace market-driven wage competition with political bargaining, introducing volatility and corruption.
[C1.7] We should not trade a clear, uniform labor floor for a complex, inequitable, and anti-competitive regime that prioritizes industry stability over economic vitality.
