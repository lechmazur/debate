[P1.1] The current antitrust framework for digital platforms is fundamentally broken. Dominant tech giants have spent the last two decades neutralizing competition not by out-innovating rivals, but by buying them out. To restore competition, we must presume that acquisitions by dominant digital platforms are anti-competitive unless the acquiring firm proves a substantial consumer benefit.
[P1.2] This necessary shift corrects a massive information asymmetry. Under the current regime, under-resourced regulators carry the burden of proving a hypothetical future: that a small, nascent startup would eventually grow into a massive competitive threat. Regulators lack the proprietary algorithms and user data to predict this accurately. Conversely, dominant platforms possess the granular data, user metrics, and market foresight to know exactly which startups threaten their moats.
[P1.3] By shifting the burden of proof, we force the entity holding all the critical information to openly justify the merger.
[P1.4] Furthermore, this presumption eliminates the "kill zone" in tech innovation. Monopolies routinely engage in "killer acquisitions," buying out emerging innovators solely to shut down their products and extinguish future threats. This dynamic discourages venture capitalists from funding startups that directly challenge dominant platforms, starving the ecosystem of true disruptors. A presumption against these buyouts protects the oxygen in the startup ecosystem, ensuring that new companies are built to compete rather than built to be swallowed.
[P1.5] Finally, digital markets are uniquely vulnerable to rapid monopolization. Extreme network effects and data lock-in mean that once a platform tips a market, it becomes virtually immune to traditional competition. Because the cost of a false negative—allowing a monopoly to permanently entrench its power—is catastrophic for market health, a precautionary approach is vital.
[P1.6] If an acquisition genuinely improves products, lowers prices, or enhances the user experience, the acquiring dominant platform possesses more than enough resources to prove it. If they cannot demonstrate a substantial consumer benefit, the acquisition is merely a shield against competition and must be blocked.

[P2.4] If an acquiring giant—armed with armies of analysts and proprietary market data—cannot articulate how a major merger will concretely benefit consumers, the deal is almost certainly a defensive killer acquisition. [P2.5] Finally, CON defends the status quo, claiming current antitrust frameworks are sufficient [C1.6]. But the last two decades prove otherwise. Digital markets tip rapidly due to extreme network effects. Relying on slow, ex-post litigation under current frameworks means regulators only intervene after the damage is irreversible.
