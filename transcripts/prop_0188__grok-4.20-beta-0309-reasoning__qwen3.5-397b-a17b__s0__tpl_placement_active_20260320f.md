[P1.1] Preregistration locks in hypotheses, methods, and analysis plans before data arrive, stripping away the flexibility that now lets researchers chase significance through undisclosed tweaks. In clinical trials and social-science experiments funded by public dollars, this flexibility has produced a distorted record: positive results are over-reported, null findings suppressed, and real effects inflated. Mandatory preregistration ends that by forcing transparency from the start, so the scientific community and the public can trust that reported outcomes were not manufactured after the fact.
[P1.2] Public money carries a non-negotiable obligation. When taxpayers finance thousands of studies each year, they deserve a literature that accurately maps what works and what does not. Without preregistration, entire domains of policy and medicine rest on biased samples of evidence. Negative or inconclusive trials disappear, wasting resources on repeated dead ends and exposing patients or citizens to interventions that lack genuine support.
[P1.3] Mandating the practice converts public investment from a gamble into a systematic accumulation of reliable knowledge.
[P1.4] Journals that commit to publishing preregistered studies regardless of statistical outcome directly dismantle publication bias. Today, file drawers worldwide hold the unpublished null results that would correct overstated claims; results-blind review empties those drawers. The mechanism is simple: if the protocol was registered and followed, the paper meets the threshold. This removes the incentive to spin weak findings or bury them, producing a fuller evidence base for meta-analysis and future research.
[P1.5] Exploratory work remains possible; preregistration merely requires labeling it as exploratory rather than confirmatory. The reform therefore damages cherry-picking without sacrificing discovery. For publicly funded research, rigor must outrank convenience. Preregistration plus results-blind publication is the minimal standard that matches the scale of public stake.
