[P1.1] The proposition asks us to make a simple but profound choice: should we treat regenerative agriculture the same way we already treat conventional farming—with meaningful subsidy support? The answer is yes, and the case rests on three interlocking arguments.
[P1.2] First, we are already subsidizing agriculture. The question is what we are buying. Conventional input subsidies—fertilizers, pesticides, irrigation infrastructure—are justified not because they maximize long-term outcomes, but because they keep the agricultural system functioning. If we accept that logic for conventional farming, we must accept it for regenerative farming.
[P1.3] The same political economy that justifies protecting farm incomes justifies protecting the soil, water, and climate that those incomes ultimately depend on.
[P1.4] Second, the precautionary principle demands action, not paralysis. Conventional agriculture is degrading topsoil at rates that, if continued, will make large-scale food production impossible within generations. It is leeching nitrogen into waterways, creating dead zones that destroy fisheries. It is releasing carbon that took millennia to sequester.
[P1.5] These are not speculative harms—they are documented, ongoing, and increasingly irreversible. Waiting for conclusive yield data before shifting support is like waiting for the house to finish burning before debating fire codes.
[P1.6] Third, the transition problem is real and subsidy-responsive. Farmers who want to shift to regenerative practices face a three-to-five-year transition period of lower yields, higher management complexity, and market uncertainty. Without financial support during that window, rational actors will not switch—even if regenerative systems are superior long-term. Subsidies are not a bribe; they are the mechanism that aligns individual risk with collective benefit.
[P1.7] The motion is not asking us to abandon evidence-based policy. It is asking us to apply the same standard we already apply to conventional agriculture—one that has never required proof of perfect outcomes, only proof of functional necessity.

[C1.6] A regenerative transition that underperforms at scale, induced prematurely by enormous subsidies, could reduce yields across major growing regions during a period of rising global food demand. That risk deserves serious weight, not dismissal.
[C1.7] Nothing about the CON position requires ignoring regenerative agriculture.
