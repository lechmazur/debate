[P1.1] The proposition asks whether remote workers relocating to dramatically cheaper regions should expect unadjusted headquarters salaries or accept location-adjusted wages. Economic coherence demands the latter. Compensation reflects local labor market conditions, not merely individual productivity. When a software engineer earning $200,000 in San Francisco moves to Boise or Eastern Europe—where comparable local talent commands $60,000—maintaining the original salary severs wages from market reality.
[P1.2] The worker voluntarily relocated precisely to capture massive cost-of-living savings; accepting adjusted pay simply splits those arbitrage gains rather than demanding full capture while remaining employed.
[P1.3] Headquarters pay bands in low-cost regions create destructive distortions. They price actual local residents out of positions, prevent companies from building sustainable teams in those markets, and invite eventual replacement by contractors who accept true local rates. If a firm pays San Francisco wages for Montana living costs, leadership eventually questions why it needs the Montana-based employee rather than five local engineers at market rates.
[P1.4] This expectation also threatens the remote-work privilege itself. Unadjusted remote workers become expensive outliers during budget reviews—obvious targets for downsizing when leadership notices that identical output costs fractions elsewhere. Location-adjusted pay preserves employment by aligning costs with regional benchmarks.
[P1.5] Finally, demanding full HQ pay while capturing massive purchasing power advantages asks colleagues in expensive cities to subsidize a lifestyle upgrade. When you exit a high-cost labor market, you should exit its pay band, trading some nominal salary for dramatic quality-of-life gains that adjusted wages still magnify.

When a software engineer ships the same features, manages the same systems, and generates the same revenue for their employer from a different location, their compensation should reflect that output—not their zip code. PRO's framework treats labor as a commodity priced by local supply rather than a value exchange priced by contribution.
[C1.2] Consider the market logic more carefully. Remote workers don't compete in their local labor market—they compete in the market where the job exists. A developer in rural Kansas interviewing with a San Francisco company faces the same competing candidates and negotiates against the same alternatives as someone living in Palo Alto. The relevant market for their labor is where the employer sits, not where the employee resides.
[C1.3] PRO's model artificially constrains workers' negotiating power by tethering them to local alternatives they've chosen to escape.
[C1.4] The arbitrage framing is particularly telling. PRO treats cost-of-living savings as something workers "capture" rather than earn—as if the company has a legitimate claim to its employees' personal financial decisions. But why should an employer profit because a worker chooses a smaller mortgage? The company's costs haven't changed.
[C1.5] The work product hasn't changed. What has changed is that the employer discovered a leverage point to extract savings from workers who revealed they could afford to accept less.
[C1.6] Location-based pay creates an ethically fraught two-tier system. It tells workers who cannot afford high-cost cities—or who have family obligations or health constraints keeping them elsewhere—that their labor is worth intrinsically less. The message is stark: your value depends on your address, disadvantaging precisely those already excluded from economic opportunity.

[P2.1] The claim that workers are paid purely for "value created" independent of location [C1.1] ignores how labor markets actually function. Compensation has always reflected both productivity and replacement cost—the competitive price to secure talent in a specific geography. When a San Francisco firm hires locally, it pays SF rates because that is the clearing price for that labor pool. When that firm hires remotely in Boise or Budapest, the replacement cost drops precipitously; paying SF wages there represents a market distortion, not a recognition of universal value.
[P2.2] [C1.2]'s assertion that remote workers compete in the employer's local market collapses under the reality of distributed work. Remote work didn't relocate employees into the San Francisco talent pool—it expanded the pool globally. The developer in rural Kansas now competes with candidates in Colombia and Croatia, not just Palo Alto. The relevant market is where the work can be sourced, not where headquarters sits.
[P2.3] The "extraction" narrative [C1.4, C1.5] inverts economic logic. Location-adjusted pay doesn't confiscate personal savings; it prevents the remote worker from appropriating location rents that belong to neither party. When an employee captures massive cost-of-living arbitrage while demanding full headquarters salary, they extract surplus from the compensation budget that no local candidate in their new region could command. This is the true arbitrage: using geographic mobility to capture coastal labor premiums while eliminating the costs that justify them.
[P2.4] Finally, the two-tier objection [C1.6] backfires. Unadjusted pay creates precisely the stratification CON fears: a privileged class of geographic arbitrageurs earning coastal premiums in interior markets, while immobile local residents remain permanently priced out of those wages. Location-adjusted pay establishes consistent standards within each regional market rather than erecting an insurmountable wall between mobile insiders and stationary outsiders.
