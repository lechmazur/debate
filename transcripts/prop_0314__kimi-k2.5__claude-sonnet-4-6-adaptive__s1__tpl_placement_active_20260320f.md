[P1.1] Professional sports franchises operate a playbook of extortion: subsidize our stadium or we relocate to the next eager city. Municipalities capitulate, issuing general obligation bonds that outlive the structures themselves while diverting scarce capital from transit and affordable housing. This is structural extraction backed by monopoly leverage, not economic development. The economic reality is substitution, not growth—fans shift discretionary spending from local restaurants to stadium concessions, while taxpayers fund the venue and owners capture 100% of the appreciation and naming rights.
[P1.2] The proposition halts this extraction with a simple fiduciary standard: no public money without enforceable mechanisms that internalize benefits. Revenue-sharing agreements treat subsidies as equity investments requiring actual dividends, not speculative goodwill. Neighborhood-benefit requirements—legally binding community hiring quotas, affordable housing set-asides, or youth programming endowments—prevent stadiums from becoming fortified islands of luxury consumption surrounded by displacement and stagnant retail. Without these conditions, cities absorb all infrastructure costs and opportunity costs while private entities pocket concession revenues and land-value premiums.
[P1.3] The asymmetry is stark and well-documented. Cities like Cincinnati still service debt on riverfront stadiums demolished decades ago, while owners departed with accumulated profits. Conversely, where conditional models prevail—tying occupancy permits to community land trusts or mandating that a percentage of luxury suite revenue flow to neighborhood schools—surrounding blocks see stabilized housing stock and sustained commercial corridors rather than seasonal spikes and off-season dead zones.
[P1.4] Unconditional subsidies violate the basic canon of public finance: never privatize gains while socializing losses. The proposition simply applies the accountability standards used for every other capital project. If a city underwrites a venue, it must secure a revenue stream or concrete spatial equity in return. Anything less is not economic development; it is corporate welfare masquerading as civic pride.

[C1.5] More fundamentally, the proposition forecloses the city's ability to weigh costs and benefits in context. Some stadiums genuinely anchor transit-oriented development. [C1.6] Cities aren't helpless children who need a federal rule mandating their homework. They need flexibility, and this proposition takes it away.
