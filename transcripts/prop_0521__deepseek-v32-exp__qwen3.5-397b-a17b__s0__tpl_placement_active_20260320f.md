[P1.1] When employers fund training, they invest in skills, but under today’s rules, that investment is lost if a worker leaves. This traps employees in roles where they may be underpaid or dissatisfied, and it discourages them from pursuing further training in the first place. Portability fixes that distortion. If a nurse earns a specialty certification funded by her hospital, she should be able to take it to another hospital without starting from scratch.
[P1.2] The same applies to a technician’s licenses or a programmer’s cloud certifications.
[P1.3] Portable training accounts benefit the entire sector. Employers gain access to a pool of pre-skilled workers, reducing hiring and onboarding costs. This is especially critical in fast-evolving fields like tech and healthcare, where sector-wide skill shortages are common.
[P1.4] Opponents may claim this discourages employer investment, but that ignores the collective advantage. When all firms in a sector contribute to and draw from a skilled labor pool, the rising tide lifts all boats. Moreover, the proposal is limited to the same sector, ensuring relevance and preventing free-riding across industries.
[P1.5] Ultimately, skills belong to the worker, not the employer. Locking benefits to a single job undermines both individual mobility and sector-wide resilience. Portability is a straightforward reform that empowers workers, strengthens industries, and makes every training dollar count.
