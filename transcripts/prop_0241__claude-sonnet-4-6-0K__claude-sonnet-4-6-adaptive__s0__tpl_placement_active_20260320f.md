[P1.1] The question is simple: what should a criminal justice system actually *do*? If the answer is "produce less crime and more human beings capable of living within society," then rehabilitation wins this debate decisively. If the answer is "make offenders suffer proportionately to their wrongs," retribution offers a satisfying story — but nothing else.
[P1.2] Retribution's core claim is that wrongdoers *deserve* to suffer, and that imposing that suffering has intrinsic value regardless of consequences. Notice what that means: even if retributive punishment produced more crime, more victims, and more broken families, the retributivist would still defend it because it gives people what they're owed. That is a theory of punishment that explicitly refuses to be accountable to outcomes. That alone should make us skeptical.
[P1.3] Rehabilitation, by contrast, treats punishment as a tool aimed at a real-world goal: returning people to society less likely to harm others. Offenders who receive education, vocational training, mental health treatment, and structured reintegration show measurably lower rates of reoffending than those who are simply warehoused. Jurisdictions that have invested seriously in rehabilitation — Scandinavian countries are the most frequently cited examples — consistently produce lower recidivism and lower overall crime. The mechanism is straightforward: if someone leaves prison able to hold a job, manage addiction, and build stable relationships, they are less likely to reoffend.
[P1.4] That protects future victims — people retribution ignores entirely.
[P1.5] Here's the central tension my opponent must answer: retribution is backward-looking. It asks what the offender deserves for what they did. Rehabilitation is forward-looking. It asks what society needs to be safer and what the offender can become.
[P1.6] A system optimized for the past cannot reduce future harm. A system optimized for the future can — and does.
[P1.7] Victims deserve a system that prevents the next victim. That requires changing offenders, not merely punishing them. Rehabilitation makes the system accountable to outcomes that actually matter. Retribution makes it accountable only to itself.
