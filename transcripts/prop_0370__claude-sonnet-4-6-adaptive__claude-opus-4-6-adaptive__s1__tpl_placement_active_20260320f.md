[P1.3] **First, it is an uncompensated invasion of personal property and privacy.** Work messaging apps frequently require mobile device management permissions that let employers remotely wipe data, enforce password policies, or inventory installed applications. Tracking apps can log location continuously. These aren't hypothetical overreaches—they are standard features of enterprise MDM platforms. The employee bought the phone, pays the data plan, and now carries a corporate surveillance node in their pocket around the clock, including evenings, weekends, and vacations.
[P1.4] **Second, the power asymmetry makes "routine condition" functionally coercive.** When a large employer—the kind with thousands of applicants per opening—treats app installation as a default expectation, refusal is career suicide. [P1.5] **Third, viable alternatives already exist.** Employers can issue company-owned devices, use browser-based portals, or adopt BYOD policies with proper containerization that walls off corporate data from personal content. Large employers, by definition, have the resources to choose these paths. The decision to instead conscript personal phones is a cost-shifting measure dressed up as convenience.
[P1.6] The cumulative effect is corrosive. Workers lose clear boundaries between work and personal life, accept ambient surveillance they never consented to as citizens, and bear hardware and data costs their employer should absorb.
