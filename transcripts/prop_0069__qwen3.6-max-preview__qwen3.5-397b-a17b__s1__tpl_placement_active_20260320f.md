[P1.1] Algorithmic reputation systems no longer evaluate isolated transactions; they stitch together fragments of our behavior into persistent scores that travel across jobs, housing, schools, and platforms. When a single risk rating or engagement metric follows you everywhere, mistakes become life sentences and normal human growth becomes a liability. A legal right to disconnect is the only mechanism that stops opaque, cross-context profiling from turning everyday life into a permanent audit.
[P1.2] These systems compound error and bias by design. A misclassified payment, a flagged social post, or a biased hiring filter does not stay contained. It migrates into tenant screening, loan pricing, and academic placement, often without disclosure or appeal. Individuals cannot negotiate their way out.
[P1.3] Platforms and data brokers operate on take-it-or-leave-it terms, and the asymmetry of information makes meaningful consent impossible. Without a statutory off-ramp, people are forced to perform for an invisible jury that never resets, chilling experimentation, punishing rehabilitation, and locking marginalized groups into feedback loops they did not create.
[P1.4] A legal right to disconnect does not ban algorithms; it draws a boundary around context. It requires that reputation data cannot be ported across unrelated domains without explicit, revocable consent, and guarantees a clean slate when someone changes jobs, moves, or enrolls in a new institution. We already accept similar firewalls: medical records are siloed, credit reporting has strict dispute and expiration rules, and employment background checks are legally bounded. Extending that logic to algorithmic scoring is both feasible and urgent.
[P1.5] The question is not whether data can be useful, but whether people remain authors of their own lives. Without a legal right to disconnect, we surrender that authorship to systems that cannot see context, cannot forgive, and cannot be challenged.
