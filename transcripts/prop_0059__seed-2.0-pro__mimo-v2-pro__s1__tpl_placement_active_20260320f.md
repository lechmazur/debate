[P1.1] Unregulated live face recognition on consumer AR glasses would eliminate the fundamental expectation of anonymity that underpins safe, free participation in public life. Unlike fixed CCTV systems, which are at least visible and operated by accountable public bodies in most cases, AR glasses let any random private individual scan every face they pass without the subject’s knowledge or consent, pull up linked personal data ranging from social media profiles to home addresses, past legal records, or even medical status, and act on that information with zero oversight.
[P1.2] This technology inflicts disproportionate harm on already marginalized groups: well-documented racial and gender bias in face recognition systems mean Black, Brown, and trans people are far more likely to be misidentified as wanted individuals, shoplifters, or other negative labels, leading to wrongful confrontation, police calls, or public humiliation with zero accountability for the wearer who can blame the algorithm’s output.
[P1.3] The narrow professional exceptions carved out in the proposition address the only legitimate use cases: law enforcement operating under a warrant to locate a missing vulnerable person, or EMTs identifying an unresponsive patient in an emergency, uses that are strictly regulated, limited to specific public good purposes, and do not expose ordinary people to pervasive, unaccountable scanning in their daily lives. The minor convenience of remembering a new acquaintance’s name at a conference or spotting a celebrity across a restaurant cannot outweigh the basic right to exist in public without being profiled, tracked, and judged by every stranger you pass.
