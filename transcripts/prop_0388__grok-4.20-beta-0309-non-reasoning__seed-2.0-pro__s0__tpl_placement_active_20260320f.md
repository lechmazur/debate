[P1.1] Human dignity demands that when the state alters a person's life trajectory through denial of benefits, deportation, or criminal sanctions, a human being—not an algorithm—must look that person in the eye and take responsibility. The proposition therefore grants every citizen a legal right to a human decision-maker for any government action that materially changes benefits, immigration status, criminal exposure, or comparable life-altering outcomes.
[P1.2] Automated systems optimize for throughput and consistency; they cannot exercise mercy, weigh unique mitigating facts, or absorb moral gravity. A parent fighting for disability support for a disabled child deserves more than an error code. An asylum seeker recounting persecution deserves more than a risk-score. These are not edge cases; they are the routine business of the administrative state.
[P1.3] Replacing human judgment with code in such domains treats citizens as data points rather than moral agents.
[P1.4] The right to a human decision-maker is not a luxury. It is the minimum condition for legitimate authority. When the state wields coercive power, accountability requires a flesh-and-blood official who can be questioned, whose reasoning can be challenged in plain language, and whose errors can be attributed to a person rather than diffused into an opaque model. Without this right, citizens lose the ability to contest decisions in any meaningful sense; they are left arguing with mathematics.
[P1.5] Opponents will claim efficiency and error reduction justify automation. Yet efficiency purchased at the price of due process is merely faster injustice. The very opacity and scale of algorithmic tools magnifies harm: a single flawed parameter can ruin thousands of lives before anyone notices. A human decision-maker, however imperfect, remains corrigible in real time and personally answerable.
[P1.6] The proposition therefore restores a foundational principle: the state must remain human when it decides the fates of humans. That is not sentiment; it is the precondition of just governance.
