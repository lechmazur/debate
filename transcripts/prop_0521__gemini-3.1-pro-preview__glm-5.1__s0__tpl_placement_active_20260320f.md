[P1.1] Tying employer-funded training accounts to a single company is an outdated relic that actively suppresses both worker potential and industry growth. [P1.2] First, portability ends the anti-competitive trap of job lock. Under the status quo, accumulated training benefits act as golden handcuffs. A nurse or a software engineer might stay in a stagnant role simply because leaving means forfeiting thousands of dollars in unused educational funds. [P1.3] When training accounts follow the worker, talent can flow freely to the companies that utilize it best. Labor mobility drives higher wages, better working conditions, and higher overall economic productivity.
[P1.4] Second, portable accounts fundamentally improve the quality of training. When an employer knows a worker loses their account upon leaving, the incentive is to restrict training to narrow, company-specific tasks. However, when workers retain their accounts, they are empowered to invest in long-term, high-value upskilling—like advanced, universally recognized industry certifications—knowing their progress won’t be erased by a job change. This shift produces a more versatile workforce.
[P1.5] Ultimately, the entire sector benefits when the labor pool is smarter, more adaptable, and equipped with highly transferable skills.
[P1.6] The inevitable objection is that portability discourages individual companies from investing in training because they fear competitors will poach their newly upskilled employees. But within a sector-wide portable system, this dynamic is neutralized. If portability is the standard, employers hire from a broader pool where other companies have also contributed to worker development. This creates a sustainable, reciprocal ecosystem where human capital is consistently funded and optimized, rather than hoarded.
[P1.7] Empowering workers to keep their training benefits is the only reliable way to build a resilient, future-proof workforce.
