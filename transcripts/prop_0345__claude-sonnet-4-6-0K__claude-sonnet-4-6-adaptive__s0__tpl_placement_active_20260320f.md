[P1.1] When a large employer issues a return-to-office mandate backed only by vague claims about "culture" and "collaboration," something important is missing: a reason anyone can actually evaluate. That gap is the heart of this debate.
[P1.2] The proposition is deliberately modest. It doesn't prohibit return-to-office mandates. It asks that large employers—organizations with enormous bargaining power over individual workers—provide role-specific business reasons rather than sweeping assertions that no one can verify or challenge. If a company can justify the mandate, it passes.
[P1.3] The question is only whether justification should be required at all.
[P1.4] The case for requiring it starts with a simple observation: roles are not interchangeable. A lab technician working with physical samples has an obvious case for on-site presence. A software engineer who has delivered measurable results from home for three years does not automatically share that case. "Culture" papers over this distinction because it applies to everyone equally and explains nothing specifically.
[P1.5] It is unfalsifiable by design—you cannot point to a quarter where culture declined, measure it, and compare. That makes it a non-reason dressed in reasonable language.
[P1.6] The stakes are concrete. Workers arranged their lives around remote work—accepting lower salaries in exchange for flexibility, relocating to affordable cities, building childcare arrangements that depend on being home. A mandate issued without specific justification forces real costs onto real people while asking them to accept "trust us" as the full explanation. That asymmetry, between employer power and worker exposure, is precisely why accountability requirements exist in employment law generally.
[P1.7] The counter-argument will likely be that managing culture is a legitimate operational judgment that courts and regulators shouldn't second-guess. But the proposition doesn't require courts to approve every decision—it requires employers to articulate a reason at the role level. That is a low bar, and the fact that many employers resist it reveals the real issue: the justifications often don't hold up to scrutiny.
