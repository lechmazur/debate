[P1.1] Dominant operating-system providers control the gateway to billions of devices. When they pre-install their own wallets, browsers, and AI assistants as defaults, they don't compete on merit—they exploit structural advantage. This self-preferencing must be barred.
[P1.2] The mechanism is straightforward: defaults are sticky. Research consistently shows most users never change pre-selected settings. When Apple makes Apple Pay the default wallet, or Google pre-loads Chrome and Google Assistant, they capture users before competitors even get a chance to demonstrate value. This isn't competition; it's capture through architecture.
[P1.3] Consider browsers. When a user sets up a new phone, the default browser handles every link tap. Competing browsers must first convince users to download, install, and change settings—surmounting friction the default never faces. The same applies to wallet taps at point-of-sale and voice queries directed at AI assistants.
[P1.4] The default doesn't win because it's better; it wins because it's first and frictionless.
[P1.5] This creates cascading harm. Competitors face a tilted playing field, discouraging investment and innovation. Why build a superior wallet or browser if the platform owner will default users into their inferior offering? The market doesn't reward quality—it rewards vertical integration and gatekeeping.
[P1.6] The counterargument that users "can choose" ignores behavioral reality. Default effects are among the most robust findings in decision science. Framing this as free choice is like saying restaurant patrons "can leave" when the only exit is through a gift shop. Technically true, practically misleading.
[P1.7] We already recognize this principle elsewhere. Telecom providers can't preference their own content. Utilities can't privilege their own appliances. When a company operates essential infrastructure, it must remain neutral.
[P1.8] Mobile operating systems are the essential infrastructure of the digital age.
[P1.9] Barring self-preferencing doesn't prevent platform owners from offering these services—it simply forces them to compete on equal footing. Users who want Apple Pay or Chrome can choose them. But that choice must be genuine, not pre-made by the gatekeeper who controls the gate.
