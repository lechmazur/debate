[P1.1] Algorithmic feeds were sold as convenience. They became engines of outrage. The proposition is simple: had chronological feeds remained default, the toxicity saturating social media today would be significantly lower.
[P1.2] The mechanism is straightforward. Engagement algorithms optimize for time on platform. Decades of behavioral research show that outrage, fear, and conflict drive more engagement than calm deliberation. A post calling someone a liar outperforms a post acknowledging common ground.
[P1.3] The algorithm learns this implicit lesson and boosts accordingly. Users who post toxic content receive algorithmic rewards—reach, visibility, followers—while measured voices get buried. This creates a structural incentive toward escalation.
[P1.4] Chronological feeds strip away this hidden hand. Users see posts from accounts they follow in the order posted. No algorithm decides that a furious fifteen-tweet thread deserves more attention than a thoughtful essay. The reward structure flattens.
[P1.5] Content succeeds or fails on its merits to the actual audience, not its capacity to trigger the engagement machinery.
[P1.6] Consider the reply-guy phenomenon. Under algorithmic sorting, a hostile reply to a viral post often receives massive visibility because engagement algorithms surface "conversations." The troll gets rewarded. Under chronology, that same reply reaches only those already following the troll—a tiny fraction.
[P1.7] The incentive collapses.
[P1.8] Platforms understood this dynamic. Facebook's own internal research documented that algorithmic ranking amplified divisive content and eroded user wellbeing. They kept the algorithm because engagement drove advertising revenue. Toxicity became a profitable externality.
[P1.9] The counterfactual matters: a social media ecosystem where users control their own information diet through deliberate choices—following, unfollowing, muting—rather than surrendering that control to an engagement optimizer. The former creates accountability. Users who post garbage lose followers. The latter creates casino-style variable rewards that keep users scrolling regardless of content quality.
[P1.10] Toxicity persists in any public forum. But algorithmic acceleration transformed ordinary social friction into a business model. Chronological defaults would have left the toxicity smaller, slower, and more easily managed.
