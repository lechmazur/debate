[P1.1] The rise of antibiotic-resistant bacteria endangers routine medical procedures, from chemotherapy to organ transplants, where secondary infections can prove fatal without effective drugs. Despite this, investment in new antibiotics has plummeted. Companies recognize that these medicines will be used sparingly—often as last-line options—and that resistance will erode their utility over time. The resulting low and unpredictable sales volumes cannot offset the high costs of discovery, clinical testing, and regulatory approval, pushing resources toward more profitable therapeutic areas with sustained demand.
[P1.2] This has created a dry pipeline with very few new antibiotic classes developed in recent decades despite urgent needs.
[P1.3] Advance market commitments resolve the core problem by creating assured demand upon successful development and approval. This mechanism supports elevated unit prices because it allows developers to recoup costs through committed volumes rather than broad market penetration. High prices thus become a feature that aligns private incentives with public needs, not a flaw to be avoided. Governments can design these commitments with caps on total expenditure and requirements for responsible use, mitigating any concerns over immediate fiscal impact.
[P1.4] Rather than hoping for altruism or regulation alone to fill the gap, these commitments harness market forces productively.
[P1.5] Without permission for such commitments, nations remain dependent on existing antibiotics that are losing effectiveness, leading to higher rates of treatment failure, longer hospitalizations, and greater mortality. The spread of multi-drug resistant strains illustrates this vividly, turning once-manageable conditions into medical emergencies requiring isolation and complex interventions. Concrete examples include the emergence of carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae, where options are limited and outcomes poor. The economic toll—billions in extra healthcare costs and productivity losses—far exceeds the expense of targeted advance purchases.
[P1.6] Permitting AMCs prioritizes long-term supply security over the short-sighted preference for low prices that fail to stimulate any new innovation, ensuring that future generations have tools to combat evolving pathogens.

[C1.6] Finally, high-price AMCs misallocate finite public health funds. [C1.7] The billions drained by these high-priced, volume-based commitments would be far better invested in systemic interventions that do not enrich pharmaceutical monopolies: advanced rapid diagnostics to prevent misuse, global genomic surveillance, and basic infection control.
