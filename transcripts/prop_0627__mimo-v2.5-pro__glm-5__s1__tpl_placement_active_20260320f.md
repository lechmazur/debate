[C1.1] The problem isn't the goal—everyone wants clean air and water. The problem is the mechanism. Constitutionalizing environmental protection sounds noble, but it asks courts to answer questions they cannot answer and grants judges power they should not hold.
[C1.2] Start with the core difficulty: definition. What counts as "minimally clean and healthy"? Every environmental standard involves trade-offs—industrial activity versus air quality, agricultural runoff versus food prices, infrastructure development versus habitat preservation. Legislatures and agencies exist precisely to navigate these competing values through democratic processes.
[C1.3] A constitutional right forces courts to substitute their judgment for that of elected bodies on questions that have no legally discoverable answer. When a community challenges a factory permit, who decides whether emissions are "minimal" enough? Judges lack the expertise, the democratic mandate, and the institutional tools to make those calls.
[C1.4] Consider the institutional consequences. Once courts recognize this right, every environmental dispute becomes a constitutional case. Regulatory agencies face constant litigation from both sides—industry groups arguing standards are too strict and violate property rights, environmental advocates arguing they're too lax and violate the new right. The predictable result is judicialization of policy, where judges rather than experts set de facto standards through piecemeal rulings.
[C1.5] This doesn't strengthen environmental protection; it paralyzes it.
[C1.6] The international comparison cuts against PRO. Many countries have environmental rights on paper, but enforcement varies wildly. Constitutional text alone guarantees nothing—ask the citizens of nations with beautiful environmental provisions and poisoned rivers. What actually protects people is functional regulatory infrastructure, adequate funding, and political accountability.
[C1.7] Those require legislative commitment, not judicial intervention.
[C1.8] Flint wasn't a failure of constitutional rights. It was a failure of basic governance—officials violated existing law and covered it up. Adding another legal tool wouldn't have helped when the problem was officials who ignored the tools they already had. The solution is better enforcement of existing statutes, not a new right that shifts responsibility from democratically accountable actors to unelected judges.
