[P1.1] Housing markets across the developed world are broken in remarkably similar ways: prices climb, supply stagnates, and local governments lurch between revenue shortfalls and policies that make the underlying dysfunction worse. The question before us is which fiscal instrument best addresses both housing supply and local public finance. Land-value taxation wins that comparison decisively, and here is why.
[P1.2] **The supply mechanism is direct.** A tax on unimproved land values penalizes holding land idle or underused. Unlike a property tax that rises when you build, an LVT stays the same whether a lot holds a parking garage or a thirty-story apartment tower. The owner's rational response is to develop or sell to someone who will. This is not speculative theory—it follows from the most basic price-theory reasoning, and places that have shifted toward land-value taxation, such as certain Pennsylvania cities that split-rate their property taxes, observed increased building permits relative to comparable jurisdictions.
[P1.3] **Transaction taxes do the opposite.** Stamp duties and transfer taxes raise the cost of buying and selling property, which directly suppresses turnover. When transactions slow, land sits with owners who may not be the highest-value users. Labor mobility falls, misallocation grows, and housing supply responds more sluggishly to demand shocks. The revenue base is also volatile—collections collapse in downturns precisely when local governments need stability.
[P1.4] **Rent caps freeze markets without fixing them.** Caps may protect sitting tenants in the short run, but they discourage new construction, encourage conversion to condominiums or short-term rentals, and reduce maintenance investment. They generate zero public revenue and often shrink the taxable housing stock over time, worsening local fiscal positions.
[P1.5] **LVT strengthens public finance structurally.** Land cannot be hidden, moved offshore, or depreciated away. Its value rises with public investment in infrastructure and services, so an LVT recaptures the value governments create. The base is stable and broadly distributed, giving municipalities a reliable, growth-compatible revenue stream.
[P1.6] The comparison is stark: one instrument expands supply and funds government; the other two contract supply, distort markets, or do both.
