[P1.1] Money is not speech. A campaign contribution is a transfer of purchasing power, not an expression of ideas—and conflating the two has corrupted democratic representation beyond recognition. When a corporation donates millions to candidates who then vote on regulations affecting that corporation's profits, we witness transactional governance, not free expression.
[P1.2] Corporations are not citizens. They cannot vote, cannot serve on juries, cannot run for office—and they should not be permitted to buy political access that actual citizens cannot match. The corporate form exists to limit liability and pool capital for economic activity, not to serve as an additional channel for wealthy shareholders to multiply their political influence. When we treat corporate treasury funds as "speech," we hand double-voting power to those who can afford shares.
[P1.3] Large individual donations pose a parallel threat. A billionaire who contributes a hundred thousand dollars to a campaign does not simply "speak more loudly"—they purchase a meeting that an ordinary constituent cannot secure, they earn phone calls that go unreturned to others, they shape policy agendas around kitchen tables where the public is not invited. This is not the marketplace of ideas; it is the marketplace of access.
[P1.4] Other democracies have already answered this question. The United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia all maintain strict corporate donation bans and individual contribution limits far lower than American thresholds—and their elections remain robust, competitive, and issue-focused. The predicted doomsday, where politics becomes somehow "speechless," has never materialized anywhere.
[P1.5] The burden here rests on those who would defend a system where pharmaceutical companies fund the politicians who regulate drug prices, where fossil fuel interests bankroll climate policy committees, where representatives answer first to their donors. If money is speech, then some citizens speak thousands of times louder than others—and democracy dies in that inequality.
