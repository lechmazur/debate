[P1.2] That is an extraordinary regulatory gap, and the proposition closes it.
[P1.3] Three reasons this mandate is necessary.
[P1.4] **First, AI chatbots hit a therapeutic ceiling that patients cannot reliably detect.** Chatbots can deliver structured interventions like cognitive-behavioral exercises effectively in early sessions. But complex presentations — trauma with dissociation, emerging psychosis, personality disorders, covert suicidality — require clinical judgment that current AI fundamentally lacks. The danger isn't session one; it's session twenty, when a deteriorating patient has normalized the chatbot as their sole provider and doesn't recognize they need more. A fixed-session handoff creates a structural safety net precisely where the patient's own judgment is most compromised.
[P1.5] **Second, the companies deploying these tools have perverse incentives against referral.** Subscription-based AI therapy platforms profit from retention. Every session a user stays is revenue. [P1.6] **Third, vulnerable populations bear disproportionate risk.** AI chatbots are most attractive to people who face barriers to traditional therapy — cost, stigma, rural isolation. These same populations are least likely to self-advocate for escalation and most likely to accept inadequate care as sufficient. [P1.7] The mandate is modest: after a fixed number of sessions, a human professional reviews the case. This isn't banning AI therapy. It's ensuring AI therapy doesn't become a dead end for people who deserve better.
