[P1.1] Universal fuel subsidies are an upside-down welfare state. Governments use scarce public money to make every liter cheaper for everyone, so the biggest benefit goes to whoever buys the most fuel. That is usually richer households with cars, generators, and energy-hungry lifestyles—not the poorest family that walks, takes a bus, or buys little fuel directly. [P1.2] They are also economically corrosive. Artificially cheap fuel encourages waste, congestion, pollution, and slow adjustment toward efficiency. When domestic prices are held below market levels, shortages, black-market resale, and cross-border smuggling become more likely. [P1.3] CON must defend not just helping the poor, but paying everyone—including the well-off—to consume more fossil fuel.
[P1.4] Targeted cash transfers do the protective job better. If fuel prices rise, compensate low-income households directly, preferably before or alongside the phaseout. [P1.5] And because the fuel price itself is no longer distorted, households and firms still have a reason to conserve, switch technologies, and invest in more efficient transport and energy use.
