[P1.1] Broadband is no longer a consumer luxury; it is the plumbing of modern economic and civic life. When private carriers refuse to build lines because the math is thin, they are not making a neutral business decision. They are consigning entire regions to second-class citizenship in a digital economy. The motion asks whether governments should step in when the market says no. The answer is yes, because commercial viability and public necessity are not the same thing.
[P1.2] Private carriers calculate return per mile of fiber. That is rational for shareholders, but it is a market failure for society. Sparsely populated areas still need telehealth consultations, remote education, precision agriculture, and the capacity to run small businesses. A child in a rural school without reliable internet does not care about population density; she cares that she cannot stream a lesson or submit homework.
[P1.3] A farmer trying to operate smart equipment or access commodity prices in real time is not helped by the fact that running cable to his county is expensive. When we treat connectivity as a pure commodity, we allow geography to harden into inherited disadvantage.
[P1.4] We have faced this exact dilemma before. Rural electrification and the postal service both required public subsidy because private actors would not serve dispersed populations. The result was not waste; it was a more integrated national economy and a stronger social contract. Broadband is the same category of infrastructure: its value lies not only in direct subscription revenue, but in the externalities of a connected workforce, reduced medical travel, and economic resilience.
[P1.5] The central clash in this debate is clear. Our opponents will defend spreadsheet logic: if the customer density is low, the wires should not go in. We defend the principle that a citizen’s opportunity should not depend on how many neighbors live within a square mile. Infrastructure is either universal or it is not infrastructure at all.

[C1.1] Private carriers refusing to build unprofitable lines are not creating a market failure—they are revealing one. The rural electrification analogy is a false parallel: electricity was a homogeneous good with near-universal demand and minimal marginal delivery costs once grid-connected, whereas broadband’s value varies by user, and alternative delivery methods like satellite make fixed-line universalism obsolete.
[C1.2] The social returns PRO cites—telehealth, remote education—are real but not unique to fiber; they can be delivered more cost-effectively through emerging technologies.
[C1.3] Worse, subsidies introduce perverse incentives. They invite carriers to overbuild in lightly populated areas where the private case is marginally weak rather than truly impossible, while doing nothing for the most remote locations where even subsidized fiber is impractical. Subsidies also create moral hazard, encouraging carriers to hold out for public funding rather than innovate or share infrastructure. They also crowd out emerging solutions: if Starlink can serve a farmstead at a fraction of the cost of a subsidized fiber line, why should taxpayers fund the latter?
[C1.4] The opportunity cost is stark. Every dollar spent on rural broadband is a dollar not spent on urban digital literacy programs, which often have higher marginal returns for marginalized communities.
[C1.5] The principle that citizens should not be penalized by geography is noble but impractical as an absolute. We do not subsidize door-to-door grocery delivery for remote households, nor do we demand that every village have a hospital. We accept that some services are more efficiently centralized, and we adapt through mobility, technology, or community solutions. Broadband is no different.
[C1.6] The claim that sparse areas ‘need’ fiber ignores that need is not static: as technologies evolve, yesterday’s necessities may become today’s luxuries. Locking in subsidized fiber today risks stranded assets tomorrow. The market’s reluctance is not a bug; it is a feature, signaling where innovation, not subsidy, should lead.
