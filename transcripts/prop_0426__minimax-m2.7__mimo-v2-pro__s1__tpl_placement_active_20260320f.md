[P1.1] Burnout is not a puzzle to be solved with notifications. It is a structural condition created by work itself, and it demands structural remedies.
[P1.2] When employees are overwhelmed, isolated, and unable to recover, the intervention that works is straightforward: give people real time away from work and genuine human contact while they are at work. Mandatory minimum vacation or unlimited-but-encouraged time off lets the nervous system actually reset. Regular in-person gatherings rebuild the trust and social texture that remote or fragmented work erodes. These are not luxuries.
[P1.3] They are the mechanisms that directly address what isolation and burnout actually are.
[P1.4] Corporate wellness apps take the opposite approach. They ask burnt-out people to do more work—log sessions, track habits, complete mindfulness modules—while leaving the conditions that caused the burnout untouched. Research on digital wellness tools shows modest, often short-lived effects, and some studies suggest they can increase guilt when employees fail to engage with them. An app cannot give someone a real weekend.
[P1.5] It cannot replicate the trust built across a table. It cannot substitute for the human synchrony that happens when people share space and laugh together.
[P1.6] The most honest way to describe wellness apps is that they transfer responsibility for a workplace problem onto the employee. The employer buys an app, issues a notification, and can claim they are addressing burnout while employees remain overworked, disconnected, and alone. Predictable time off and in-person gatherings do not allow that sleight of hand. They force real changes to how work is structured and how people relate to each other.
[P1.7] When the goal is reducing isolation and burnout, the question is not which solution feels modern or scalable. It is which solution actually reaches the cause. Time off and in-person gathering do. Wellness apps mostly do not.
