[P1.1] The fundamental question is whether government can punish people for existing in public space when it offers them nowhere else to go. The answer is no.
[P1.2] Consider what anti-encampment enforcement without alternatives actually looks like: officers arrive at a tent encampment, order residents to disperse within hours, and confiscate or destroy belongings that cannot be moved. Residents receive citations for "unauthorized camping" or "obstructing sidewalks"—fines they cannot pay, warrants they cannot resolve, criminal records that make obtaining housing and employment harder. Then they relocate to another sidewalk or underpass, and the cycle repeats.
[P1.3] This is not law enforcement serving public order. It is the state punishing poverty itself.
[P1.4] The proposition establishes a minimal condition for legitimacy: before criminalizing the act of surviving outdoors, cities must offer genuinely accessible shelter, storage for belongings, and services addressing the causes of homelessness. "Genuinely available" matters because shelter systems routinely turn people away when full, exclude those with mental illness or pets, impose religious requirements, or sit in areas too dangerous for vulnerable people. A bed that cannot be reached is not an alternative.
[P1.5] Without this condition, anti-encampment laws criminalize status—punishing people for being homeless while refusing to create conditions allowing them not to be. Courts increasingly recognize this violates basic constitutional protections against cruel and unusual punishment. But the moral logic predates any ruling: a just state does not punish what it does not permit citizens to avoid.
[P1.6] This proposition does not ban enforcement. It requires that enforcement occur within a framework where meaningful exits from street life exist. Cities retain authority to regulate public space—provided they first create alternatives. That balance is the minimum threshold for legitimate exercise of state power over the most vulnerable.
