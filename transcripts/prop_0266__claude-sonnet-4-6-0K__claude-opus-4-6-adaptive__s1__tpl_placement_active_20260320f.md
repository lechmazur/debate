[P1.1] When you open a delivery app and pay $18 for a $12 meal, the missing $6 is not a mystery—it is a fee structure the app designed and then deliberately hid inside a single inflated price. The question here is whether the app should be required to surface a cheaper path when that price gap is material. The answer is yes, and the reason is both principled and practical.
[P1.2] Start with information asymmetry. The app knows, right now, that the restaurant three blocks from you offers direct online ordering at a lower total cost. The customer does not know this. Requiring disclosure does not restrict anyone's choice—it completes the transaction by giving the buyer the same relevant facts the platform already holds.
[P1.3] Informed choice is the foundation of any honest market. A rule that forces disclosure when the price gap is material simply closes an information gap the platform exploits.
[P1.4] Second, consider who bears the cost of silence. Delivery fees are not spread evenly. Consumers in lower-income households, who use these apps precisely because they lack the time or transport for alternatives, pay the full markup without ever learning the restaurant accepts direct orders. The restaurant, meanwhile, often loses fifteen to thirty percent of the order value to commission.
[P1.5] A simple disclosure—"Order directly at restaurantname.com and save $6"—redistributes bargaining power back toward both parties without banning anything.
[P1.6] Third, the "material difference" threshold built into the proposition is genuinely limiting. This is not a rule that triggers for fifty-cent gaps. It applies when the price difference is large enough to affect a reasonable consumer's decision. That calibration already protects platforms from frivolous disclosures; CON cannot credibly claim this crushes the business model.
[P1.7] The core of this debate is simple: platforms profit from hiding information they possess. The proposition says that when the concealment costs consumers real money, disclosure is required. That is not radical regulation—it is basic market transparency, and the burden is on CON to explain why opacity deserves protection.
