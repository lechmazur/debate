[P1.1] Private equity operates on a fundamental mismatch with the necessities of elder care. The core mandate of an elder-care facility is to provide stable, long-term, patient-centered support. In stark contrast, the private equity business model relies on aggressive debt structuring, ruthless cost-cutting, and short-term asset flipping to maximize investor returns. When this financial model collides with vulnerable seniors, the results are devastating, making government restriction a moral and practical necessity.
[P1.2] First, private equity ownership systematically degrades the quality of care through severe staffing reductions. Because elder care is labor-intensive, the quickest way for a newly acquired facility to generate the cash flow required to service a leveraged buyout is to cut staff. Facilities are routinely stripped of registered nurses and frontline aides. The mechanism is straightforward: fewer hands mean fewer turned patients, leading to bedsores; fewer staff means missed medications and rising infection rates.
[P1.3] Mortality rates and emergency room visits measurably rise when private equity firms take over nursing homes. The profit margins are quite literally extracted from a decline in human health.
[P1.4] Second, private equity subjects critical healthcare infrastructure to dangerous financial engineering. A common tactic is to separate the operating company from the physical real estate. The private equity firm sells the building to a real estate investment trust and forces the care provider to pay exorbitant rent to operate in the building it used to own. This extracts immense upfront capital for the investors while saddling the care facility with unsustainable overhead.
[P1.5] If the facility goes bankrupt under this artificial weight, the investors still walk away wealthy, but the elderly residents are subjected to sudden, traumatic evictions and closures.
[P1.6] Elder care is not a standard consumer market where business failure simply means a closed storefront. It is a vital social infrastructure serving a physically vulnerable population with essentially zero market mobility. When residents cannot advocate for themselves and cannot easily switch providers if care declines, the government must step in. We must restrict private equity from treating our elders as leveraged assets for short-term financial extraction.
