[C1.1] Mandatory asylum quotas sound principled in the abstract. In practice, the EU already ran this experiment—and it failed decisively. The 2015 Council relocation scheme aimed to redistribute 160,000 asylum seekers from Greece and Italy. After two years, fewer than a third were actually relocated.
[C1.2] Hungary and Poland refused outright. Most member states dragged their feet. The scheme did not collapse because of some fixable design flaw; it collapsed because compulsory redistribution clashes with deep structural realities of the European Union.
[C1.3] Three problems make quotas the wrong instrument.
[C1.4] First, quotas are unenforceable without coercion that the EU cannot credibly apply. The Union has no federal police, no capacity to physically move people against the will of receiving states, and no political appetite for sanctions severe enough to compel compliance. Infringement proceedings take years and produce fines that reluctant states treat as the cost of refusal. A policy that depends on voluntary compliance from unwilling governments is not burden-sharing—it is burden-announcing.
[C1.5] Second, quotas ignore the preferences and agency of asylum seekers themselves. People relocated to countries with weak labor markets, unfamiliar languages, or no existing diaspora communities frequently leave for Germany, Sweden, or the Netherlands anyway. The 2015 scheme saw relocated individuals abandon their assigned states within months. Forced geographic assignment doesn't reduce secondary movement; it just adds an expensive administrative step before secondary movement happens.
[C1.6] Third, better alternatives exist. Financial transfers, joint processing centers, and investment in reception capacity in frontline states address the actual bottleneck—resources—without the political toxicity and practical futility of compulsory relocation. The EU can share burdens through money, expertise, and Frontex deployments rather than headcounts that no mechanism can enforce.
[C1.7] The proposition asks the EU to double down on a model already tested and already broken. Solidarity is a worthy goal, but mandating redistribution of human beings across unwilling states achieves neither solidarity nor effective protection. It produces political backlash, non-compliance, and secondary flight—leaving frontline states no better off than before.
