[C1.1] Treating home repair, cooking, and personal finance as mandatory core academics commits a category error: it confuses foundational intellectual development with vocational training. Every hour diverted to drilling specific procedural tasks—how to patch drywall or reconcile a checkbook—is an hour stolen from the transferable cognitive skills that enable adults to teach themselves these very procedures when needed.
[C1.2] The institutional reality is harsher than the aspiration. "Core" status requires standardized facilities, credentialed instructors, and assessed outcomes. Converting English classrooms into wood shops or chemistry labs into commercial kitchens demands capital and liability coverage that underfunded districts lack. When such courses are mandated without adequate resources, they devolve into worksheet-based simulations: students label diagrams of circuits or fill out fake tax forms while affluent peers learn on actual equipment.
[C1.3] The equity argument [P1.4] collapses here—mandatory practical courses often widen the gap between those who receive genuine training and those who suffer through hollow compliance exercises.
[C1.4] Finally, the claim that these are universal skills that cannot be acquired elsewhere [P1.5] misunderstands adult life. Personal finance regulations, home technologies, and nutritional science evolve continuously. A graduate who memorizes today's tax code or today's smart-home wiring faces obsolescence within five years. These are not enduring disciplines like mathematics or literature; they are context-specific, just-in-time competencies best acquired through targeted community programs, digital tutorials, or family mentorship when the actual need—buying a house, managing a chronic condition, renovating a specific kitchen—arises.
[C1.5] Conferring core status upon rapidly changing procedural knowledge creates a false credential while displacing the timeless intellectual foundations that actually prepare students for an unpredictable future.
