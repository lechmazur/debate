[P1.1] The case for the proposition rests on a simple chain: hiring decisions impose real costs on real people, the tools we use to make them must clear a meaningful evidence bar, and instruments like MBTI and DISC do not clear it. When something falls that far short, "we like using it" is not a defense.
[P1.2] Start with the psychometrics. The MBTI sorts people into binary types on dimensions that are actually continuous, and a substantial share of takers land in a different "type" when retested weeks later. An instrument that cannot reliably tell you what someone is today cannot predict how they'll perform next year. DISC is built on similar quadrant logic and was never validated as a job-performance predictor.
[P1.3] Even its publishers typically warn against using it for selection. When the vendors themselves disclaim the use case, employers shouldn't be staking careers on it.
[P1.4] Compare the alternatives. Structured interviews, work-sample tests, cognitive ability measures, and conscientiousness-focused Big Five assessments have decades of meta-analytic support showing meaningful correlations with job performance. MBTI and DISC do not sit alongside these tools; they sit far below them. Choosing the weaker instrument when stronger ones exist is not neutral — it is an active harm to candidates who get screened out by noise.
[P1.5] Then consider the downstream damage. A bad hire is expensive, but a bad rejection is invisible and unaccountable. Type-based team-building also encodes stereotypes: the "ENTJ leader," the "high-D dominant personality." Managers begin assigning work, trust, and promotions based on a four-letter label that is closer to a horoscope than a diagnostic.
[P1.6] That is a discrimination risk and a culture risk dressed up as science.
[P1.7] The motion does not say ban all assessment. It says stop using these specific tools because their predictive validity is too low to justify the weight placed on them. The honest answer to a weak instrument is to put it down, not to keep rolling the dice because the dice come in a nice box.
