[P1.2] First, childcare subsidies structurally exclude the most deeply impoverished children. Subsidy programs almost universally require parents to be consistently employed or enrolled in school. [P1.3] A monthly CTC, in contrast, provides a universal baseline of financial security, directly lifting households above the poverty line regardless of parental employment status.
[P1.4] Second, direct cash transfers are vastly more administratively efficient. Subsidizing childcare carries heavy bureaucratic overhead. Governments must build systems to distribute vouchers, certify and inspect providers, and police complex, fluctuating eligibility rules. [P1.5] The CTC simply leverages existing tax infrastructure to deliver funds via direct deposit, ensuring that nearly 100% of the allocated budget actively reduces poverty.
[P1.6] Third, the CTC matches the diverse realities of low-income families. Formal childcare subsidies offer a rigid, single-use benefit. A monthly cash payment empowers parents to allocate resources where the immediate need is highest—whether that means paying rent to avoid eviction, buying groceries, fixing a car to commute to work, or compensating a relative for babysitting.
