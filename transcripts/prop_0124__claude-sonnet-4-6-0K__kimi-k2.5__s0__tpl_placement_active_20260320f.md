[P1.3] The case for medical assistance in dying rests on three interlocking arguments: autonomy, compassion, and the adequacy of safeguards.
[P1.4] Autonomy first. Competent adults already hold the legal right to refuse life-sustaining treatment, including food, water, and ventilators. The philosophical foundation for that right is bodily self-determination. A person who cannot physically remove a feeding tube has exactly the same interest in ending unbearable suffering as one who can.
[P1.5] Denying MAID to the former while allowing withdrawal for the latter creates a morally arbitrary outcome based on physical capacity, not on any principled distinction about the value of the choice.

Refractory pain, existential terror, and the complete loss of dignity are real clinical phenomena that palliative specialists themselves acknowledge cannot always be resolved. [P1.7] Safeguards address the abuse concern directly. Jurisdictions that have implemented MAID—Canada, the Netherlands, Belgium, Oregon—show no credible evidence of systematic coercion or a "slippery slope" toward involuntary deaths among vulnerable populations. Independent assessment by two physicians, psychiatric evaluation where indicated, waiting periods, and written documentation are not hypothetical protections; they work in practice.
[P1.8] The opposition must explain why one person's right to die with dignity should be overridden by another's discomfort with the concept. That is not a sufficient answer.

When a physician withdraws a ventilator, the proximate cause of death remains the patient's underlying pathology; the physician steps back to let nature take its course. In MAID, the physician becomes the proximate cause—the lethal agent is introduced precisely because without it, the patient would continue living. This is not rhetorical hair-splitting; it determines whether medicine's telos remains healing or shifts to termination when healing proves inconvenient.
[C2.2] [P2.3]'s comparison to palliative sedation fails. Palliative sedation intends to relieve intractable suffering, accepting the foreseen but unintended possibility of hastened death as a side effect—the doctrine of double effect. MAID intends death itself as the therapeutic outcome. Conflating these erases the ethical boundary that protects patients from physicians who might decide that death is the optimal "treatment" for difficult cases.
[C2.3] Regarding the slippery slope [P2.4], the PRO demands proof of "involuntary deaths" before acknowledging risk, reversing the burden of caution. We do not wait for corpses to prove a system unsafe; we require affirmative evidence that lethal authority cannot be misused. The documented expansion from terminal cancer to mental illness and dementia demonstrates that categories dilute precisely because "strict safeguards" are politically negotiable, not medically fixed. Once killing enters the toolkit, the definition of "unbearable" inevitably inflates to accommodate resource constraints and social discomfort.
[C2.4] Finally, the claim that palliative care and MAID can simply coexist [P2.5] ignores institutional reality. Creating a subsidized exit fundamentally alters the therapeutic conversation—from "how can we support you" to "have you considered your options"—a shift that targets the economically burdensome patient, not merely the treatment-resistant symptom.
[C2.5] The motion asks us to trust that physicians can safely wield lethal power. The evidence suggests we cannot.
