[P1.1] Shrinkflation is a deceptive practice where package sizes shrink while prices stay the same, hiding real price increases. For example, a 500g cereal box reduced to 450g but still priced at $5 raises the unit cost from $1 to $1.11 per 100g—a 11% hike consumers miss because labels don’t highlight the change. Supermarkets and apps currently display only the total price, forcing shoppers to calculate unit costs themselves, a task most skip due to time constraints. This disproportionately harms low-income households, who depend on unit prices to compare value.
[P1.2] Requiring clear shrinkflation and unit-price disclosures isn’t just about transparency—it’s about preventing exploitation. Businesses argue compliance costs are high, but updating labels is trivial compared to the consumer harm of hidden fees. If apps and stores must flag size reductions and unit-price changes, shoppers can make informed choices, and honest competition thrives. The alternative is a marketplace where confusion is a profit strategy—a standard we should reject.
[P1.3] Let’s mandate clarity: no more hidden price hikes through shrinkflation. This isn’t about overregulation; it’s about ensuring the free market works as intended. When prices are transparent, businesses compete on value, not trickery. Consumers deserve to know if they’re paying more for less.
[P1.4] Current unit pricing is often buried in fine print or inconsistent across products, making comparisons nearly impossible. For instance, one brand might list price per 100g while another uses per serving, creating confusion. Shrinkflation exploits this by reducing size without adjusting the unit price display, leaving shoppers unaware. Mandating clear, standardized unit-price updates when sizes change would close this loophole.
[P1.5] Businesses might claim this adds administrative burden, but digital systems can automate these updates—no more excuses for opacity. The real cost isn’t to companies; it’s to consumers who unknowingly subsidize shrinkflation through repeated overpaying. This policy isn’t radical—it’s a basic consumer protection. Let’s stop letting hidden price hikes erode trust in our markets.
