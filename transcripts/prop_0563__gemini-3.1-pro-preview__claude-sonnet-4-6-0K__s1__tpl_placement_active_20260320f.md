[C1.1] The proposition rests on a seductive chain of logic: commit now, deter forever, and the costs take care of themselves. Each link in that chain is far weaker than the opening admitted.
[C1.2] Start with the resource trade-off, because the motion demands we accept it openly. A standing commitment means not a temporary surge but a permanent, legally or politically entrenched obligation—one that crowds out funding regardless of what else the world demands. The Indo-Pacific is precisely where American military planners most urgently need investment: new submarines, pre-positioned munitions, hardened logistics in Guam and Japan. A standing Ukraine commitment competes directly with those needs.
[C1.3] You cannot simultaneously argue that China watching Ukraine proves deterrence matters and ignore that deterring China requires hardware, readiness, and forward presence that costs real money. The proposition trades the priority deterrence theater for a secondary one.
[C1.4] The cost-effectiveness argument is facially appealing but empirically underspecified. We are told we are degrading Russia's military at bargain rates. [C1.5] The proposition offers none. A commitment with no exit logic is not strategic—it is a blank check written against undefined goals.
[C1.6] On escalation prevention: the argument that faltering aid leads to Russian aggression against NATO members requires us to believe Russia, already severely weakened, would then *expand* its military operations to territory covered by Article 5. That assumption does the heaviest lifting in the PRO case and is asserted rather than demonstrated.
[C1.7] The core problem is this: the proposition asks us to enshrine a standing commitment *even when it reduces other priorities*. That clause is the debate. Priorities exist because resources are finite and threats are plural. Locking in one commitment institutionally, regardless of cost, is not strategy—it is abdication of strategic judgment.
