[P2.1] The stewardship argument in [C1.1] fundamentally misunderstands how advance market commitments work. The entire mechanism decouples revenue from volume. Under the UK's subscription model, the government pays a fixed annual fee regardless of how many doses hospitals use. There is no "full high unit cost" windfall for each extra prescription because the company has already been paid its guaranteed amount.
[P2.2] The marginal revenue from pushing unnecessary prescriptions under a properly structured AMC is zero or near-zero. CON is describing the pathology of the *current* system—volume-based sales—and attributing it to the very tool designed to eliminate that pathology.
[P2.3] The access argument in [C1.3] conflates two separate policy questions. An AMC is a commitment by a purchasing country. Nothing about permitting AMCs in wealthy nations prevents tiered pricing, voluntary licensing, or pooled procurement for low-income countries. Differential pricing across income levels is standard pharmaceutical practice.
[P2.4] But the far more devastating point is this: if no AMC exists and the developer goes bankrupt—as Achaogen did—there is no drug to make accessible at any price, anywhere. Zero access is worse than expensive access, and expensive access in rich countries can subsidize affordable access elsewhere.
[P2.5] The alternative proposed in [C1.4]—direct R&D grants with mandatory pricing controls—sounds tidy but collapses under scrutiny. Grants fund inputs; AMCs reward outputs. A grant pays a company regardless of whether its candidate fails in Phase III. An AMC pays only when a qualifying antibiotic actually reaches patients.
[P2.6] This is precisely why AMCs are more efficient with public money: they transfer development risk to the private sector while guaranteeing a market for success. Mandatory low pricing attached to grants also recreates the original problem—investors see capped returns on a high-risk, decade-long endeavor and walk away. We have decades of evidence that grant-funded antibiotic research without market incentives has not sustained a viable commercial pipeline.
[P2.7] The central clash remains clear: CON worries about the price tag on drugs that exist. PRO worries about the catastrophe when those drugs never get made. Only one of those problems is irreversible.
