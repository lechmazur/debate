[C1.2] Start with the mobility that exists. Students from low-income families who attend elite universities experience enormous earnings gains—dramatically larger than comparable students at less selective institutions. A student from the bottom income quintile who attends a highly selective college has roughly the same earnings trajectory as wealthy peers by their early thirties. That is not window dressing.
[C1.3] That is the most powerful individual mobility mechanism in American life. The proposition must explain away these gains, and it cannot do so by pointing to the composition of the entering class.
[C1.4] PRO confuses two distinct questions: "Who gets in?" and "What does the institution do?" An institution can simultaneously under-admit poor students and be a genuine engine of mobility for those it does admit. A hospital that treats too few uninsured patients is not thereby an institution of disease. The correct policy response is to widen access—exactly what reforms like need-blind admissions, no-loan financial aid, test-optional policies, and legacy preference bans are doing.
[C1.5] Harvard, Princeton, and others now offer full rides to families earning under roughly $75,000. These are not cosmetic gestures; they represent billions in redistributed resources.
[C1.6] Now consider PRO's policy alternative: capping endowment exemptions and treating these schools as quasi-hereditary estates. This would reduce the very financial aid dollars that fund low-income attendance. Tax the endowment and you tax the scholarship. The proposition's own remedy would shrink mobility while claiming to serve it.
[C1.7] Elite universities are imperfect instruments of fairness. But "imperfect engine of mobility" is not "institution of inheritance." The distinction matters enormously, because the reforms that expand access depend on recognizing these institutions as worth fixing—not as enemies to be punished.
