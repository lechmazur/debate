[P1.1] The question is whether courts should recognize a fundamental right to a minimally clean and healthy environment enforceable against governments. The answer must be yes.
[P1.2] Start with what environmental degradation actually does to people. Contaminated water causes cancer clusters. Air pollution triggers asthma and heart disease. Toxic waste sites leach into backyards.
[P1.3] These are not abstract policy preferences—they are direct assaults on life and health. When a government permits a landfill beside a school or lets lead poison a city's water pipes, it has actively participated in harming its citizens. A right to a minimally clean environment is simply the logical extension of rights we already recognize: the right to life, to bodily integrity, to property.
[P1.4] Opponents will argue this belongs in legislatures. But legislatures have failed systematically. Environmental harm concentrates in poor and minority communities with the least political power—precisely the scenario where majoritarian processes offer no protection. Courts exist to enforce rights that democracy tramples.
[P1.5] The standard here is deliberately minimal: not pristine wilderness, not perfect air, but the basic conditions necessary for human health and dignity. This is not asking courts to administer environmental policy. It is asking them to say that some governmental actions—or sustained inaction—fall below the constitutional floor of what a legitimate government may inflict on its people.
[P1.6] Consider Flint, Michigan. Government decisions poisoned an entire city's water supply. The victims had no meaningful constitutional recourse. That failure is exactly what this proposition addresses.
[P1.7] Rights exist to constrain government power. A government that can legally expose its citizens to known carcinogens and toxic contamination without constitutional limit has escaped one of the core purposes of constitutionalism itself. Courts must step in where political processes fail—because some harms are too permanent, too irreparable, and too fundamental to leave unprotected.

[C1.3] What about naturally occurring arsenic in groundwater? A constitutional right requires a judicially manageable standard, but environmental health involves continuous scientific tradeoffs—acceptable risk levels, cost-benefit analysis, competing land uses. Courts cannot draw the line between "minimally clean" and "not clean enough" without making precisely the policy judgments they are institutionally unfit to make. Every contaminant has a dose-response curve; there is no bright constitutional boundary.
[C1.4] Second, constitutionalizing this right distorts democratic governance. Environmental protection requires constant, dynamic balancing of economic activity, energy needs, public health, and ecological preservation. These are quintessentially legislative choices. When courts declare a "right," they freeze the analysis: the question shifts from "what policy best serves complex public interests?" to "has the government violated a right?"
[C1.5] This removes ongoing tradeoffs from accountable democratic bodies and hands them to unelected judges crafting remedies they lack the capacity to implement or monitor.
[C1.6] Third, the proposition solves the wrong problem. Consider Flint, which PRO invokes. Flint's water crisis involved violations of existing statutory law—the Safe Drinking Water Act, state environmental regulations, negligence standards. The failure was enforcement, not a gap in constitutional rights.
[C1.7] A constitutional right to clean water would not have added a single safeguard that existing law did not already provide. What Flint needed was competent oversight and political will, not judicial invention of new fundamental rights.
[C1.8] The deeper issue is that not every serious harm warrants constitutionalization. Poverty causes devastating health consequences. Inadequate healthcare kills people. Yet we recognize these as policy problems requiring legislative solutions, not judicial decrees.
[C1.9] Environmental quality belongs in the same category—important, urgent, but fundamentally a matter of democratic policy choice, not constitutional adjudication. Courts should not be the bodies deciding what level of pollution a society must tolerate.
