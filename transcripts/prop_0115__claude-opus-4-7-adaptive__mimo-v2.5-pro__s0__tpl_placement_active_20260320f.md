[P1.1] Buy-now-pay-later isn't a checkout feature. It's credit. A consumer receives goods today and owes scheduled payments tomorrow, with late fees, collections risk, and credit consequences if they miss. The question isn't whether to call it credit—economically it already is.
[P1.2] The question is whether the rules should match the function. They should.
[P1.3] Three reasons carry the motion.
[P1.4] First, equivalent risk demands equivalent protection. Credit cards come with disclosure standards, dispute rights, chargeback protections, billing-error procedures, and ability-to-repay norms because borrowers need them. A shopper splitting a $600 mattress into four payments faces the same harms a card user faces: hidden fees, surprise interest on deferred-interest plans, double-charges, defective goods, and debt spirals from stacking multiple loans. Treating BNPL as a "feature" strips those protections precisely when the borrower is least sophisticated—at the impulse moment of checkout, often on a phone, often without comparing terms.
[P1.5] Second, BNPL uniquely encourages debt stacking. Unlike a single credit line with a visible balance, a user can open six BNPL plans across six merchants in an afternoon, none reported to bureaus, none visible to the next lender. That's not a smaller risk than a credit card—it's a larger one, because no underwriter sees the whole picture. Credit-card-style rules (reporting, ability-to-repay checks, consolidated disclosures) are the minimum needed to make this visible.
[P1.6] Third, the "ordinary checkout feature" framing is the harm. It's designed to feel frictionless, like choosing gift wrap. That UX deliberately obscures that you're signing a loan. Light-touch rules let providers exploit that illusion.
[P1.7] Credit-card-style rules force the product to behave like what it actually is—a loan with rights attached.
[P1.8] CON will say BNPL is small-dollar, often interest-free, and innovative. Fine—then the rules can be proportionate within the credit-card framework. But "smaller" and "newer" don't justify stripping dispute rights and underwriting from millions of borrowers. Match the regulation to the function, not the marketing.

[C1.2] Credit-card regulation didn't emerge from thin air. It evolved to address specific harms: revolving balances that compound indefinitely, minimum-payment structures that trap borrowers in decades-long debt, opaque variable interest rates, and aggressive marketing to people who can't repay. The CARD Act, Truth in Lending disclosures, ability-to-repay rules—these are surgical responses to a product designed to profit from persistent, compounding consumer debt.
[C1.3] BNPL is structurally different in ways that matter. A typical BNPL plan splits a purchase into four fixed installments over six to eight weeks. There is no revolving balance. There is no compounding interest on the vast majority of plans.
[C1.4] The repayment timeline is short and predetermined. The consumer cannot spiral into a decade-long minimum-payment trap because the product doesn't have one. Applying credit-card machinery to this structure is like prescribing chemotherapy for a common cold—the treatment is designed for a disease the patient doesn't have.
[C1.5] What would actually happen under credit-card-style regulation? Ability-to-repay checks on a $40 sweater. Full TILA disclosure booklets at checkout. Bureau reporting infrastructure that would either exclude thin-file borrowers entirely or bury them in credit-marketing databases they never asked to join.
[C1.6] The PRO side waves this away as "proportionate within the framework," but credit-card rules aren't a menu—they're an interlocking system built around revolving debt. You cannot adopt the framework without adopting its assumptions.
[C1.7] The right regulatory approach is bespoke: targeted rules addressing BNPL's *actual* risks—stacking visibility, late-fee caps, clear pre-checkout disclosure, and complaint resolution. [C1.8] Credit-card regulation solves credit-card problems. BNPL needs its own playbook.
