[P1.1] The strongest argument for device-light early education isn't ideological nostalgia—it's developmental timing. Children in the primary grades are not simply small adults who happen to be learning to read; they are in a critical window where the physical acts of learning—gripping a pencil, forming letters, turning pages—are themselves cognitive events. Handwriting engages more neural pathways than tapping a glass screen, and the research literature on this point is consistent enough that multiple education systems have recently reintroduced mandatory cursive or handwriting instruction precisely because they lost ground when they abandoned it too quickly.
[P1.2] The proposition is also carefully designed. It doesn't ban screens from schooling; it sequences them. Young children get paper-first learning, and tablets remain available once foundational literacy and numeracy are established. That sequencing matters because early screen exposure undermines the very skills devices will later demand.
[P1.3] A child who cannot sustain attention on a page will not become a better reader by being handed a tablet—she will become a better swiper.
[P1.4] Distraction is not a bug in current devices; it is an architectural feature. Even locked-down educational tablets carry the affordance of scrolling, tapping, and rapid reward cycles that are neurologically calibrated to interrupt sustained focus. Paper carries none of those affordances. A worksheet cannot notify you.
[P1.5] A book cannot auto-play the next video. For a six-year-old still developing inhibitory control, that structural difference is decisive.
[P1.6] CON will likely argue that technology access improves equity and engagement. But equity in broken habits is no gain. If devices in early grades accelerate shallow processing, the students most disadvantaged by that tradeoff are those who lack compensating supports at home—exactly the students equity arguments are meant to protect.
[P1.7] The central question is not whether technology belongs in education. It does, later. The question is whether the developmental sequence matters. It does, profoundly—and device-light early grades preserve it.
