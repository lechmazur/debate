[P1.2] These companies know, with GPS precision, that their vehicles are ridden on sidewalks and through pedestrian plazas every single day. They collect this data. They use it to optimize fleet placement. Yet they do remarkably little to prevent the foreseeable injuries that result.

[P1.5] When a company possesses a cost-effective tool to prevent foreseeable harm and declines to use it, tort law has traditionally responded by imposing liability.
[P1.6] Right now, the injured pedestrian bears almost all the cost. The rider is often judgment-proof—uninsured, riding anonymously, sometimes a tourist. The company collects its fee and walks away. [P1.7] Increased legal responsibility would realign incentives. Companies would invest in geofencing, rider education, and insurance pools—not out of altruism, but because their bottom line would demand it.
