[P1.1] Across-the-board minimum wage increases are blunt instruments. They treat the entire economy as a monolith, applying the same wage floor to a high-margin tech firm in a major city and a struggling independent retail shop in a rural county. This creates a disproportionate risk of employment loss, as businesses that cannot absorb the mandated hike are often forced to automate jobs, reduce hours, or close their doors entirely.
[P1.2] Sectoral bargaining and wage boards offer a superior alternative by decentralizing wage-setting to the industry level. By bringing together employer representatives and worker organizations, these mechanisms allow for negotiated wage standards that reflect the specific economic realities of a sector. Instead of a one-size-fits-all mandate that catches small, vulnerable businesses in the crossfire, these systems allow for "productivity bargaining." Employers and workers can negotiate wage increases alongside adjustments to operations, scheduling, or training, ensuring that compensation growth is sustainable and productive rather than purely extractive.
[P1.3] Crucially, this structure significantly lowers the risk of employment loss. When employer representatives are at the table, they can ensure wage floors are set at levels the industry can actually bear, preventing the catastrophic financial "cliff" that often triggers layoffs during aggressive, blanket federal hikes. This collaborative approach transforms wage-setting from a zero-sum conflict into a process of establishing sector-wide standards that stabilize the market and neutralize the incentive for firms to compete solely by suppressing wages.
[P1.4] The economic logic is clear: industry-specific standards are inherently more accurate than national ones. By tailoring the wage floor to the capacity, productivity, and regional variation of a specific sector, we can achieve meaningful, durable pay gains for low-wage workers without triggering the job destruction inherent in broad-spectrum policies. It is a more precise, equitable, and stable way to raise the floor for the American workforce.
