[P1.1] Tight housing markets already leave working and middle class local families locked out of homeownership, and large institutional investors are making that crisis drastically worse. These firms operate with massive cash reserves, allowing them to outbid ordinary local buyers by tens of thousands of dollars, waive inspections and appraisal contingencies, and buy up dozens or hundreds of homes at a time before local families even have a chance to tour properties. In many of the most strained markets, these investors have purchased roughly a quarter of all single-family homes sold in recent years, pulling critical supply off the market for owner-occupants.
[P1.2] The harms are not abstract: a local elementary school teacher who has spent five years saving for a 20% down payment on a starter home cannot compete with a firm offering 10% over asking price all cash with no conditions. When those firms win bids, they convert the homes to rental units, charge above-market rent, and raise rates annually at levels that far outpace local wage growth, locking the same families out of both homeownership and affordable rental housing, and cutting them off from the intergenerational equity building that has been the core of middle class stability for generations.
[P1.3] This proposed restriction is narrowly targeted: it applies only to large institutional buyers, only in markets where crowd-out of local buyers is already documented, and does not impact small local landlords, individual second home buyers, or institutional investment in new housing construction. It frees up existing limited supply for the people who actually live and work in these communities, without slowing new housing growth.
