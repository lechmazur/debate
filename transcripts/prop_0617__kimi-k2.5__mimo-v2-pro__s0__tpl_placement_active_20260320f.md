[P1.1] Digital markets defy traditional antitrust because they race on speed, not price. When Google bought YouTube or Facebook acquired Instagram, regulators faced a paradox: the harms were speculative, but the integration irreversible. We must flip the burden of proof.
[P1.2] Dominant platforms operate in zero-price ecosystems where consumer welfare cannot be measured by dollars saved. Value is extracted through data harvesting, attention capture, and ecosystem lock-in rather than prices. Under current law, agencies must prove future price increases to block a deal—a logical impossibility when the product is free and the monopoly manifests as reduced innovation or privacy erosion. A presumption of anti-competitiveness aligns doctrine with reality: these are not ordinary mergers in widget markets, but strategic fortifications of data moats.
[P1.3] The information asymmetry is structural. When Amazon buys a nascent competitor or Google acquires a mapping startup, only the acquirer knows whether the target threatens its core business. Regulators see only present revenue, not foreclosed futures. The "killer acquisition" pattern—buying firms precisely to neutralize competitive threats—thrives because proof of intent requires psychic foresight.
[P1.4] Shifting the burden forces dominant platforms to demonstrate concrete, verifiable consumer benefits rather than vague synergies or efficiency claims.
[P1.5] Most critically, merger remedies are illusory here. Once Instagram's data flows into Facebook's social graph or Waze integrates with Google Maps, structural separation becomes technically impossible. The status quo offers regulators a choice between over-permissiveness and irreversible error. A rebuttable presumption preserves legitimate deal flow while requiring dominant firms to prove substantial consumer benefit before the cage locks shut.
[P1.6] This is not regulation by speculation. It is realism about power, information, and irreversibility.
