[P1.1] The central question here is simple: should the EU let climate ambition be held hostage by the threat of trade retaliation? The answer is no, and here is why.
[P1.2] The EU has spent years constructing a serious carbon pricing regime through its Emissions Trading System. Every tonne of steel, cement, or aluminum produced inside the EU carries a carbon cost. Every equivalent tonne imported from a country without comparable pricing does not. That asymmetry is not free trade—it is a subsidy for pollution.
[P1.3] A carbon border adjustment mechanism corrects that distortion and makes the single market's climate rules coherent. Without it, European producers face a competitive disadvantage not because they are less efficient, but because their foreign competitors are allowed to externalize climate costs onto the planet.
[P1.4] The retaliation argument, which will dominate the opposing case, proves too much. By that logic, no meaningful climate regulation is ever worth defending, because any standard that raises costs invites complaint. The WTO's general exceptions explicitly recognize measures necessary to protect the environment. A border adjustment calibrated to domestic carbon prices is not a disguised protectionist tariff—it is a mirror.
[P1.5] Courts and trade tribunals distinguish between these categories, and the EU has legal standing to make that case.
[P1.6] Now consider the stakes if the EU retreats. Carbon leakage—where production simply migrates to dirtier jurisdictions—undermines every ton reduced domestically. Worse, it signals to every other economy that climate policy caves under commercial pressure. The EU's leverage as the world's largest consumer market is precisely the instrument that can move global norms.
[P1.7] Retaliation by trading partners is a short-term cost weighed against the long-term benefit of establishing that high-carbon imports have a price.
[P1.8] The choice is not between free trade and climate protection. It is between a rules-based system that prices carbon consistently and one that rewards whoever is willing to pollute most cheaply.
