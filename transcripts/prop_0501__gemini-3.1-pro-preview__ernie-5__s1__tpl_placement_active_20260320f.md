[P1.1] Public universities exist primarily to serve the public good of their host regions. Capping international recruitment under these conditions is a necessary step to protect local communities and domestic students from displacement.
[P1.2] While international students bring valuable diversity and revenue, treating them as unconstrained financial assets creates severe negative externalities. Universities collect the lucrative tuition, but the surrounding municipality absorbs the infrastructural shock. Because international students lack local family networks and generally cannot commute from outside the region, they exert intense, highly localized pressure on the private rental market near campuses.
[P1.3] This artificial demand spike drives up rents and displaces vulnerable local residents. Critically, it also harms the domestic students the university was built to serve. Leaving capacity unconstrained transforms a publicly funded asset into an engine of regional inequality.
[P1.4] Implementing a cap provides a vital forcing function. It requires public universities to align their financial ambitions with the physical realities of their host cities. If a university wishes to expand its international cohort, it must first invest in building the necessary on-campus housing to accommodate that growth. Without a cap, universities have zero financial incentive to fix the housing deficits they actively worsen.
[P1.5] They will simply continue to privatize the revenue of global enrollment while socializing the housing costs onto the broader community.
[P1.6] Public universities cannot act as rogue developers, importing massive population surges into communities lacking the infrastructure to house them safely.
