Without a disclosure requirement, listeners have no way to distinguish genuine endorsement from synthetic fabrication.
[P1.2] This isn't hypothetical. Voice-cloning technology has reached the point where a few minutes of sample audio can produce speech nearly indistinguishable from the original speaker. Creators and ad agencies face a clear economic incentive to scale production by generating host-read spots synthetically, eliminating scheduling friction and reducing costs. The audience, meanwhile, continues to interpret the ad as a personal recommendation backed by the host's reputation.
[P1.3] That gap between perception and reality is textbook consumer deception.
[P1.4] Existing regulatory frameworks already recognize this principle. Endorsement disclosure rules require creators to flag paid partnerships and material connections because audiences deserve to know when someone has a financial motive. [P1.5] If we require disclosure of financial relationships, requiring disclosure of synthetic speech is a straightforward, logically consistent extension.
[P1.6] The burden of this mandate is minimal. A brief label—audio, visual, or textual—is all that's needed. It doesn't ban the technology. It doesn't impose creative restrictions.
