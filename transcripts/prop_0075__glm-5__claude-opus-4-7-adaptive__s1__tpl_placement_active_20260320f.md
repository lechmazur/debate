[P1.1] Religious freedom deserves protection—but not a blank check. The distinction between core religious functions and commercial activity marks where legitimate accommodation ends and unjustified privilege begins.
[P1.2] Consider a church hiring its senior minister. The decision involves interpreting doctrine, spiritual leadership, and representing the faith to its congregation. Anti-discrimination rules that forced churches to consider applicants who reject their teachings would directly undermine religious exercise itself. This is core religious function.
[P1.3] Now consider a hospital run by a religious organization. It serves patients of all faiths, receives public Medicare and Medicaid funds, employs workers through secular labor markets, and advertises services to the general public. When such an institution fires a nurse for being gay—or refuses to treat a transgender patient—it is not exercising religion in any meaningful sense. It is a commercial actor wielding religious affiliation as a shield for discrimination.
[P1.4] The stakes are not theoretical. Patients turned away from the only hospital in their rural county. Teachers fired from schools that serve diverse communities. Social workers barred from adoption agencies that hold state contracts.
[P1.5] In each case, the institution has stepped into commercial and public spheres where anti-discrimination norms serve essential functions: ensuring access to healthcare, economic opportunity, and basic dignity.
[P1.6] Opponents will argue that religious institutions should control their own affairs without state interference. But when those institutions run hospitals, universities, and charitable enterprises that serve and employ the broader public, they have left the realm of purely religious affairs. They benefit from public infrastructure, public trust, and often public funding. They cannot simultaneously claim the privileges of religious autonomy and the benefits of commercial participation.
[P1.7] The motion simply requires drawing a line that already exists in principle: religious exemptions belong where religion is actually being practiced, not wherever religious ownership extends. A framework that protects the minister but not the hospital administrator, the seminary but not the commercial university, honors both religious freedom and the equal dignity of every person seeking care, work, or service.

[C1.1] The proposition sounds moderate, but it smuggles in a radically narrow theology: that religion happens in the sanctuary on Sunday, and everything else is "commercial." That is not how most faiths understand themselves, and it is not how a free society should treat them.
[C1.2] Start with what religion actually is. For Catholics, Jews, Muslims, evangelicals, and countless others, feeding the hungry, healing the sick, sheltering the orphan, and teaching the young are not commercial sidelines. They are commanded acts of faith—often the central ones. A Catholic hospital is not a hospital that happens to be Catholic; its mission, its ethics, its reason for existing is religious.
[C1.3] The "tightly tied to core religious functions" test pretends otherwise, quietly redefining centuries of religious practice as mere business.
[C1.4] Second, institutional integrity is indivisible. A religious school cannot transmit a coherent vision of the good life if it loses control over who teaches math, who runs the dorm, who coaches the team. Every employee shapes the community the faith is trying to form. The minister-versus-administrator line the opposition wants to draw is a line religious traditions themselves reject—and the state has no business telling a faith which of its own roles are "really" religious.
[C1.5] Third, the proposition misdescribes the stakes. The real choice is not "protect patients" versus "protect bigots." It is whether pluralism tolerates institutions that think differently from the state. Label religious adoption agencies "commercial" and many simply close—as Catholic Charities did in Massachusetts and Illinois—leaving fewer placements for vulnerable children, not more.
[C1.6] The communities these charities serve are the first losers.
[C1.7] Finally, the test is unworkable. Courts would be forced to adjudicate what counts as "core" religion—exactly the entanglement the First Amendment was meant to prevent. A mature framework balances concrete harm against mission integrity case by case. It does not exile faith from the hospital, the classroom, and the soup kitchen by redefinition.

[P2.6] The opposition asks us to treat a Catholic hospital as identical to a Catholic church. But the hospital heals bodies through secular medical science; the church shepherds souls through doctrine and worship.
