[P1.1] Accessibility that exists in theory but not in practice is not accessibility at all. When a wheelchair user must book paratransit 24 hours ahead, wait an hour past the scheduled pickup, and endure a circuitous route that turns a fifteen-minute trip into ninety minutes, the system has not provided meaningful access—it has provided a separate and unequal service that penalizes disability.
[P1.2] The proposition demands parity in wait times and convenience because timing is the currency of modern transportation. A job interview, a medical appointment, a child's school pickup—these are time-sensitive commitments. When wheelchair users cannot reliably predict their arrival time, they lose control over their own lives. The technical availability of accessible vehicles matters little if they arrive too late to matter.
[P1.3] Ride-hail companies already possess the technology to dispatch vehicles efficiently. They can incentify drivers of accessible vehicles, pool accessible rides, and deploy surge pricing strategically. Public transit agencies can mandate that new bus and rail fleets be universally accessible while prioritizing accessible vehicles on high-ridership routes. The barrier is not technical impossibility—it is the choice to treat wheelchair access as an afterthought rather than a design requirement.
[P1.4] Opponents will cite cost. But we already accept that public infrastructure must bear costs to ensure equal access—curb cuts, elevators, audible signals. We do not ask blind pedestrians to fund their own crossing signals or wheelchair users to pay surcharges for ramp access to public buildings. Transportation is no different.
[P1.5] The cost of inclusion belongs to the system, not the individual.
[P1.6] A transportation system that works for some but not all has failed its public purpose. This proposition merely requires that wheelchair users be treated as passengers, not problems to be scheduled around.
