[P1.1] Aging is inevitable, yet our current approach to long-term care relies on a fantasy: that families can indefinitely absorb the catastrophic costs of chronic decline without social support. This is not just inefficient; it is economically destructive and morally precarious.
[P1.2] First, reliance on private savings and family care creates a massive, hidden tax on economic productivity. When we rely on informal family care, we effectively mandate that one family member—disproportionately women—exits the workforce to provide unpaid labor. As demographics shift toward smaller families and higher dual-income requirements, this model is reaching a breaking point. It depletes family wealth, stifles careers, and drags down aggregate GDP.

[P1.4] Second, relying on means-testing creates a "poverty trap." The current system in many places effectively requires families to exhaust their life savings, sell assets, and pauperize themselves before they qualify for assistance. This is the opposite of economic planning; it is a systematic destruction of middle-class wealth. A universal or near-universal system pools the risk of chronic disability across the entire population, preventing the arbitrary ruin of families unlucky enough to face high care costs.
[P1.5] It treats long-term care as what it actually is: an insurable, predictable lifecycle risk that individual private savings are fundamentally ill-equipped to hedge against.
[P1.6] Private insurance markets for long-term care remain expensive and prone to failure because they cannot effectively pool the high, correlated risks of late-life disability without cherry-picking the healthy, which leaves the most vulnerable stranded. Only a universal public framework provides the stability to ensure dignity. By moving to a social insurance model, we stop viewing elder care as an individual failure to save, and start viewing it as a shared foundation for a stable society. The path forward is not pauperization through means-testing, but the collective security of a universal system.
