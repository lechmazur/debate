[C1.1] Prioritizing residents with special booking rights or discounts at fragile heritage sites during peak seasons does not restore any lost "soul"—it fractures the very economic and managerial incentives that keep those sites standing at all. Heritage infrastructure is expensive to maintain, restore, and protect from wear; in high-pressure cities the reliable, high-volume revenue from international visitors subsidizes opening hours, conservation staff, and security that locals alone could never fund. Discriminating against that revenue stream in peak months forces either higher baseline taxes on the same residents or deferred maintenance that accelerates decay of the monuments everyone claims to cherish.
[C1.2] The supposed injustice of "competing on market terms" is illusory. Locals already enjoy massive structural advantages: they live minutes from the sites, can visit off-peak when prices are lower or crowds thinner, and often hold annual passes or discounted citizen cards that already exist in places like Rome, Barcelona, or Kyoto. Creating formal two-tier booking windows during the busiest periods invites fraud, administrative overhead, and resentment from visitors who then feel treated as walking ATMs. That resentment travels: fewer return visits, lower overall income, and pressure to raise general admission prices even for residents later.
[C1.3] Moreover, the stewardship argument collapses under scrutiny. Residents do not automatically become better custodians simply by flashing an ID at the gate; many never visit these sites at all, while passionate local volunteers and professionals already shape conservation policy regardless of ticketing. Turning museums into de-facto community centers during tourist surges risks concentrating wear on fragile objects from repeated local use and dilutes the revenue needed for climate-controlled displays or archaeological protection.
[C1.4] The policy therefore damages the sites it pretends to save, burdens city budgets, breeds enforcement problems, and offers locals benefits they largely already possess in less coercive forms. Cities should manage tourism with better crowd-flow tools and year-round pricing strategies, not by declaring their own citizens a protected class at the ticket booth.
