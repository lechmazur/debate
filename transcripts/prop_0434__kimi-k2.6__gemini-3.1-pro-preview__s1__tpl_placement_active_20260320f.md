[P1.1] Noise disputes are the most common complaint in multi-unit housing, yet they are resolved almost entirely through testimony and attrition. One party claims disruption; the other claims hypersensitivity. Without objective measurement, these cases degenerate into credibility contests that favor the wealthy, the legally connected, or simply the tenant willing to outlast an eviction threat. Certified noise-monitoring evidence should be admissible more broadly because it replaces asymmetric warfare with facts.
[P1.2] A certified device is calibrated to industry standards, time-stamped, and tamper-resistant. It records decibel levels, not conversation content, producing data equivalent to a utility meter or a speedometer. Municipal codes already set numeric noise limits; it is absurd that courts and landlords routinely ignore the one tool capable of proving whether those limits were breached. Consider a tenant enduring chronic low-frequency bass from upstairs.
[P1.3] Under current practice, they are dismissed as overly sensitive or told to buy earplugs. A certified log showing repeated violations of the municipal threshold transforms a subjective grievance into a documented, enforceable lease violation.
[P1.4] Landlords benefit just as much as tenants. Property managers currently hesitate to intervene in noise conflicts because they lack neutral documentation, fearing fair-housing retaliation claims or wrongful eviction suits if they appear to take sides. Certified evidence gives them an objective, color-blind trigger for lease enforcement, stripping disputes of personal bias and reducing legal exposure for everyone involved, including neighbors left to absorb the cost of prolonged conflict.
[P1.5] Opponents may invoke privacy, but a decibel monitor is acoustically anonymous. It captures volume at a wall or property line, not words or identities. It is far less invasive than video doorbells and hallway cameras that landlords deploy without controversy today. The motion does not mandate surveillance in every unit; it simply removes arbitrary barriers to admitting neutral, scientific proof where it is gathered.
[P1.6] The central question is whether rental and nuisance disputes should turn on who tells a better story, or on whether measurable harm actually occurred. Choosing facts over friction is why the proposition must pass.
