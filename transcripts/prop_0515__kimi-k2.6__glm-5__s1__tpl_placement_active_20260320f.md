[P1.1] Early literacy is the gatekeeper to all future learning. When children fail to read fluently by age eight, the deficit compounds across every subject, narrowing life chances before a child has chosen them. Too many students still finish the early years unable to decode confidently. Structured reading instruction—systematic, explicit phonics taught through a carefully sequenced curriculum—must be the non-negotiable foundation of early literacy policy, not something traded away for the sake of classroom autonomy.
[P1.2] Structured instruction treats reading as a code that must be taught, not intuited. By breaking language into sound-symbol relationships and building fluency through cumulative, deliberate practice, it ensures no child is left to infer what they were never shown. Autonomy sounds like respect, but scattered across thousands of individual classrooms it produces a patchwork of quality: some teachers use proven methods, while others fall back on guessing strategies like multi-cueing or sight-word memorization that bypass decoding entirely. The result is predictable and destructive variation in outcomes that tracks not student ability, but which adult a child draws.
[P1.3] That variation is not neutral; it is actively regressive. Children from literacy-poor homes arrive without extensive print exposure and face the steepest climb. They cannot afford a year with an autonomous instructor who rejects systematic phonics for an unproven alternative, because no one at home will compensate. Structured curricula act as an equity guarantee, delivering baseline instructional quality regardless of a teacher’s personal ideology or training gaps.
[P1.4] Delegating curriculum design to classrooms effectively grants the right to experiment with a child’s only chance at a literate future.
[P1.5] Curriculum design is a delivery system for proven outcomes, not expressive individual art. Policy should mandate structured reading programs and reserve autonomy for implementation details—pacing, culture, relationship-building—while keeping the instructional core standardized and protected from drift. The state’s duty is to ensure every child learns to read. Prioritizing adult preference over structured instruction betrays that duty.
