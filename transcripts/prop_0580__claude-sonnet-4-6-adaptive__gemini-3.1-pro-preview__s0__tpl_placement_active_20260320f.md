[P1.1] The fastest way to reduce child poverty is to put money in the hands of families who are actually poor. Monthly child tax credit payments do exactly that. Childcare subsidies do not, at least not with the same reach, the same efficiency, or the same immediacy.
[P1.2] Start with coverage. Childcare subsidies are structurally limited to families who use formal, licensed childcare—a category that excludes rural families with no nearby provider, families where a grandparent or relative watches the children, and families where one parent has stepped back from the workforce entirely. Cash payments reach every eligible child regardless of care arrangement. If the goal is reducing poverty across the entire child population, a tool that works for all of them wins before the analysis even gets difficult.
[P1.3] The 2021 American experience makes the case vivid. When the Child Tax Credit was expanded, made fully refundable, and delivered monthly, researchers tracking poverty in near-real time observed child poverty fall by roughly half. When payments stopped, the rate shot back up within months. That is a nearly instantaneous policy response affecting millions of children—something no childcare infrastructure expansion could plausibly replicate in that timeframe or at that cost.
[P1.4] Cost-effectiveness is where the comparison becomes stark. Childcare subsidies require building and maintaining provider networks, credentialing workers, running administrative eligibility systems at the state level, and managing vendor payments. Much of that spending never reaches a child. Cash credits route through the existing tax infrastructure.
[P1.5] Overhead is dramatically lower, meaning a larger share of every dollar actually reduces poverty.
[P1.6] CON will argue that childcare enables parents to work, generating income that reduces poverty long-term. That mechanism is real but limited: many poor families already have employed parents, and their poverty persists because wages are too low, not because childcare is unavailable. Monthly cash payments address the income gap directly. That is the sharper, faster, broader tool—and cost-effectiveness tracks all three dimensions.
