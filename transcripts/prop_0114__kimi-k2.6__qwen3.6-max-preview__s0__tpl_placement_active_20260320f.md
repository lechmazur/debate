[P1.1] The economies we claim to measure in GDP would collapse without the daily labor of feeding, bathing, transporting, and medically monitoring elderly parents, disabled partners, and young children. Yet when a worker leaves paid employment to perform this essential maintenance of human life, pension systems record nothing but absence. That absence hardens into destitution in old age. The proposition asks governments to stop pretending that unpaid care is economically irrelevant, and instead credit caregivers within social-insurance frameworks.
[P1.2] The current arrangement extracts a brutal wealth transfer from caregivers—still disproportionately women—to the formal labor market. Someone who spends a decade out of paid work to care for an aging parent does not cease productive activity; she merely performs it in a zone the state chooses not to see. When she retires, contributory pension formulas punish her with reduced benefits or none at all, effectively taxing her for keeping relatives out of costly institutions. This is not private charity; it is socially necessary labor that the state free-rides upon, concentrating old-age poverty on those who sacrificed earnings.
[P1.3] Social-insurance credits solve the problem without requiring governments to pay full wages during care. A credit acknowledges that care work substitutes for costly state services: home care, nursing facilities, child care. By recording contributions during caregiving periods, the state treats care as what it actually is—indispensable economic infrastructure—while protecting caregivers from poverty in later life and preventing care interruptions from derailing retirement security. The mechanism is straightforward: if the state can track years of salaried employment, it can track verified caregiving responsibilities and credit them through existing channels.
[P1.4] Opponents will protest cost or administrative difficulty, but societies already administer vastly complex tax, pension, and benefit systems. The true fiscal burden lies in the status quo: forced institutionalization, emergency interventions, and elderly poverty from contribution gaps. Recognizing care through pension credits is not a handout. It is a correction to a ledger that records care work as zero.
