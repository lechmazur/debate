[P1.1] Water-stressed cities face a defining choice: keep reaching farther for someone else's water, or close the loop on the water they already have. Direct potable reuse is the smarter priority, and the case rests on three pillars: resilience, cost trajectory, and ethics.
[P1.2] **Resilience.** Imported water depends on infrastructure stretching hundreds of miles—aqueducts, pumping stations, canals—all vulnerable to earthquakes, drought in the source basin, political disputes, and climate shifts. When Southern California's supplies from the Colorado River get curtailed, or when Cape Town's distant reservoirs dry up, the entire city faces crisis. Direct potable reuse draws from wastewater that is generated locally every single day, rain or shine. It converts a liability into an asset and decouples a city's survival from distant hydrology it cannot control.
[P1.3] **Cost trajectory.** Long-distance water transfers require massive capital for conveyances and enormous ongoing energy costs for pumping, often over mountain ranges. These costs only climb as source basins themselves face scarcity and demand premium prices. Advanced treatment for potable reuse—reverse osmosis, advanced oxidation, UV disinfection—has fallen steeply in cost over the past two decades and continues to improve. Windhoek, Namibia has operated direct potable reuse safely since the 1960s.
[P1.4] More recently, facilities in Texas and elsewhere have demonstrated that per-gallon costs for advanced purified water are competitive with or cheaper than new imported supplies, especially once you factor in conveyance energy and right-of-way acquisition.
[P1.5] **Ethics.** Importing water from distant basins externalizes the problem. It dries out rural communities, damages ecosystems at the source, and creates zero-sum conflicts between agricultural regions and thirsty cities. Prioritizing reuse means a city takes responsibility for its own demand rather than strip-mining another community's water future.
[P1.6] The "yuck factor" is real but manageable; public acceptance has grown wherever utilities invest in education and transparency, as Singapore's NEWater program demonstrates. Technology answers the safety question. The remaining question is political will.
[P1.7] Water-stressed cities should build the future with the water they already possess, not gamble on increasingly unreliable imports from basins that are themselves running dry.
