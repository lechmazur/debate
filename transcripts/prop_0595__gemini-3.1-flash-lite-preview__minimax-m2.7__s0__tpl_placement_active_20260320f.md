[P1.1] Workplace AI is not just another office software update; it is a fundamental shift in the power dynamic between employer and employee. When management deploys systems for algorithmic scheduling, performance monitoring, or automated hiring, they are codifying management practices into opaque code. Without a requirement to negotiate, large employers face no friction when introducing systems that intensify labor, erode privacy, or automate tasks based on flawed datasets.
[P1.2] The fundamental argument for mandatory negotiation through elected worker councils is the correction of an inherent information asymmetry. Employers possess the technical roadmap for AI deployment, but workers possess the operational reality. Management often prioritizes efficiency metrics that, in practice, create hazardous or impossible working conditions. By requiring negotiation, we ensure that the people closest to the work—who understand where automated systems break down or create toxic stress—have a seat at the table to flag these issues before they become entrenched policies.
[P1.3] This is not an attempt to stifle innovation, but to ensure it is sustainable and human-centric. Unilateral AI deployment is a gamble with the company’s most valuable asset: its human workforce. Conversely, codetermination frameworks create a feedback loop where AI tools are adjusted to complement human expertise rather than replace or surveil it.
[P1.4] Legal requirements for negotiation transform AI from a tool of top-down control into a tool for collaborative optimization.
[P1.5] If we leave AI deployment solely to the unilateral discretion of large employers, we accept a workplace governed by unaccountable black boxes. Requiring negotiations with elected worker councils ensures that the future of work is not just efficient, but democratic, stable, and transparent.
