[P1.1] Low-income and low-density neighborhoods already face endemic transit neglect: regular ridehail drivers regularly skip pickups in majority-low-income areas, local bus routes run once an hour if at all, and residents without cars are roughly 3 times more likely to miss work shifts due to transit failures, per widely cited public transit equity research. [P1.2] The proposed requirement for reasonably similar reliability across neighborhoods is not an overreach: it is a basic quid pro quo for the use of shared public infrastructure that residents of low-demand, low-income areas pay into just as much as residents of wealthy districts. A single mom working a night janitor shift in an outer-ring low-income neighborhood should not have to wait 2 hours for a ride when a downtown office worker can get one in 2 minutes, especially when the robotaxi service only exists because the city let them test on all public roads for years.
[P1.3] Critics will argue this rule cuts into profits, but the requirement only calls for reasonably similar reliability, not identical service levels, and cities can tie extended operating permits to compliance, giving fleets a clear incentive to balance margin goals with public obligation. This rule also grows the customer base for robotaxi services long-term, unlocking ridership from communities that have been locked out of private ride services entirely, generating sustainable new revenue streams that offset the cost of serving lower-demand areas.
