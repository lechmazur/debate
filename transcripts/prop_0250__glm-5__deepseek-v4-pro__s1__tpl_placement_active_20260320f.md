[P1.1] Democracy's legitimacy rests on a simple proposition: the governed must have a voice in choosing those who govern. Yet in stable democracies worldwide, that voice has grown faint. Turnout rates hover between fifty and seventy percent in many developed nations, with off-cycle and local elections often drawing far fewer. The result is not a representative democracy but a captured one—where the preferences of the motivated, the affluent, and the partisan few outweigh the needs of the disengaged many.
[P1.2] Compulsory voting with proportional civil penalties directly addresses this structural failure. The mechanism is straightforward: a small fine or administrative fee for non-participation, comparable to a parking ticket, enforced through standard government processes. Australia has employed this system for decades, consistently achieving turnout above ninety percent. The penalty is not punitive in intent but normative in effect—it signals that voting is a civic duty, not merely a right to be exercised at whim.
[P1.3] The representative payoff is substantial. When nearly everyone votes, parties cannot afford to cater exclusively to their base. They must build coalitions that reach the median citizen, not the median primary voter. Research consistently shows that non-voters skew younger, poorer, and more likely to belong to marginalized groups.
[P1.4] Voluntary voting systematically excludes these voices, producing policy outcomes that favor older, wealthier, and more entrenched interests.
[P1.5] Opponents will cry coercion. But a modest fine, proportionate to income and avoidable by simply showing up—or in many systems, by submitting a blank ballot—hardly constitutes oppression. We mandate jury service and tax payment; we require school attendance for children. These obligations are the price of collective self-governance.
[P1.6] Voting is no different.
[P1.7] The question before us is whether we accept a democracy that represents only those motivated to participate, or whether we build a system that actively draws every citizen into the political community. Stable democracies have the institutional capacity to enforce participation. They also have the most to lose from gradual delegitimization. Mandatory voting is the remedy.
