[P1.1] The primary reason housing remains unaffordable in advanced economies is that restrictive zoning and slow permitting processes limit the construction of new homes and apartments to levels below what growing populations require. Streamlining these processes reduces delays and compliance expenses, enabling private developers to produce more housing units in locations with strong demand. The resulting increase in supply intensifies competition among properties, which naturally brings down both rental rates and purchase prices for a wider range of households.
[P1.2] Rent control and expanded tenant protections operate differently by fixing prices and increasing landlord obligations without expanding the number of units. Rent caps often prevent rents from reaching levels that justify new investments, leading to fewer apartment complexes being built and existing ones being removed from the market. Tenant protections like restrictions on evictions or requirements for renovations raise the costs and legal risks of providing rental housing, which can cause some owners to convert units to owner-occupied housing or abandon the business.
[P1.3] Concrete experience shows that jurisdictions emphasizing easier building approvals achieve higher rates of housing addition that help stabilize or lower cost trends over the medium term. Supply growth directly counters the scarcity that drives high prices, extending affordability to new residents and young families who otherwise compete for a static inventory. In contrast, the regulatory approach of controls preserves conditions for some while reducing opportunities for others, often resulting in misallocated housing and lower overall quality.
[P1.4] The effectiveness comparison favors supply expansion because it leverages market forces to increase quantity, avoiding the deadweight losses and unintended contractions in supply that accompany direct price and contract interventions. This path leads to greater total availability and sustained pressure toward affordability without the need for continuous enforcement. Tenant protections, by raising barriers to entry and exit in the market, tend to freeze the stock in place and limit the dynamic response needed for long-term relief.

[C1.1] Affordability is not an abstraction about total units; it is the rent a nurse pays this month. Streamlining permitting and zoning may accelerate construction, but in advanced economies it overwhelmingly channels private investment toward luxury and market-rate units where developer margins are highest. Without mandatory affordability requirements, easing rules simply lets the market build what is most profitable—high-end condos and studios for professionals—not the family apartments that working households need. Even in regions with rapid construction, market rents routinely outpace wages for ordinary workers, proving that volume alone does not create accessibility for those in low-wage sectors.
[C1.2] It is a trickle-down bet that relief will eventually arrive, but it comes years too late for families facing eviction now.
[C1.3] The deeper flaw is that supply expansion in built-up advanced economies often accelerates displacement rather than solving it. When neighborhoods are upzoned, land values spike, existing affordable rentals are demolished for premium developments, and longtime residents are priced out to peripheries. The new square footage rarely offsets the affordable stock destroyed, especially when global capital treats housing as an asset class rather than shelter. The filtering theory—that yesterday’s luxury becomes tomorrow’s middle-income housing—breaks down when units are converted to short-term rentals or demolished for denser speculation before they age into lower price brackets.
[C1.4] Rent control and strengthened tenant protections, by contrast, deliver guaranteed cost reductions immediately. They cap the share of income families devote to housing, prevent arbitrary displacement, and preserve the existing affordable stock that decades of market construction have failed to replicate. In cities where construction lags run for years because of labor shortages, high material costs, and finite urban land, waiting for supply to bend the price curve is a recipe for sustained homelessness.
[C1.5] Effectiveness must be measured by certainty, speed, and who benefits. Market-led supply offers none for households in greatest need. Tenant protections and rent stabilization directly reduce what vulnerable people pay and keep them housed. That is the only meaningful test of affordability, and on that standard the regulatory approach wins decisively.

[P2.5] Weighing the two strategies, supply expansion proves more effective for increasing affordability because it enlarges the resource base instead of redistributing a constrained one.
