[C1.3] The EU cannot police the world, and pretending otherwise trades enforceable accountability for performative symbolism.
[C1.4] Next, legal coherence and fragmentation. Territorial jurisdiction in international law generally attaches to the actor or the point of market entry, not to diffuse downstream effects. If every regulator claims authority based on impacts, a single AI system confronts a patchwork of conflicting high-risk definitions, conformity assessments, and liability regimes. [C1.5] The smarter path is to regulate what the EU actually controls. [C1.6] Finally, the GDPR comparison fails. Data protection traces specific, identifiable data flows to defined controllers. High-risk AI impacts are probabilistic, mediated by local operators, and often indirect. Conflating the two regimes invites legal chaos.
[C1.7] Impact-based jurisdiction promises universal protection but delivers unenforceable rules and global regulatory fragmentation. The EU should anchor its high-risk regime in entities it can actually hold accountable: those within its own jurisdiction.
