[P1.1] Ransomware is not an uncontrollable force of nature; it is a calculated, highly lucrative business model. [P1.3] As long as paying remains a legal option, attackers know their extortion will eventually yield a payout.
[P1.4] A legal prohibition fundamentally alters this dynamic. It removes the agonizing choice from the victim's shoulders. When paying is strictly illegal, hackers lose their leverage. Extorting an entity becomes functionally useless if that entity legally cannot transfer the funds.
[P1.5] The ransomware industry will collapse when its return on investment drops to zero.
[P1.6] Crucially, this policy does not abandon victims; it empowers them. The proposition mandates government-funded victim support. [P1.7] Allowing private entities to independently fund international cybercrime under duress is an ongoing failure of national security. [C1.5] Criminalize the payment and the silence becomes near-total, making the problem harder to see and harder to fight.

Government bureaucracies cannot scale to serve thousands of simultaneous victims quickly enough. The proposition trades a working, if imperfect, emergency option for a theoretical replacement that does not yet exist.
[C1.7] The real path to reducing ransomware runs through international law enforcement coordination, mandatory security standards, and better resilience investment—not through prohibitions that punish victims while leaving the attackers untouched.
