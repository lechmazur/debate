That uncertainty will produce widespread self-censorship, removing legitimate adult content along with anything risky rather than narrowing exposure for children.
[C1.2] The parasocial relationship argument does not justify a unique ban. Viewers already encounter unfiltered gambling talk in private group chats, forum threads, and live streams with no disclosure rules at all; pushing promotion off open platforms merely relocates it to harder-to-monitor venues where the same vulnerable audience can still find it without any disclaimers. The financial incentive claim cuts both ways: if platforms and payment processors continue to profit from young users, they will adapt by hiding activity rather than losing revenue, which is exactly what happened when earlier ad restrictions on alcohol pushed branding into social media.
[C1.3] Treating influencers as equivalent to corporate advertisers also ignores that many are individual commentators sharing outcomes, strategies, or losses. Criminalizing their speech when a small slice of their audience is underage would require proving audience composition after the fact, inviting selective prosecution based on complaints rather than measurable harm. That mechanism favors loud campaigns against certain creators while leaving others untouched.
[C1.4] Platform-level age verification and improved parental controls already target the actual point of access without punishing speech. Betting sites can and do block or verify accounts at signup; extending those obligations is narrower and more effective than retroactively judging every video or post against an unknowable minor threshold.
