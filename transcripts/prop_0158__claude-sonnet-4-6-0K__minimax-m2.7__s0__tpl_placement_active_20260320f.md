[P1.2] Proprietary EV charging ecosystems create a fragmentation problem that directly undermines the public interest case for electrification. That is a market that has been deliberately structured to create lock-in, and the locked-out party is the public.
[P1.3] Open interoperability standards solve this at the infrastructure layer. Protocols like OCPP for network communication and ISO 15118 for vehicle-charger handshake already exist. The technology is not hypothetical. What's missing is the mandate.
[P1.4] Without it, dominant networks have every incentive to resist compatibility because incompatibility is their competitive moat—not the quality of their chargers, not their reliability, not their price, but the simple fact that you cannot easily use a competitor.
[P1.5] This matters for policy for three reasons. First, public investment follows vehicle adoption. Governments subsidizing charging networks are paying for a public good, and public goods should not generate private monopoly rents. [P1.6] Third, and most urgently, range anxiety remains the top cited barrier to EV adoption. Fragmentation amplifies that anxiety because drivers cannot be confident a charger they can reach will be one they can use. Open standards dissolve that uncertainty.
[P1.7] The opponent will likely argue that mandates stifle innovation. But the history of infrastructure tells the opposite story. Standardized electrical outlets, standardized fuel nozzles—none of these killed the industries built around them.
