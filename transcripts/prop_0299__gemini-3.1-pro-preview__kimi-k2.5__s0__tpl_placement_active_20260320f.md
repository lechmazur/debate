[P1.1] Cultural memory is the bedrock of human heritage, yet humanity is losing minority languages and community archives at an alarming rate. Treating this preservation as a private or charitable responsibility guarantees that only the histories of the wealthy and majority groups will survive. Governments must establish standing public funds to protect this endangered heritage for three fundamental reasons.
[P1.2] First, the private market and philanthropy are structurally incapable of protecting marginalized cultures. Private funding prioritizes profitability, broad appeal, and return on investment. A language spoken by only a few dozen elders cannot generate the commercial viability required by the free market. Similarly, relying on charity leaves preservation subject to the fickle tastes of wealthy donors.
[P1.3] When cultural survival relies on volatile grant cycles or crowdfunding, vulnerable histories inevitably vanish.
[P1.4] Second, governments bear a unique, reparative moral obligation. The endangerment of minority languages and oral histories is rarely a natural accident; it is frequently the direct result of historical state policies. From forced assimilation programs and Indigenous boarding schools to institutionalized discrimination, governments have historically been the architects of cultural erasure. Because the state engineered the destruction of these languages and archives, the state owes a standing, structural commitment to their recovery and preservation.
[P1.5] Third, cultural preservation requires institutional continuity. Revitalizing a language or maintaining a community archive is not a short-term project that can be completed in a single philanthropic funding cycle. It requires decades of steady infrastructure: training educators, recording elders, and securely maintaining digital databases. Standing public funds provide the systemic stability necessary for this long-term survival, free from the threat of a sudden economic downturn erasing a charity's endowment.
[P1.6] Cultural memory is a fundamental public good, much like a public library or a national park. Leaving it to the private sector treats the heritage of minority communities as an expendable luxury. By establishing standing public funds, we recognize that preserving the full spectrum of human history is a permanent public duty, not a charitable afterthought.
