[P1.1] The newest class of obesity drugs represents a genuine medical advance. But medical advance and sound reimbursement policy are different questions, and conflating them is exactly what pharmaceutical manufacturers want.
[P1.2] When a health system decides to reimburse a drug *broadly*—meaning across millions of people who meet a clinical threshold—it is making a multi-decade fiscal commitment. GLP-1 drugs, which people must take indefinitely to sustain benefits, illustrate the stakes vividly. Even modest per-patient annual costs, multiplied across a substantial fraction of the adult population, can consume a health budget's entire growth margin. A system that commits to broad reimbursement at list price, with no outcome accountability, has surrendered all leverage permanently.
[P1.3] That is why outcome-based pricing or spending caps are not obstacles to access—they are the *condition* that makes broad access fiscally possible. Without them, a finance ministry facing an open-ended liability will either refuse coverage entirely or impose rationing so severe that broad access never materializes anyway. The choice is not "conditions versus access." It is "structured access versus no access."
[P1.4] The outcome-based element matters for a second, independent reason: the evidence base for long-term preventive benefit, while promising, is not fully established. We have strong short-term weight and cardiometabolic data, but we do not yet have decades of mortality and morbidity outcomes at population scale. Pricing tied to real-world results means manufacturers are paid for what the drugs actually deliver, not for what trial populations suggest they might deliver. That is not punitive—it is how a rational buyer prices genuine uncertainty.
[P1.5] Finally, manufacturers launching drugs at high prices for conditions as prevalent as obesity are not acting from thin margins. They are exploiting a negotiating vacuum. Spending caps and outcome contracts fill that vacuum. They give health systems the leverage to say yes to broad coverage in a way that does not eventually force painful cutbacks elsewhere.
[P1.6] The question is not whether these drugs deserve coverage. It is whether coverage is sustainable. These conditions make it so.
