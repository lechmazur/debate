[P1.1] When a rural hospital closes, the nearest alternative is often an hour or more away. That distance is not an inconvenience — it is a death sentence for stroke victims who have a narrow treatment window, for accident victims bleeding on a county road, for mothers in complicated labor. The market is not pricing in those lives. The market is pricing in revenue per bed.
[P1.2] Market consolidation toward regional centers follows a clear logic: concentrate volume, reduce overhead, improve specialist utilization. That logic works perfectly well if you are a spreadsheet. It works catastrophically if you are a diabetic farmer two counties from the nearest endocrinologist, or an elderly resident who lost driving ability and now lives an hour from any emergency room by ambulance. The consolidation model assumes mobility and time that rural populations structurally do not have.
[P1.3] The case for subsidy is not charity — it is correcting a market failure. Rural communities generate enormous externalities that private hospital arithmetic ignores: they produce the food supply, maintain critical infrastructure, and house populations with specific occupational health risks. The public benefit of keeping those workers and families medically accessible vastly exceeds what any local facility can recover through billing. That gap is precisely what government intervention exists to close.
[P1.4] CON will likely argue that subsidies are inefficient and that telemedicine or mobile clinics can substitute for physical facilities. Those alternatives are genuinely useful at the margins. They cannot deliver emergency surgery, manage complex inpatient care, or substitute for the stabilizing function a local ER performs before any transfer is possible. The question is not whether rural populations deserve some care; it is whether the specific threshold of local physical access justifies public cost.
[P1.5] The evidence of preventable rural mortality says yes.
[P1.6] The government already subsidizes roads to rural areas, knowing that market logic would never build them. Medical access deserves the same recognition: it is infrastructure, not a luxury consumer product.
