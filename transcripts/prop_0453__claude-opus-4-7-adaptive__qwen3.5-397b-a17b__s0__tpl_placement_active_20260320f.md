[P1.1] Housing is not just another line item in the CPI basket. It is the single largest expense for most households, the primary vehicle of wealth and inequality, and the asset class whose inflation does the most damage to the real economy over time. That is why central banks should treat runaway shelter costs as a first-order target, even at the cost of a sharper downturn.
[P1.2] Start with the welfare math. A recession is painful but cyclical: output gaps typically close within a couple of years, employment recovers, and policy has well-worn tools to cushion the blow. A sustained housing price spiral is structural. Once rents and home prices detach from incomes, they stay detached for a generation.
[P1.3] Young workers are locked out, labor mobility collapses, family formation is delayed, and cities hollow out their essential workforce — nurses, teachers, transit operators — who cannot live where they work. That damage compounds; a recession's damage mean-reverts.
[P1.4] Second, housing inflation is uniquely self-reinforcing. High shelter costs feed directly into wage demands, service-sector prices, and inflation expectations, because rent is a non-negotiable cost for every worker. Let it run, and the central bank eventually has to induce a *deeper* recession to break it — the Volcker lesson. Acting decisively now is the recession-minimizing path over a ten-year horizon, not the recession-maximizing one.
[P1.5] Third, only the central bank can credibly do this. Fiscal and zoning fixes are real but slow and politically captured by incumbent homeowners. Interest rates are the one lever that bites on housing demand within quarters. Declining to pull it because a recession might follow effectively subsidizes asset-holders at the expense of renters and future buyers — a regressive choice dressed up as prudence.
[P1.6] The proposition does not say ignore employment forever. It says *prioritize* — accept recession risk as the price of breaking a housing spiral that, left alone, inflicts larger and longer-lasting harm. On any honest welfare comparison, that trade is worth making.
