[P1.1] An actor’s face and voice are not interchangeable production assets; they are the foundation of their livelihood. AI de-aging and synthetic voice tools do not merely smooth wrinkles or adjust audio levels. They extract, replicate, and generate new expressive content from a performer’s biometric signature. When a studio uses those tools to materially alter a living actor’s work, it is no longer editing a performance—it is manufacturing one.
[P1.2] That shift demands explicit consent and direct compensation.
[P1.3] The economic stakes are immediate. Generative AI can stretch a single day’s work into weeks of screen time, replace future casting, or reshape a performance in ways that damage an actor’s brand. Without compensation, studios capture the full commercial value of a performer’s likeness while the actor bears the career risk. Paying for synthetic alterations simply recognizes that an actor’s identity is a revenue-generating asset being licensed.
[P1.4] Explicit consent closes the loophole where boilerplate contracts authorize uses a performer never agreed to. “Material alteration” draws a workable line: standard grading or routine ADR stays untouched, but generating a cloned voice or reconstructed face that carries new emotional weight triggers the rule. A separate agreement ensures performers control their identity, while giving studios a legally secure pathway to innovate.
[P1.5] The opposition will claim this stifles creativity or equates AI with traditional post-production. But conventional tools modify what was performed. AI synthesis generates what was not. Treating biometric replication as standard editorial work strips autonomy and invites exploitation.
[P1.6] Consent and compensation are the baseline safeguards that keep technological progress from becoming labor extraction.

[C1.3] The right venue for these protections is the negotiating table, where definitions can be tuned, carve-outs written, and trade-offs made. A blanket rule mandating fresh "explicit consent and compensation" every time a studio "materially alters" a performance overrides that bargaining and freezes the terms in their least flexible form.
[C1.4] Now the vagueness. "Material alteration" is doing enormous work in PRO's case and almost no work in the real world. Is smoothing a line reading material? Re-timing a syllable so a joke lands?
[C1.5] Synthesizing a breath between two takes? Dubbing the film into Spanish using the actor's own vocal timbre — a use that expands their global reach? Each of these can "carry new emotional weight." Under PRO's rule, every one becomes a renegotiation point, and any single performer can hold an entire production hostage long after wrap.
[C1.6] That hostage problem isn't hypothetical. It is exactly why studios, unions, and actors negotiate consent up front, in writing, with scope. PRO's regime replaces a workable system with a veto, and vetoes don't help working actors — they help A-listers who already had leverage. Day-players, dubbing artists, and background performers lose roles when productions decide the legal exposure isn't worth it.
[C1.7] Finally, the framing. Conventional editing also "manufactures" performances: the Kuleshov effect, ADR, prosthetics, motion capture cleanup. AI is a continuation, not a rupture. The right question is governance through contract and bargaining — not a rigid mandate the motion writes into stone.
