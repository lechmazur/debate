[P1.1] The proposition is about whether deletion and privacy mean anything. When a person removes a post, abandons an app, or keeps a search, location ping, or draft non-public, that is a choice about audience and duration. Data brokers reverse that choice: they buy scraps people never meant for open circulation, preserve them indefinitely, and sell them to strangers. A society that lets intermediaries monetize your discarded or hidden traces has effectively abolished practical privacy.
[P1.2] The harm is not abstract. Deleted and non-public traces are often the most intimate and misleading kind of data: late-night searches, old photos, geolocation trails, inferred sexuality, health concerns, financial distress, relationship history. Stripped from context and frozen in time, they become tools for exclusion and manipulation—screening tenants, pricing insurance, targeting scams, chilling speech, enabling stalking, or punishing people for youthful mistakes they tried to retract. Archiving turns “temporary” into permanent vulnerability; selling turns vulnerability into an industry.
[P1.3] The usual defense—consent and market choice—collapses here. People do not meaningfully consent to downstream trafficking of traces they deleted or never made public, especially through unread terms, shadow profiles, and data sharing chains they cannot trace. Nor can ordinary citizens bargain, audit, or correct what they cannot even see. This is the classic case for prohibition: a market built on information asymmetry, irrevocable harms, and the commodification of people who never truly agreed.
[P1.4] And the rule is narrower than critics pretend. It does not forbid companies from keeping necessary internal security logs, honoring legal retention duties, or using genuinely public information. It forbids a specific business model: third-party brokers archiving and selling private or deleted traces of private citizens. If deletion is to mean deletion, and privacy is to mean more than a temporary obstacle before resale, that business model has to end.
