[C1.1] Dignity must carry greater moral weight than bodily autonomy in medical decisions because it anchors choices to the objective status of persons rather than allowing the body to become a site of unrestricted self-disposal. Autonomy frames the person as a sovereign owner free to reject any treatment or invite any intervention; dignity, by contrast, insists that certain uses of the body degrade the human being irrespective of consent.
[C1.2] This difference appears sharply in end-of-life choices. Autonomy would permit a patient to secure physician-assisted death on the ground that continued existence lacks value to the individual. Dignity rejects that permission because it holds that human life retains worth that cannot be extinguished by preference or suffering, obligating continued care and protection instead. Societies that subordinate dignity to autonomy convert death into an elective medical procedure and thereby weaken the duty to sustain the vulnerable.
[C1.3] The same ordering explains why medical systems rightly override certain autonomous requests. A competent adult who demands amputation of a healthy limb to match a body-identity disorder asserts pure control over flesh, yet dignity supplies the counterweight by treating the intact human form as non-negotiable. Autonomy alone cannot explain why such demands are refused; dignity supplies the reason by preserving the moral boundary between person and object.
[C1.4] Historical patterns confirm the risk of reversal. When bodily self-determination eclipsed dignity-based limits, practices such as eugenic sterilization and the selective withdrawal of care from disabled newborns gained ethical cover as exercises of freedom. These were not failures of autonomy enforcement but predictable results once dignity ceased to constrain what individuals may choose to do to themselves or what society may accept.
[C1.5] Autonomy claims precision because it rests on consent, yet consent itself depends on external conditions of duress, poverty, or isolation that dignity can question. Dignity therefore functions as the more reliable safeguard, requiring that medical choices serve the enduring worth of the person rather than ratify whatever preference arises.
