[C1.1] The motion sounds like simple justice, but it bakes in three errors that should sink it.
[C1.2] First, misallocation of responsibility. Residential schools were state projects. Governments wrote the laws, compelled attendance, funded per-capita grants, set the assimilationist policy, and used police power to seize children from their families. Churches were contractors—often eager, often complicit, sometimes monstrous—but they operated inside a framework the state designed and enforced.
[C1.3] When you tell the contractor to pay while the principal architect controls the narrative, you let the actual sovereign off cheap. Reparative justice should follow power and intent, and that points first at the Crown or the federal government, not at religious orders that are today often shells of what they once were.
[C1.4] Second, the collective-liability problem. A parish in 2024 is not the same moral agent as a missionary order in 1890. The nuns and priests who ran those schools are dead. The endowments PRO gestures at are largely tied to current schools, hospitals, food banks, and parishes that serve poor and Indigenous communities right now.
[C1.5] Forcing those entities to liquidate assets doesn't reach the perpetrators; it taxes today's congregants and defunds today's services—often the very populations the motion claims to help. That isn't reckoning; it's a transfer from one vulnerable group to a fund administered by lawyers.
[C1.6] Third, the better alternative. Voluntary contributions, truth-telling, opening archives, returning land, and supporting survivor-led healing are things many orders have actually done and can keep doing without the obligation framework the motion installs. The moment you make it a "should"—a duty enforceable by moral or legal pressure—you replace conscience with compliance, you trigger insurance-driven denial and litigation defense, and you slow the very disclosures survivors most need. Coerced money buys silence; voluntary participation buys truth.
[C1.7] The motion mistakes the agent, mistakes the wallet, and mistakes the mechanism. Reject it.

[P2.3] More fundamentally, many orders were not passive contractors. They lobbied for contracts. They fought to keep children longer. They built institutional prestige on the back of these schools.

[P2.5] The collective liability objection is real but overstated. Legal continuity matters. When the Jesuits or the Oblates operate hospitals and schools today, they are not morally unrelated to the orders that committed the harm. Inheritance transfers obligation.

The Indian Residential Schools Settlement Agreement only succeeded because it combined legal obligation with non-judicial mechanisms.
[P2.7] Survivors and their advocates explicitly rejected "voluntary" precisely because it produced broken promises and empty apologies. The motion does not mandate litigation; it creates a moral and structural expectation that material contribution should follow acknowledgment.
