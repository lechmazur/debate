[P1.1] The core problem is not that digital markets are competitive; it is that they look competitive right until they are not. When a dominant digital platform buys a smaller rival, the most dangerous anti‑competitive effect is not an immediate price hike—it is the quiet elimination of a future threat. By the time the damage is visible, the market has tipped and cannot be unscrambled. That is why antitrust authorities need the tool this proposition creates: a structural presumption that shifts the burden of proof where it belongs.
[P1.2] Digital platform markets do not behave like ordinary markets. Network effects mean each additional user makes the service more valuable, creating a powerful gravitational pull toward a single winner. Data feedback loops amplify the gap: more users generate more data, which fuels better products, which attract still more users. In such an environment, a dominant incumbent can buy a tiny innovator not because it is profitable today, but because it sits at the edge of a network that could one day erode the incumbent’s dominance.
[P1.3] These so‑called “killer acquisitions” are prophylactic strikes against competition itself. The target’s product is often shut down, redirected, or absorbed in ways that leave consumers with one fewer independent option and no realistic path back.

[P1.5] Regulators are left trying to impose behavioural remedies on a platform that already controls the infrastructure. The only reliable remedy is prevention, and prevention demands a rule that catches threats before they vanish.

[C1.1] The proposition’s blanket presumption is a sledgehammer that would crush innovation rather than protect it. Digital markets are not the static monopolies the other side implies; they remain dynamic and contestable. TikTok upended social media despite Facebook’s dominance, while AWS faces serious and growing competition from Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud. A rule that presumes every acquisition by a large platform is anti-competitive until the firms prove substantial consumer benefit would deter precisely the kinds of deals that have fueled competition and consumer choice, such as Google’s purchase of Android, which provided the open foundation enabling Samsung, Xiaomi, and countless other manufacturers to challenge Apple’s iOS.
[C1.2] The startup ecosystem depends critically on acquisitions as a vital exit mechanism. Venture capital funding models are built on the expectation that successful startups can be acquired, especially for high-risk, long-horizon innovation projects. If acquisitions by dominant platforms become effectively impossible to justify under this vague standard, venture capitalists will redirect their investments away from ambitious startups, fearing no viable path to liquidity. The ‘substantial consumer benefit’ test is particularly problematic because many of the real benefits that acquisitions create—such as seamless integration of services, reduced friction for users, or accelerated development of new features—are incremental in nature and cannot be predicted or proven with certainty to a regulator’s satisfaction before the fact.
[C1.3] Existing antitrust law already provides authorities with the necessary tools to challenge mergers that are likely to harm competition. The real issue in digital markets is not a lack of legal authority but rather a lack of enforcement resources and political will. This crude, across-the-board presumption would replace careful, evidence-based, case-by-case analysis with a blunt instrument that would effectively give regulators veto power over any strategic move by successful digital firms. Far from protecting competition, such an approach would petrify digital markets, stifling the dynamism that has delivered immense consumer benefits and continues to drive innovation forward.
