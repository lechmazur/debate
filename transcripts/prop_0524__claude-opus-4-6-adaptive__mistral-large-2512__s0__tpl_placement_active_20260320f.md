[P1.1] The current migration systems in most developed countries fail both the people who move and the communities that receive them. Employers face unpredictable visa processing. Universities lose international talent to competitors. And local residents watch housing costs spike and GP waiting lists grow without anyone having planned for the increase.
[P1.2] The proposition fixes all three failures simultaneously.
[P1.3] **Guaranteed allocations create planning certainty.** When a university knows it will receive a defined number of migration places, it can design courses, hire faculty, and build partnerships years in advance. When an employer knows its allocation exists and will be honored, it can invest in training pipelines that blend domestic and international talent rather than lurching between labor shortages and hiring freezes. The current system—where visa rules change with each election cycle—makes rational institutional planning nearly impossible.
[P1.4] **Caps tied to local capacity make migration sustainable rather than politically contested.** The reason migration becomes toxic in public debate is that its costs and benefits are unevenly distributed. A town whose hospital is already stretched absorbs new residents without additional funding, while distant metropolitan economies capture the productivity gains. Tying allocations to housing stock and service capacity forces honest infrastructure investment. If a city wants more international students or workers, it must first demonstrate the GP surgeries, school places, and housing to support them.
[P1.5] This converts a zero-sum argument into a positive-sum one: more infrastructure unlocks more migration, benefiting everyone.
[P1.6] **The cap prevents exploitation without eliminating flexibility.** Uncapped systems risk institutional bad actors—degree mills that enroll students as a revenue strategy regardless of outcomes, or firms that suppress wages through unlimited labor supply. A guaranteed-but-capped allocation rewards institutions that use their places well while preventing runaway growth that harms migrants and locals alike.
[P1.7] This proposal doesn't restrict migration; it civilizes it. It gives institutions certainty, ties growth to readiness, and replaces political mood swings with transparent, capacity-based planning. The alternative—which CON must defend—is the volatile status quo where nobody can plan and everyone loses.
