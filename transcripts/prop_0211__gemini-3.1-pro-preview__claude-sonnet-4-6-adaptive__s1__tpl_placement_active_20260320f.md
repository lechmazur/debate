[P1.1] Preventive obesity drugs are a medical breakthrough, but they pose an unprecedented threat to health system budgets. Because roughly forty percent of the adult population meets the clinical criteria for obesity, broad reimbursement at current list prices would be financially catastrophic. Unchecked spending on these lifetime medications would rapidly cannibalize finite health budgets, crowding out essential, life-saving care in oncology and emergency medicine. Spending caps are not merely an administrative tool; they are a mathematical necessity to keep public and private health systems solvent.
[P1.2] The staggering cost of these medications is justified only by the promise of long-term savings—averting future heart attacks, strokes, and joint replacements. Yet, there is a massive gap between clinical trial efficacy and real-world adherence. In practice, a significant percentage of patients abandon these treatments within the first year due to gastrointestinal side effects or lifestyle friction, leading to rapid weight rebound. Without outcome-based pricing, health systems pay premium list prices upfront, risking tens of thousands of dollars per patient for temporary weight loss that yields zero downstream medical savings.
[P1.3] Outcome-based pricing forces manufacturers to stand behind their claims. If a drug successfully sustains weight loss and prevents cardiovascular events in the real world, the manufacturer is compensated for that immense value. If the patient discontinues the drug and the clinical benefits evaporate, the public should not foot the bill for a failed intervention.
[P1.4] Demand for these drugs is practically limitless. Conditioning broad coverage on these mechanisms aligns financial incentives with actual public health benefits. It forces pharmaceutical monopolies to share the risk of real-world failure, encourages them to invest in patient adherence programs, and protects our medical safety nets. Failing to demand spending caps or outcome-based pricing amounts to writing a blank check for a lifetime prescription, socializing the massive financial risk while entirely privatizing the reward.

[C1.2] Consider what happens when negotiations stall, as they routinely do. While payers and manufacturers argue over contract terms, patients with obesity—a condition tied to cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and reduced life expectancy—go without coverage. The proposition essentially says: no deal, no access.
[C1.3] That is not a pricing reform; it is a hostage arrangement with patients as collateral.
[C1.4] Outcome-based contracts are also far harder to execute for chronic preventive medications than the PRO framing implies. Acute interventions with clear, measurable endpoints—a tumor response, a surgical complication—lend themselves to outcomes contracting. A lifetime preventive drug is different. What exactly triggers payment or clawback?
[C1.5] Weight maintained at six months? Cardiovascular events averted over ten years? Who tracks adherence? Who resolves disputes when a patient discontinues because of life circumstances rather than drug failure?
[C1.6] These are not administrative details; they are fundamental operational problems that have caused similar contracts in other therapeutic areas to collapse or never be implemented at all.
[C1.7] Spending caps carry a different risk: if the cap is set below a manufacturer's threshold, they simply don't agree to broad coverage in that market. Several national health systems have already experienced drug unavailability because negotiated prices fell outside manufacturer minimums. The proposition's conditions might produce no broad reimbursement at all—the exact opposite of its stated goal.
[C1.8] None of this means costs don't matter. Prescribing criteria, clinical eligibility thresholds, volume rebates, and formulary design are workable tools. The motion rejects all of them unless one narrow mechanism is accepted first. That is too high a price. </s>
