[P1.1] Transmission expansion and regional market integration deliver both grid reliability and decarbonization faster, cheaper, and more certainly than long-duration energy storage subsidies. Three arguments establish this.
[P1.2] **First, geographic diversity is the most powerful tool against renewable intermittency.** When the wind dies in Texas, it is often blowing in the Dakotas. When solar output drops in the Pacific Northwest, the Desert Southwest is still producing. A well-connected grid turns local weather into a manageable portfolio problem rather than an existential reliability crisis. Transmission lines that connect diverse climate zones provide a form of "virtual storage" at a fraction of the capital cost per usable megawatt-hour.
[P1.3] The principle is straightforward: electrons move at near light speed across wires that last fifty-plus years, while batteries degrade, lose round-trip efficiency, and must eventually be replaced.
[P1.4] **Second, transmission investment is proven and deployable now.** High-voltage lines, HVDC corridors, and regional market coupling are mature technologies with decades of operational track records across Europe, Scandinavia, and parts of North America. Long-duration storage—iron-air batteries, hydrogen electrolysis and reconversion, compressed air—remains largely pre-commercial or early-commercial. Subsidizing technologies that have not yet demonstrated bankable cost curves at scale is a gamble; building transmission is an infrastructure decision with well-understood engineering and financing. When the proposition asks where to invest *primarily*, the answer should favor the option with lower execution risk during a critical decarbonization window.
[P1.5] **Third, regional market integration unlocks enormous economic efficiency.** Integrated markets reduce curtailment of renewables, lower wholesale electricity prices through broader competition, and allow regions to share reserves instead of each building redundant backup capacity. Europe's internal energy market saved billions during the 2022 energy crisis by enabling cross-border flows that no amount of local storage could have replaced at comparable cost.
[P1.6] Long-duration storage may play a complementary role, but making it the *primary* investment vehicle means betting reliability and climate targets on unproven technology while ignoring the single highest-value infrastructure deficit most grids face today: inadequate transmission.
