[P1.2] Defaults are fate. Study after study confirms that the overwhelming majority of users never change default settings. When Google makes Chrome the default browser on Android devices representing over 70% of the global mobile market, or when Apple pre-selects Apple Pay and Safari on over a billion iPhones, they capture markets not by offering superior products but by rigging the choice architecture. Competing browsers and payment systems don't lose because users reject them—they lose because users never meaningfully encounter them.
[P1.3] The innovation consequences are severe. When dominant platforms can simply default their way to victory, they face weaker pressure to improve. The result is a stagnant ecosystem where the powerful stay powerful regardless of merit.
[P1.4] History offers a clear warning. Microsoft's bundling of Internet Explorer didn't just harm Netscape—it demonstrated how platform dominance combined with default preferencing distorts entire markets. The remedy then was intervention, and the same logic applies today to wallets, browsers, and especially AI assistants, which will soon mediate how billions of people access information and conduct commerce. If OS providers lock in their AI as the unchangeable default, they predetermine the winner of the most consequential technology race of our era.
[P1.5] This remedy is modest. Apple can still offer Apple Pay; Google can still promote Chrome. They simply cannot exploit their operating-system monopoly to rig the starting line. Users who genuinely prefer these services will choose them freely.
[P1.6] The principle is straightforward: the referee cannot also be a player.
