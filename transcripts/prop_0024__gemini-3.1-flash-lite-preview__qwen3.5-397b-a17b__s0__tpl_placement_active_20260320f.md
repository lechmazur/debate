[P1.1] The housing affordability crisis in advanced economies is, fundamentally, a supply problem. When population growth and urbanization outpace the construction of new units, vacancy rates plummet and prices skyrocket. Expanding housing supply through zoning reform and streamlined permitting directly addresses this root cause by allowing the market to build the inventory required to meet demand.
[P1.2] Regulatory hurdles and restrictive zoning laws act as an artificial cap on the number of homes available. By removing these barriers, we reduce the cost of entry for developers and encourage density in areas where it is most needed. This creates a competitive rental market where landlords must offer better value, naturally checking rent increases through the availability of alternatives rather than through government mandates.
[P1.3] In contrast, rent control and extreme tenant-protection expansions are palliative measures that fail to address the scarcity driving the crisis. While they may provide immediate, localized stability for incumbents, they impose severe long-term costs on the broader population. Rent caps incentivize landlords to defer maintenance and divert capital away from housing, leading to a degradation in the quality of existing stock. Furthermore, by creating a "lock-in" effect, these regulations drastically reduce housing mobility, preventing workers from moving to where jobs are available and trapping residents in units that no longer fit their needs.
[P1.4] Critically, rent control shrinks the future housing pool. When returns on new development are limited or unpredictable due to restrictive regulatory environments, investment flows elsewhere, exacerbating the very shortage that drives high prices. We cannot regulate our way out of a scarcity crisis by artificially suppressing prices while ignoring the underlying lack of inventory. The most effective path to affordability is ensuring that the pace of construction matches the pace of demand.
[P1.5] If we prioritize supply, we solve the structural deficiency; if we prioritize heavy-handed regulation, we merely shift the burden from one tenant to another while the overall market stagnates.
