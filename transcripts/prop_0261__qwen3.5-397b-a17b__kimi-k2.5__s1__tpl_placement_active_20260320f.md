[P1.1] When you purchase a physical book, you own an object. You can sell it, gift it, or bequeath it. When you purchase an eBook, you own nothing. You hold a revocable license that terminates upon death.
[P1.2] This distinction creates a profound injustice: corporations confiscate paid assets simply because the buyer died. Digital libraries are not mere services; they are accumulated property with financial and sentimental value. A lifetime of purchasing music, movies, and books represents a significant portion of a modern estate. To deny transfer is to erase that value.
[P1.3] Consider the inconsistency. A shelf of physical novels passes to next of kin effortlessly. A Kindle containing the identical titles locks forever. The content is the same; the utility is the same; only the corporate fine print differs.
[P1.4] Law must correct this market failure. Current terms of service allow platforms to retain revenue while denying delivery, effectively double-charging families who must repurchase what was already paid for. This is not protection of intellectual property; it is windfall profiteering off grief. As physical media disappears, this loss compounds across generations.
[P1.5] Feasibility is not a barrier. Technical mechanisms for legacy contact already exist within major ecosystems. The obstacle is legal permission, not engineering capacity. By mandating transferability, we align digital rights with fundamental property principles.
[P1.6] An asset purchased with permanent intent should not vanish due to medium. It ensures that wealth stored in digital formats remains wealth, not locked data. It respects the deceased's intent to provide for their family. We cannot allow licensing loopholes to override inheritance rights.
[P1.7] If you paid for it, it belongs to your estate. The law must guarantee that digital death does not mean digital dispossession. Property rights should not expire with breath. The status quo treats consumers as perpetual renters in their own homes.
[P1.8] We must restore ownership.

[C1.1] The proposition asks the state to tear up contracts that consumers voluntarily signed. When you "buy" a digital book, you are not purchasing paper and ink; you are purchasing a limited, non-exclusive license to access encrypted files under strict conditions. That license is priced precisely because it is non-transferable and terminal. To mandate inheritance transfer is to force publishers to sell a different, more valuable product—perpetual, transferable ownership—while prohibiting them from charging the market rate for it.
[C1.2] This is not a loophole; it is a pricing tier. Physical books cost more to produce and distribute, yet are resellable; digital licenses are cheaper precisely because they cannot be resold or bequeathed. Consumers who wanted transferable assets could have purchased physical media, DRM-free downloads, or family-plan subscriptions. By guaranteeing post-mortem transfer, the law would either inflate prices for all users or, more likely, force platforms to withdraw retail licenses entirely, replacing them with rental-only models that help nobody.
[C1.3] Moreover, the category "digital libraries" conceals a grave privacy threat. A deceased person's account does not merely contain bestsellers; it holds annotations revealing political views, medical searches, romantic correspondences, and purchase histories. The right to digital dignity is meaningless if the state automatically bequeaths your inner life to relatives you may have despised, estranged, or suffered abuse from. Inheritance should not be a forced excavation of the dead’s psyche.
[C1.4] Enforcing such guarantees also requires unraveling encryption and identity systems designed to prevent account sharing. If licenses become heritable, platforms face insurmountable verification challenges: proving death, preventing fraud, distinguishing legitimate heirs from hackers exploiting the policy. The infrastructure required to police this would surveil grieving families while degrading security for living users.
[C1.5] The market already provides inheritance-friendly options for those who prioritize them. Do not destroy the affordable license model to solve a problem better addressed by a will, a conversation, or a password manager.

[C2.5] Finally, probate courts verify bank accounts through documentary evidence and institutional relationships; verifying heirship to a tech account with global users and no physical branches invites fraud at scale. We should not dismantle affordable access to solve a problem better handled by estate planning.
