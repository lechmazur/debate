[P1.1] Planetary defense is the one natural disaster humans can actually prevent outright — not mitigate, not evacuate, but cancel. That asymmetry is the whole case.
[P1.2] Every other catastrophic risk we fund permanently because response time matters: standing armies, nuclear early-warning radar, CDC stockpiles, coast guards. We don't treat pandemic response as "mostly a research niche" and scramble to build hospitals after the outbreak. Yet an asteroid is the cleanest version of this logic. The threat is physical, predictable by orbital mechanics, and — crucially — defeatable only if the hardware already exists when the warning comes.
[P1.3] That last point is where the research-niche posture fails. Deflection works through tiny velocity changes applied years or decades before impact. DART demonstrated that a kinetic impactor can measurably shift an asteroid's orbit — the physics is settled. But a settled physics principle in a PDF does not deflect anything.
[P1.4] If a city-killer is spotted with 18 months of warning and the response is "let's convene a program, design a spacecraft, qualify a launcher, and negotiate an international mandate," we lose. [P1.5] The cost objection collapses under scale. Insurance against low-probability, unbounded-severity events is exactly where governments, not markets, must act, because no private entity can internalize the downside.
[P1.6] And the research-only posture is self-undermining: without deployment pull, the survey, tracking, and interceptor technologies stagnate. Operational infrastructure is what forces the warning time, the catalog completeness, and the response doctrine to actually exist.
