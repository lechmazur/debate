[P1.1] Public funding demands public accountability. When the government subsidizes education, it acts as a steward of taxpayer dollars and a guarantor of a child’s future. Funding religious schools without requiring comparable civic, curricular, and non-discrimination standards violates both of these foundational duties.
[P1.2] First, public money must not fund discrimination. State revenues are collected from all citizens, regardless of race, religion, sexual orientation, or disability. It is a profound violation of the civic contract to take taxes from a marginalized citizen and hand them to an institution that legally refuses to admit their child or hire them as a teacher. If a religious school wishes to enforce exclusionary sectarian rules, it has the freedom to do so—but it must do so on its own dime.
[P1.3] Public funds must serve the public equitably.
[P1.4] Second, the state has a compelling interest in the educational baseline of its future citizens. Curricular standards ensure that every child gains the basic literacy, numeracy, scientific understanding, and civic knowledge required to participate in a modern, democratic economy. Without these safeguards, public funds can be funneled into schools that teach pseudoscience, omit core historical facts, or fail to prepare students for secular employment. The ultimate victim in that scenario is the child, whose future autonomy and upward mobility are severely restricted by a substandard education.
[P1.5] Finally, shared standards promote necessary social cohesion. A core purpose of state-funded education is to foster a shared civic reality. If the government unconditionally funds religious schools, it actively incentivizes the fracturing of society into isolated, sectarian silos where children are never exposed to diverse viewpoints or common democratic values.
[P1.6] This proposition does not ban or restrict religious schools from existing. It simply establishes a fair, common-sense boundary: if you demand the privileges of public financing, you must accept the responsibilities of public standards. The government’s primary obligation is to the welfare of the student and the equity of the taxpayer, neither of which should be sacrificed to unconditionally subsidize private religious instruction.
