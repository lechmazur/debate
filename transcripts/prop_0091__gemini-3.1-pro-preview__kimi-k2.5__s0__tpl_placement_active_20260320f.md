The consumer has already paid for the copper, the heating elements, and the microchips.
[P1.3] Placing a software lock on these features forces the owner to pay a perpetual ransom to use their own physical property. This shifts the automotive market from a model of authentic ownership to a predatory system of permanent rent-seeking.
[P1.4] Second, this model guarantees massive environmental and economic waste. Manufacturing automotive hardware requires mining raw materials, refining metals, and expending significant energy. When automakers install standard hardware across an entire fleet to simplify their assembly lines, but only a fraction of owners pay to "unlock" those features, the world is left manufacturing millions of pounds of dead weight. Worse, every car on the road becomes less efficient, burning extra gas or draining electric battery life to continually haul around dormant hardware that will never be used.
[P1.5] This is ecologically irresponsible and fundamentally inefficient.
[P1.6] Finally, artificial software locks undermine reliability and the secondary market. If physical features are tied to active server pings and corporate accounts, basic vehicle functions become vulnerable to server outages, corporate bankruptcies, and arbitrary subscription price hikes. When the car is eventually sold to a second owner, automakers often reset the subscriptions, stripping the vehicle's intrinsic value and forcing the new owner to pay the manufacturer all over again to activate the exact same physical capabilities.
[P1.7] If a consumer buys the hardware, they must control the hardware. We should protect genuine property rights against perpetual corporate monetization.
