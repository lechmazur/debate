[C2.1] PRO’s assurance that good institutional design tames elite capture in SWFs ignores the reality that fragile states cannot simply import Norway’s governance. Paper safeguards—oversight boards, reporting requirements—routinely fail when the same political class that controls the fund also appoints the overseers. Nigeria’s Excess Crude Account has suffered repeated off-budget withdrawals, circumventing constitutional limits. Timor-Leste’s Petroleum Fund has been drained beyond its sustainable withdrawal rule, with Parliament overriding the very safeguards PRO cites.
[C2.2] Azerbaijan’s SOFAZ has financed white-elephant projects and unproductive subsidies, not broad-based development. These are not exceptions; they show that the SWF model puts a tempting pot of money under political control, and even “independent” bodies become instruments of patronage in low-trust environments.
[C2.3] Cash transfers, by contrast, remove the government’s ability to quietly raid the resource pot. Every citizen becomes an auditor: when cash fails to arrive, the theft is personal, immediate, and politically explosive. That is far more potent than disclosing a portfolio statement that few can parse and fewer can enforce. A rural mother knows whether her family received its share; she cannot verify whether a fund’s overseas investment was overpriced to benefit a minister’s crony.
[C2.4] The claim that cash distributions inevitably trigger Dutch disease is overstated and fixable. Governments can spread transfers over time, link them to financial inclusion that encourages saving, or use a portion to fund public goods—all while the bulk of windfall wealth remains in citizens’ hands. And the behavioral evidence is not one-sided: large-scale unconditional cash programs in developing countries show recipients invest in livestock, small enterprises, and education—expanding productive capacity rather than merely consuming. Even if some windfalls are spent, that spending directly improves welfare today, which is not a failure but a development goal.
[C2.5] Above all, direct distributions affirm a simple truth: the resources belong to the people, not to a fund managed by the same elites who have historically failed them. SWFs too easily become a deferred promise that never arrives, while cash empowers the real engines of development—households making decisions for their own futures.
