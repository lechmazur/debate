[P1.1] Consent is necessary for ethical transactions, but it functions as a floor, not a ceiling. The proposition asks whether consent alone suffices, and the answer is clearly negative: a transaction can be fully consensual yet remain ethically bankrupt when it exploits structural desperation, inflicts collateral damage on vulnerable third parties, or commodifies human dignity.
[P1.2] Consider the parent who "consents" to sell a kidney to feed their children, or the worker who "agrees" to hazardous conditions because homelessness is the only alternative. These are not rare edge cases; they illustrate how economic duress hollows out the moral substance of consent. When survival itself is the bargaining chip, agreement becomes a mechanism of extraction rather than an expression of autonomous choice. The mere absence of physical coercion cannot sanitize transactions that systematically prey upon catastrophic inequality.
[P1.3] Furthermore, bilateral consent cannot legitimize harms to external parties. Two sophisticated actors might freely contract to trade addictive substances that devastate surrounding communities, or to sell carbon offsets that enable environmental destruction. The consent of the contracting parties does not extinguish the moral accounting of broader suffering their exchange creates. Ethics demands we weigh consequences that extend far beyond the signatures on the page.
[P1.4] Finally, certain markets inherently corrupt social equality regardless of individual willingness. When we allow votes, judicial outcomes, or human beings themselves to become commodities, we transform civic dignity into price tags. Even "voluntary" participation in such markets erodes the foundational conditions for equal citizenship and mutual respect.
[P1.5] Therefore, consent is merely the threshold of ethical scrutiny, not its satisfying conclusion. We must evaluate power asymmetries, external harms, and the intrinsic nature of the goods exchanged. Without these additional guardrails, consent collapses into a moral shield for exploitation and systemic harm.
