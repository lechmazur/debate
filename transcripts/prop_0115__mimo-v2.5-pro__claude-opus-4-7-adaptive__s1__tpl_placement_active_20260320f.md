[C1.1] Pro frames this as "if it walks like credit, regulate it like credit." That sounds tidy, but it collapses two questions that must stay separate: *whether* BNPL needs some consumer protections, and *whether* the right template is the full credit card regime. Concede the first; deny the second. That distinction is the whole debate.
[C1.2] Start with the product itself. The dominant BNPL form is pay-in-four: zero interest, short duration, fixed installments, no revolving balance, no compounding. A credit card is an open-ended revolving line where the lender profits precisely when you fail to pay in full. BNPL profits primarily from merchant fees—the merchant pays for conversion, the consumer pays nothing if they pay on time.
[C1.3] Those are opposite business models. Regulating them identically punishes the structure that is actually less dangerous to consumers.
[C1.4] Now look at what "much more like credit cards" actually imports: full Truth-in-Lending-style disclosures designed for APR-bearing revolving debt, ability-to-repay underwriting modeled on multi-thousand-dollar credit lines, CARD Act fee architecture, and heavyweight compliance overhead. Apply that to a $60 four-installment split and two things happen. One, providers exit the small-ticket, thin-margin segment, because the per-transaction compliance cost exceeds the per-transaction revenue. Two, the consumers Pro claims to protect—younger users, thin-file borrowers, people declined for credit cards—get pushed toward the actual high-cost alternatives: overdraft, payday loans, subprime cards at 30%+ APR.
[C1.5] That is a regulatory own-goal.
[C1.6] The honest path is targeted, proportionate rules: standardized payment-schedule disclosure, clear late-fee limits, dispute and refund rights tied to the underlying purchase, and credit-bureau reporting so the "stacking" problem Pro raises is visible. Several of those are already arriving through existing consumer-finance authority without importing the entire credit card code.
[C1.7] So the clash is this: Pro needs to show that the credit card framework, specifically, is the right fit—not merely that *some* regulation is warranted. A pay-in-four installment is not a revolving credit line, and forcing it into that box harms the very people Pro names.
