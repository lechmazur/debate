[P1.1] Raising the minimum wage is a blunt instrument for industries like hospitality, cleaning, or gig delivery, where work varies wildly even within a single city. A wage floor set for all ignores the difference between a profitable downtown hotel and a rural care home. It can’t address scheduling stability, training pay, or safety standards—issues as critical as the hourly rate.
[P1.2] Sectoral bargaining fixes this. Representatives from workers and employers in a specific industry sit down to set a full suite of minimum standards: wages, benefits, working conditions, and career pathways. This creates tailored rules that actually fit the sector’s reality. For fast food, it might mean predictable hours and heat protections.
[P1.3] For hotel housekeeping, it could involve quotas and injury prevention protocols.
[P1.4] This model isn’t theoretical; it’s proven in many European economies where it delivers both higher productivity and lower working poverty. It’s efficient, setting one framework for thousands of businesses instead of forcing each to navigate a shifting patchwork of local wage laws. Crucially, it gives workers a direct voice in shaping their conditions, fostering stability and reducing turnover. Minimum wage hikes are a periodic political fight that leaves the underlying fragmentation of service work untouched.
[P1.5] Sectoral bargaining is a durable system for raising standards across the board.

[C1.1] Sectoral bargaining sounds appealing in the abstract, but the proposition fails precisely where it claims to succeed: in fragmented service industries, where workers are hardest to organize and employer associations barely exist, sectoral bargaining is the least workable tool available.
[C1.2] The PRO case rests on a European analogy that doesn't survive scrutiny. High-functioning sectoral bargaining in Germany or the Netherlands was built over decades alongside dense union infrastructure, employer federations willing to sit at a national table, and legal frameworks that make collective agreements binding across entire industries. In the fragmented service sectors the proposition targets—gig delivery, domestic cleaning, informal care work, small hospitality—none of those preconditions exist. Pointing to European outcomes without those foundations is like praising a cathedral and concluding anyone can build one.
[C1.3] Here is the central problem: sectoral bargaining requires someone to bargain. The workers least protected in fragmented service industries—undocumented cleaners, algorithm-managed gig workers, migrant care workers—are precisely the workers who cannot organize, cannot be credibly represented, and will be excluded from whatever sectoral table eventually forms. Minimum wage hikes, by contrast, are self-executing. They require no union card, no formal employment relationship, and no seat at a table the worker was never invited to.
[C1.4] When a floor goes up, it lifts everyone in reach automatically.
[C1.5] The PRO also frames minimum wage as only touching the hourly rate while sectoral bargaining covers scheduling, safety, and training. But nothing prevents legislatures from mandating scheduling stability, heat protections, or training standards directly—and several jurisdictions have done exactly that. The wage floor is a platform, not a ceiling. Sectoral bargaining, meanwhile, risks hardening into a cartel: established employers and incumbent unions negotiating frameworks that raise barriers to entry, squeeze out smaller competitors, and freeze out the workers not already inside the system.
[C1.6] The case for sectoral bargaining assumes institutional capacity that fragmented industries, by definition, lack. Minimum wage hikes work precisely because they don't need that capacity. That asymmetry is decisive.
