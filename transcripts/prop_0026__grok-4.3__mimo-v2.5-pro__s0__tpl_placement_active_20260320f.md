[P1.1] When a person faces a medical decision, their right to direct what happens to their own body outweighs appeals to dignity because autonomy anchors moral responsibility in the one who must endure the consequences. Dignity, by contrast, usually enters as an external standard that others apply to judge whether a choice meets some ideal of human worthiness. Autonomy recognizes that individuals possess unique knowledge of their physical and emotional limits that no external dignity metric can capture accurately. This distinction matters most when competent adults make decisions that diverge from conventional views of a meaningful existence.
[P1.2] A patient with late-stage cancer who refuses further chemotherapy does so after weighing how additional cycles will diminish remaining quality of life against any marginal extension of time. An objection that choosing comfort care dishonors the dignity of fighting illness imposes a narrative of perseverance that the patient has already rejected as mismatched to their situation. The dignity claim here functions less as a universal truth and more as a way to pressure the individual into continuing treatment others find symbolically appropriate.
[P1.3] Such pressure substitutes collective sentiment for the specific knowledge the patient holds about their own tolerance for suffering and dependency.
[P1.4] Treating dignity as the superior consideration also creates practical risks of expanded control. Medical ethics that elevate dignity can justify overriding competent refusals of treatment, such as blood transfusions for certain religious patients or experimental procedures that seem too novel or undignified. Autonomy limits such overrides to cases where clear harm to others is involved, keeping the moral line drawn at the boundary of the person's own body. This boundary protects the pluralism of personal values that dignity standards tend to flatten under one approved vision of a life worth living.
[P1.5] By contrast, once dignity claims are allowed to trump autonomy, the decision-making power shifts to whomever defines what counts as dignified in that moment, eroding the core protection medical ethics should provide against unwanted interference.
