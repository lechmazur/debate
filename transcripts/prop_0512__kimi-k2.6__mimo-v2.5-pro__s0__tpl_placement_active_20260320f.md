[P1.1] Hyperscale data centers are modern industrial giants disguised as clean, weightless clouds. A single campus can consume as much electricity as a mid-sized city and millions of gallons of water daily to keep servers from overheating. When governments offer uncapped tax incentives to attract them, they are not simply inviting investment; they are socializing infrastructure costs while privatizing returns.
[P1.2] The proposition is straightforward: taxpayers should not be forced to underwrite grid overload and aquifer depletion so that global technology firms can book marginal savings. Without a cap tied to full cost internalization, communities routinely end up on the losing side of the ledger. Local residents face rising utility rates to fund emergency transmission upgrades. In drought-stressed regions, billions of gallons are diverted to server farms that pay virtually nothing in property taxes, even as neighboring farms face restrictions.
[P1.3] Meanwhile, the promised job creation is often a handful of security guards and technicians, not the broad employment boom used to justify the giveaway. The economic calculus is broken because the public bears the downside while shareholders capture the upside.
[P1.4] Capping incentives unless every kilowatt and gallon is paid for at market cost restores balance. Internalization means direct payment for grid reinforcement and water sourcing, not vague promises of future community benefit. It tells investors that entry is welcome, but only on terms that do not hollow out public assets. It halts the subsidy arms race between jurisdictions that leaves every state poorer and every corporation richer.
[P1.5] Most importantly, it respects a basic principle of public finance: the beneficiary of a resource should bear its full cost. Governments can still compete for digital infrastructure, but they must do so with eyes open rather than with blank checks written on strained grids and shrinking reservoirs. That is not hostility to growth; it is the minimum standard for a responsible deal.

[P2.5] CON warns that the standard creates regulatory uncertainty [C1.6]. The opposite is true.
