Major cities must apply this logic to public transit by making it permanently fare-free and funding it through broad-based taxation.
[P1.2] First, transit fares are inherently regressive. They function as a flat tax on movement, disproportionately penalizing the low-income workers who rely on the system most. Eliminating fares immediately removes a barrier to economic mobility. Even if initial ridership gains are modest, the impact on current riders is transformative.
[P1.3] Fare-free transit guarantees that an empty bank account never traps a citizen in their neighborhood, ensuring universal access to jobs, education, and healthcare.
[P1.4] Second, fare collection is fundamentally inefficient. Transit agencies spend vast resources purchasing, maintaining, and enforcing payment infrastructure. Furthermore, collecting fares causes extensive "dwell time"—the minutes buses waste idling while passengers tap cards or fumble for exact change. When all-door boarding is free, transit becomes significantly faster and more reliable, while operators are freed from the dangerous, distracting role of fare enforcement.
[P1.5] Finally, broad-based taxation is the most economically rational funding model. Fareboxes rely on a user-pays system that ignores the massive positive externalities of public transit. Who actually benefits when a transit network runs smoothly? Local businesses, who receive a reliable stream of workers and consumers.
[P1.6] Property owners, whose real estate values surge when connected to transit lines. Even dedicated car commuters benefit from the reduced traffic congestion and lower emissions that a well-funded transit system provides. Because the entire urban ecosystem profits from public transit, the entire ecosystem should fund it through equitable, broad-based taxes.
[P1.7] Making transit fare-free is not just a transportation upgrade; it is a commitment to economic justice that makes cities fundamentally more efficient, equitable, and resilient.
