[P1.1] Presumed consent works because defaults matter. Behavioral research consistently shows that changing from opt-in to opt-out dramatically increases participation—even when the opt-out option remains freely available. Organ donation is the textbook case: countries with presumed consent systems consistently achieve higher transplant rates than comparable opt-in nations. The gap isn't explained by cultural differences alone; when countries switch systems, donation rates rise.
[P1.2] Spain's transformation after adopting presumed consent is the clearest example, but the pattern holds across multiple nations.
[P1.3] Why? Because most people support organ donation in principle but never complete the paperwork to register. Opt-in systems punish ordinary procrastination and inertia with death sentences for patients on transplant waiting lists. Presumed consent aligns the default with majority preference while preserving every individual's right to refuse.
[P1.4] And that right to refuse is what eliminates the ethical objection. Presumed consent is not conscription—no one is forced to donate. Anyone who objects for religious, personal, or any other reason simply registers that choice. The system changes which group bears the administrative burden: under opt-in, willing donors must navigate registration; under presumed consent, objectors opt out.
[P1.5] Since objectors are a small minority with strong motivation to act, while willing donors are a large majority with weak motivation, the allocation of burden that presumed consent creates is both more efficient and more just.
[P1.6] Families actually experience less distress under presumed consent. In opt-in systems, families confront a surprise question at the worst moment of their lives—often without knowing what their loved one wanted. Presumed consent sets a clear social expectation: donation is the norm, objecting is straightforward, and families face less agonizing ambiguity. Countries operating under presumed consent report smoother, less conflict-laden conversations with families precisely because expectations are established in advance.
[P1.7] The proposition asks whether transplant rates rise without ethical cost or added distress. They do, they haven't, and they won't.
