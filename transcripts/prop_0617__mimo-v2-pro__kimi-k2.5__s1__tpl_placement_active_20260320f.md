[P1.1] Dominant digital platforms operate in markets defined by extreme network effects, data monopolies, and winner-take-all dynamics. Their acquisitions are not normal mergers; they are strategic maneuvers to neutralize threats and entrench dominance. When a platform like Google buys a nascent mapping service or Facebook acquires a rising photo-sharing app, the goal is often to absorb competitive pressure, not to innovate for consumers. The current antitrust framework fails because it requires authorities to prove harm in fast-moving markets where evidence becomes clear only after competition is extinguished.
[P1.2] By then, the startup ecosystem is chilled, and consumers face fewer choices and less innovation long-term.
[P1.3] Therefore, we must flip the script. Antitrust authorities should presume acquisitions by dominant platforms are anti-competitive. This shifts the burden where it belongs: on the acquiring firm to demonstrate a clear, substantial consumer benefit. This is not a radical idea; it is a prudent safeguard against the unique power of digital gatekeepers.
[P1.4] Platforms can exploit their scale to identify and swallow potential rivals before they grow into full threats. The Instagram acquisition allowed Facebook to eliminate a competitor in photo-sharing and secure its social media monopoly, a move that likely harmed future innovation and consumer choice.
[P1.5] Without a presumption of harm, regulators are perpetually playing catch-up. The pace of digital markets means traditional, evidence-heavy reviews are outmatched. A presumption creates a necessary speed bump, forcing platforms to justify deals that might otherwise slip through. It protects the competitive process itself, ensuring that new innovators can emerge without fear of being simply bought and buried.
[P1.6] We must recognize that in digital markets, unchecked acquisitions are a primary vector for anti-competitive harm, and the law must reflect that reality.
