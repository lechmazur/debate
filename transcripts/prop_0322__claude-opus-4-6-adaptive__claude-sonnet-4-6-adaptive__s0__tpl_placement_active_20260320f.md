[P1.1] Host-read ads command premium rates for one reason: listeners trust the host. When someone hears their favorite podcaster personally endorse a mattress or a VPN, the implicit message is that a real human being chose to lend their credibility to this product. AI-generated voice replication breaks that implicit contract while leaving the listener none the wiser. A mandatory disclosure requirement is the straightforward fix.
[P1.2] **The deception is real and material.** Voice-cloning technology can now reproduce a speaker's cadence, tone, and vocal quirks with startling fidelity. If an ad sounds like the host read it but was actually synthesized or scripted from a likeness model, the audience is making purchasing decisions based on a false premise—that the host personally stands behind the product. This is not a hypothetical worry; major platforms and ad-tech firms are already marketing AI-read ad insertion tools to scale "host-read" spots across thousands of episodes without the host recording a single take.
[P1.3] **Disclosure norms already exist for exactly this kind of problem.** The FTC requires influencers to tag sponsored content. Securities law mandates conflict-of-interest disclosures. Pharmaceutical ads must list side effects. The principle is consistent: when information asymmetry could distort a consumer's decision, the law steps in with a simple transparency requirement.
[P1.4] Requiring a brief label—"this endorsement was generated using AI voice technology"—imposes negligible cost and enormous informational benefit.
[P1.5] **Without a legal mandate, market incentives run the wrong way.** Advertisers pay more for host-read spots precisely because they feel authentic. Disclosing that the spot is AI-generated undercuts that premium. Neither the creator licensing their voice nor the advertiser buying it has a financial reason to volunteer the truth. Voluntary self-regulation will predictably fail for the same reason it fails whenever the party holding the information profits from withholding it.
[P1.6] The ask here is minimal: a short, standardized disclosure. The harm it prevents—systematic consumer deception leveraging parasocial trust—is significant and growing. Transparency is the floor, not the ceiling.
