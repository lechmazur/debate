[P1.1] The current system of enterprise-level bargaining is failing workers and destabilizing our economy. It leaves millions in fragmented, low-unionization sectors—like hospitality, retail, and gig work—with no real power to negotiate, resulting in suppressed wages, erratic schedules, and a race to the bottom on standards. This isn’t just unfair; it’s economically irrational. It pits workers against each other and companies against competitors who cut corners, stifling innovation that relies on a skilled, stable workforce.

This system doesn’t erase enterprise deals; it establishes a baseline so negotiations can focus on lifting performance above a decent minimum, not fighting for the bare essentials.
[P1.3] The result is a more resilient economy. When an entire sector has predictable labor costs and trained workers, businesses can plan and invest. Workers have security and a path to advancement, boosting consumer spending and social stability. [P1.4] Adopting sectoral bargaining is a decisive step toward shared prosperity.

[C1.1] Sectoral bargaining sounds tidy in theory, but it exports the problems of one workplace to every workplace in an industry. Here is the core case against it.
[C1.2] First, it freezes the economy in amber. A single sector-wide floor treats a rural diner and a Manhattan hotel chain, a struggling startup and an incumbent giant, as if they share the same cost structure. They don't. When you standardize wages and conditions across firms that differ radically in productivity, you either set the bar low enough to be meaningless or high enough to kill marginal firms and the jobs inside them.
[C1.3] France and Italy, with strong sectoral coverage, have also chronically high youth and long-term unemployment. That isn't a coincidence; it's what happens when entry-level labor is priced by committee.
[C1.4] Second, it entrenches insiders at the expense of outsiders. The parties at the sectoral table are established unions and large employers—exactly the actors with an interest in writing rules that favor incumbents, credentialed workers, and existing business models. Gig platforms, immigrants, young workers, and disruptive competitors are the ones locked out. PRO frames this as solidarity; in practice it's a cartel with a friendlier name.
[C1.5] Third, it's democratically and administratively fragile. Who defines "the sector"? Is Amazon retail, logistics, or tech? Every boundary decision becomes a lobbying war, and every agreement binds non-consenting firms and non-member workers to terms they never negotiated.
[C1.6] That's not bargaining—it's regulation dressed as contract.
[C1.7] Fourth, we already have the better tools PRO is reaching for: statutory minimum wages, scheduling laws, OSHA-style standards, and sector-specific wage boards for genuine problem industries. These are transparent, democratically accountable, and adjustable. They deliver the floor without handing quasi-legislative power to private parties.
[C1.8] The decisive comparison: PRO promises a floor and assumes no ceiling, no exclusion, no rigidity. Every real sectoral system shows all three. Targeted regulation beats industry-wide cartelization.
