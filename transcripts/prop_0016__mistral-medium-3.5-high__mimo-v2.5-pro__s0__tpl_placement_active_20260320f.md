[P1.1] Homelessness is fundamentally a housing and service-access problem, and cities that treat it as such achieve what policing never can: actual reductions in homelessness. Finland’s Housing First policy, which provides permanent housing with attached support services as a right, has reduced homelessness by 40% nationwide. Utah’s program cut chronic homelessness by 91% in a decade. These work because they address the root cause—people need stable housing, and many need support to maintain it.
[P1.2] Stability is the platform for solving underlying issues like mental illness or addiction.
[P1.3] Policing fails because it misdiagnoses the problem as disorder rather than deprivation. Arresting people for sleeping in public doesn’t create housing; it creates criminal records that make obtaining housing, jobs, or benefits harder. The financial waste is clear: in many cities, the annual cost of managing a chronically homeless person through emergency services and policing can exceed $100,000, while permanent supportive housing costs a fraction of that and actually solves the problem. Sweeps don’t end homelessness; they just displace people, often destroying their few possessions—medications, IDs, documents—making recovery harder.
[P1.4] Most damning, policing treats symptoms while ignoring root causes. Homelessness rises with housing costs; cities with more affordable housing have less homelessness. The solution is to build more housing and provide services, not to criminalize the lack of a home. Every dollar spent on enforcement is a dollar not spent on the actual solution.
[P1.5] The evidence is overwhelming: housing ends homelessness; policing just punishes people for being poor and leaves the underlying problem untouched.
