[P1.1] An AI risk score is an alarm, not an adjudication. Public agencies may use alarms to prioritize investigation, route files, or detect patterns. But they should never cut off a person’s benefits, suspend a visa, or treat a claim as fraudulent solely because a model has labeled them suspicious without meaningful human review. The central principle is simple: when the state deprives someone of a legal interest, the state must be able to give a reason grounded in evidence about that person, not just point to a machine’s suspicion.
[P1.2] This is not a technical quibble. Risk models are built on administrative data that is often messy, incomplete, and full of proxies. A benefit recipient may be flagged because of a mismatched address, a shared phone number, an unusual work pattern, or a data entry error. A visa holder may be flagged because a model sees a travel pattern that resembles prior violations but misses the legitimate family, medical, or work context.
[P1.3] Even a model that is “mostly accurate” creates wrongful suspensions when applied at public scale. And the person harmed is often least able to absorb the delay: rent is due, medicine is needed, a job or family arrangement depends on status.
[P1.4] Meaningful human review is the minimum safeguard that turns a warning into a decision. It requires someone with authority to look at the underlying facts, consider obvious innocent explanations, and take responsibility for the outcome. A rubber stamp is not enough; neither is a post-hoc appeal after the damage is done. The review must occur before the suspension because the harm of government deprivation is immediate and often irreversible.
[P1.5] The opposing position has to defend something far more extreme than efficiency. It must defend suspension based solely on an opaque suspicion, with no accountable person checking whether the case actually makes sense. That invites bureaucratic laundering: the agency blames the algorithm, the algorithm cannot explain itself, and the individual is left proving a negative. Fraud control matters, but suspicion is not proof.

Demanding human pre-review on every flag means either freezing the system or letting obvious fraud through — and the people harmed by "letting it through" are real: identity-theft victims, taxpayers, and legitimate applicants whose queues get crowded out by fraud rings.
[C1.3] Second, "meaningful human review" is not the safeguard PRO pretends it is. A caseworker glancing at a screen for ninety seconds satisfies the rule and protects no one. The actual protections against wrongful deprivation are procedural: prompt notice, fast reversal, presumptive restoration when the person responds, audit trails, and liability for the agency. A well-designed automated suspension with a 48-hour cure window can protect rights *better* than a human signature on a file the human never really read.
[C1.4] PRO is fetishizing the human as a talisman.
[C1.5] Third, PRO's own framing concedes the case. They admit alarms can "prioritize investigation" and "route files." But routing a suspicious claim into a verification track — where benefits pause until the applicant confirms identity — *is* a suspension triggered solely by the model. If that's permitted, "never" is already false.
[C1.6] If it's forbidden, agencies lose the basic tool every bank, every insurer, every payment processor uses to protect customers.
[C1.7] The right rule is not "never act on a model." It is "act proportionately, reversibly, and accountably." PRO's absolute rule sacrifices that nuance for a slogan.
