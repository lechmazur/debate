[P1.1] Paywalled private genealogy platforms lock marginalized groups out of their own family histories, a harm no market incentive will ever fix. A Black descendant of enslaved people in the U.S. whose ancestors are only named in handwritten plantation probate records faces a $15 to $25 monthly subscription fee just to access documents that are inherently part of their identity, a cost millions of low-income families cannot afford. Indigenous people seeking records of relatives taken to residential schools, or survivors of wartime displacement searching for lost family, face the same arbitrary barrier, simply because private firms prioritize revenue over equitable access.
[P1.2] Private platforms also have no incentive to prioritize high-impact, low-profit record sets. They devote most of their digitization budgets to popular, high-demand records like 20th century census data from wealthy countries, leaving unprocessed the adoption files of children separated during the U.S. and Canadian Sixties Scoop, rosters of enslaved people held on Caribbean sugar plantations, and displacement records of Roma communities during the Holocaust. These records are critical not just for personal identity, but for building legal cases for reparations, supporting truth and reconciliation processes, and reuniting separated families: public goods that private markets will never adequately deliver.
[P1.3] Finally, publicly funded digitization comes with enforceable guardrails that private firms systematically reject. Private genealogy sites regularly sell user search data to third parties, including real estate firms that target Indigenous heirs with fraudulent property buyout offers, and debt collectors mining old estate records. Public systems can be built to center descendant privacy first, restrict commercial use of sensitive records, and prioritize access for affected communities before general public release, eliminating the perverse profit incentives that put vulnerable groups at risk.

We should not trade a vibrant, evolving market for a static, underfunded, and state-sanctioned bottleneck.
