[P1.1] Patents are a tool to serve public health, not a suicide pact that allows monopolies to withhold life-saving treatments. When the price of survival is set beyond reach, the social contract of the patent system fails. Countries must treat access to essential medicines as grounds for compulsory licensing whenever prices exceed a defined affordability threshold to correct this market failure and protect human life.
[P1.2] The pharmaceutical market for essential medicines does not function like a normal consumer market. Demand is fundamentally inelastic; patients cannot choose to forgo cancer treatments, insulin, or antiretrovirals. Armed with government-granted monopolies, pharmaceutical companies routinely exploit this desperation, setting prices that bankrupt healthcare systems and leave millions without care. A patent is meant to incentivize the creation of medicines, but a medicine that a patient cannot afford is functionally non-existent to them.
[P1.3] Establishing a defined affordability threshold creates a transparent, objective trigger to prevent this abuse. It replaces arbitrary political interventions with a predictable standard. Crucially, this policy acts as a powerful market deterrent. Pharmaceutical companies can easily avoid compulsory licensing by simply pricing their essential medicines fairly, staying below the threshold.
[P1.4] This mechanism forces companies to align their pricing with the economic reality of the populations they serve, rather than maximizing global profit margins at the expense of local access.
[P1.5] Furthermore, compulsory licensing strikes the necessary balance between innovation and access. It is not property theft; international trade frameworks require that patent holders receive reasonable royalties when a compulsory license is issued. The innovator remains compensated for their research and development. However, their monopoly power to extort the public is rightfully revoked.
[P1.6] The primary purpose of medical innovation is to heal people, not to produce inaccessible luxury goods. By adopting an affordability threshold as the trigger for compulsory licensing, countries can guarantee that when science produces a life-saving treatment, no patient is left to die simply because they could not pay a monopoly premium.

[C1.1] Patents are not a suicide pact; they are the contract that makes pharmaceutical research possible. The moment we treat patents as automatically voidable whenever a price crosses an arbitrary line, we do not fix market failure—we collapse the market itself. The proposition promises access today by destroying the mechanism that produces the medicines of tomorrow.
[C1.2] The claim that innovators remain protected because they receive “reasonable royalties” fundamentally misunderstands drug development. Bringing a new molecule from discovery to pharmacy shelves typically requires more than a decade of research, billions in sunk costs, and failure rates that dwarf most other industries. Patent protection exists because royalties set after the fact by public bodies rarely compensate for that risk. When governments can void monopoly pricing at will, capital flees to safer investments.
[C1.3] The result is not cheaper cancer drugs; it is fewer cancer drugs being invented in the first place.
[C1.4] Moreover, the idea of a “defined affordability threshold” sounds precise but is administratively naive. What is affordable in Norway is catastrophic in Nepal. A single metric either triggers constantly in poor nations—devastating any incentive to serve them—or is set so loosely that it never triggers at all. Either way, the threshold becomes a political football rather than an objective safeguard, replacing negotiated solutions with bureaucratic warfare.
[C1.5] When governments seize patents unilaterally, originators routinely restrict technology transfer, withhold active pharmaceutical ingredients, or withdraw new products from those markets entirely. Patients waiting for compulsory generic copies often face years of regulatory delay, substandard alternatives, and broken supply chains. By contrast, voluntary licensing and tiered pricing have expanded access to treatments for HIV and hepatitis without ripping up the innovation contract.
[C1.6] Access to essential medicines is urgent, but automatic compulsory licensing is the wrong instrument. It sacrifices long-term supply for short-term price controls. Countries should strengthen purchasing power, expand voluntary agreements, and use existing flexibilities with surgical precision—not strap a hair trigger to the patent system and hope the laboratory lights stay on.

[P2.1] My opponent argues that an affordability threshold is "administratively naive" because what is affordable in Norway is catastrophic in Nepal [C1.4]. This fundamentally misreads the proposition. We are advocating that individual *countries* use a defined threshold. Nepal sets its trigger based on its local health budget; Norway sets its own.
[P2.2] Rather than a naive global metric, this framework forces exactly what the opposition claims to want: localized pricing. If a company wants to maintain its monopoly in Nepal, it must price for Nepal.
[P2.3] CON also warns that compulsory licensing will collapse pharmaceutical R&D, claiming that royalties cannot compensate for sunk costs [C1.2]. This relies on the false premise that medical innovation only survives through unlimited price extortion. An affordability threshold does not eliminate profit; it limits the price *per unit*. Innovators can still achieve massive returns through high-volume sales.
[P2.4] Furthermore, an unaffordable drug generates zero revenue from the millions of patients who cannot buy it. Earning a reasonable royalty on a locally produced generic actually generates value from a market segment the originator was failing to serve. Capital will not flee an industry that guarantees a captive, global market of billions of patients; it will simply adapt to a high-volume, fair-price model.
[P2.5] Finally, CON praises "voluntary licensing and tiered pricing" as the superior alternative [C1.5]. But pharmaceutical companies rarely offer equitable tiered pricing out of charity; they do it when forced. Historically, voluntary agreements only materialize when the threat of compulsory licensing is credible. An objective affordability threshold is not a "hair trigger" to destroy patents—it is the exact market leverage required to make those voluntary negotiations work.
[P2.6] If a company prices essential medicines affordably, the compulsory license is never used.
[P2.7] We cannot sacrifice guaranteed access today over the hollow threat that pharma will abandon a highly lucrative global industry. When a life-saving medicine exists but is deliberately withheld from dying patients to protect artificial profit margins, the patent system has failed its only legitimate public purpose.

Most medicines never reach blockbuster status; they rely on global margins across all markets to recoup astronomical failure rates. Firms calculate expected global returns before launching or investing. [C2.5] The threshold becomes a wall, not a bridge.

The proposition demands licensing *whenever* prices exceed the threshold, not whenever a minister feels like negotiating. Replace predictable patent rights with automatic expropriation, and you do not get better table manners; you get an empty table.
