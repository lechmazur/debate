[P1.1] Governments must require social media platforms to provide interoperable, user-portable messaging and social graph access to qualified third-party services. The dominant platforms have built unassailable moats: your friends, family messages, and community history are held hostage inside proprietary systems. This lock-in distorts markets, suppresses innovation, and concentrates power over public discourse in the hands of a few executives.
[P1.2] Interoperability changes that equation. Users could message contacts on any compliant service without switching apps or abandoning their network, exactly as email crosses providers today. Portable social graphs would let people migrate to better moderation tools, stronger privacy defaults, or specialized interfaces while keeping the relationships that give the network its value. The result is genuine consumer sovereignty: platforms would compete on merit rather than inertia.
[P1.3] This is not radical. Telecommunications regulators long ago mandated number portability and interconnection; those rules delivered lower prices and rapid feature growth. Social media today is more essential to daily life than landlines ever were, yet it operates under far weaker competitive discipline. Requiring qualified third parties to meet defined security and data-handling standards simply extends established regulatory logic to the dominant communications medium of our era.
[P1.4] Critics will warn of security risks or weakened moderation. Those objections protect incumbent rents, not users. Qualified access can be gated behind audited protocols, rate limits, and liability rules that preserve safety without preserving monopoly. The real danger is the status quo: unaccountable giants shaping what billions see and say while deterring any rival that might offer superior alternatives.
[P1.5] Interoperability dismantles that barrier. It is the minimum condition for a competitive, user-centered information ecosystem.
