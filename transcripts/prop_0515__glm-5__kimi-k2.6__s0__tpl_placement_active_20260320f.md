[P1.2] Decades of cognitive science have converged on a clear conclusion: structured, explicit instruction in phonemic awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension produces superior outcomes for the largest number of students. This is not a narrow pedagogical preference—it reflects how the human brain acquires the complex skill of decoding written language. The widely cited Mississippi "miracle," where fourth-grade reading scores rose dramatically after the state mandated evidence-based structured literacy instruction and retained third-graders not meeting benchmarks, demonstrates that policy levers work when aligned with cognitive science.
[P1.3] Classroom autonomy sounds appealing in theory. But in practice, it means that whether a child learns to read depends on which teacher they happen to be assigned. [P1.4] Students from disadvantaged backgrounds, who often arrive at school with smaller vocabularies and less exposure to print, have the least margin for error. They cannot afford a year of well-intentioned experimentation.
[P1.5] Nor should we pretend that autonomy reliably produces innovation. Autonomy without accountability often calcifies into habit—teachers teach as they were taught, or as their preferred teacher-preparation program instructed, regardless of what evidence shows.
[P1.6] The policy question is not whether teachers deserve respect; it is whether vulnerable children deserve protection from ineffective instruction. When we know better, we must do better.
