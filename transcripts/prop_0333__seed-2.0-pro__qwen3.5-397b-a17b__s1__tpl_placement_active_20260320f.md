[P1.1] Earlier venue closing times fail to reduce nightlife disorder and drunk driving for two core reasons, while late-night weekend transit extensions directly solve both failures at once.
[P1.2] First, early forced closures create concentrated, unmanageable risk peaks. When every bar, club, and restaurant kicks patrons out at the same arbitrary time, hundreds or thousands of intoxicated people spill onto sidewalks simultaneously, fighting for limited rideshares, loitering in crowds that spark fights and public disturbance, and flooding roadways at the exact same time with impaired drivers who cannot secure a ride. We saw this play out when multiple cities implemented 10pm hospitality curfews during the first year of the COVID pandemic: DUI stops and street disturbance reports spiked sharply in the 30 minutes immediately after curfew, as the mass exodus overwhelmed available safe transport options.
[P1.3] Second, early closures do not reduce overall alcohol consumption or associated risk—they shift it to unregulated, higher-risk spaces. [P1.4] Late-night transit extensions eliminate both gaps. They let patrons leave venues gradually on their own schedule, eliminating the mass exodus risk peak, and give all people in entertainment districts a low-cost, reliable alternative to driving drunk, no matter when they choose to head home. Disorder drops because people leave the area immediately instead of loitering waiting for rides, and drunk driving crashes fall drastically as riders opt for transit over getting behind the wheel.
