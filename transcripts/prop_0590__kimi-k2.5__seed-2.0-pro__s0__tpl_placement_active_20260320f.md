[P1.1] High-risk artificial intelligence does not check passports at the border. When an algorithm diagnoses cancer in a Berlin hospital, sorts résumés for a Parisian firm, or determines credit scores for Milanese families, the harm it causes depends entirely on its impact inside the European Union—not on whether its developers file taxes in Delaware, Singapore, or Tallinn. Brussels must regulate these systems based on where the damage lands, not where the servers sit.
[P1.2] The current provider-based approach creates a dangerous enforcement gap. A healthcare AI developed in a jurisdiction with weak oversight can flood European markets through cloud deployment, leaving regulators powerless if the company maintains no physical EU presence. Basing jurisdiction on impact solves this: if the system touches European patients, it falls under European law, full stop. This ensures accountability travels with the risk.
[P1.3] Impact-based rules also prevent regulatory arbitrage that hollows out protection. Without this standard, firms simply relocate headquarters to regulatory havens while continuing to profit from European data and users. The result is a race to the bottom where high-risk systems evade the AI Act’s safeguards precisely because they are high-risk enough to need evasion.
[P1.4] Brussels already recognizes this principle. The GDPR’s extraterritorial scope applies to any processing that affects Union residents, regardless of where the controller is established. Extending this logic to high-risk AI is not expansionism; it is consistency. When stakes involve medical safety, fundamental rights, and democratic integrity, jurisdiction must follow harm.
