[P1.1] The EU’s high-risk AI rules exist first and foremost to protect EU residents from harm, and applying them based on impact location is the only way to deliver on that core promise. If a Singapore-based firm sells a hiring algorithm to German employers that systematically discriminates against Muslim and migrant applicants, there is no logical reason its overseas headquarters should exempt it from accountability to the people it harms within EU borders. This is not an untested regulatory model: the GDPR already applies to any company processing the data of EU data subjects, regardless of where the firm is based, and that established framework can be directly adapted to scope high-risk AI rules with minimal new administrative burden for firms.
[P1.2] Applying rules by impact also eliminates a damaging competitive disadvantage for EU-based AI providers. Under a headquarters-based rule, a Polish startup building a high-risk medical diagnostic tool has to meet strict EU safety standards to sell to Spanish hospitals, while a competing firm based in a low-regulation jurisdiction can sell the exact same class of tool to those same hospitals with zero oversight, undercutting the EU firm on cost while putting patient lives at risk.
[P1.3] Finally, a headquarters-based rule creates an obvious race to the bottom: high-risk AI providers can simply relocate their official headquarters to jurisdictions with no AI safety rules to entirely evade EU oversight, rendering the entire high-risk regime functionally useless for anyone willing to engage in trivial regulatory arbitrage. There is no upside to tying rules to headquarters location that justifies putting EU residents at risk, kneecapping domestic AI firms, and hollowing out the core purpose of the AI Act.
