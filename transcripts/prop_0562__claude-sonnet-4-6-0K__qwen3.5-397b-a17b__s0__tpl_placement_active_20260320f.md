Canada's Seasonal Agricultural Worker Program has operated for decades, channeling hundreds of thousands of workers through orderly, documented pathways with high return rates. The workers get safety and legal protections; the employers get reliable labor; and the pressure on irregular crossings is directly relieved.
[P1.3] Border enforcement, by contrast, operates on a fundamentally different logic: it raises the cost and danger of crossing without removing the underlying demand. The result is predictable. [P1.4] Enforcement spending grows while irregular populations stabilize or even expand, because the pull of unmet labor demand remains intact.
[P1.5] The comparison the debate demands is one of effectiveness. Legal pathways attack the root cause. [P1.6] Opponents will argue enforcement is a necessary complement. That may be true at the margins, but the proposition is about what is more effective as the primary lever. Expanding legal pathways changes the incentive structure fundamentally. Enforcement merely hardens the obstacle.
