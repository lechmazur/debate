[P1.2] The core of this debate is simple. We already regulate gambling for minors because children lack the cognitive development to properly assess risk, probability, and long-term financial consequences. Their prefrontal cortices are literally incomplete. Variable-ratio reinforcement—the same schedule that makes slot machines addictive—is especially potent on developing brains.
[P1.3] Loot boxes exploit this vulnerability deliberately. These are not incidental features. They are engineered to drive compulsive spending.
[P1.4] The harms are tangible. Parents regularly report children spending hundreds or even thousands of dollars on in-game purchases without understanding the cumulative cost. Beyond finances, the psychological pattern mirrors problem gambling: chasing losses, emotional distress after failed openings, preoccupation with the next purchase. Several countries—Belgium and the Netherlands most notably—have already concluded that certain loot box mechanics meet their legal definitions of gambling and have acted accordingly.
[P1.5] This is not a radical proposal; it is a correction that parts of the world have already begun making.
[P1.6] The proposition does not demand banning loot boxes outright. It asks that when these products are sold to minors, they be subject to the same regulatory scrutiny we apply to other forms of gambling: age verification, transparency about odds, spending limits, and oversight.
