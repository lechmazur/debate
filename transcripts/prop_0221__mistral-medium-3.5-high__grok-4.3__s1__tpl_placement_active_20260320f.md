[P1.1] High-dosage tutoring delivers outsized learning gains at a fraction of the cost of class size reduction. Education research consistently shows intensive tutoring—three or more sessions per week with well-trained tutors—produces effect sizes of roughly a third to half a standard deviation in math and reading, equivalent to an extra year of learning for struggling students. The mechanism is clear: tutoring provides immediate, targeted feedback and mastery-based progression, while smaller classes still require teachers to divide attention among 15–20 students.
[P1.2] Cost efficiency clinches the comparison. Reducing class size from 25 to 15 requires about two-thirds more teachers per grade, with payroll costs scaling linearly and gains tapering off as classes shrink. High-dosage tutoring leverages part-time tutors or paraprofessionals at lower wages, delivering targeted instruction in 30–45 minute sessions. At scale, such programs typically cost a few thousand dollars per student per year—far below the ten thousand plus per student cost of hiring additional certified teachers.
[P1.3] Crucially, tutoring can be concentrated on the students who need it most, maximizing impact per dollar. Even conservative estimates show tutoring’s cost per learning gain beats class size reduction by several times, making it the superior investment for immediate, measurable progress in core subjects.

[C1.1] Reducing class sizes delivers steady gains across the full school day by letting one certified teacher maintain tighter control over pacing, behavior, and feedback for every student present. A drop from 25 to 15 pupils cuts the number of children who slip through gaps in reading and math without anyone noticing until the next test, an advantage that accumulates in every lesson rather than depending on scheduled 30-minute pull-outs.
[C1.2] High-dosage tutoring cannot match this consistency at scale. Finding, training, and retaining thousands of reliable tutors each year introduces turnover and quality variation that study pilots rarely capture. When tutors are scarce or underpaid, sessions get canceled, mismatched to student needs, or delivered by less experienced staff, eroding the effect sizes that look impressive in small trials.
[C1.3] The per-dollar calculation also shifts once real system costs are included. Adding tutors requires new layers of scheduling, space, materials, and coordination with classroom teachers; these administrative burdens do not shrink proportionally with volume the way shared classroom overhead does. Smaller classes, by contrast, use the same buildings and curriculum already in place and allow the existing teacher to spread instructional effort more evenly.
[C1.4] In practice, the students who gain most from tutoring are often the same ones who still spend most of their time in larger groups once the tutor leaves. Class-size reduction changes the baseline environment for all instruction rather than layering an expensive add-on that reaches only selected children for limited hours. That structural difference makes the claimed cost advantage for tutoring disappear once implementation realities replace pilot assumptions.
