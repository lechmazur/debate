It means that stopping a tonne of methane from entering the atmosphere today delivers climate benefits that carbon dioxide-focused interventions simply cannot match on the timescale that matters most—the next two decades, when we are closest to crossing dangerous temperature thresholds.
[P1.2] Carbon capture and storage operates on CO₂, a molecule that persists in the atmosphere for centuries. Reducing that stock matters enormously for long-run stabilization. But the near-term benefit of capturing CO₂ is modest because the marginal tonne you capture today would have warmed the planet slowly and cumulatively. Methane, by contrast, drives rapid radiative forcing and then largely breaks down in roughly twelve years.
[P1.3] Plug the leak now, and the warming effect dissipates within a generation. That is near-term benefit in the most direct sense possible.
[P1.4] Oil and gas infrastructure is already the single largest industrial source of methane emissions globally. Tightened leak detection and repair requirements would close that gap quickly because the infrastructure already exists; you are not building anything new, you are maintaining what is there.
[P1.5] CCS, meanwhile, carries enormous capital costs, long construction timelines, and persistent questions about permanence and geological storage integrity. The IEA and other bodies have noted that CCS deployment has chronically lagged projections. Equivalent spending on methane leak detection buys faster deployment, faster results, and a verified climate signal within years, not decades.
[P1.6] The central comparison is simple: for near-term climate benefit per dollar spent, methane wins on physics, speed, and deployment reality.
