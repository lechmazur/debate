[P1.1] The question before us is not whether diverse clinical trials are scientifically preferable—they are—but whether we should mandate them even at the cost of delayed approvals. I say yes.
[P1.2] First, safety requires representation. A drug tested predominantly on young white men may reveal little about how a 70-year-old Black woman will respond. Pharmacokinetics differ by age, sex, and ethnicity. Enzyme expression varies.
[P1.3] Body composition changes with age. When we approve a medication on incomplete evidence, we do not simply slow progress—we transfer the unknown harm onto populations excluded from the trial. That is not a slowdown of approval; that is a hidden cost, paid by patients rather than regulators.
[P1.4] Second, history shows this matters concretely. For decades, cardiovascular drugs were prescribed to women at doses derived entirely from male subjects. When researchers finally studied sex-specific responses, dosing recommendations shifted. Had these trials been representative from the start, patients would have received safer care sooner.
[P1.5] The current system—approve fast, discover gaps later—is not faster overall. It is slower, more chaotic, and more dangerous.
[P1.6] Third, the "material slowdown" argument overstates the cost. Regulatory agencies already take a decade or more to approve drugs. Designing diverse trials from the outset does not multiply timelines geometrically; it improves the quality of each data point and reduces the need for post-market surveillance studies that drag on for years anyway.
[P1.7] Fourth, proportionate representation is a matter of justice. When a drug reaches market tested only on certain populations, everyone else becomes an unwitting experiment at the point of prescription. Communities that fund research and bear taxation have a legitimate claim to evidence that applies to them.
[P1.8] The motion asks us to choose between speed and equity. I argue we are actually choosing between speed-plus-risk and slower-plus-safe. That trade is not worth making.
