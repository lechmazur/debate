[P1.1] Precise location data is not just another data point. It answers where you sleep, where you worship, which clinic you visited on a Tuesday morning, and whether you attended a political protest. A comprehensive record of your movements is, effectively, a map of your private life—and right now, data brokers sell that map to anyone willing to pay, without your knowledge and without any meaningful expiration.
[P1.2] The case for this prohibition rests on three interlocking claims.
[P1.3] First, the harm is concrete and severe. Domestic abusers have used commercially purchased location data to track victims who fled. Journalists have demonstrated that location records sold by brokers can be reverse-engineered to identify individuals even when "anonymized." Law enforcement has purchased location feeds to conduct surveillance that would otherwise require a warrant.
[P1.4] Each of these harms flows directly from the current arrangement, where consent is neither required nor time-bounded.
[P1.5] Second, existing consent frameworks are structurally inadequate. Buried in app permissions, one-time opt-ins granted years ago follow users indefinitely—through divorces, relocations, changes in health status, changes in political circumstance. A consent model without time limits is not genuine consent; it is a snapshot imposed on a moving life. The proposition's requirement of explicit, time-limited opt-in directly addresses this gap by ensuring consent remains current and affirmative.
[P1.6] Third, individuals cannot protect themselves through market choice alone. Most people have no idea which brokers hold their location history, let alone how to opt out of firms they've never heard of. This is a textbook information asymmetry that only regulatory intervention can correct. Requiring explicit opt-in shifts the default: brokers must earn permission rather than exploit its absence.
[P1.7] The central question this debate resolves is whether precise location data warrants special protection. It does, because its harms are irreversible, because current consent is fictional, and because no market mechanism fills the gap. Prohibition of unconsented sales is the proportionate and necessary response.

[C1.1] The proposition sounds like a clean win for privacy. It isn't. It targets one narrow link—broker resale—while leaving the actual pipeline of location exposure wide open, and it imposes real costs on legitimate uses to buy a privacy benefit that is largely symbolic.
[C1.2] Start with the mismatch between the harm and the remedy. The abuser stalking a fleeing partner does not typically buy a broker feed; they use shared accounts, Find My, a cheap tracker, or a compromised phone. The police agency dodging warrants can contract directly with app developers or device makers, who are not "data brokers" under any normal definition. Every horror story PRO cites traces back to first-party collection.
[C1.3] Ban broker resale and the app on your phone still harvests the same data, sells it under a "marketing analytics" label, or shares it with "partners." You have moved the pea under a different shell.
[C1.4] Second, "explicit, time-limited opt-in" is consent theater. We already ran this experiment with cookie banners. Forcing re-consent at intervals doesn't produce thoughtful decisions; it produces reflexive clicking, and it entrenches the giants—Google, Apple, Meta—who own the surface where consent is requested. Small competitors, independent researchers, and public-interest users of aggregated mobility data lose access; the incumbents keep theirs through first-party relationships.
[C1.5] Third, the collateral damage is concrete. Aggregated location data underwrites traffic routing, transit planning, retail site decisions, pandemic response modeling, wildfire and disaster logistics, and fraud detection that protects the same consumers PRO invokes. A prohibition framed at the broker layer sweeps in these uses or forces them into the hands of whoever can afford bespoke first-party deals.
[C1.6] The right comparison isn't "prohibition versus status quo." It's prohibition versus a comprehensive privacy framework—purpose limitation, bans on government purchase of data that would need a warrant, fiduciary duties on collectors. That approach attacks the harm at its source. The motion as written attacks a symptom and calls it a cure.
