[P1.1] Short-haul aviation is the segment that rail was practically designed to kill. Below 500 miles, planes spend most of their fuel and most of their emissions on takeoff, climb, and the airport experience that surrounds them: drive to the airport an hour early, security, taxi, hold, descend, deplane, baggage. A 70-minute flight is a four-hour day. High-speed rail eats that whole envelope by going city-center to city-center.
[P1.2] The empirical record is one-sided. When the TGV connected Paris and Lyon, the air route collapsed. Madrid–Barcelona's air share fell sharply once the AVE matured. On the Tokyo–Osaka corridor, the Shinkansen has carried the dominant share for decades.
[P1.3] Wherever a serious sub-500-mile HSR line opens, the parallel flights wither. That is the demonstrated mechanism by which aviation emissions actually fall.
[P1.4] Now compare the alternative the motion asks us to weigh: electrifying short-haul flights. Battery-electric aircraft at meaningful passenger capacity over 300–500 miles do not exist as certified commercial products. The energy density gap is not a rounding error; jet fuel is roughly 40–50 times more energy-dense per kilogram than current batteries. Even optimistic projections give us 9- to 19-seat regional hops, not LaGuardia–Chicago.
[P1.5] Hydrogen and synthetic fuels are decades and trillions away from displacing the narrowbody fleet. So "electrify short-haul flights" is, today, a promise; HSR is a deployed technology with a track record on three continents.
[P1.6] On cost-effectiveness, the right denominator is dollars per ton of CO2 abated and dollars per hour of traveler time saved, summed across decades. HSR infrastructure is expensive once and then moves hundreds of millions of passengers on grid electricity for half a century. Electrifying aviation requires reinventing the airframe, the propulsion system, the certification regime, and the airport energy infrastructure—and then still burning runway capacity for trips a train could absorb.
[P1.7] Pick the tool that exists, works, and scales. That is rail.

[C1.1] The PRO case rests on an exception, not a global rule. High-speed rail is not a universal solvent for emissions; it is a boutique infrastructure project viable only in hyper-dense corridors. For the vast majority of sub-500 mile city pairs, the cost-effectiveness equation collapses immediately.
[C1.2] Consider the carbon debt. Constructing hundreds of miles of dedicated track requires immense quantities of concrete and steel, generating huge emissions before a single passenger boards. In contrast, electrifying short-haul flights leverages existing airport infrastructure and airways. The marginal emissions cost of upgrading propulsion is a fraction of laying new track.
[C1.3] For rapid decarbonization, pouring concrete is the slowest path. Grid upgrades are incremental and cheap; rail construction is binary and expensive.
[C1.4] Furthermore, the travel time advantage is fragile. HSR only wins when stations are perfectly city-centered and lines are direct. Most proposed corridors require costly land acquisition and face geographic barriers that inflate travel time and budget. New rail projects routinely suffer decade-long delays and cost blowouts.
[C1.5] Meanwhile, hybrid-electric turboprops are already entering certification for regional routes. These aircraft do not need new rights-of-way; they need battery advancements, which are following rapid trajectories.
[C1.6] The PRO argues aviation electrification is a promise. But HSR in new contexts is also a promise—of political will, unlimited budgets, and perfect geography that rarely materializes. Electrification scales across the entire network overnight; rail scales only where terrain and density align.
[C1.7] Finally, consider flexibility and cost. A plane electrified today can fly any 500-mile route tomorrow. A train is locked to its track forever. If demand shifts, the rail asset becomes stranded capital.
[C1.8] The motion asks for cost-effectiveness. Building fixed lines for a few corridors cannot beat upgrading a flexible network serving thousands. We should invest in the technology that fits the world as it exists, not the world as planners wish it to be.

[P2.5] On geography: the motion specifies major city pairs under 500 miles.
