[P1.1] Medicine is supposed to work on people—all people. The proposition stands on a simple, devastating fact: drug metabolism, disease presentation, and treatment response vary systematically across age, sex, and ethnicity. When trials exclude these groups, we are not running neutral science. We are running science on a subset of humanity and then prescribing the results to everyone else.
[P1.2] The consequences are not hypothetical. Cardiovascular drugs have been found to perform differently in women than in the male-dominant trial populations that generated approval data. Certain metabolic enzymes critical for drug clearance appear at different frequencies across ethnic populations, meaning a dose calibrated on one group may be toxic or ineffective for another. Older patients—often the primary users of approved drugs—have historically been underrepresented despite having different pharmacokinetics due to reduced renal function and polypharmacy.
[P1.3] Approving drugs faster on the basis of narrow data does not help these patients. It exposes them to uncalibrated risk while giving clinicians false confidence.
[P1.4] The CON position will argue that representation requirements slow approvals and therefore cost lives during the delay. This trade-off sounds painful, but it is largely illusory. The delay is a one-time cost; the harm from a badly characterized drug is cumulative and often irreversible. A drug recalled, relabeled, or quietly avoided because post-market safety signals emerge in underrepresented groups represents a far larger systemic failure than a modest extension of the trial period.
[P1.5] Preventable adverse events, stratified along demographic lines, are not a side effect of slow science—they are the direct product of fast, narrow science.
[P1.6] There is also an equity dimension that cannot be separated from the scientific one. Communities that bear higher burdens of certain diseases—often minority and older populations—have historically been excluded from the trials studying those very diseases. Proportionate representation is not a procedural nicety. It is the condition under which approval data becomes trustworthy for the people who need the drug most.
[P1.7] The central clash in this debate is simple: speed for whom, and safety measured how?
