[P1.1] Corporate "net-zero" claims currently function as a dangerous loophole. By purchasing cheap, loosely verified carbon offsets, companies claim climate neutrality while continuing to emit greenhouse gases at scale. We must legally limit offsets to a small fraction of corporate footprints, mandating that the vast majority of reductions happen within the company’s own value chain. Without this legal ceiling, offsets are just an accounting trick.
[P1.2] The voluntary market is flooded with phantom credits—such as paying to protect forests that were never actually threatened, or planting trees that remain highly vulnerable to future wildfires and cannot guarantee permanent carbon storage.
[P1.3] Allowing unlimited offsets actively delays the systemic economic transformation required to halt climate change. It creates a massive capital misallocation problem. When a heavy-industry, aviation, or logistics firm can simply buy carbon credits for a few dollars a ton, it destroys the internal business case for structural upgrades. It disincentivizes capital expenditure in vital technologies like green steel, zero-emission fleets, and industrial electrification.
[P1.4] By legally capping offsets, we force companies to internalize the true cost of their pollution. This mandates the redirection of billions of dollars away from dubious offset brokers and into tangible research, development, and deployment of clean technology—ultimately driving down the "green premium" for the entire global economy.
[P1.5] Furthermore, unrestricted offset usage misinterprets the role of a carbon sink. Offsets should exist exclusively as a last resort to neutralize residual, truly hard-to-abate emissions—the final fraction of a footprint that current technology cannot yet eliminate. Permitting companies to use offsets for half or more of their emissions allows them to operate business-as-usual and lock in fossil-fuel infrastructure for decades. A strict legal limit ensures offsets serve their only valid purpose: cleaning up the unavoidable margins, while forcing the core business to genuinely decarbonize.
