[P1.1] Predictive AI for worker risk scores is inherently discriminatory, because it trains on historical workplace data that codifies decades of bias against women, racial minorities, disabled workers, and caregivers. If a company’s past leadership penalized workers who took family leave or filed discrimination complaints, the algorithm will mark any worker who fits those demographic profiles as lower productivity or higher turnover risks, even if their actual work record is spotless. This is not “predicting performance” — it is automating and scaling past unfairness with no transparent paper trail for workers to appeal.
[P1.2] The unionization prediction use case is an active assault on legally protected labor rights. Current law bars employers from retaliating against workers for organizing, but AI risk scores let companies quietly cut hours, reassign shifts, or even fire pro-union workers without leaving explicit evidence of retaliation. Workers cannot prove they were targeted for their organizing activity when the only justification is a black-box algorithm score the company refuses to disclose, rendering core labor protections functionally unenforceable.
[P1.3] Turnover and productivity predictions also create self-fulfilling prophecies that punish workers for harms they never caused. If an algorithm flags a worker as likely to quit, the company will withhold raises, training opportunities, and promotions to avoid “wasting” resources on them. When that worker leaves because they have no path to grow, the algorithm counts that as a successful prediction, even though the company’s own action caused the turnover. The minor efficiency gains companies claim from these tools are negligible compared to the widespread harm to worker mobility, fair treatment, and basic labor rights that justify a full ban.
