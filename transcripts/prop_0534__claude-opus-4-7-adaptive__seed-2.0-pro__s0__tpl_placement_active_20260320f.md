[P1.1] A self-custody wallet is, at bottom, a key. It is a string of numbers that lets a person control their own property directly, without asking a bank for permission. The motion says that capability should stay legal unless it is tied to specific criminal conduct. I'll defend that on three grounds: rights, precedent, and consequences.
[P1.2] First, rights. Holding a cryptographic key is closer to remembering a password or owning a safe than to operating a financial institution. Criminalizing the default possession of keys punishes a status, not an act. In every other domain we reject that framing — we don't outlaw cash wallets, private diaries, or end-to-end encrypted messengers because criminals sometimes use them.
[P1.3] The burden should sit on the state to show specific wrongdoing, not on the citizen to justify owning their own keys.
[P1.4] Second, precedent. The rule "legal by default, illegal when tied to crime" is how we already treat cash, cars, firearms in many jurisdictions, encryption software, and prepaid cards. It works because it targets conduct rather than tools. A blanket ban or licensing regime for self-custody would invert that structure and set a precedent that any dual-use technology can be outlawed wholesale on the basis of worst-case users.
[P1.5] Encryption fought this fight in the 1990s; losing it now for wallets would ripple outward.
[P1.6] Third, consequences. Banning self-custody doesn't delete crypto; it just forces everyone onto custodial intermediaries. That concentrates risk — think Mt. Gox, FTX, Celsius — exposes ordinary users to counterparty failure, and gives sophisticated criminals, who will simply use foreign or illicit tools, a comparative advantage over law-abiding users.
[P1.7] Meanwhile, dissidents, sanctioned journalists, and people in collapsing currencies lose a lifeline. Law enforcement already has powerful tools: blockchain analytics, on-ramp KYC, subpoenas, and prosecution of specific illicit transfers. Those are conduct-based and they work.
[P1.8] The question isn't whether crypto crime exists. It's whether we punish the key or the act. Punish the act.
