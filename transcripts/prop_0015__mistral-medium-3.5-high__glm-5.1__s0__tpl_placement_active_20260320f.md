[P1.1] Work requirements reduce dependency by making welfare a bridge, not a destination. For able-bodied adults without dependents, these policies establish a clear expectation: contribute through work, training, or service, or lose benefits. This simple condition transforms assistance from a passive handout into an active pathway to self-sufficiency. The results are dramatic and consistent.
[P1.2] Following 1996 welfare reform, which introduced work requirements, TANF caseloads fell dramatically within a decade, while employment among single mothers rose substantially, demonstrating a direct link between work requirements and reduced dependency. For SNAP, the pattern holds: states with work requirements for able-bodied adults without dependents report significantly higher employment rates than states without, with many moving from assistance to work. Even in Medicaid, where work requirements are newer, early adopting states documented thousands transitioning to employer-sponsored coverage. These are expected outcomes of tying benefits to effort.
[P1.3] Harm to those genuinely unable to comply is contained by design. Comprehensive exemptions cover disabled individuals, seniors, pregnant women, primary caregivers, and often those in drug treatment or experiencing homelessness. Many apparent non-compliers are actually success stories—people who found jobs and no longer need benefits. Administrative errors affect a small minority and are usually temporary, with most resolved within weeks.
[P1.4] Without work requirements, the harm is broader and more insidious: able-bodied adults remain trapped in long-term assistance, losing valuable workforce skills, professional connections, and motivation, which perpetuates intergenerational cycles of dependency that damage entire communities. The comparison is clear: work requirements lift many out of dependency while affecting few, whereas no requirements entrench dependency for many while protecting a few from temporary hardship. The net effect is unambiguous—work requirements reduce dependency more than they harm people who genuinely cannot comply, fostering a more self-reliant society.
