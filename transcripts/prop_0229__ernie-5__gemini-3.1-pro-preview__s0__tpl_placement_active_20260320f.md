[P1.1] Rehabilitation is a more important aim of criminal punishment than retribution because it directly addresses the root causes of criminal behavior, reducing recidivism and promoting long-term public safety. [P1.2] This demonstrates that rehabilitation works where retribution fails: it transforms offenders into productive citizens rather than merely punishing them. Furthermore, rehabilitation is cost-effective. Incarceration is expensive, costing tens of thousands of dollars per inmate annually, and without rehabilitation, prisons become revolving doors. [P1.3] Beyond practical benefits, rehabilitation also addresses the moral imperative to heal. Retribution often leaves victims feeling empty, as punishment does not undo harm. Rehabilitation, by preventing future crimes, protects potential victims and can include victim-offender mediation, providing closure and healing that retribution cannot offer. Finally, retribution is inherently backward-looking, fixated on punishing past actions without regard for future consequences.
[P1.4] Rehabilitation, however, is forward-thinking, aiming to prevent crime before it happens by addressing systemic issues like poverty, addiction, and lack of opportunity. A justice system that prioritizes rehabilitation is not only more humane but also smarter—it invests in people rather than just punishing them, creating a society where crime is less likely to occur in the first place. This makes rehabilitation the superior aim, as it builds a safer, more just society for everyone, whereas retribution perpetuates a cycle of harm without solving the underlying problems.
