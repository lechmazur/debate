[P1.1] Demand-side industrial policy—purchase incentives, procurement mandates, performance standards, and deployment subsidies—delivers cleaner technology faster, cheaper, and with fewer distortions than large producer subsidies. Three interlocking arguments make the case.
[P1.2] **First, demand-side policy lets markets pick winners; supply-side subsidies let politicians pick them.** When governments hand billions to specific factories, they freeze technology choices years before products reach consumers. The result is predictable: lobbying determines who gets funds, and recipients face weaker pressure to innovate because their revenue comes partly from the treasury rather than from beating competitors. Demand-side tools like consumer tax credits or clean electricity standards are technology-neutral by design. They reward whoever delivers the best product at the lowest cost.
[P1.3] That competitive pressure is exactly what drives the learning curves that have slashed solar panel and lithium-ion battery costs over the past decade.
[P1.4] **Second, demand-side policy is more capital-efficient.** A consumer rebate or procurement contract only pays out when a unit is actually sold or deployed. Producer subsidies pay out when a factory is built—regardless of whether it ever operates at capacity. We've already seen cautionary examples: subsidized battery or solar plants that underperformed or shuttered when market conditions shifted, leaving taxpayers holding the bill for idle capacity. Demand pull ensures public money tracks real-world outcomes, not ribbon-cutting ceremonies.
[P1.5] **Third, demand-side policy builds durable domestic markets that attract private investment on their own.** Investors care about whether customers will buy the product. A credible, long-term demand signal—say, a vehicle emissions standard tightening through 2035—gives manufacturers the confidence to site factories without needing a direct capital grant. The U.S. electric vehicle market illustrates this: consumer tax credits and fleet standards created demand that pulled billions in private factory investment. The demand signal did the heavy lifting; the factory subsidies were largely redundant for firms already chasing a guaranteed market.
[P1.6] Producer subsidies concentrate risk in government, invite rent-seeking, and ossify technological choices. Demand-side policy disperses decisions to markets, ties spending to results, and sustains the competitive dynamism clean tech needs. Countries should favor it.
