[P1.1] Fragmented service industries—hospitality, care work, cleaning, retail—share a common structural problem: workers are scattered across thousands of small employers, turnover is high, and individual workplaces are nearly impossible to organize. The standard response, repeatedly raising the statutory minimum wage, treats this as purely a price problem. It isn't. And that misdiagnosis is why minimum-wage hikes, however necessary, keep falling short.
[P1.2] Sectoral bargaining attacks the actual problem. By bringing together employer associations and worker representatives across an entire industry, it sets binding floors for wages, scheduling, overtime, and safety—simultaneously. A hotel cleaner's quality of life depends not just on her hourly rate but on whether she gets predictable hours, paid sick leave, and manageable workloads. A single minimum-wage number cannot touch any of that.
[P1.3] Sectoral agreements can, and routinely do.
[P1.4] The coverage argument is decisive. Australia's modern award system and the German sectoral model demonstrate that when agreements are extended to all employers in a sector by regulation, free-rider dynamics collapse. Every employer faces the same floor, so no one gains competitive advantage by undercutting on labor conditions. That is exactly the structural fix fragmented industries need.
[P1.5] Minimum-wage legislation, by contrast, leaves the multi-dimensional race to the bottom intact; employers simply compete on scheduling abuse, benefit denial, and unsafe pacing.
[P1.6] Minimum-wage hikes also suffer from chronic political friction. They require legislative action, invite industry lobbying, and tend to cluster in bursts followed by years of real-wage erosion. Sectoral agreements can be renegotiated on shorter cycles, responsive to actual conditions in a specific industry rather than the political calendar of an entire economy.
[P1.7] The PRO position does not require abandoning minimum-wage floors. It claims something narrower and clearly true: for the specific problem of setting comprehensive labor standards in fragmented service sectors, sectoral bargaining is the sharper, more durable, and more appropriate instrument.
