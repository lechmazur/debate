[C1.1] The proposition fundamentally misunderstands the difference between material property defects and the subjective, volatile nature of community dynamics. Mandating disclosure for noise complaints, party activity, or package theft transforms landlords into legal arbiters of neighborhood behavior. This is not a simple reporting task; it is a profound expansion of landlord liability.
[C1.2] Consider the legal trap: "Severe noise" or "recurring complaints" are entirely subjective metrics. If a landlord discloses that noise complaints have occurred, and a tenant disagrees with the classification, or vice versa, the landlord is immediately vulnerable to litigation. This policy invites a surge in frivolous "failure to disclose" lawsuits, where tenants use disputed noise logs as a pretext to break leases without penalty. By forcing landlords to certify the behavioral quality of neighbors, you inevitably turn housing providers into insurers of third-party actions they cannot realistically control.
[C1.3] Furthermore, this proposal incentivizes the weaponization of the complaint process. If a tenant knows that a documented history of noise complaints allows them an easy out from their lease, they have a perverse incentive to manufacture or exaggerate reports. This creates an adversarial atmosphere that destroys the landlord-tenant relationship and destabilizes the building culture.
[C1.4] Finally, we must consider the stigmatization effect. Labeling a building with an official "history of package theft" or "noise complaints" creates a permanent mark on that asset. This drives down property values and penalizes the very residents the policy aims to help, by damaging the desirability and financial viability of their housing. We should demand transparency regarding the physical state of a unit—like plumbing, mold, or structural safety—but forcing property owners to act as social arbiters and crime reporters is a regulatory overreach that will raise housing costs and diminish the stability of the rental market for everyone.
