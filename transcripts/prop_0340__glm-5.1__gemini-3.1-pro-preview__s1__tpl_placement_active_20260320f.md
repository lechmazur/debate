[P1.1] The question before us is not whether recommendation algorithms should exist—it is who decides what they optimize for. Right now, one metric governs billions of people's information diets: engagement. Platforms pick the objective, then ratchet every feed toward it. The proposition demands that users gain far more control over those objectives.
[P1.2] That shift is overdue on three grounds.
[P1.3] First, autonomy. When YouTube autoplays or Instagram refreshes, the optimization target is already chosen—retention, time-on-site, ad exposure. Calling this "personalization" masks a bait-and-switch: the system personalizes *how* to keep you, not *what* you came for. A professional checking LinkedIn for industry insights gets the same underlying optimization as someone scrolling bedtime memes, because the platform's goal is identical.
[P1.4] Genuine personalization requires letting the user set the goal—learning, connection, entertainment, or mixture thereof—and letting the algorithm serve that goal.
[P1.5] Second, welfare. A single-objective feed produces well-documented externalities: rabbit holes, doom-scrolling, outrage spirals. These are not accidents; they are the predictable output of an engagement-maximizing system applied universally. Giving users goal control would not eliminate all harms, but it would let someone who notices they are spiraling switch to a "stay informed, don't inflame" mode.
[P1.6] Today that switch does not exist. One size fits none—except the advertiser.
[P1.7] Third, feasibility. Every major platform already lets advertisers pick optimization goals: conversions, reach, brand awareness. The infrastructure for goal-setting exists; it just serves paying customers, not users. Extending that infrastructure to readers is a design choice, not a technical frontier.
[P1.8] The opposition must defend a striking position: that platforms know what billions of individuals should want better than those individuals do themselves. The burden they carry is showing that universal single-objective feeds produce better outcomes than user-directed ones—and they must define "better" without just borrowing the platform's own engagement metric.
