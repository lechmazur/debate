[P1.1] **Opening Statement**
[P1.2] The electric vehicle transition is too important—and too dependent on public infrastructure investment—to let proprietary charging systems fragment the market and hold consumers hostage. Governments should require open interoperability standards for public EV charging networks, and the case rests on three reinforcing pillars: consumer welfare, market efficiency, and the logic of public investment.
[P1.3] Start with the consumer. Imagine a driver arriving at a charging station with a low battery, only to find their vehicle incompatible with that network's connector or payment system. This isn't hypothetical—it has been the lived reality for EV drivers in markets where competing standards divide the landscape. When your phone could use any USB port, you didn't have to think about it.
[P1.4] Proprietary charging networks force drivers to research compatibility, carry multiple accounts, and route trips around network coverage rather than convenience. Open standards eliminate this friction entirely, which is precisely why the EU mandated the Combined Charging System and why even Tesla has begun opening its Supercharger network to other vehicles.
[P1.5] Now consider market efficiency. Proprietary systems create lock-in effects that punish drivers for their initial vehicle choice. If you buy a car tied to one network, switching becomes costly—not because of superior technology, but because of artificial incompatibility. Open standards ensure that charging infrastructure competes on price, reliability, and coverage rather than on captive customer bases.
[P1.6] That competitive pressure drives innovation where it matters: faster charging, better uptime, and wider geographic reach.
[P1.7] Finally, there is the principle of public investment. Governments worldwide are spending billions subsidizing EV charging infrastructure through tax credits, direct grants, and permitting fast-tracks. When public money builds a network, the public has a legitimate interest in ensuring that network serves everyone, not just the customers of one company. We don't allow proprietary toll roads funded by highway dollars to exclude certain car brands.
[P1.8] The same logic applies here.
[P1.9] Open interoperability standards are not anti-market—they are the precondition for a functioning market. The question is whether we want charging infrastructure that serves drivers or one that traps them.
