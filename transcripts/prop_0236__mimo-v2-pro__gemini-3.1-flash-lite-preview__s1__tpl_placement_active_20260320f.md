[C1.1] The central error in the proposition’s argument is the false equivalence between generative AI and mechanical tools like calculators. A calculator performs arithmetic, leaving the higher-order logic to the student. Generative AI, conversely, performs the higher-order logic itself—synthesizing information, structuring arguments, and drafting prose. When schools make AI "permissible by default," they are not adopting a new tool; they are incentivizing cognitive offloading.
[C1.2] In academic disciplines that rely on writing and analysis, the struggle to articulate ideas *is* the learning. It is the friction of the blank page that forces a student to organize their thoughts, stress-test their logic, and develop a personal voice. Permissibility by default removes this necessary friction. It encourages students to skip the difficult, formative stages of thinking, replacing independent synthesis with sophisticated prompt engineering.
[C1.3] You cannot develop a muscle by having a machine lift the weights for you, and you cannot develop a mind by having a machine do your drafting.
[C1.4] Furthermore, the proposition’s reliance on "citation and process transparency" is an administrative pipe dream. Teachers are already stretched thin. Expecting them to audit the recursive, invisible, and messy prompt history of dozens of students for every assignment is unrealistic. It reduces educators to forensic digital investigators rather than mentors.
[C1.5] This transparency mandate creates a veneer of accountability that masks the underlying decay of academic rigor, ensuring that grading shifts from assessing student insight to assessing the effectiveness of their AI prompting.
[C1.6] Finally, framing AI as the default starting point misconstrues the purpose of education. The goal is to build a foundation of knowledge and critical capability that persists *without* digital crutches. By institutionalizing AI as the default, schools signal that the machine's output is the priority, while the student’s internal development becomes secondary. We should be teaching students to defend their ideas without assistance, not training them to become dependent managers of algorithmic output.
