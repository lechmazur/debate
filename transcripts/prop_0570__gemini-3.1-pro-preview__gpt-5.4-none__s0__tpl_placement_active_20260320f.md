[P1.1] Artificial intelligence is an exceptional drill sergeant but a fundamentally flawed mentor. In secondary education, we must deploy AI where it excels—mastery practice—while fiercely protecting human-led writing feedback.
[P1.2] For mastery practice, AI is revolutionary. Deterministic subjects like grammar mechanics, foreign language conjugations, and foundational math require repetition and immediate correction. AI provides infinite patience, offering real-time, personalized drills that adapt to a student's pace until a concept is locked in. By handling these objective tasks, AI operates flawlessly and frees up educators to focus on higher-order teaching.
[P1.3] However, writing is not a deterministic task with a single correct output. Secondary school is the critical developmental window where students learn to synthesize complex ideas, develop a unique voice, and understand their audience. Making AI the primary source of writing feedback severely jeopardizes this process.
[P1.4] First, large language models are statistical prediction engines designed to generate the most probable, average text. When AI corrects student writing, it inherently pushes that writing toward a bland, homogenized baseline. It strips away the idiosyncratic voice, creative phrasing, and rhetorical risks that define authentic human expression, replacing them with a sterile, formulaic tone.
[P1.5] Second, AI evaluates structure over substance. It easily spots missing transition words or passive voice, but it frequently fails to grasp thematic depth. It is notorious for rewarding rigid, robotic structures while completely missing profound logical leaps or the emotional resonance of a student's argument.
[P1.6] Finally, writing is a vulnerable act of human communication. A human teacher understands a student’s background, recognizes when a struggling student finally takes a bold intellectual risk, and provides the empathetic encouragement necessary for growth. An algorithm cannot care about what a student has to say. If we outsource writing feedback to machines, we reduce writing to a mechanical compliance exercise rather than a powerful tool for connecting with the human world.
[P1.7] AI should build the foundational scaffolding, but humans must guide the voice.
