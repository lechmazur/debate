[P1.1] Critical minerals are not ordinary commodities. They are irreplaceable inputs for missile guidance systems, fighter jet engines, satellite components, and the entire electric-grid transition. When a firm closely tied to a rival state acquires a critical-mineral asset, it gains something no tariff or trade remedy can easily reverse: structural control over a supply chain that your military and economy cannot function without.
[P1.2] The proposition asks for a presumptive block, not an absolute ban. That distinction matters. It means the default posture of national-security review shifts to denial unless the acquirer demonstrates the transaction poses no strategic risk. This is justified by three reinforcing arguments.
[P1.3] **First, the asymmetry of consequences.** Approving a dangerous acquisition is far harder to undo than blocking a benign one. Once ownership transfers, proprietary geological data, processing know-how, and offtake contracts migrate to the new parent. Unwinding that after a geopolitical crisis erupts is practically impossible. The cost of a false negative—letting a threatening deal through—dwarfs the cost of a false positive, which merely redirects investment to other buyers.
[P1.4] **Second, rival-state-tied firms are not normal market participants.** Firms with close ties to rival governments operate under political direction, benefit from opaque subsidies, and can be compelled to weaponize supply. China's consolidation of rare-earth processing and its temporary export restrictions against Japan in 2010 illustrate how mineral dominance converts directly into coercive leverage. A presumptive block recognizes this structural reality rather than pretending each deal is a routine commercial transaction.
[P1.5] **Third, supply-chain diversity depends on keeping assets available to allied or independent operators.** Every critical-mineral deposit acquired by a rival-tied firm is one fewer deposit available to diversify supply. Blocking these acquisitions preserves the competitive landscape that market-based resilience depends on.
[P1.6] The burden should fall on the acquirer to prove safety, not on the state to prove danger after the fact. When the asset is irreplaceable and the buyer answers to a hostile capital, caution is not protectionism—it is the minimum rational posture.
