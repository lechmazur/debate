[P1.1] When body cameras were rolled out across American police departments, advocates promised a revolution in accountability. The logic seemed airtight: constant recording would deter misconduct, provide irrefutable evidence when incidents did occur, and rebuild shattered trust between law enforcement and the communities they served. Nearly a decade later, that revolution never arrived.
[P1.2] The gap between promise and performance is not a matter of opinion—it is measurable. Studies from departments in Washington D.C., Las Vegas, and across the United Kingdom found that body cameras reduced civilian complaints against officers but showed no statistically significant effect on actual use of force. Complaints dropped because behavior was documented more carefully, not necessarily because officers suddenly became more restrained. That distinction matters enormously.
[P1.3] The problem runs deeper than statistics. Officers in most jurisdictions retain substantial discretion over when to activate their cameras. Footage goes missing, storage systems fail, and recordings conveniently capture the moments before an incident ends. Even when footage is preserved, it rarely translates into accountability: prosecutors decline charges against officers with disturbing regularity, internal affairs investigations treat video evidence as one factor among many, and police unions successfully negotiate contract provisions that limit discipline regardless of what the footage shows.
[P1.4] Consider the logic of accountability. Video evidence is only as powerful as the system willing to act on it. An officer who kneels on a prone suspect's neck for nine minutes while cameras roll is not held accountable if prosecutors decline to file charges, if review boards rubber-stamp findings, or if departments settle quietly out of court. The camera captured the moment; it did not fix the system that allowed the killing to happen in the first place.
[P1.5] Body cameras brought procedural improvements—better documentation, marginally faster dispute resolution—but they were never the structural overhaul that accountability actually requires. Advocates sold a substitute for the harder work of reforming police culture, independent oversight, and prosecutorial willingness to hold officers accountable. The cameras did not fail us; we expected too little from them and too much from a device.
