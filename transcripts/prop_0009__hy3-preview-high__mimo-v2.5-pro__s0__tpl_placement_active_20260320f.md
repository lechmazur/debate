[P1.1] Solitary confinement exceeding fifteen consecutive days inflicts severe, irreversible psychological harm that meets every standard definition of torture. Humans are inherently social creatures; even brief isolation triggers acute stress responses, but the two-week mark is where damage shifts from temporary to permanent. Prolonged lack of meaningful human contact, environmental stimuli, and routine erodes cognitive function, induces paranoia, hallucinations, and self-harming impulses, and leaves many confined individuals unable to reintegrate into general population or society for years after release, with some never regaining full cognitive function even after years of community support.
[P1.2] This is not incidental harm—it is the direct, predictable result of subjecting a person to total sensory and social deprivation for more than fifteen days.
[P1.3] There is no valid penological justification for stints this long. Shorter, temporary isolation may be used to prevent immediate harm to others, but confining someone for three weeks or more without reprieve produces no measurable safety or rehabilitative benefit. Nor does it aid rehabilitation: confined individuals rarely engage in educational or behavioral programming, as isolation destroys the capacity to focus or learn. Corrections systems that rely on extended solitary do not see lower rates of prison violence; instead, they produce more volatile confined populations, with higher rates of self-harm and post-release recidivism to violent crime.
[P1.4] The incapacitation effect of fifteen days of isolation is identical to the first day, yet the harm compounds exponentially after that point.
[P1.5] Abolishing confinement over fifteen days does not eliminate all use of isolation, only the stretches that cross into wanton, unnecessary torture that serves no legitimate correctional purpose. The burden rests entirely on opponents to explain why permanently damaging a person’s mind is an acceptable price to pay for a safety measure that does not work. No civilised jurisdiction should sanction state-inflicted psychological ruin as a routine correctional tool.
