[P1.1] Proactive takedowns are a failed experiment. They play an expensive game of whack-a-mole that inflames persecution complexes while failing to stop viral falsehoods from migrating to darker corners of the internet. When platforms delete political content, they do not eliminate the idea; they signal that it is too dangerous to refute, gifting it the glamour of suppressed truth.
[P1.2] Community notes invert this logic entirely. Instead of pretending that centralized moderation teams can arbitrate contested political realities through deletion, these systems distribute the burden of verification across the user base. [P1.3] This approach defeats misinformation more effectively than takedowns because it eliminates the Streisand effect. When a misleading video about election procedures stays up but carries a crowdsourced correction from diverse ideological perspectives, the refutation travels with the falsehood rather than chasing it through replication accounts. The correction gains authority precisely because it appears alongside the disputed claim, demonstrating rather than asserting where the error lies.
[P1.4] Centralized moderation inevitably encodes the blind spots of corporate monocultures, generating skepticism about whose truth gets protected. [P1.5] Pragmatically, proactive takedowns cannot scale. Billions of daily posts make comprehensive review either bankruptingly slow or reliant on blunt automation that savages context. Community notes concentrate human attention precisely where misinformation gains traction, mobilizing collective intelligence against viral claims without the collateral damage of over-censorship.
[P1.6] Governance should prioritize correction over cancellation. By making community notes the primary tool rather than the emergency exception, platforms preserve democratic discourse while still addressing harm—achieving legitimacy that takedowns forfeited long ago.
