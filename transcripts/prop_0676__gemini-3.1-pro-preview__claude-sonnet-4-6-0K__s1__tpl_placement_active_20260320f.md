[P1.1] Automated video and personality assessments in hiring are not neutral tools for efficiency; they are opaque, discriminatory filters often built on contested science. Governments must heavily restrict their use because they systemize bias at scale and fundamentally undermine equal opportunity in the labor market.
[P1.2] First, these technologies rely on flawed proxies that structurally discriminate against vulnerable populations. Automated video assessments evaluate candidates based on eye contact, vocal intonation, pacing, and facial micro-expressions. This framework heavily penalizes neurodivergent applicants, such as those with autism, who may not display "standard" eye contact or affect. It also disadvantages non-native speakers, people from differing cultural backgrounds, and individuals with speech impediments or physical tics.
[P1.3] By equating algorithmic conformity with professional competence, employers are effectively weeding out diverse talent under the guise of objective data. Furthermore, the psychological premise of many automated personality and emotion-recognition algorithms is highly contested by experts. Predicting job performance based on automated trait profiling frequently amounts to digital pseudoscience.
[P1.4] Second, automated assessments create a black-box hiring system that denies candidates fundamental fairness and recourse. When a human hiring manager discriminates, their bias is localized, and there are legal mechanisms to investigate and challenge their decisions. When an algorithm rejects a candidate because their personality score or facial analysis flagged them as a poor fit, the applicant is given no explanation and has no way to appeal. Because a few dominant software platforms supply these tools to thousands of companies, a single biased algorithmic model can invisibly blacklist a candidate from entire sectors of the economy.
[P1.5] Finally, the power dynamic in hiring is inherently coercive. Job seekers cannot simply "opt out" of these invasive biometric and psychological evaluations if they want to earn a livelihood and pay their rent. Because the free market incentivizes cheap, rapid applicant filtering over fairness, companies will not self-regulate. Only strict government intervention can protect candidates from being systematically locked out of the economy by unchecked, discriminatory code.
