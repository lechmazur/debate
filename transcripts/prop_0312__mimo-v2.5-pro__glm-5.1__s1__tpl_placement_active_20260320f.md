[C1.1] The proposition sounds democratic, but it's built on a false premise: that free-to-air television equals access. In reality, free-to-air requires a household within broadcast range, with a working television and antenna, watching on a fixed schedule. Meanwhile, a streaming subscription on a phone—something the vast majority of households already own—delivers games on demand, anywhere, with replays, alternate angles, and multilingual commentary. The "access" argument romanticizes a delivery mechanism that is itself exclusionary to anyone without the right hardware, geography, or schedule flexibility.
[C1.2] Second, the cultural-experience framing ignores that sports leagues are not public utilities. They are enterprises that employ thousands, fund youth development pipelines, and sustain entire local economies. When leagues secure premium broadcasting rights—whether through streaming platforms or cable—they unlock revenue that directly subsidizes stadium safety improvements, community outreach, and affordable ticket initiatives. Starving leagues of that revenue to preserve a 20th-century distribution model doesn't help fans; it impoverishes the ecosystem that serves them.
[C1.3] The on-ramp argument also collapses under scrutiny. Children discover sports through social media highlights, video games, school programs, and friends—not solely because a game happened to appear on a broadcast channel they could flip past. The English Premier League's global audience explosion happened alongside increased paywall adoption, not before it. Demand follows quality and narrative, not free availability.
[C1.4] Finally, "meaningful share" is a policy prescription without a principle. What counts? Ten percent? Thirty?
[C1.5] Who enforces it, and at what cost to league autonomy? The motion asks us to override contractual freedom and market negotiation for a nostalgic ideal, burdening leagues with an ill-defined mandate while offering no mechanism and no accountability. That's not justice for fans. It's regulation that entrenches a declining technology at the expense of the very institutions it claims to protect.
